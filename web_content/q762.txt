--- [Bindy Johal - Wikipedia] ---
Bhupinder "Bindy" Singh Johal (January 14, 1971 – December 20, 1998) was an Indo-Canadian gangster from Vancouver, British Columbia. A self-confessed drug kingpin, he was known for his outspoken nature, blatant disregard for authority and his longtime rivalry with his former Punjabi Mafia Bosses turned rivals; Robbie Kandola and the Dosanjh Brothers, who led a rival Punjabi Mafia faction, while Johal controlled his own faction alongside international Drug lord Ranjit Cheema. On December 20, 1998, Johal was fatally shot in the back of the head at a crowded nightclub in Vancouver.

Bhupinder Singh Johal (17 January 1971 – 20 December 1998), commonly known as Bindy Johal, was an Indo-Canadian gangster of Punjabi Sikh descent who led organized crime operations in Vancouver, British Columbia, primarily involving drug trafficking, extortion, and contract killings through his "Elite" hit squad.[1][2] Born in Punjab, India, Johal immigrated to Canada with his family at age four and began his criminal career in high school, escalating to notoriety in the 1990s for a flashy lifestyle funded by illicit earnings reportedly reaching C$500,000 to C$900,000 weekly from narcotics and related rackets.[3][1] His 1995 trial for the murders of Jimmy and Ron Dosanjh, one of the most expensive in Canadian history, collapsed due to key witness recantations, leading to acquittal despite substantial evidence presented.[4] Johal was fatally shot in the head at the Palladium nightclub in Vancouver on 20 December 1998, an assassination linked to escalating rivalries within the Punjabi mafia underworld.[5] Posthumously, Johal has attained cult status among certain Indo-Canadian youth as a symbol of defiance against systemic marginalization, though academic analyses frame this as a folk devil exacerbating community concerns over gang proliferation and integration failures.[6][4] Early Life Birth and Family Background Bhupinder Singh Johal, known as Bindy Johal, was born on January 14, 1971, in Punjab, India.[6] His family, part of the Jatt Sikh Johal clan, immigrated to Canada when he was four years old, settling in Vancouver, British Columbia, in 1975. [4] Johal was raised in a Sikh household by his parents, though specific details about his father's or mother's occupations or backgrounds remain undocumented in public records.[3] The family's migration aligned with broader patterns of Punjabi Sikh immigration to Canada during the 1970s, driven by economic opportunities and family reunification policies.[1] Early accounts describe Johal as temperamental from a young age, traits possibly influenced by the challenges of adapting to a new cultural environment, but no verified evidence links these directly to familial dynamics.[7] Upbringing and Early Environment Bhupinder Singh Johal immigrated to Vancouver, British Columbia, from Punjab, India, at the age of four in 1975, alongside his parents, who were part of the Jatt Sikh Johal clan within the Punjabi-Sikh diaspora.[3] His family settled in the Greater Vancouver area, where his father worked as a mill laborer and his mother as a secretary, reflecting the modest socioeconomic circumstances common among early South Asian immigrants seeking economic opportunities in Canada during the 1970s.Following his parents' separation, Johal was primarily raised by his mother in a single-parent household, an environment that sources describe as initially unremarkable amid the challenges of immigrant adaptation, including cultural transitions and community integration within Vancouver's expanding Punjabi-Sikh enclaves. These neighborhoods, concentrated in areas like Surrey and East Vancouver, featured tight-knit ethnic networks but also socioeconomic pressures such as limited upward mobility and intergenerational clashes between traditional Punjabi values and Western influences, which some analyses link to heightened youth vulnerability in the diaspora.[1][6]Johal exhibited early behavioral issues, characterized by a temperamental disposition and aversion to authority, leading to his expulsion from secondary school for disciplinary violations.[7] This pattern, noted in retrospective accounts, occurred against the backdrop of a burgeoning Indo-Canadian youth subculture in the 1980s, where rapid immigration growth—Vancouver's South Asian population surged from under 10,000 in 1971 to over 100,000 by 1991—fostered environments of both communal support and latent tensions, including exposure to petty crime and gang precursors amid economic marginalization for some families.[4][6] Limited primary documentation exists on his precise daily influences, but the absence of structured paternal guidance post-separation is cited as a contributing factor to his early defiance. Entry into Crime Initial Delinquencies Johal's first documented criminal offense occurred in 1989 while he was a student at Sir Charles Tupper Secondary School in Vancouver, British Columbia. At age 18, he assaulted the school's vice-principal in a brutal attack that necessitated emergency medical treatment for the victim. [3] For this assault, Johal was expelled from the school and sentenced to 60 days in jail. No prior arrests or juvenile records have been publicly detailed in available accounts. First Major Alliances Johal's initial entry into organized crime involved aligning with the Dosanjh brothers, Jimmy and Ron, who were prominent Indo-Canadian gangsters controlling aspects of Vancouver's drug trade and extortion rackets in the late 1980s and early 1990s. Following a youth detention sentence for assaulting his high school vice-principal, during which he was exposed to gang networks, Johal joined their operations upon release, serving primarily as an enforcer and hitman.[6] This alliance marked his transition from petty delinquency to structured criminal activity, where he handled violent debt collection and cocaine distribution through dial-a-dope schemes on the streets.[6][3]The Dosanjhs provided Johal with protection and opportunities to build influence within the emerging Punjabi underworld, amid competition from established groups like Hells Angels affiliates.[6] However, the relationship deteriorated due to disputes over control and profits, leading to accusations that Johal orchestrated Jimmy Dosanjh's shooting death in an East Vancouver alley around late February 1994 and Ron Dosanjh's murder in April 1994.[8][9] Johal, along with associates, faced first-degree murder charges for both killings but was acquitted in 1995 following a mistrial influenced by juror misconduct.[6] These events solidified Johal's reputation for ruthlessness while fracturing early alliances and sparking retaliatory violence in Vancouver's gang scene.[6] Criminal Activities Drug Trafficking and Extortion Bindy Johal engaged in drug trafficking operations primarily centered on cocaine distribution in Vancouver's Lower Mainland during the 1990s. Initially, he sold cocaine through a dial-a-dope network for the Dosanjh brothers, leaders of an early Punjabi organized crime group, before hijacking their cocaine business following Jimmy Dosanjh's imprisonment.[1][3] His enterprise sourced cocaine from South American suppliers and distributed it via associates in Metro Vancouver's gang networks, contributing to turf wars over control of the trade.[1]Johal's group reportedly generated significant revenue from drug distribution alongside other illicit activities like auto theft, with estimates of CAD 4 million annually at its peak in the mid-1990s.[6] He was convicted of racketeering related to these operations, reflecting structured efforts to dominate local cocaine markets through violence and alliances, such as with the Los Diablos gang introduced via associate Faizal Dean.[1]Extortion formed a core component of Johal's enforcement tactics, often tied to debt collection in the drug trade. Death The Nightclub Assassination On December 20, 1998, at approximately 4:30 a.m., Bindy Johal was assassinated by gunfire at the Palladium nightclub in Vancouver, British Columbia.[5][22] Johal, then 27 years old, was dancing on the crowded dance floor when he was shot multiple times from behind, sustaining fatal wounds including one to the back of the head.[23][6] The attack unfolded amid a packed venue with hundreds of patrons present, yet the perpetrator or perpetrators fled the scene undetected, leaving no immediate arrests.[5][22]Emergency responders pronounced Johal dead at the location, with Vancouver Police confirming the homicide shortly thereafter and sealing off the nightclub for investigation.[22] The shooting drew swift media attention due to Johal's notoriety as a self-admitted drug trafficker and figure in local organized crime, though authorities reported no suspects or motives publicly at the time.[5] Ballistic evidence indicated close-range fire from a handgun, consistent with targeted execution rather than random violence.[23] The incident marked a violent end to Johal's public activities, which had included court appearances and media interviews in the preceding years.[6] Motives and Suspects The assassination of Bindy Johal on December 20, 1998, at the Marriott Hotel nightclub in Vancouver was widely attributed to retaliatory motives stemming from his extensive role in drug trafficking disputes and contract killings within British Columbia's Indo-Canadian underworld. --- [Much before Goldie Brar, Bindy Johal was the Indo-Canadian ...] ---
MUMBAI: Canada has not only been home to Khalistan sympathisers, but also provided refuge to Indian gangsters wanted by enforcement agencies. Even as the names of Goldie Brar and Arsh Dalla do the rounds as wanted gangsters, with the National Investigation Agency (NIA) hot in their pursuit, a name that comes to mind is Binda Johal, who could be described as the original Indo-Canadian gangster. Such was Johal's notoriety that Indo-Canadian director Deepa Mehta, who is well-known for films such as 'Fire' and 'Earth', made a critically panned movie, 'Beeba Boys', inspired by the gangster, with Randeep Hooda playing Jeet Johar, the character modelled after him. Born in India on January 14, 1971, Johal found himself in Vancouver at the age of four when his family landed there. After he was the target of an armed assault in the aftermath of the assassination of Indira Gandhi, his parents removed his turban and got his hair cut. Not that it was end of the troubles that visited him -- only, he was now the perpetrator, not the victim. He was expelled from one school for having brutally assaulted his vice-principal and from another for the possession of a dangerous weapon. From then on, Johal started flirting with crime and sank deeper into it. All this was happening at a time when Vancouver's Hispanic immigrants were being overshadowed by South Asians. Interestingly, one of those who went to school with Johal was Harjit Sajjan, who later rose to become Canada's avowedly pro-Khalistan defence minister. Johal became a part of an old Hispanic gang called Los Diablos ('The Devils'), which became more Indo-Canadian, and eventually became known as the Punjabi Mafia. The crime newbie became infamous as a hit-man working for Jimmy and Ron Dosanjh, the more established gangsters. He betrayed and killed both of them as he got into the business of pushing cocaine. On March 14, 1991, Colombian drug trafficker Teodoro Salcedo was murdered in Vancouver. Jimmy Dosanjh was charged with the murder and arrested. When Dosanjh was in jail awaiting his trial, which ended in his acquittal, Johal, who was diagnosed to have an antisocial personality disorder, made himself the leader of the gang. At the peak of his career, which spanned drug running, manufacturing synthetic narcotics such as Fentanyl and executing hit jobs, Johal was reported to have amassed a personal fortune pegged at Canadian $4 million. He was also said to have links with the terrorist organisation, Babbar Khalsa International, and charged with the killing of investigative journalist Ron Dosanjh. Not one to live his life in the shadows, Johal soon acquired a public profile for his flashy lifestyle, media appearances and penchant for blowing up money in nightclubs. When he was finally arrested and brought to trial for the killing of the Dosanjh brothers, it took a scandalous turn because it turned out that juror Gillian Guess was involved in a romantic relationship with Johal's co-accused Peter Gill. This trial collapsed thereafter and Johal walked out a free man, only to be implicated in a strip club stabbing and a number of drive-by shootings. Johal was barely 29 when he met the same fate that he visited upon his victims. He was gunned down at the Palladium nightclub in Vancouver by an unidentified assailant in the presence of 300 witnesses, none of whom came forward to point out the attacker. He may not have been brought to justice, but Johal did get his poetic justice.
