--- [Necoclí - Wikipedia] ---
Necoclí is a town and municipality in Antioquia Department, Colombia. Located on the eastern shore of the Gulf of Urabá, it is part of the Urabá Antioquia sub-region. The population is predominantly Afro-Colombian.
It was founded as a Spanish city called San Sebastián de Buena Vista. One of Colombia's oldest towns, it was founded in 1509 by Pedro de Heredia, who died in 1555.
Some early reports of the town can be found in chapter 9 of Pedro Cieza de León's Crónica del Perú (1553).
Necoclí Airport is a general aviation airport, without scheduled flights. Buses link the town with Cartagena, the department capital of Medellín, and other points. There are scheduled launches to two towns without road access, Capurganá and Acandí, across the gulf and near the Panamanian border.

Necoclí Geography and Environment Location and Physical Features Necoclí lies in the Urabá subregion of Antioquia Department, northwestern Colombia, along the eastern shore of the Gulf of Urabá, which connects to the Caribbean Sea. The municipal seat is positioned at approximately 8°26′N 76°47′W, with an elevation of 6 meters above sea level.[11] The area encompasses coastal lowlands typical of the Caribbean fringe, facilitating maritime access and influencing local ecosystems. The municipality covers 1,256 square kilometers of terrain dominated by plains and gentle undulations, punctuated by hills not exceeding 200 meters in elevation.[11][12] Inland from the coast, the landscape features tropical lowland formations, including river valleys and forested zones. Principal waterways, such as the Necoclí, Bobal, and Caimán Viejo rivers, originate in the surrounding highlands and discharge into the Gulf of Urabá, shaping the region's hydrology and sediment distribution.[13] This configuration contributes to dynamic coastal processes, including sediment deposition and estuarine environments. Climate and Environmental Challenges Necoclí features a tropical climate with average annual temperatures of 26.6 °C and daily highs reaching approximately 30 °C, alongside lows around 25 °C. Annual precipitation averages 2,367 mm, distributed unevenly with drier conditions from January to March and heavier rainfall throughout the rest of the year, contributing to high humidity and frequent wet weather.[14] Deforestation poses a significant challenge, with the municipality retaining 56.9 thousand hectares of natural forest in 2020—covering 44% of its land area—but losing 416 hectares in 2024 alone, equivalent to 248 kilotons of CO₂ emissions. This loss stems from agricultural expansion, logging, and land use changes in the surrounding Urabá region.[15] Coastal erosion and sedimentation plague the Gulf of Urabá shoreline near Necoclí, driven by natural sediment dynamics and intensified by human activities such as river damming and land clearance, leading to receding coastlines and failed short-term mitigation efforts like breakwaters. Pollution from urban runoff, agribusiness wastewater, and agrochemicals from banana plantations further degrades water quality, with studies documenting metal accumulation in sediments and overall ecosystem contamination.[16][17][18] Swamps like Marimonda and El Salado exhibit deteriorating water quality due to anthropogenic pressures, including untreated domestic sewage and inadequate wastewater infrastructure in nearby communities. Recent surges in migrant transit—over 500,000 annually through the Darién Gap route—have exacerbated beach and river pollution with plastic waste and human refuse, straining local cleanup capacities and threatening marine ecosystems.[19][20][21] Broader regional vulnerabilities, such as mangrove degradation—Colombia lost 14% of its mangroves (nearly 50,000 hectares) over the past 40 years—and flood risks from intense rainfall, compound these issues, with heavy precipitation triggering landslides and inundation in low-lying coastal areas.[22][23]History Founding and Colonial Era The territory comprising modern Necoclí was inhabited by Carib indigenous groups prior to European contact, who maintained semi-nomadic societies focused on fishing, hunting, and rudimentary agriculture in the coastal Gulf of Urabá region.[24] These communities resisted early incursions, contributing to the challenges faced by initial Spanish expeditions.[25] On January 20, 1510, Spanish explorer Alonso de Ojeda established San Sebastián de Urabá as the first fortified European settlement on the South American mainland, located near the present-day site of Necoclí on the eastern shore of the Gulf of Urabá.[26] This outpost, comprising wooden structures and defensive palisades, aimed to secure Spanish claims in the newly designated province of Nueva Andalucía but encountered immediate hardships, including supply shortages and hostile encounters with local Carib warriors armed with poisoned arrows and spears.[27] The venture, backed by 70 settlers and limited livestock, failed within months; Ojeda abandoned it in favor of seeking aid from Hispaniola, marking an early illustration of the logistical and military difficulties in continental colonization.[26] Throughout the 16th century, the Necoclí area remained a peripheral frontier of Spanish influence, with sporadic expeditions for reconnaissance and resource extraction rather than sustained settlement, as indigenous resistance and tropical diseases deterred permanent occupation.[28] Archaeological evidence, including ceramics, metal tools, and structural remnants uncovered in the early 2000s, corroborates brief colonial interactions blending indigenous and European material culture, though no major towns emerged until later colonial expansions elsewhere in Urabá.[29] The region's integration into the Viceroyalty of New Granada proceeded slowly, primarily through encomienda systems granting labor rights over native populations to Spanish grantees, but persistent unrest limited economic outputs like pearl fishing and basic provisioning.[30]Post-Independence Developments Following Colombian independence in 1819, Necoclí's territory experienced limited settlement and development as part of the sparsely populated Urabá frontier, with colonial-era outposts largely abandoned and effective recolonization delayed until the late 19th century.[24][31] Early post-independence activities centered on resource extraction, including hunting of turtles from nearby Barú island by colonists in the 1820s.[24] By the mid-19th century, extractive economies gained traction, notably the harvesting of raicilla (ipecac root, used medicinally) in areas like Mulatos and near Necoclí's nascent urban center, amid broader regional displacements following slavery's abolition in 1851.[24][32] Administratively, the territory shifted dependence to the newly founded Turbo (also known as Pisisi) in 1839, remaining a subordinate corregimiento or district without municipal status until 1978.[24] Into the early 20th century, Necoclí functioned as a small caserío of Caribbean coastal colonists, sustaining through subsistence agriculture—such as rice, yuca, maize, and ñame—and hunting of deer, wild pigs, and birds, with population remaining modest amid the region's isolation.[24][33] This period reflected Urabá's broader pattern of intermittent, extractive colonization rather than sustained urban or infrastructural growth, constrained by geographic challenges and national priorities focused elsewhere.[34]20th-Century Conflicts and Growth In the mid-20th century, Necoclí, as part of the Urabá region in Antioquia, experienced escalating land disputes and violence tied to agricultural expansion, particularly the banana industry, which began commercial consolidation around 1963 with the establishment of large plantations and export infrastructure.[35] This growth attracted colonizers and laborers, but also fueled conflicts between landowners, emerging unions, and early guerrilla groups like the Ejército Popular de Liberación (EPL), which exerted influence in northern Urabá through extortion and control of rural areas starting in the 1970s.[36] Colombian banana exports surged from modest levels in the 1950s to over 1 million tons annually by the 1990s, driving economic activity in coastal municipalities like Necoclí through related commerce, fishing, and port-related trade, though benefits were unevenly distributed amid weak state presence.[37] The intensification of Colombia's armed conflict in the 1980s and 1990s profoundly impacted Necoclí, as guerrilla factions, including the EPL and FARC dissidents, clashed with paramilitary groups formed to counter leftist insurgencies and protect agribusiness interests, particularly banana plantations targeted for "revolutionary taxes."[38]Paramilitary incursions escalated in northern Urabá, with groups under Fidel and Carlos Castaño establishing dominance by the late 1980s, leading to forced recruitment, including minors aged 10-17, and the creation of local "armies of death" numbering nearly 2,000 fighters by the mid-1990s.[39] A notable event was the March 22, 1990, massacre on the road to Necoclí, where paramilitaries executed at least 25 civilians suspected of guerrilla sympathies, exemplifying the cycle of retaliatory violence that displaced thousands and stalled infrastructure development.[40] Despite pervasive insecurity, Necoclí saw modest population and economic growth, with municipal inhabitants rising from under 10,000 in the 1960s to over 30,000 by 1993, supported by banana-related jobs and informal trade, though homicide rates in Urabá exceeded national averages, often linked to union killings—over 800 banana workers assassinated between 1986 and 2001, many in nearby zones influencing Necoclí's labor dynamics.[41]Paramilitary control from the 1990s facilitated some agribusiness expansion by suppressing strikes, but at the cost of human rights abuses, including massacres and disappearances, which reports from Colombia's Centro Nacional de Memoria Histórica attribute to intertwined economic and ideological motives rather than isolated criminality.[38] By century's end, these conflicts had entrenched Necoclí as a frontier of contested territory, hindering sustainable growth while banana production accounted for much of the region's GDP contribution.[37]Demographics Population Trends The resident population of Necoclí municipality grew substantially from 9,456 inhabitants in 1964 to 27,689 in 1985, reflecting accelerated self-identified ethnically as follows: approximately 48% as Afro-Colombian (negro, mulato, afrodescendiente, or afrocolombiano), 48% without specific ethnic affiliation (predominantly mestizo), and 4% This ethnic makeup reflects the Urabá region's historical patterns of African-descended labor in colonial agriculture and mining, mestizo colonization from Antioquia's interior since the 19th century, and residual indigenous presence from pre-Columbian groups displaced by settlement and violence.[47] Culturally, the Afro-Colombian majority shapes local expressions through Caribbean-influenced music like bullerengue and mapalé, oral traditions, and cuisine featuring seafood and plantains, often showcased in festivals such as the Fiesta del Mar. Local authorities identify tourism as the sector with the highest growth potential, driven by the area's biodiversity and coastal access, though infrastructure limitations have historically constrained international appeal.[50] Development plans highlight its role in diversifying beyond primary production, with emphasis on sustainable practices to preserve environmental assets. Fishing encompasses artisanal coastal operations and inland aquaculture, focusing on species like tilapia and cachama for local consumption and markets. This activity integrates with the Gulf of Urabá's marine resources, contributing to food security and minor exports, though yields remain modest due to traditional methods and environmental pressures. Government reports note its enrichment of the economic base alongside agriculture, with potential for expansion through better harbor facilities.[50] Agriculture dominates land use, with key crops including plantains, coconuts, corn, rice, and assorted tropical fruits suited to the fertile soils. Cattle ranching, particularly Cebú breeds, supports dairy production and a local cheese industry, complementing crop outputs for both subsistence and commercial sales.[50] These activities underpin rural employment, though vulnerability to weather and market fluctuations persists, as outlined in municipal development strategies aiming for agropecuary dynamism.Economic Shifts from Migration Transit Necoclí's economy, previously reliant on domestic tourism, fishing, and subsistence agriculture, underwent a profound transformation beginning around 2021 as migrant transit volumes surged through the Darién Gap route. Local businesses rapidly adapted to cater to the influx, with hotels, restaurants, and informal vendors proliferating to provide lodging, meals, and supplies such as boots, backpacks, and waterproof gear essential for the jungle crossing. By 2023, daily migrant arrivals peaked at nearly 1,000, fueling commerce and generating thousands of jobs in a historically marginalized region, where migration-related services supplanted traditional sectors as the primary income source.[6][4][51] This shift manifested in tangible economic indicators, including the dollarization of transactions—many establishments began pricing and accepting U.S. dollars due to migrants' preferences and the influx of foreign currency. Transport operators reported an 88% increase in migrant passages in mid-2023, driving demand for boat services to Capurganá and beyond, while real estate saw a boom from migrant-focused accommodations. Organized groups, including elements of the Clan del Golfo, extracted fees from these operations, with estimated profits from migrant transport packages alone reaching $17.5 million to $25 million in 2023, injecting illicit revenues into local circuits but also distorting market dynamics.[4][52][53] The dependency on transit flows introduced volatility; over 520,000 migrants crossed the Darién in 2023, but by early 2024 and into 2025, declining northward movements and emerging reverse migrations—approximately 100 individuals daily arriving from Panama—signaled potential contraction, prompting concerns over unsustainable reliance on ephemeral demand. Farmers increasingly abandoned agriculture for higher-margin migrant services, exacerbating food import needs and long-term vulnerabilities in a town where tourism had not fully recovered post-COVID-19.[7][51][54]Role as Migration Transit Point Strategic Position and Routes Necoclí occupies a coastal position on the eastern shore of the Gulf of Urabá in the Urabá Antioquia subregion of Antioquia Department, northwestern Colombia, providing direct maritime access to the Darién Gap region near the Panama border.[52] This location, roughly 60 miles southeast of the Darién Gap's primary crossing points, positions Necoclí as a critical embarkation hub for migrants avoiding the impassable land barrier of the Darién, a dense rainforest stretch lacking roads that spans the Colombia-Panama frontier.[7] The Gulf of Urabá serves as a natural corridor, enabling boat transport that bypasses overland obstacles while connecting inland routes from Medellín and Turbo to border-adjacent areas.[9] The dominant migration pathway from Necoclí involves commercial speedboats departing from its port, crossing the Gulf of Urabá to Colombian enclaves like Capurganá or Acandí, which lie adjacent to the Panamanian border.[55] These vessels, often limited to capacities accommodating around 500 migrants daily across multiple boats, transport passengers northward in trips lasting several hours, after which travelers undertake the arduous 7- to 15-day jungle trek through the Darién Gap toward Panama's Bajo Chiquito reception point.[56][7] Alternative land routes from Necoclí to these embarkation sites exist but are less utilized due to security risks and terrain challenges posed by armed groups in the region.[8] Necoclí's airport further enhances its strategic role, facilitating rapid influx from South American origins or domestic hubs like Medellín, funneling migrants into the maritime leg without reliance on extended bus travel through conflict-prone Urabá territories.[6] This multimodal connectivity—combining air, sea, and foot—has solidified Necoclí's status as a primary gateway, with over 230,000 migrant departures recorded from the municipality in the year leading to September 2023 alone.[52] The routes' reliance on local operators underscores vulnerabilities to weather disruptions, capacity constraints, and control by smuggling networks affiliated with groups like the Gulf Clan.[57]Major Migration Waves and Statistics Migration through Necoclí, a primary embarkation point for the Darién Gap route to Panama, remained limited --- [Necocli: Crabs, coconuts and volcano swimming] ---
The predominantly Afro-Colombian town of Necocli, at the northern tip of Antioquia, provides the perfect location to experience the more untouched parts of Colombia’s Caribbean coastline. Although Necocli is one of Colombia’s oldest towns, founded in 1509 by Spanish conquistadors, it has not made much headway in terms of tourist attractions. Instead, the town’s charm relies on its surrounding natural beauty, which provides visitors with the opportunity to relax and soak up the sun. Sun is one attraction that Necocli provides in abundance, being one of the few places in Colombia that remains unaffected by the year’s devastating rains, a fact that my travel companion’s peeling back can attest to. Although the town is situated right by the water’s edge, in the Gulf of Uraba, visitors are better off headquartering further down the beach, in order to maximize the tranquility that is this region’s greatest asset. Travelers have the option of spending their sleeping hours in tents, hammocks, guest houses or even up high on an open wooden chalet, where they can be truly intoxicated by the refreshing sea breeze. That said, after drinking a few rum and coconut cocktails straight out of freshly collected coconuts, the beach itself may seem the most appealing place to rest. Each morning, you can wake up and gorge yourself on sweet, juicy mangos before embarking on a crabbing adventure, perhaps the most rewarding experience the area has to offer, if you manage to succeed in your mission, that is. Yet with the help of some local advice, delivered in the form of a 10-year-old, you will not fail to catch crabs in abundance and take them back to cook in a delicious coconut soup. If hunting for your dinner sounds unappealing, then the delicious sea food on offer at any number of places around Necocli will surely hit the spot — even if the local delicacies of iguana or turtle do not. One day trip well worth the time is a visit to the volcano that rests about a 40 minute walk outside Necocli. This hidden gem is so devoid of tourism that one could be forgiven for questioning the wisdom behind jumping straight into the murky, muddy crater of the volcano. Nevertheless, the brave (or brainless) ones who do decide to take the plunge will be richly rewarded, and not just in the supposed wonders that it can do for the skin. The feeling is truly bizarre as you half-float, half-sink in the midst of this active volcano, toying with the squelchy, infirm floor beneath you. Anybody attempting a visit would do well to bring a spare bottle of water to wash with afterwards, as there are certainly no showers or taps provided by the local tourism authority, if one even exists. Once safely back on the western facing beach, you can watch a spectacular sunset, before lighting up the night sky yourself with ever impressive bonfires or simply sitting back to gaze at the luminescent shooting stars that never seem to stop whizzing by. Necocli’s location on the Gulf of Uraba also provides the perfect starting point from which to catch a boat and explore the nearby picturesque paradises of Capurgana and Sapzurro, in the department of Choco, close to the Panama border.
