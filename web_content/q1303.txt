Kirk and D. Rudy at Palomar Observatory in California, it resides in the inner region of the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter.[1][2]The asteroid's orbit has a low eccentricity of 0.104 and an inclination of 2.97° relative to the ecliptic, placing its perihelion at 1.994 AU and aphelion at 2.456 AU, ensuring it remains safely within the main belt without close approaches to inner planets beyond 1.009 AU to Earth.[1] Its geometric albedo of 0.231 contributes to an absolute magnitude of 13.45, making it visible under dark skies at opposition with apparent magnitudes around 14–15.[1] Over 4,800 astrometric observations, spanning from 1942 to 2025, have refined its ephemeris, confirming its stable main-belt trajectory with a Tisserand parameter of 3.638 with respect to Jupiter.[1][2]Named in honor of the Bohemian writer Franz Kafka (1883–1924), renowned for works like The Trial, The Castle, and "The Metamorphosis" that explore themes of alienation and bureaucracy, the asteroid's designation reflects a tradition of honoring literary figures in minor planet nomenclature.[1][2] Physical details remain limited, with no confirmed spectral type, though its size and albedo suggest a stony composition typical of S-type or related asteroids in the inner belt; a rotation period of approximately 2,766 hours (over 115 days) indicates a slow tumbler.[1] As of 2025, 3412 Kafka poses no collision risk and continues to be monitored by surveys like Pan-STARRS and Catalina Sky Survey for refined orbital data.[2] Discovery and Designation Discovery Circumstances 3412 Kafka was discovered on January 10, 1983, by American astronomers Randolph Kirk and Donald Rudy at Palomar Observatory, California, United States.[2][1] The discovery occurred during a systematic search for minor planets as part of ongoing sky survey efforts at the facility.[2]On the night of discovery, the asteroid was observed twice at an apparent magnitude of 17, with right ascension 07h 24m 44.51s and declination +23° 51' 13.9" for the first position, followed by 07h 24m 42.51s and +23° 51' 15.3" for the second.[2] These initial astrometric measurements provided preliminary position and motion data, indicating non-stellar movement consistent with a solar system object.[2]Confirmation observations were promptly obtained at the same observatory on January 11 and 12, 1983, yielding additional positions such as 07h 23m 32.88s, +23° 51' 33.7" on the 11th, and two more on the 12th at magnitudes around 17.[2] Further follow-up in February 1983 from Palomar solidified the trajectory, leading to the provisional designation 1983 AU2.[2] These early observations distinguished it from background stars and confirmed its asteroidal nature.[2] Provisional and Final Designation Upon its discovery on 10 January 1983 at Palomar Observatory, the asteroid was given the provisional designation 1983 AU2, following the standard convention established by the International Astronomical Union (IAU).
