--- [Lygia Pape - Wikipedia] ---
Lygia Pape (7 April 1927 – 3 May 2004) was a Brazilian visual artist, sculptor, engraver, and filmmaker, who was a key figure in the Concrete movement and a later co-founder of the Neo-Concrete Movement in Brazil during the 1950s and 1960s. Along with Hélio Oiticica and Lygia Clark, she was an important artist in the expansion of contemporary art in Brazil and pushed geometric art to include aspects of interaction and to engage with ethical and political themes.

--- [Eat Me | Hammer Museum] ---
Lygia Pape, Artist Eat Me , 1975 Lygia Pape's Eat Me presents a very tight shot of the mouths of two men (the artists Artur Barrio and Cláudio Sampaio) filmed by a static camera. The pace of the film speeds up as the mouths swallow and spit out fragmented objects. We see the white of the teeth, along with lips, tongue, and beard hairs in images at once attractive and repulsive. The image alludes to the Brazilian cultural metaphor of anthropophagy—the idea of devouring the other to absorb its energy—which served as an allegory of the violence gripping Brazil at the time the piece was made. The work brings together ideas of the abject, contamination, and the visceral, appealing to sensorial perceptions that create knowledge of the body. En Eat Me (Cómeme) de Lygia Pape, una boca en un primerísimo primer plano está filmada con una cámara fija. Se trata de bocas masculinas (son los artistas Artur Barrio y Cláudio Sampaio) filmadas en un ritmo que se acelera mientras tragan y expulsan objetos fragmentados. Vemos los blancos de los dientes, los labios, la lengua, los pelos de la barba, en imágenes que mezclan atracción y repulsión. Es una imagen que cita la metáfora de la antropofagia brasileña: devorar, devorarse al otro para tomar su energía. Una metáfora cultural que también funciona como alegoría de la violencia en ese momento en el Brasil. Una obra que condensa nociones de lo abyecto, la contaminación, la visceralidad. Apelan a percepciones sensoriales que crean conocimiento del cuerpo.
