Toronto's first mayor, William Lyon Mackenzie, was appointed in 1834 after his Reform coalition won the new City of Toronto's first election and he was chosen by the Reformers. The most recent election to the office of mayor was a by-election on June 26, 2023 in which Olivia Chow was elected. Chow formally took office on July 12, 2023.
If a vacancy occurs, the City of Toronto Act explicitly states that the deputy mayor of Toronto assumes certain limited mayoral powers, but remains deputy mayor during a vacancy. They do not become an acting or interim mayor.

Instead, after each annual election of aldermen and councilmen, the assembled council would elect one of their members as mayor. For all other years, mayors were directly elected by popular vote, except in rare cases where a mayor was appointed by council to fill an unexpired term of office. The "City of Toronto" has changed substantially over the years: the city annexed or amalgamated with neighbouring communities in 1882, 1891, 1908, 1912, and 1967. The most sweeping change was in 1998 when the six municipalities comprising Metropolitan Toronto – East York, Etobicoke, North York, Scarborough, York, and the former city of Toronto – and its regional government were amalgamated into a single City of Toronto (colloquially dubbed the "megacity") by an act of the provincial government. The newly created position of mayor for the resulting single-tier megacity replaced all of the mayors of the former Metro municipalities. It also abolished the office of the Metro Chairman, which had formerly been the most senior political figure before amalgamation. Outgoing North York Mayor Mel Lastman was elected as the first mayor of the amalgamated Toronto in 1997 and re-elected in 2000. This is a list of mayors of Toronto, Ontario, Canada. Toronto's first mayor, William Lyon Mackenzie was appointed in 1834 after his Reform coalition won the new City of Toronto's first election, and Mackenzie was chosen by the Reformers. History From 1834 to 1857, and again from 1867 to 1873, Toronto mayors were not elected directly by the public. Instead, after each annual election of aldermen and councilmen, the assembled council would elect one of their members as mayor. For all other years, mayors were directly elected by popular vote, except in rare cases where a mayor was appointed by council to fill an unexpired term of office. Prior to 1834, Toronto municipal leadership was governed by the Chairman of the General Quarter Session of Peace of the Home District Council. Through 1955 the term of office for the mayor and council was one year; it then varied between two and three years until a four-year term was adopted starting in 2006. (See List of Toronto municipal elections.) The "City of Toronto" has changed substantially over the years: the city annexed or amalgamated with neighbouring communities or areas 49 times from in 1883 to 1967. The most sweeping change was in 1998, when the six municipalities comprising Metropolitan Toronto—East York, Etobicoke, North York, Scarborough, York, and the former city of Toronto–and its regional government were amalgamated into a single City of Toronto (colloquially dubbed the "megacity") by an act of the provincial government. The newly created position of mayor for the resulting single-tier mega-city replaced all of the mayors of the former Metro municipalities. It also abolished the office of the Metro Chairman, which had formerly been the most senior political figure in the Metro government before amalgamation. According to Victor Loring Russell, author of Mayors of Toronto Volume I, 14 out of the first 29 mayors were lawyers. According to Mark Maloney who is writing The History of the Mayors of Toronto, 58 of Toronto's 64 mayors (up to Ford) have been Protestant, white, English-speaking, Anglo-Saxon, property-owning males. There have been two women (Hall and Rowlands) and three Jewish mayors (Phillips, Givens[3] and Lastman). Art Eggleton is the longest-serving mayor of Toronto, serving from 1980 until 1991. Eggleton later served in federal politics from 1993 until 2004, and was appointed to the Senate of Canada in 2005. David Breakenridge Read held the post of mayor of Toronto for the shortest period. Read was mayor for only fifty days in 1858. No Toronto mayor has been removed from office. Toronto's 64th mayor, Rob Ford, lost a conflict of interest trial in 2012, and was ordered to vacate his position; but the ruling was stayed pending an appeal, which Ford won to remain in office. Due to his substance abuse admission and controversy in 2013, Council stripped him of many powers on November 15, transferring them to the Deputy Mayor.[6] From May until July, 2014, Ford took a leave of absence from the mayoralty to enter drug rehabilitation. --- [Mayor of Toronto] ---
The mayor of Toronto is the head of Toronto City Council and chief executive officer of the municipal government. The mayor is elected alongside city council every four years on the fourth Monday of October; there are no term limits. While in office, mayors are styled His/Her Worship.
Olivia Chow has served as the 66th and current mayor of Toronto since July 12, 2023, after winning the 2023 by-election.

List of mayors of Toronto Origins and Evolution of the Mayoralty Establishment in 1834 and Early Governance The Town of York was incorporated as the City of Toronto on March 6, 1834, through the City of Toronto Act enacted by the Legislature of Upper Canada, marking the formal establishment of municipal governance under British colonial administration.[7] This transition endowed the new city with a council comprising a mayor and aldermen, initially elected by eligible male property owners, who then selected the mayor from among themselves, emphasizing elite representative selection over direct popular mandate. William Lyon Mackenzie, a Scottish-born reformer and newspaper publisher, was chosen as the inaugural mayor on March 7, 1834, following the Reform slate's victory in the first aldermanic elections.[8] With a population exceeding 9,000 residents—driven by waves of British immigration—the early municipal powers were circumscribed to enacting bylaws for essential local functions, including fire prevention (the first bylaw passed on May 5, 1834), market regulation, and basic infrastructure such as streets and public markets housed in the repurposed Town Hall.[9][4] Early governance under Mackenzie focused on addressing the causal pressures of rapid urbanization, including sanitation, policing rudimentary markets, and limited public works, though fiscal constraints and dependence on provincial oversight restricted autonomous action. Mackenzie's tenure highlighted tensions between local reformers seeking accountable administration and the entrenched Family Compact elite, whose influence perpetuated grievances over land speculation and unequal representation. His outspoken criticism of colonial corruption as mayor directly fueled broader discontent, culminating in the Upper Canada Rebellion of December 1837, where Mackenzie led an armed uprising from Toronto against perceived authoritarian rule, resulting in his flight to the United States after its suppression.[10] This event underscored the causal linkage between mayoral advocacy for democratic reforms and provincial backlash, temporarily disrupting municipal stability but affirming the office's role in nascent political mobilization.[11] By the mid-1840s, the mayoral selection process shifted to annual direct elections by popular vote among qualified electors, aligning with evolving principles of local self-governance and reducing reliance on council appointment, though terms remained one year and eligibility was confined to propertied males. This adaptation responded to empirical demands for accountability amid continued population influx and infrastructural strains, laying groundwork for the office's expansion while preserving its ceremonial and administrative primacy within a council-dominated structure.[12]Adaptations Through Municipal Reorganizations The Municipality of Metropolitan Toronto was established effective April 15, 1953, through provincial legislation to manage explosive post-World War II suburban population growth, which had rendered the City of Toronto's boundaries inadequate for coordinating infrastructure like highways, water supply, and sewage systems across expanding fringe areas. [13] Rapid suburbanization created acute disparities in service delivery, as inner-city resources strained under demands from unincorporated townships and new municipalities unable to independently finance essential urban extensions such as roads and transit. [13] This reorganization centralized regional planning under a new upper-tier council comprising representatives from the city and suburbs, while preserving the Toronto mayor's position as head of the lower-tier city government but integrating the mayor as an ex-officio member subject to Metro Council's authority on cross-jurisdictional issues. [14] The creation of a separate Metro Chairman position, initially appointed by the province and later elected from 1988 onward, formalized a distinct regional executive role independent of the city mayor, amplifying inherent frictions between core-city fiscal conservatism—focused on dense urban services—and suburban demands for expansive infrastructure investments. [15] Early chairmen, such as Frederick Gardiner (1953–1961), aggressively pursued metropolitan integration to override local parochialism, evident in initiatives like the Gardiner Expressway, which prioritized regional connectivity over isolated municipal preferences and exposed governance tensions rooted in divergent tax bases and development paces. [15] This dual structure mitigated immediate sprawl-induced chaos but perpetuated divided loyalties, with the chairman wielding veto-like influence on budgets and planning, subordinating the mayor's agenda to broader imperatives. [16] By the 1990s, the two-tier framework engendered fiscal pressures through overlapping administrative layers, including redundant planning departments, procurement processes, and service boards across six lower-tier municipalities, which inflated per-capita operating costs and contributed to aggregate debt accumulation exceeding sustainable levels without provincial subsidies. [17] Empirical analyses of pre-amalgamation data highlighted inefficiencies, such as duplicated emergency services and welfare administration, where fragmented governance led to uncoordinated spending and competitive tax undercutting, empirically correlating with rising regional deficits amid stagnant revenue tools limited to property taxes. [18] These strains, unaddressed by incremental reforms, underscored the causal limitations of federated models in handling unified metropolitan economies, where service silos fostered waste rather than economies of scale. [17] In December 1997, Ontario's Progressive Conservative government under Premier Mike Harris legislated the amalgamation of Metropolitan Toronto's seven constituent municipalities into a single City of Toronto, effective January 1, 1998, explicitly to eliminate documented redundancies in governance and achieve projected savings of up to 20% in administrative costs through consolidated operations and staff reductions. [18] Harris's rationale, articulated in the Who Does What report and Bill 148, emphasized empirical evidence of over-governance—such as multiple mayoral offices and councils serving a contiguous urban area—as drivers of fiscal bloat, overriding objections from municipal associations that framed opposition in terms of preserved local representation rather than efficiency metrics. [17] This top-down intervention reflected provincial authority to enforce structural rationalization, prioritizing causal fixes to sprawl-era legacies over decentralized models critiqued for perpetuating inequities in service funding.
