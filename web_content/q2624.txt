Contents A Palace for Education The story of the Sarmiento Palace began with a kind lady named Petronila Rodríguez de Rojas. When she passed away in 1882, she left her large piece of land, about 6 hectares (15 acres), to the city. She wanted the land to be used for education and charity. Her wish was for a school for at least 700 girls, along with a church and a home for the elderly. The city hired two architects, Carlos Adolfo Altgelt and Hans Altgelt, to design the school. Construction started in 1886. Mrs. Rojas also wanted the building to have a big museum and a library. Building Design and Opening The palace was designed in a special style called "eclectic." This means it mixed different ideas from other styles. It was inspired by Second Empire architecture and looked like buildings from the French and German Renaissance Revival architecture. The building was finished in 1888. The Petronila Rodríguez de Rojas School officially opened its doors in 1893. From School to Ministry This impressive building was soon chosen for government use. In 1903, the National Education Council moved in. This council used to manage Argentina's national secondary schools. The building was later renamed the Sarmiento Palace. This was to honor Domingo Sarmiento, a former Education Minister and President of Argentina. He believed strongly in "the education of a sovereign people" during his time as president from 1868 to 1874. In 1980, the Ministry of Education moved into the palace. Even though its official name is Sarmiento Palace, many people call it the Pizzurno Palace. This is because of the street next to it, which was named after Pablo Pizzurno. He was an important teacher and a pioneer of the primary school system in Argentina. Location and Surroundings The palace is located across from the green Rodríguez Peña Plaza. This makes it a quiet spot in the busy Barrio Norte area of Recoleta. In the 1950s, a playground called Petronila Rodríguez de Rojas Plaza was created next to the palace. The Pizzurno Palace, officially known as the Palacio Sarmiento, is a historic eclectic-style edifice in Buenos Aires, Argentina, constructed between 1886 and 1888 as the initial realization of philanthropist Petronila Rodríguez de Miranda's bequest for a girls' school named after her.[1][2] Designed by Argentine architects Carlos Adolfo Altgelt and his brother Hans Altgelt—both trained in Europe—the building draws from French Second Empire and German Renaissance Revival motifs, featuring a central pavilion flanked by wings, ornate facades with sculptural elements, and interiors adapted for administrative functions.[1][2] Originally intended as a girls' school, it was repurposed shortly after completion to house the National Council of Education and later the Ministry of Justice and Public Instruction, reflecting the era's emphasis on centralized state education under Domingo Faustino Sarmiento's influence—though popularly dubbed "Pizzurno" after the adjacent street and the surname of prominent educator brothers Pablo and Alejandro Pizzurno.[1][2] Designated a National Historic Monument in 1989, it continues to serve as the headquarters of Argentina's Ministry of Education, symbolizing the nation's pedagogical heritage.[1] No major controversies mar its record, though its shift from educational to bureaucratic use underscores pragmatic governmental adaptations over strict philanthropic intent.[2] History Land Acquisition and Early Foundations The land for what would become the Pizzurno Palace was donated to the city of Buenos Aires by Doña Petronila Rodríguez de Rojas following her death in February 1882. Born in 1815, Rodríguez de Rojas inherited a substantial property encompassing four blocks bounded by the streets of Córdoba, Callao, Montevideo, and Marcelo T. de Alvear in the Recoleta neighborhood. Her will, opened that year before notary Juan Bautista Cruz with executor Juana Bosch, specified the donation's conditions: the site was to host a church dedicated to Nuestra Señora del Carmen (later the Iglesia del Carmen), an asylum, and a three-story school for up to 700 girls operated by the Order of Siervas de Jesús Sacramentado, equipped with a museum, library, and multiple classrooms, all to bear her name as Escuela Petronila Rodríguez.[2][3]This bequest reflected Rodríguez de Rojas's intent to establish an integrated educational and religious complex, funding derived from her estate to support public welfare amid Buenos Aires's late-19th-century urban expansion. However, the school was not constructed on the donated land as stipulated; instead, it operated temporarily in rented facilities at Capdevilla and Paraguay streets, then Junín and Paraguay, before briefly occupying part of the site and ultimately dissolving in 1903, with a later namesake school (No. 27) established in Parque Chas in 1934. The palace building, designed by architects Carlos Adolfo Altgelt and Hans Altgelt, broke ground in 1886 on the core portion of the property, laying the physical foundations for what was initially envisioned as the school's structure but repurposed shortly after.[4][2][3]The early foundations thus originated from this conditional donation, which prioritized girls' education but faced implementation challenges due to shifting municipal priorities, including the provisional housing of federal tribunals in the completed edifice upon its 1893 inauguration. A small plazoleta named after Rodríguez de Rojas now adjoins the site, acknowledging the donor amid the partial fulfillment of her legacy.[4][2] Construction and Architectural Commission The construction of the Pizzurno Palace, originally conceived as an educational facility, was commissioned by the municipal government of Buenos Aires following a significant land donation. In the mid-1880s, heiress Petronila Rodríguez de Rojas gifted a substantial plot in the Recoleta neighborhood to the city specifically for the establishment of a public school aimed at advancing education, reflecting the era's emphasis on institutional development in Argentina.[2][5] This donation prompted the authorities to initiate the project as the Escuela Petronila Rodríguez, prioritizing a monumental structure to symbolize educational progress.The architectural commission was awarded to cousins Carlos Adolfo Altgelt, an Argentine-German architect trained in Europe, and Hans Altgelt, who collaborated on the design. Their selection aligned with Buenos Aires' burgeoning adoption of eclectic European influences, drawing from Carlos Altgelt's prior experience with Second Empire and Renaissance Revival styles in local projects. Construction commenced in 1886 and concluded in 1888, involving ornate stonework, multi-story facades, and robust materials suited to the site's urban context.[2][6][7]Oversight during construction emphasized durability and aesthetic grandeur, with the Altgelt duo managing engineering details to accommodate the building's intended role as a flagship educational institution under the National Council of Education's purview. The project's timely completion underscored efficient municipal coordination, though expansions were later considered by 1909 to address growing administrative needs.[6][8] No major delays or controversies marred the build, establishing it as a exemplar of late-19th-century public works in the city. and Hans Altgelt.[2][9] The building's creation stemmed from the 1882 will of Petronila Rodríguez de Rojas, a wealthy porteña who donated the land bounded by Córdoba, Callao, Montevideo, and Marcelo (February 2017) | The Sarmiento Palace,[1] commonly known as the Pizzurno Palace, is an architectural landmark in the Recoleta section of Buenos Aires and the location of the Argentine Ministry of Education.[2] A will left by a local heiress, Petronila Rodríguez de Rojas, stipulated that her roughly 6 hectares (15 acres) lot in uptown Buenos Aires be used for an educational and charitable compound to include a church, an old-age asylum and a girls' school for no less than 700 pupils. Her 1882 death accordingly left the property to the city, which commissioned German Argentine architects Carlos Adolfo Altgelt and his cousin Hans Altgelt to design the requisite school. Work began in 1886[3] on the building which, per Mrs. Rojas' wishes, would include extensive museum and library facilities, as well. The building's design was eclectic, inspired by Second Empire architecture influenced by both French and German Renaissance Revival architecture. It was completed in 1888, and the Petronila Rodríguez de Rojas School was inaugurated in 1893. The majestic landmark was soon earmarked for use as government offices, however, and the National Education Council was installed there in 1903; the National Education Council (since dissolved) administered Argentina's system of national secondary schools. The building was rebaptized as the Sarmiento Palace in honor of former Education Minister and President Domingo Sarmiento, who made "the education of a sovereign people" a policy centerpiece during his prosperous 1868-74 tenure. The building's location facing leafy Rodríguez Peña Plaza created an urban oasis in the otherwise bustling Barrio Norte section of the upscale Recoleta ward. A lot immediately to the south of the building was converted into Petronila Rodríguez de Rojas Plaza (a playground) as a belated homage to the civic-minded lady during the 1950s (as was a primary school in the Parque Chas neighborhood, in 1934). The last dictatorship, which dissolved the Education Council in 1978, transferred the Ministry of Education to the building in 1980. Still officially known as the Sarmiento Palace, the building is popularly known as the Pizzurno Palace for the side street built facing it, which was renamed in honor of pedagogian and local primary school system pioneer Pablo Pizzurno, following his death in 1940.
