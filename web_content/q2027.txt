The Mazda R360 is a kei car manufactured and marketed by Mazda from May 1960 to 1969. It was the first passenger car produced by the company. The R360 is a two-door coupé with a 2+2 seating arrangement. It has a rear-engine, rear-wheel-drive layout and was designed to be inexpensive and light weight.

--- [Mazda:the Mazda R360 Coupe | Great Cars of Mazda] ---
Mazda launched its first passenger car, the R360 Coupe, throughout Japan on May 28, 1960. The launch coincided with a period of improved income levels and changing lifestyles, and the beginning of the boom in demand for car ownership in Japan. The automotive industry was in full swing, brought to fruition by such factors as the first All Japan Motor Show in 1954 (later to become the Tokyo Motor Show), the national car concept put forward by the Ministry of International Trade and Industry (MITI — currently the Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry) in 1955, trade liberalization and other measures that stimulated the economy. That said, the cars of the period, even the micro-mini cars for the masses, were expensive and hard to come by. However, people still dreamed of having their own car and Mazda's R360 Coupe was born from this desire, breaking the mold of the micro-mini car of that period. In the development of the R360 Coupe, Mazda used new technology and thoroughly raised its manufacturing efficiency. In so doing, it managed to introduce models with very low sticker prices — 300,000 yen for the manual transmission version and 320,000 yen for the first car in the country equipped with a torque converter. By May 20, 1960, just prior to the R360 Coupe's launch, Mazda had already received orders for as many as 4,500 units. The car's popularity increased even further after the launch, and by August Mazda was selling in excess of 2,000 units per month. In December of that year the company saw record-breaking sales of 4,090 units. The total production figure for 1960 amounted to 23,417 units, giving Mazda a 64.8% production share of the micro-mini car market. In helping to fulfill people's dreams of owning a car, the R360 Coupe played a leading role in the development of Japan's motorization. The Mazda R360 Coupe. The attraction of the R360 Coupe was not price alone. It featured a torque converter and a host of other cutting-edge technologies Mazda had developed. The R360 Coupe was also the first micro-mini passenger car to be equipped with a four-stroke engine, realizing better durability and fuel economy than its two-stroke counterpart. The engine also used a substantial amount of magnesium alloy. Combined with a light monocoque body, light alloy hood and other weight-saving measures, this made the R360 Coupe the lightest car produced in Japan at only 380 kilograms, with concomitant gains in fuel economy and driving performance. The car had a four-wheel independent suspension system employing torsion rubber for a nimble and comfortable ride. The stylish and functional coupe form enclosing the 2+2 cabin also led the field in Japan's car design. A car so advanced it completely broke with convention, the R360 Coupe heralded the future of Mazda's passenger cars. Excerpts from the original catalogue.

--- [Grokipedia: Mazda R360] ---
The Mazda R360 is a compact, rear-engined kei car manufactured by Mazda Motor Corporation from 1960 to 1966, representing the company's debut in the passenger vehicle market with a lightweight 2+2 coupedesign powered by a 356 cc air-cooled V-twin engine producing 16 horsepower.[1][2][3]Launched on May 28, 1960, the R360 was engineered to meet Japan's strict kei car regulations, which limited engine displacement to 360 cc and overall dimensions to promote affordable urban mobility amid post-wareconomic growth.[2][3] Its monocoque body construction, weighing just 380 kg, incorporated innovative lightweight materials such as an aluminum bonnet and magnesium alloy components in the engine and gearbox to achieve exceptional fuel efficiency of 32 km per liter.[1][2] The vehicle featured four-wheel independent suspension with torsion rubber bushings for smooth handling, rack-and-pinion steering, and a choice of four-speed manual or two-speed automatic transmission—the latter using Japan's first torque converter in a kei car for easier urban driving.[2][3]Priced at approximately 300,000 yen for the manual version (equivalent to about two months' salary for an average worker at the time), the R360 democratized car ownership in Japan, receiving 4,500 pre-launch orders and selling 23,417 units in its debut year—capturing 64.8% of the micro-mini car market and 15% of Japan's overall passenger car sales.[1][2] The R360 remained in production until 1966, with 65,737 units built overall and special orders continuing until 1969. To expand its lineup, Mazda introduced the related P360 sedan (also known as the Carol) in 1962, available in two- and four-door configurations with a 358 cc inline-four engine.[3] Notable design elements included plexiglass sliding side windows, a curved rear windshield for better visibility, and a functional aesthetic that prioritized aerodynamics and space efficiency in a 3,000 mm overall length and 1,300 mm width.[2][3]The R360's success established Mazda's reputation for engineering innovation, particularly in lightweight construction and efficient powertrains, influencing subsequent models like the Carol and laying the groundwork for the company's rotary engine era.[2][3] Today, surviving examples are rare collector's items, primarily found in Japan, valued for their historical significance in automotive miniaturization and Mazda's transformation from a truck maker to a global car producer.[3] Development and Introduction Background and Design Goals Mazda, originally known as Toyo Kogyo Co., Ltd., began operations in 1920 but initially focused on manufacturing machinery and cork products before pivoting to transportation vehicles in the late 1920s.[4] By 1929, the company had entered the motorcycle market, followed by three-wheeled trucks in 1931, which became its primary output during the pre-war and wartime periods.[4] In the post-World War II era, Japan's economy was in recovery, marked by widespread poverty, limited infrastructure, and a reliance on bicycles and scooters for personal mobility, creating demand for affordable, compact vehicles to support urban commuting and small business needs.[5][6]To capitalize on this, Toyo Kogyo decided in the late 1950s to develop its first passenger car, targeting the emerging kei car category established by Japanese government regulations in 1949 to revive the auto industry with low-displacement, lightweight vehicles.[5] These rules, revised in 1955 to limit engines to 360 cc and vehicles to a maximum length of 3.4 meters, width of 1.48 meters, and curb weight of 400 kg, offered significant tax and insurance benefits to encourage adoption of economical transport options over two-wheeled alternatives.[7] The Mazda R360, launched on May 28, 1960, as the company's inaugural four-wheeled passenger vehicle, was designed specifically to meet these kei car standards, positioning it as an accessible entry into car ownership for average Japanese families during a period of rising incomes but constrained roadways.[2][7]The primary design objectives for the R360 emphasized extreme lightness to stay under the 400 kg curb weight limit, achieving just 380 kg through measures like a monocoque body and aluminum components, while ensuring affordability at around ¥300,000 to appeal to urban commuters seeking reliable daily transport.[8][9] Engineers targeted a top speed of 80-90 km/h suitable for Japan's speed limits and road conditions, alongside fuel efficiency exceeding 30 km/L at steady highway speeds to minimize operating costs and compete with fuel-thrifty scooters.[10][11] These goals aligned with the kei class's incentives for lower taxes and insurance, fostering widespread personal vehicle use in a nation rebuilding from wartime devastation.[7] Engineering Innovations The Mazda R360 employed a rear-engine, rear-wheel-drive layout, with the engine mounted longitudinally behind the rear axle to optimize interior space utilization and achieve balanced weight distribution for improved handling in its compact kei car class.[10][12] This configuration allowed for a low center of gravity and efficient packaging, contributing to the vehicle's nimble performance without compromising passenger room.[2]To minimize weight while maintaining structural integrity, the R360 featured a steel monocoque body integrated with lightweight components, including aluminum alloy panels for the hood and engine cover, a magnesium alloy transmission casing, and acrylic resin for the side and rear windows instead of heavier glass.[2][13][12] These material choices resulted in a total curb weight of just 380 kg, making it the lightest four-seater production car in Japan at the time and enhancing fuel efficiency and acceleration.[2][14]The R360 was the first kei car to use a four-stroke engine, which provided reduced noise, greater durability, and cooler operation compared to the two-stroke engines prevalent in competing models.[15][16] Its suspension system consisted of four-wheel independent suspension with torsion rubber springs, while four-wheel drum brakes ensured reliable stopping power.[2][17] This combination delivered a smooth and stable ride suited to urban environments.[3] Production and Market Impact Manufacturing Details The Mazda R360 was manufactured exclusively at Mazda's Hiroshima Plant in Hiroshima, Japan, commencing full production in May 1960.[18] This facility, originally established for three-wheeled vehicle and truck assembly, enabled efficient scaling for the R360's kei car production by integrating it into existing lines originally designed for commercial vehicles.[19] The plant's layout supported high-volume output, with the R360 achieving rapid assembly through streamlined processes tailored to the model's compact design.Total production of the R360 coupe reached 65,737 units over its primary run, reflecting assembly line innovations that optimized for kei car efficiency, including modular component handling to boost throughput.[3]Manufacturing techniques emphasized lightweight construction, utilizing a pressed-steel monocoque body shell supplemented by aluminum for the bonnet and engine cover to reduce weight without compromising structural integrity.[15] These methods, combined with shared tooling from Mazda's truck operations, allowed cost-effective production while meeting Japan's stringent kei vehicle regulations.Mainstream production of the R360 concluded in December 1966, as Mazda shifted focus to the front-engine Carol 360 introduced in February 1962.[20] However, limited assembly continued until 1969 for specialized versions adapted for disabled drivers, available only as made-to-order units.[10] Sales and Reception The MazdaR360 experienced an immediate commercial success upon its launch in May 1960, receiving over 4,500 orders on the first day of sales. By the end of the year, production reached 23,417 units, capturing a dominant 64.8% share of Japan's micro-mini car market through its emphasis on fuel economy, lightweight construction, and reliable four-stroke engine.[3][2][21]Despite facing stiff competition from the cheaper Subaru 360, which retailed for around ¥250,000 compared to the R360's ¥300,000 price, the Mazda model outsold its rival and other kei cars due to its superior build quality and smoother four-stroke power delivery, as noted in contemporary assessments of its advanced suspension and overall refinement.[22][21][22]The R360 received positive reception in Japan for its urban practicality, innovative design, and role as an accessible family vehicle in a post-war economy, establishing it as Mazda's breakthrough product that solidified the company's entry into passenger car manufacturing. However, reviewers pointed out its limited power for highway use, with a top speed of around 56 mph restricting it primarily to city driving.[16][15][23]Exports were minimal and focused on select markets, with approximately 700 left-hand-drive units produced for export to various markets and 41 right-hand-drive units sent to Australia, while the vehicle remained predominantly a domestic product in Japan.[21][10] Design and Specifications Body and Chassis The Mazda R360 featured a two-door coupe body style designed for the kei car class, incorporating a 2+2 seating layout with minimal rear passenger space to maximize efficiency within strict dimensional limits.[24] Its exterior adopted rounded, bubble-like aesthetics reminiscent of contemporary microcars, prioritizing aerodynamic efficiency and compact proportions for urban maneuverability.[22]Key dimensions of the R360 included an overall length of 2,980 mm, width of 1,290 mm, height of 1,290 mm, wheelbase of 1,760 mm, and ground clearance of 180 mm, ensuring compliance with Japan's kei vehicle regulations while providing a stable footprint.[17] These measurements contributed to its agile handling and ease of parking in crowded city environments.[25]The chassis utilized a monocoque steel body construction for structural integrity and rigidity, with aluminum panels applied to the bonnet and engine cover to achieve significant weight savings—resulting in a curb weight of just 380 kg—and enhanced rust resistance compared to all-steel designs.[3][14] It featured four-wheel independent suspension with torsion rubber bushings. This lightweight approach, drawing from innovative material choices, helped the R360 outperform rivals in fuel economy and responsiveness.[16]Styling elements emphasized practicality and simplicity, including front doors that facilitated access to the rear seats, a fixed curved rear window constructed from Plexiglas for durability and weight reduction, and round headlights integrated into the fenders for a cohesive, streamlined appearance.[24][22] Engine and Drivetrain The Mazda R360 was powered by a compact 356 cc air-cooled, four-stroke V-twin engine with a 90-degree configuration and overhead valves (OHV), featuring a bore of 60 mm and stroke of 63 mm, along with a compression ratio of 8.0:1 and a single carburetor.[26][3] This engine, derived from Mazda's motorcycle technology, produced 16 PS (11.8 kW) at 5,300 rpm and 2.2 kg⋅m (22 N⋅m) of torque at 4,000 rpm, emphasizing efficiency and compactness suitable for kei car regulations.[26][15] The forced air-cooling system utilized a fan for effective heat dissipation.[10][26]The drivetrain adopted a rear-engine, rear-wheel-drive (RER) layout, with the engine positioned longitudinally at the rear and power transmitted to the rear wheels via a propeller shaft, contributing to the vehicle's balanced weight distribution and maneuverability.[3][15] Transmission options included a four-speed manual gearbox in the standard KRBB variant or a two-speed torque converter automatic (branded as Torque Drive) in the KRBC variant, marking an early adoption of automatic shifting in Japan's kei car segment.[10][3]The fuel system supported regular gasoline, stored in a 16-liter tank located at the rear, enabling practical range for urban use while adhering to the era's efficiency standards.[26][17] This powertrain configuration, with its motorcycle-inspired simplicity, allowed the R360 to meet stringent kei car dimensions and power limits while providing dependable performance for everyday driving.[3][27] Interior Features and Colors The Mazda R360's interior was designed for practicality and economy, featuring a simple 2+2 seating layout with front and rear bench seats upholstered in vinyl that could accommodate four passengers, though the rear space was notably tight for adults.[28] The front seats folded forward to facilitate access to the rear, and the overall trim was minimal, including a basic glovebox and ashtray for storage and convenience.[29] Analog gauges, consisting of a speedometer and a small temperature indicator, were mounted centrally on the steering column for driver visibility, reflecting the car's no-frills approach. A standard basic heater and defroster provided essential climate control, while no radio was included as factory equipment.[28]Safety features were rudimentary by modern standards, with optional lap belts available for occupants and an unpadded dashboard that offered little impact protection.[28]Exterior color options for the standard R360 were limited to single-tone finishes in Opal Green, Maroon Rouge, or Somerset Blue, chosen to appeal to the post-war Japanese market's preference for vibrant yet practical hues.[30] The DeLuxe variant, introduced in 1962, expanded choices with two-tone schemes such as Blue over Cream or Red over Cream, adding a touch of upscale styling without significantly increasing weight or cost.[30] Inside, upholstery, carpet, and door panels came in either red or blue vinyl to complement the exterior palette, maintaining the lightweight construction essential for the kei car classification.[31] Variants B360 Pickup The Mazda B360 was introduced in February 1961 as a light commercial pickup truck, adapting the R360 kei car's platform into a front-engine, rear-wheel-drive utility vehicle designed primarily for cargo transport in Japan's urban environments.[1][32]It featured a 356 cc air-cooled four-stroke V-twin engine producing 16 hp initially, upgraded to a water-cooled 358 cc inline-four version with 20 hp by 1963, sharing core technology with the R360 but repositioned at the front for improved load distribution and accessibility.[33][34] The vehicle offered a payload capacity of approximately 350 kg, suitable for small-scale deliveries, with a compact overall length of about 3 meters and a wheelbase of 1.99 meters to navigate narrow streets.[35][36]Design adaptations included a conventional cab-forward layout with a short bonnet, a drop-side cargo bed for easy loading, and leaf-spring rear suspension to handle payloads effectively, distinguishing it from the passenger-oriented R360coupe.[32][37] Available in standard and deluxe trims, the B360 catered to practicality with basic instrumentation and optional enhancements like bench seating for two. Production continued until 1968, marking Mazda's early entry into the kei truck market.[38]In Japan, the B360 gained popularity among small businesses for its affordability and maneuverability, serving as a reliable workhorse for trades like delivery and farming, in contrast to the R360's focus on personal commuting.[35] P360 (Carol) Sedan The Mazda P360, also known as the Mazda Carol, was introduced in 1962 as a four-door sedan variant of the R360, expanding the lineup to a four-seater configuration while adhering to kei car regulations. It utilized the same 356 cc air-cooled four-stroke V-twin engine producing 16 hp as the R360 coupe, with a slightly longer body at 3,040 mm to accommodate rear doors and seating for four passengers.The P360 featured a more practical sedan body style with improved rear access, maintaining the rear-engine, rear-wheel-drive layout and monocoque construction for lightweight efficiency. Transmission options included the four-speed manual and two-speed automatic from the R360. Production of the P360 continued until 1970, with over 84,000 units sold, helping Mazda capture a broader share of the kei car market.[21] Transmission Options The Mazda R360 featured two primary transmission options designed to cater to different driver preferences within the constraints of the kei car class. The standard setup on the KRBB variant was a 4-speed manual transmission, providing direct control suited to the vehicle's rear-wheel-drive layout and compact 356 cc V-twin engine.[17][39]In contrast, the KRBC variant introduced a pioneering 2-speed Torque Drive semi-automatic transmission, which utilized a torque converter and required manual selection between low and high gears via a floor-mounted lever, marking the first such system in a Japanese kei car for simplified operation without a full clutch pedal.[21][22] This option enhanced usability for novice drivers in urban settings, though it lacked an overdrive gear, limiting highway efficiency.[25]The manual transmission offered superior fuel economy at 32 km/L with two passengers, compared to 25 km/L for the automatic, reflecting the efficiency trade-offs of the torque converter under light loads.[17][40][41] The automatic was available as an optional upgrade at additional cost, broadening the R360's appeal in Japan's post-war market where ease of driving was increasingly valued.[15] Legacy Influence on Mazda and Kei Cars The Mazda R360, introduced in 1960 as the company's inaugural passenger car, marked the establishment of Mazda's passenger vehicle division and catalyzed its transition from truck manufacturing to a broader automotive producer. Its immediate commercial triumph, with 4,500 units ordered prior to launch and 23,417 sold by year's end, captured 64.8% of Japan's kei car market and 15% of the overall domestic passenger car sector, providing crucial financial stability.[2] This success enabled Mazda to invest in ambitious research and development, including securing a rotary engine license from NSU in 1961 for approximately ¥280 million and funding subsequent experiments that led to models like the 1967 Cosmo Sport, while laying the groundwork for global expansion in the late 1960s.[42]In the kei car category, the R360 pioneered the adoption of a four-stroke engine in the microcar class, featuring a 356 cc air-cooled V-twin that delivered superior fuel efficiency, quieter operation, and greater durability compared to the prevalent two-stroke designs of competitors. Weighing just 380 kg through innovative lightweight construction—incorporating a monocoque body, aluminum components, and the "gram strategy" for weight reduction—it set new benchmarks for performance and economy within Japan's strict kei regulations, which limited engine displacement to 360 cc and dimensions to foster affordable mobility. This approach influenced rivals such as Suzuki and Daihatsu to shift toward four-stroke and lightweight strategies, contributing to the evolution of kei standards and prompting regulatory adjustments in the 1960s to accommodate growing demand for refined microcars.The R360's formula directly inspired its successor, the 1962 Mazda Carol, a rear-engined, four-passenger sedan with a 358 cc four-cylinder engine while maintaining kei compliance, achieving a two-thirds market share in its debut year.[43] As an industry milestone, the R360 demonstrated the viability of microcars as practical daily transport, accelerating Japan's motorization and bolstering auto exports during the 1960s by showcasing engineering prowess that enhanced the nation's global automotive reputation. Cultural and Collectible Significance The Mazda R360 holds significant collectible value today, with survivors becoming increasingly rare due to the passage of time and susceptibility to rust, particularly in regions with harsh winters or salted roads. Of the approximately 65,000 units produced between 1960 and 1966, only a small fraction remain roadworthy, estimated at around 700 left-hand-drive examples globally, many of which were built for U.S. servicemen stationed in Japan. Restored examples in good condition can command prices ranging from $10,000 to $20,000 USD in enthusiast markets as of 2025, reflecting their status as a cornerstone of kei car history, though parts scarcity poses challenges for maintenance.[28][21][4]Culturally, the R360 stands as an icon of 1960sJapan, embodying post-war ingenuity and the nation's economic resurgence through affordable, innovative mobility. Launched amid Japan's recovery efforts, it symbolized the democratization of car ownership, capturing two-thirds of the kei car market and enabling ordinary families to embrace four-wheeled transport for the first time. The vehicle has appeared in various media, including films and television series that highlight its quirky charm, and is preserved in prominent institutions such as the Mazda Museum in Hiroshima and the Classic Automobil Museum Frey in Germany, where it underscores Mazda's foundational spirit.[44][28][45][22]In modern enthusiast circles, the R360 is appreciated for its endearing, bubble-like design and playful handling, often celebrated as a precursor to Mazda's philosophy of lightweight, driver-focused vehicles. The model's 60th anniversary in 2020 sparked renewed interest through dedicated articles and Mazda's own restoration efforts, filling gaps in public knowledge and inspiring occasional homages, such as scale model replicas and custom builds. Preservation efforts are supported by active communities, including online groups in Japan and the U.S., as well as international clubs like the Vintage Japanese Motor Union, where owners share restoration tips amid ongoing challenges with sourcing original components. Its original sales success, exceeding 23,000 units in the first year alone, continues to underscore its lasting nostalgic appeal.[22][21][46][28]
