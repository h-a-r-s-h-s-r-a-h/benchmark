--- [Nancy M. Amato - Wikipedia] ---
Nancy Marie Amato is an American computer scientist noted for her research on the algorithmic foundations of motion planning, computational biology, computational geometry and parallel computing. Amato is the Abel Bliss Professor of Engineering and Head of the Department of Computer Science at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. Amato is noted for her leadership in broadening participation in computing, and is currently a member of the steering committee of CRA-WP (formerly known as CRA-W), of which she has been a member of the board since 2000.

Preparata | | Doctoral students | Lydia Tapia | | Website | cs engineering | Nancy Marie Amato is an American computer scientist noted for her research on the algorithmic foundations of motion planning, computational biology, computational geometry and parallel computing.[1] Amato is the Abel Bliss Professor of Engineering and Head of the Department of Computer Science at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign.[2] Amato is noted for her leadership in broadening participation in computing, and is currently a member of the steering committee of CRA-WP (formerly known as CRA-W), of which she has been a member of the board since 2000.[citation needed] Education Amato received both a Bachelor of Arts degree in Economics and a Bachelor of Science degree in Mathematical Sciences from Stanford University in 1986.[2] She received an MS in Computer Science from the University of California, Berkeley in 1988, with advisor Manuel Blum.[3] In 1995, she received a PhD in computer science from the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign under advisor Franco P. Preparata for her thesis "Parallel Algorithms for Convex Hulls and Proximity Problems".[2] Career and research She joined the Department of Computer Science at Texas A&M University as an assistant professor in 1995. She was promoted to associate professor in 2000, to professor in 2004, and to Unocal professor in 2011. In July 2018, Amato was named the next head of the Department of Computer Science at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, starting in January 2019.[4][5] Amato has several notable results. Her paper on probabilistic roadmap methods (PRMs) is one of the most important papers on PRM. It describes the first PRM variant that does not use uniform sampling in the robot's configuration space.[6] She wrote a seminal paper with one of her students that shows how the PRM methodology can be applied to protein motions, and in particular protein folding. This approach has opened up a new research area in computational biology.[7] This result opens up a rich new set of applications for this technique in computational biology. Another paper she wrote with her students represents a major advance by showing how global energy landscape statistics, such as relative folding rates and population kinetics, can be computed for proteins from the approximate landscapes computed by Amato's PRM-based method.[8] In another paper, she and a student introduced a novel technique, approximate convex decomposition (ACD), for partitioning a polyhedron into approximately convex pieces.[9] Amato also co-leads the STAPL project with her husband Lawrence Rauchwerger, who is also a computer scientist on the faculty at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. Preparata | | Doctoral students | Lydia Tapia | Nancy Marie Amato is an American computer scientist. She is well-known for her research in areas like motion planning (how robots move), computational biology (using computers to study living things), and parallel computing (making computers work on many tasks at once). Nancy Amato is currently a top professor and the head of the Computer Science Department at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. She is also a leader in helping more people get involved in computer science. She has been part of the CRA-WP committee since 2000, which works to make computing a field for everyone. Contents Education and Early Career Nancy Amato studied at Stanford University. In 1986, she earned two degrees there. She received a Bachelor of Arts in Economics and a Bachelor of Science in Mathematical Sciences. Later, she continued her studies in computer science. She earned her Master's degree from the University of California, Berkeley, in 1988. In 1995, she completed her PhD in computer science at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. Her PhD research focused on "Parallel Algorithms for Convex Hulls and Proximity Problems." Career and Research Discoveries After finishing her PhD, Nancy Amato joined Texas A&M University in 1995. She started as an assistant professor in the Computer Science Department. She worked her way up, becoming a full professor in 2004. In 2018, Nancy Amato was chosen for a big role. She became the head of the Computer Science Department at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. She started this new job in January 2019. Key Research Areas Nancy Amato has made many important discoveries in computer science. - Robot Motion Planning: One of her key papers is about probabilistic roadmap methods (PRMs). This is a way to help robots figure out how to move around obstacles. Her work showed a new way to do this, making it more efficient. - Understanding Proteins: She also applied her motion planning ideas to computational biology. She showed how these methods could help understand how proteins move and fold. Proteins are tiny building blocks in living things. This research opened up a whole new area of study. It helps scientists learn more about how our bodies work. - Analyzing Protein Behavior: In another important paper, she and her students found a way to calculate how proteins fold. They could figure out how fast they fold and how many different ways they can fold. This helps scientists understand protein behavior better. - Breaking Down Shapes: Nancy Amato also developed a technique called approximate convex decomposition (ACD). This method helps break down complex 3D shapes (like a polyhedron) into simpler, more manageable pieces. This is useful in many computer graphics and design tasks. - STAPL Project: She also helps lead the STAPL project. This project creates a special computer program library using C++. It helps make other computer programs run faster by using many computer processors at once. Her husband, Lawrence Rauchwerger, who is also a computer scientist, works with her on this project.
