--- [Pittosporum divaricatum - Wikipedia] ---
Pittosporum divaricatum is a species of plant in the Pittosporaceae family. It is a New Zealand endemic shrub growing up to 3 metres high. It has densely entangled branches growing in a divaricating form typical of many New Zealand small leaved shrubs.
It exhibits a form of dimorphism with leaves differing in form between the juvenile and adult stages and in the adult leaves where two distinct forms are found. The juvenile leaves are narrowly lanceolate to obovate with margins pinnatifid to dentate. The adult leaves exist in two forms, either entire, or almost so, or deeply lobed or toothed. The flowers are borne singly on the end of shoots. The petal are up to 5mm long and very dark red, often appearing black, subtended by sepals which are 2mm long. The fruits contain between 2 and 6 seeds.

The Pittosporum divaricatum is a special type of plant that belongs to the Pittosporaceae family. It's a shrub that grows only in New Zealand, meaning it's endemic to that country. This plant can grow up to 3 metres (about 10 feet) tall. One cool thing about this shrub is its branches. They grow very close together and are tangled, forming a dense, bushy shape. This style of growth is called "divaricating," and it's common for many small-leaved shrubs found in New Zealand. Contents What Makes Its Leaves Special? Pittosporum divaricatum has a unique feature called dimorphism. This means its leaves look different depending on how old the plant is. Young Plant Leaves When the plant is young, its leaves are narrow and shaped like a spear or an oval. Their edges can be deeply cut or have small teeth. Adult Plant Leaves As the plant grows older, its leaves change. Adult plants can have two different kinds of leaves: - Some leaves are smooth and whole, with no cuts or teeth. - Other leaves are deeply lobed or toothed, similar to the young leaves but on an adult plant. Flowers and Seeds The flowers of Pittosporum divaricatum are quite small and grow one by one at the very end of the plant's shoots. Flower Appearance - The petals of the flowers are tiny, only about 5 millimetres (0.2 inches) long. - They are a very dark red color, so dark that they often look black. - Underneath the petals are small green parts called sepals, which are about 2 millimetres (0.08 inches) long. Fruit and Seeds After the flowers bloom, the plant produces fruits. Each fruit contains a small number of seeds, usually between 2 and 6.
