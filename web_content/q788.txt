Mirza Muhammad Murad Bakhsh (9 October 1624 – 14 December 1661) was a Mughal prince and the youngest surviving son of Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan and Empress Mumtaz Mahal. He was the Subahdar of Balkh, until he was replaced by his elder brother Aurangzeb in the year 1647.

Murad Bakhsh Mirza Muhammad Murad Bakhsh (9 October 1624 – 14 December 1661) was a Mughal prince and the youngest surviving son of Emperor Shah Jahan and Empress Mumtaz Mahal.[1][2] Born at Rohtasgarh Fort in Bihar, he rose to prominence as the subahdar of Gujarat, where he suppressed local rebellions and asserted Mughal authority through military expeditions.[2][3] In the brutal war of succession triggered by Shah Jahan's incapacitation in 1657, Murad initially proclaimed himself emperor in Ahmedabad and formed a tactical alliance with his brother Aurangzeb to defeat the favored heir Dara Shikoh, yet this partnership dissolved amid mutual suspicions, leading to his imprisonment and execution on trumped-up murder charges at Gwalior Fort.[3][4] His ambitions and downfall exemplified the ruthless fraternal rivalries that defined Mughal imperial transitions, underscoring the precarious balance of loyalty and betrayal in sustaining dynastic power.[3] Early Life and Family Background Birth and Parentage Muhammad Murad Bakhsh was born on 9 October 1624 at Rohtasgarh Fort in Bihar.[5][2] He was the youngest surviving son of Mughal prince Khurram, who later became Emperor Shah Jahan upon ascending the throne in 1628, and his principal consort Mumtaz Mahal.[5][1]Mumtaz Mahal, originally Arjumand Banu Begum, had married Khurram in 1612 and bore him at least 14 children, though only seven survived infancy, including four sons: Dara Shikoh, Shah Shuja, Aurangzeb, and Murad Bakhsh himself.[5] At the time of Murad's birth, his father was engaged in military campaigns in the Deccan under Emperor Jahangir, with Rohtasgarh serving as a key fortress in the Mughal domain.[6]Siblings and Upbringing Murad Bakhsh was the youngest of Shah Jahan's four surviving sons by Mumtaz Mahal, with elder brothers Dara Shikoh (born 1615), Shah Shuja (born 1616), and Aurangzeb (born 1618).[2][7] His sisters included the influential Jahanara Begum (born 1614) and Roshanara Begum (born circa 1617), as well as Gauhar Ara Begum (born 1631, the last child of Mumtaz Mahal, who died in childbirth).[8][9] These siblings formed the core of the imperial family dynamics, marked by favoritism toward the eldest son Dara Shikoh and underlying tensions that later erupted in the war of succession.[10] As a Mughal prince, Murad Bakhsh's upbringing occurred amid the opulence of the imperial court following his birth on 9 October 1624 at Rohtasgarh Fort in Bihar, where Shah Jahan was overseeing military campaigns. Orphaned by his mother's death in 1631, he was raised under Shah Jahan's direct patronage, which favored him as the youngest son and instilled a bold, martial character described in contemporary accounts as that of a "gallant swashbuckler, brave as a lion."[2][7] Like his brothers, his early training emphasized military prowess, administrative skills, and Islamic scholarship, though historical records portray him as impulsive and less disciplined than Aurangzeb, with early postings to provinces like Balkh and the Deccan testing his capabilities from adolescence.[11] This environment of princely rivalry and rigorous preparation for governance foreshadowed his later roles, despite characterizations in some sources as the family's "black sheep."[11][12]Administrative and Military Career Governorship of Multan and Gujarat Murad Bakhsh was appointed subadar of Multan in 1642 at the age of 18, marking his initial major administrative role under his father Shah Jahan.[13] He held the position until around 1646, when he departed for further imperial assignments, including military expeditions.[14] Specific policies or campaigns during this tenure are sparsely documented, but as a frontier subah, Multan involved oversight of revenue collection, fort maintenance, and defense against regional threats. In March 1654, Murad Bakhsh was transferred to the prosperous subah of Gujarat as subadar, a key maritime and commercial province.[15] During his governorship, which lasted until the war of succession erupted in late 1657, he focused on restoring stability amid local unrest.[16] Notably, he suppressed rebellions by the Koli communities, indigenous groups resisting Mughal authority through guerrilla tactics in rural and coastal areas.[16] This pacification effort strengthened imperial control over trade routes and ports like Surat, vital for Mughal revenue from customs duties. Evidence of his authority includes silver rupees minted under his name at Gujarat mints such as Ahmedabad and Surat, bearing dates from his brief but active rule.[13] These coins, issued for about 220 days in some cases, reflect standard Mughal fiscal practices under princely oversight.[17] His administration emphasized efficient revenue extraction from Gujarat's wealthy merchant class and agrarian base, though detailed records of reforms or associates are limited in contemporary accounts.[18] Murad retained the governorship even after Shah Jahan's illness in 1657, using it as a base for his subsequent imperial ambitions.Key Policies, Campaigns, and Associates During his tenure as viceroy of Gujarat starting in 1654, Murad Bakhsh focused on restoring administrative order disrupted by prior instability, including suppressing rebellions by the Koli tribes, a semi-nomadic group known for resisting Mughal authority in the region's coastal and hilly areas. His military response involved deploying imperial forces to quell these uprisings, which had intensified under previous governors, thereby securing revenue collection from key ports like Surat. Financially, Murad Bakhsh pursued policies of heavy borrowing to fund his administration and personal expenditures, raising significant loans from prominent local merchants, including the Jain banker Shantidas, whose influence extended to imperial politics through such dealings in Surat.[19] This approach strained relations with nomadic trading communities like the banjaras, whom he oppressed through excessive taxation and restrictions, leading some to abandon trade routes essential for provisioning Mughal armies. In military campaigns, Murad Bakhsh commanded Mughal forces during the Balkh expedition of 1646–1647, departing from his base in Multan with an army of approximately 60,000 troops to conquer the Uzbek-held city of Balkh in Central Asia on Shah Jahan's orders.[20] The Mughals captured Balkh on July 2, 1646, with minimal resistance, but harsh winter conditions forced a withdrawal, resulting in heavy losses and his subsequent disgrace, including the revocation of his mansab rank.[21] Key associates included imperial nobles and local financiers who supported his governance, such as diwans handling revenue in Gujarat and military commanders during the Balkh push, though records highlight tensions with overambitious subordinates who influenced his decisions toward indulgence over sustained conquest.[22] His reliance on merchant lenders like Shantidas underscored alliances with economic elites in Gujarat's trading hubs, prioritizing short-term liquidity over long-term fiscal stability.[19]Involvement in the War of Succession Proclamation and Initial Strategies Upon receiving news of Shah Jahan's serious illness on 8 September 1657, Murad Bakhsh, the Mughal viceroy of Gujarat, began consolidating his authority in the province to position himself for the ensuing power struggle. On 30 November 1657, he formally proclaimed himself emperor at Ahmedabad, adopting the title Ghazi ud-din Muhammad Murad Bakhsh Padshah.[23] This declaration aimed to legitimize his rule locally and rally support against his elder brother Dara Shikoh, who had assumed effective control of the imperial court in Agra as regent.[14] To assert sovereignty and finance military preparations, Murad immediately ordered the minting of silver rupees bearing his name and titles at Surat, marking a symbolic break from Shah Jahan's authority.[14] Concurrently, he dispatched a contingent of 6,000 troops to Surat to impose a forced loan of 500,000 rupees on wealthy merchants, providing essential funds for troop recruitment and logistics while securing the vital port against potential threats from Shah Shuja's naval forces in Bengal. These measures reflected Murad's strategy of rapid resource mobilization in Gujarat, leveraging his governorship to build an army estimated at 20,000–25,000 men, primarily cavalry, drawn from local Mughal nobles and Afghan mercenaries loyal to his command. Murad's initial military strategy focused on northward advance toward Agra to confront Dara's forces directly, bypassing immediate coordination with distant rivals. In early 1658, he mobilized his army from Ahmedabad, crossing the Narmada River and engaging in preliminary skirmishes with imperial detachments sent by Dara, which tested his troops' resolve but avoided decisive commitment until potential allies could converge. This aggressive posture, however, exposed vulnerabilities, as Murad's independent thrust risked isolation amid the multi-front contest, prompting overtures to Aurangzeb in the Deccan for joint action against Dara.[24]Alliance with Aurangzeb and Major Battles In early 1658, following his proclamation as emperor in Ahmedabad on November 30, 1657, Murad Bakhsh marched northward from Gujarat to join forces with Aurangzeb, who was advancing from the Deccan toward Agra. The brothers concluded a secret treaty near Burhanpur, whereby Aurangzeb would assume the throne as emperor, while Murad would receive sovereign authority over Punjab, Kabul, Kashmir, and associated territories as a partitioned share of the empire.[25] The allied army, numbering approximately 80,000 under Aurangzeb's overall command with Murad leading a contingent on the left wing, first engaged imperial forces loyal to Dara Shikoh at the Battle of Dharmat on April 15, 1658. Opposing them was Maharaja Jaswant Singh's army of 30,000 to 60,000 troops, dispatched to block the advance. Murad's division exploited a gap by attacking Jaswant Singh's camp directly, contributing to the decisive Mughal victory that resulted in heavy casualties for the imperial side, including the death of several key commanders, and cleared the path to Agra.[26][25] The alliance culminated in the Battle of Samugarh on May 29, 1658, near Agra, where the combined forces of Aurangzeb and Murad—totaling around 60,000—faced Dara Shikoh's larger army of over 100,000, bolstered by artillery and Rajput contingents. Positioned on the left, Murad, enraged by enemy cannon fire, launched an unauthorized but effective cavalry charge with his sowars against Dara's right flank, commanded by Rao Chhatarsal Hada. This breakthrough shattered the opposing line; Murad personally slew the Rajput second-in-command, Ram Singh Rathore, using a composite bow, turning the tide in a battle marked by intense close-quarters fighting and heavy artillery exchanges. The victory, achieved with significant losses on both sides, allowed Aurangzeb to capture Agra and effectively end Dara's claim.[3][25]Betrayal, Capture, and Downfall Following the victory at Samugarh on 29 May 1658, Aurangzeb turned against his ally Murad Bakhsh, despite their earlier treaty sworn on the Quran that promised Murad control over Punjab, Kabul, Kashmir, and Thatta in exchange for joint rule. Aurangzeb employed subterfuge, dispatching agents to foment defections among Murad's officers and circulating propaganda that portrayed Murad as unstable and unfit, thereby isolating him militarily and politically.[11] On 25 June 1658, Murad, encamped near Mathura, was induced to enter Aurangzeb's tent under assurances of a formal partition agreement. There, amid a feast involving wine—prohibited under Islamic law—Murad was abruptly seized by Mughal troops led by Shaista Khan, Aurangzeb's uncle and a key commander. This calculated betrayal, executed without resistance due to Murad's diminished forces, resulted in his immediate confinement in Delhi's Salimgarh Fort.[11][13] Murad's capture dismantled his imperial pretensions, including his self-proclamation as emperor earlier that year, and extinguished any prospect of co-rulership, allowing Aurangzeb to consolidate power unchallenged after defeating residual threats from Dara Shikoh and Shah Shuja. Transferred to Gwalior Fort by January 1659 for stricter security, Murad's downfall symbolized the ruthless fraternal competition inherent in Mughal succession struggles, where alliances dissolved into treachery upon tactical advantage.[11]Imprisonment, Execution, and Historical Assessment Charges and Execution In late 1661, following three years of imprisonment after his betrayal and capture during the war of succession, Murad Bakhsh was brought to trial on charges of murder. The accusation centered on his earlier execution of Ali Naqi, a clerk (or diwan) in Gujarat's provincial administration, whom Murad had put to death around 1657 for alleged embezzlement and misconduct while serving as governor.[3][11] The timing and revival of this charge—despite its prior occurrence under Murad's authority without immediate legal repercussions—have been viewed by historians as politically expedient, providing Aurangzeb a pretext to eliminate a potential rival under the guise of judicial process.[3] Aurangzeb reportedly encouraged Ali Naqi's son to file the formal complaint, leveraging the qazi (Islamic judge) to convict Murad despite questionable evidentiary merits, as the killing had been an administrative act rather than a personal crime.[11] The trial proceeded swiftly in prison, resulting in a death sentence upheld by orthodox Islamic legal standards emphasizing qisas (retaliation for murder). On December 14, 1661, Murad was beheaded in his cell at Gwalior Fort, where he had been confined since 1658; contemporary accounts in Persian chronicles like the Maasir-i-Alamgiri describe the execution as a deliberate act to foreclose any future claims to the throne.[3][11] This event marked the final elimination of Shah Jahan's sons as active threats to Aurangzeb's rule, underscoring the ruthless realpolitik of Mughal succession struggles.[3]Character Evaluations and Legacy Contemporary accounts and later historical assessments portray Murad Bakhsh as a figure of martial valor but limited political acumen. French traveler François Bernier, who observed the Mughal court in the 1650s, noted Murad's inferiority to his brothers in judgment and address, suggesting a deficiency in diplomatic finesse despite his administrative roles in Gujarat and Multan. Historian Stanley Lane-Poole characterized him as a "gallant swashbuckler, brave as a lion, frank and open-handed," highlighting his boldness in battle, such as his decisive charge at the Battle of Dharmat in 1657 where he personally slew the Rajput commander.[7][3] Mughal chronicles and European observers also noted personal flaws, including a temperament prone to impetuosity and indulgence in alcohol, which undermined his governance and decision-making.[27] For instance, during his viceroyalty in Gujarat, reports of excessive drinking and favoritism toward associates like Shaista Khan contributed to perceptions of irresponsibility.[2] These traits, combined with overambition during the succession war, led to his alliance with Aurangzeb, whom he naively trusted, resulting in betrayal after the Battle of Samugarh on May 29, 1658.[28] Murad Bakhsh's legacy endures as a cautionary tale of fraternal rivalry and the perils of Mughal imperial succession, where princes vied ruthlessly for the throne, often ending in elimination of rivals.[29] His execution on December 14, 1661, on charges of treason fabricated by Aurangzeb, exemplifies the emperor's consolidation of power through elimination of potential threats, a pattern repeated with brothers Dara Shikoh and Shuja.[3] Scholar Munis D. Faruqui argues that such princely competitions, while destabilizing, were integral to maintaining the empire's vitality until the 18th century, though Murad's failure highlighted the risks of underestimating rivals' cunning.[29] Posthumously, he is remembered less for enduring contributions than for embodying the tragic ambition of lesser princes overshadowed by Aurangzeb's ascendancy, with his lineage continuing marginally through descendants like Izid Bakhsh.References - https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/History_of_Aurangzib/Volume_1/Chapter_13 - https://handwiki.org/wiki/Biography:Shah_Jahan

Murad's siblings included his two politically powerful sisters, the princesses: Jahanara Begum and Roshanara Begum as well as the heir-apparent to his father, his eldest brother, Crown Prince Dara Shikoh and the future Mughal Emperor Aurangzeb. Personal life In 1638, Murad Bakhsh, at the age of fourteen years married the Safavid princess, Sakina Banu Begum, a daughter of Shah Nawaz Khan Safavi. She was the younger sister of his elder sister-in-law, Dilras Banu Begum, who was Aurangzeb's wife. Governorship He was appointed to Subadar of Multan (1642), of Balkh (16 February 1646 to 9 August 1646), of Kashmir (20 August 1647 to July 1648), of Deccan (25 July 1648 to 14 September 1649), and Kabul (23 January 1650 to 1654), of Gujarat (March 1654), and Malwa. Courtiers War of succession On 30 November 1657, he proclaimed himself emperor at Ahmedabad, after reports that his father was ill. During the same year he received the Ottoman ambassador Manzada Husain Agha, who arrived in the port of Surat and was on his way to meet Shah Jahan in Agra. Manzada Husain Agha mentions his disappointment regarding the wars between Shah Jahan's sons. Murad Bakhsh joined hands with Aurangzeb to defeat Dara Shikhoh, the eldest son of Shah Jahan. In fact it was the ferocious charge led by Murad Bakhsh and his Sowars that eventually turned the outcome of the battle in favor of Aurangzeb during the Battle of Samugarh. On 7 July 1658, while he was in a tent with his brother Aurangzeb, he was intoxicated and was secretly sent to the prison and transferred to Gwalior Fort from January 1659. He faced a trial that sentenced him to death for having murdered former Diwan clerk named Ali Naqi, in 1661. Aurangzeb then replaced Murad Bakhsh as the Subedar of Gujarat and placed Inayat Khan as the new Mughal commander of Surat. Military promotions Death On 14 December 1661, after three years in prison, he was executed at Gwalior Fort. With the last of his brothers now dead, Aurangzeb was the undisputed emperor of the Mughal Empire.
