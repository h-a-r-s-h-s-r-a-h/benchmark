Jett Williams (born Antha Belle Jett; January 6, 1953) is an American country music singer and songwriter best known as the posthumous and previously unacknowledged daughter of country legend Hank Williams Sr. and Bobbie Jett, a brief romantic partner of the singer.[1][2] Born in Montgomery, Alabama, just five days after her father's death on January 1, 1953, Williams was legally adopted in December 1954 by her paternal grandmother, Lillian Stone, who renamed her Catherine "Cathy" Yvonne Stone.[3][4] Following Stone's death in February 1955, Williams experienced multiple foster placements and name changes, remaining unaware of her true parentage until investigations in the early 1980s revealed a hidden 1952 contract outlining custody arrangements between her parents.[5][6]In 1985, Williams—then working as a recreational officer—launched a series of legal battles in Alabama and New York courts to establish her biological ties and claim a share of her father's multimillion-dollar estate, which generated approximately $1 million in annual royalties at the time.[2] A Montgomery County Circuit Court ruling in 1987 confirmed her as Hank Williams Sr.'s daughter, supported by the discovered contract and blood tests, granting her partial royalties; the Alabama Supreme Court reopened the estate in 1989, though disputes with her half-brother Hank Williams Jr. and other heirs continued into the 1990s.[2][4] Honoring her biological parents, she legally changed her name to Jett Williams in the late 1980s and transitioned to a full-time music career, debuting professionally in 1989 with performances blending her father's classic hits and her original songs.[1][2]Williams has since built a notable career preserving her father's legacy while establishing her own, collaborating with surviving members of his backing band, the Drifting Cowboys, under producer Owen Bradley and performing at venues including Nashville's Grand Ole Opry, where she paid tribute to Hank on what would have been his 80th birthday in 2003.[2][7] She has released several albums featuring her own songs and tributes to her father. In 1990, she published her autobiography, Ain't Nothin' as Sweet as My Baby, detailing her tumultuous early life and quest for identity.[5] In 2006, she and Hank Williams Jr. won court rulings affirming ownership of her father's early radio performances.[8] Now age 72 (as of 2025) and semi-retired from touring, Williams remains active in philanthropy and legacy preservation efforts, including support for music education and Hank Williams-related initiatives.[3] Early Life Birth and Immediate Aftermath Jett Williams was born Antha Belle Jett on January 6, 1953, in Montgomery, Alabama, just five days after her father, Hank Williams Sr., died of heart failure en route to a performance on January 1, 1953.[4][2] Her mother, Bobbie Jett, had carried the pregnancy during a brief romantic relationship with Hank Williams that occurred between his two marriages; the two were never wed.[2]In October 1952, as Bobbie Jett's pregnancy progressed, she and Hank Williams entered into a formal agreement stipulating that she would relinquish her parental rights upon the child's birth, with Williams assuming custody and responsibility for the infant's care.[2][9] This arrangement reflected Williams' acknowledgment of paternity, though the child remained legally classified as illegitimate under contemporary laws.[9]Williams' last will and testament, executed in November 1952, bequeathed his entire estate to his then-wife, Billie Jean Jones Williams, and their son, Randall Hank Williams, explicitly making no provision for unborn children or potential illegitimate offspring.[9] This omission contributed to the immediate postwar denial of formal paternity recognition for the newborn, complicating her status amid the family's grief and legal proceedings following Williams' sudden death.[9]Faced with acute financial hardship—exacerbated by the loss of promised support from Williams—and intense social stigma surrounding unwed motherhood in the 1950s South, Bobbie Jett ultimately decided to place the infant for adoption shortly after birth.[2][6] The agreement's terms, intended to secure the child's future under Williams' care, were rendered moot by his death, leaving Jett in uncertain immediate circumstances.[2] Adoption and Upbringing Jett Williams, born Antha Belle Jett, was placed for adoption shortly after her birth on January 6, 1953, in Montgomery, Alabama. Her biological mother, Bobbie Jett, who had a brief relationship with Hank Williams, agreed to the adoption as part of a custody arrangement intended to involve Hank's family. In December 1954, at approximately 23 months old, she was legally adopted by her paternal grandmother, Lillie Stone (also known as Lillian Williams Stone), who renamed her Catherine Yvonne Stone and provided care in Montgomery until Lillie's death in February 1955.[1][10]Following Lillie's passing, the two-year-old Jett became a ward of the state of Alabama and was placed in temporary foster care. At age three, in 1956, she was adopted by Wayne Deupree, a successful businessman, and his wife, Louise, a couple residing in Mobile, Alabama; they renamed her Cathy Louise Deupree and raised her in their middle-class household. Wayne and Louise provided a stable environment, while the family attended church regularly and emphasized education. Jett enjoyed a typical Southern childhood, participating in school activities, playing with neighborhood children, and developing an early affinity for country music through radio broadcasts of artists like Hank Williams—unaware at the time of any personal connection.[2][11][12]Wayne Deupree died in 1983. Jett maintained a close bond with Louise, who supported her through high school in Mobile and later encouraged her pursuit of her biological heritage. Throughout her upbringing, Jett had no direct contact with Bobbie Jett, who offered no financial or emotional support and lived out of state; Bobbie died in 1974 from undisclosed causes, still estranged from her daughter. Unbeknownst to Jett, her biological heritage remained hidden, allowing her to grow up with a sense of normalcy focused on academics and emerging musical hobbies, such as singing in school talent shows and learning guitar informally.[13][14] Discovery of Parentage Initial Revelations In the mid-1980s, Jett Williams, then known as Cathy Adkinson, intensified her search for confirmation of her biological origins following the death of her adoptive father, Wayne Deupree, in 1983. Although she had learned at age 21 from her adoptive mother of a possible connection to Hank Williams as her father and that her biological mother was Bobbie Jett, she sought definitive proof. This came in 1984 when documents surfaced, including a pre-birth custody agreement signed by Hank Williams and Bobbie Jett in October 1952, confirming Hank Williams Sr. as her parent.[2][1]The agreement stipulated that Hank Williams would assume full custody and financial responsibility for the unborn child, with his mother, Lillie Stone Williams, also involved in the arrangements; after Hank's death on January 1, 1953, Lillie briefly held temporary custody before facilitating the child's adoption, though no financial support was ultimately provided to Bobbie Jett as anticipated. Jett, who had grown up unaware of this musical heritage in Mobile, Alabama, described the discovery as overwhelming and obsessive, prompting her to pursue verification through blood tests with known relatives and extensive review of archival records from Hank Williams' life.[4][5]Amid these steps, Jett reached out to Hank Williams Jr., her potential half-brother, who initially rejected the connection and expressed skepticism about her claims. Over time, however, he acknowledged a familial tie, though their relationship remained strained during the initial period.[2][3] Name Change and Identity Affirmation Following the 1987 court confirmation of her parentage, Jett Williams, born Antha Belle Jett, legally changed her name in 1987 to Jett Williams, a combination of her mother Bobbie Jett's maiden name and her father Hank Williams' surname.[1] This change symbolized her embrace of her biological heritage after years of secrecy surrounding her adoption and upbringing.
