Haji Muhammad Nawaz Khokhar was a Pakistani politician and businessman from Islamabad who represented the Pakistan People's Party (PPP) as a Member of the National Assembly (MNA) during the 1980s and 1990s, serving as Deputy Speaker from 4 November 1990 to 18 July 1993.[1][2]Elected to the National Assembly in the constituencies of 1985–1988, 1990–1993, and 1993–1996, he held the position of Federal Minister for Science and Technology from 31 July to 5 November 1996.[2][3][4]In 2004, Khokhar was convicted by an accountability court on charges related to misuse of authority and sentenced to three years' imprisonment with a fine of Rs 1.8 million.[5]A veteran PPP loyalist known for his influence in Rawalpindi and Islamabad politics, as well as business ventures, he was the father of senator Mustafa Nawaz Khokhar and died on 9 January 2021, after which his funeral drew condolences from PPP leadership.[4][2][6] Early life and education Family background and upbringing Haji Muhammad Nawaz Khokhar was born around 1947 into the Khokhar clan, a landed and politically active family exerting local influence in the Rawalpindi region of Punjab, Pakistan.[7]He grew up in Koral, a village near Rawalpindi, amid family landholdings that faced disruptions from the expansion of Islamabad as Pakistan's federal capital starting in the 1960s, which altered local socio-economic dynamics through rising land values and urban development pressures.[7] This environment of clan-based networks and territorial changes exposed him early to the interplay of rural influence, kinship obligations, and emerging political opportunities in the twin cities area.As the recognized head of the Khokhar clan, Khokhar upheld verifiable kinship ties central to the family's cohesion, notably as the elder brother to Imtiaz Khokhar (also known as Taji Khokhar), who shared in the clan's local prominence but faced separate legal entanglements later in life.[7][8] These familial structures, rooted in Punjabi tribal traditions, fostered his initial worldview oriented toward leveraging clansolidarity for navigating regional power dynamics, independent of formal institutions.[7] Education and early professional pursuits Khokhar completed his education as a graduate of Gordon College in Rawalpindi, a prominent institution known for its role in producing notable figures in Pakistan's public life.[7] Public records provide limited details on his formal schooling beyond this attainment, with no verified accounts of primary or secondary education or advanced degrees.[7]Prior to his formal entry into national politics, Khokhar's early professional activities remain sparsely documented, with indications of involvement in local affairs in Islamabad during the late 1960s.[9] He transitioned toward political engagement around this period, aligning with the formation of the Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP) and assuming roles such as general secretary in its early organizational structure, marking a shift from personal or administrative endeavors to public service preparation amid Pakistan's evolving political landscape under Ayub Khan's regime.[9] This foundational phase preceded his electoral debut in the 1980s, reflecting a pragmatic buildup of networks in the capital region during the Zia-ul-Haq era's precursors.[7] Political career Initial involvement and party affiliations Khokhar's political engagement began with the Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP), where he joined as one of its early members shortly after its founding in 1967 and rose to serve as secretary general for the Rawalpindi division during the 1970s.[6] This role positioned him within the party's grassroots organizational structure in Punjab, amid the turbulent post-1971 era following the PPP's rise to power under Zulfikar Ali Bhutto.The 1977 military coup by General Zia-ul-Haq, which led to the execution of Bhutto in 1979 and a ban on political parties, prompted a pragmatic pivot in Khokhar's career. He participated in the non-partisan elections orchestrated by Zia's regime, contesting as an independent in the 1985 National Assembly polls for an Islamabad constituency, leveraging the regime's controlled electoral framework as a pathway to formal political office despite the absence of party symbols or platforms.[10]With the partial restoration of party politics in the late 1980s, Khokhar transitioned affiliations away from the PPP, aligning with the Islami Jamhoori Ittehad (IJI)—a nine-party conservative alliance backed by elements of the military establishment to oppose Benazir Bhutto's PPP in the 1988 and 1990 elections. This shift integrated him into the orbit of Nawaz Sharif's Pakistan Muslim League faction, foreshadowing his later PML-N ties and exemplifying the opportunistic realignments prevalent in Pakistan's post-Zia landscape, where ideological consistency often yielded to alliances offering electoral viability and patronage amid institutional instability. Elections to the National Assembly Haji Muhammad Nawaz Khokhar secured election to the National Assembly from constituency NA-35 (Federal Capital Territory, encompassing Islamabad) in the non-partisan general elections of February 25, 1985, serving until 1988.[11][10] Contesting as an independent amid a ban on political parties under General Zia-ul-Haq's military regime, Khokhar leveraged clan networks from the Khokhartribe, prominent in Islamabad's rural periphery, to emphasize infrastructure and development priorities for the capital's underserved areas. These polls, managed directly by military authorities, featured low voter participation—nationally around 57%—and widespread reports of pre-election engineering, such as candidate vetting and suppression of opposition voices, to consolidate regime support.[12]Khokhar was reelected from the same NA-35 seat in the general elections of October 24, 1990, representing the Islami Jamhoori Ittehad (IJI) alliance, with his term lasting until 1993.[13][14] His platform continued to highlight local governance and territorial advancement in the Federal Capital, bolstered by tribal affiliations that provided grassroots mobilization in a constituency blending urban bureaucracy with peri-urban villages. The 1990 contests occurred under civilian restoration but with documented military intervention, including intelligence-led manipulations like ballot stuffing and undue influence on polling agents, aimed at countering the Pakistan Peoples Party's resurgence; national turnout hovered near 43%, reflecting disillusionment amid such tactics.[15]In the October 6, 1993 elections, following the Supreme Court's intervention in political crises, Khokhar won NA-35 again, this time under the Pakistan Muslim League-Nawaz (PML-N) banner, holding the seat through 1996.[16][17] Campaigning persisted on constituency-specific concerns like land rights and civic amenities, capitalizing on familial and clan sway to represent indigenous interests against transient urban elites. While 1993 polls saw higher national turnout of about 60% and were deemed comparatively freer due to judicial oversight, isolated irregularities persisted in rural pockets, including voter intimidation typical of establishment-influenced dynamics in federal territories.[18] Tenure as Deputy Speaker Haji Muhammad Nawaz Khokhar served as the 13th Deputy Speaker of the National Assembly of Pakistan from November 4, 1990, to July 18, 1993, a tenure that concluded with the dissolution of the assembly by President Ghulam Ishaq Khan.[1][7] Elected to the position by assembly members shortly after the 1990 general elections, he received 143 votes against opponent Fateh Muhammad M. Hassani's 47, reflecting support from the ruling Islami Jamhoori Ittehad (IJI) coalition.[19][14]In this role, Khokhar deputized for Speaker Gohar Ayub Khan, presiding over sessions to ensure orderly conduct, enforce procedural rules, and oversee debates on legislative matters during the IJI government's term under Prime MinisterNawaz Sharif.[7] His responsibilities included maintaining decorum amid opposition challenges from the Pakistan People's Party (PPP), which had been ousted from power earlier in 1990, and facilitating the assembly's functions in a politically charged environment marked by efforts to pass economic stabilization measures.[20] The deputy speakership positioned him as a key figure in parliamentary oversight, acting as a procedural guardian without direct policy-making authority.[19]Khokhar's oversight extended to routine assembly operations, including question hours and committee referrals, during a period of governmental focus on fiscal reforms following the prior administration's economic instability.[7] No major controversies directly attributed to his procedural decisions are recorded in available parliamentary records from this era, underscoring a tenure oriented toward institutional continuity rather than partisan flashpoints.[1] The assembly's abrupt dissolution in July 1993, amid escalating executive-judicial tensions, ended his service without further extension.[1] Role as Minister of Science and Technology Haji Muhammad Nawaz Khokhar was appointed Federal Minister for Science and Technology on 31 July 1996 in Prime Minister Benazir Bhutto's second cabinet. His term concluded on 5 November 1996 with the dissolution of the National Assembly by President Farooq Leghari, who cited corruption, nepotism, and extra-constitutional actions as grounds for dismissal. This 97-day period occurred during escalating political instability, including opposition protests and judicial probes into government misconduct.The Ministry of Science and Technology, under Khokhar's oversight, managed ongoing programs for scientific research and industrial application, including entities like the Pakistan Council of Scientific and Industrial Research focused on applied R&D in areas such as chemicals and materials. However, the short tenure precluded substantive new policy launches or institutional reforms, with no verifiable records of increased funding allocations, patent surges, or project completions tied to his direct interventions.Pakistan's science and technology sector faced systemic constraints during this era, including chronic underfunding—R&D expenditure hovered below 0.3% of GDP—and bureaucratic inefficiencies that hampered implementation regardless of leadership changes. Political volatility, culminating in the government's ouster, further eroded prospects for sustained impact, as ministries operated under interim uncertainty and resource reallocations. Khokhar's role thus exemplified the challenges of advancing technical development amid governance disruptions, yielding no measurable long-term advancements in the sector. Personal life and associations Family and descendants Haji Muhammad Nawaz Khokhar was married and fathered three sons.[9] His son Mustafa Nawaz Khokhar pursued a political career, serving as a senator for the Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP) from 2018 to 2022 and advocating on human rights issues.[6] The other two sons engaged in business activities, diverging from Mustafa's public political role.[9]The Khokhar family's influence persisted through familial networks in the Islamabad and Rawalpindi areas, where clan leadership roles facilitated intergenerational continuity in local affairs, though individual trajectories varied—Mustafa's PPP affiliation contrasted with his father's earlier PPP ties and subsequent shifts to other parties like PML-Q.[7] No verifiable public records detail specific family-owned real estate holdings directly attributable to Nawaz Khokhar's immediate descendants beyond general business involvement.[9] Connections to influential figures Khokhar maintained a close personal friendship with Malik Riaz, the founder of Bahria Town, Pakistan's largest private real estate developer, which reportedly began during the Pakistan Peoples Party's (PPP) tenure in 1993 and provided mutual political and business support.[7] In July 2015, Riaz accompanied Khokhar to a Rawalpindi police station to file a complaint against alleged blackmailers targeting Bahria Town operations.[21] The two collaborated publicly, such as in May 2018 when Riaz inaugurated a mosque in Bahria Town with Khokhar present, highlighting their shared involvement in philanthropic initiatives amid Bahria's expansion in urban housing projects around Islamabad and Rawalpindi.[22]These ties extended through Khokhar's family, particularly his younger brother Imtiaz Khokhar (alias Taji Khokhar), a figure accused of facilitating land acquisitions for Bahria Town via coercive methods, including threats to landowners in disputes over properties in areas like Lohi Bher.[23] Taji Khokhar, described in reports as a notorious land mafia operative in Rawalpindi, allegedly worked on Riaz's behalf in navigating regulatory hurdles and resolving encroachments, contributing to Bahria Town's growth into a major infrastructure provider but also drawing allegations of favoritism and illegal land grabs that bypassed standard allocation processes.[24] While such networks arguably accelerated housing development benefiting urban populations, critics pointed to potential cronyism, as family influence reportedly enabled preferential access to federal capital land amid Islamabad's post-1967 expansion, though no direct convictions tied Khokhar himself to these claims.[7][25]In politics, Khokhar forged strong alliances with Pakistan Muslim League-Nawaz (PML-N) leadership, including hosting Nawaz Sharif during the party's formative years and rising to deputy speaker of the National Assembly from 1990 to 1993 under the Islami Jamhoori Ittehad coalition, which later evolved into PML-N.[7] His cross-party maneuvering, from early PPP membership to PML-N centrality, positioned him as a bridge to establishment figures, yielding mutual benefits in electoral support and policy facilitation for development projects, though it invited scrutiny over loyalty shifts during military-influenced transitions like the 1985 non-party elections.[7] These connections underscored elite networks in Pakistani politics, where personal ties often intersected with urban real estate gains, balancing infrastructure advancements against persistent charges of undue influence in land and regulatory matters.[26] Death and immediate aftermath Final years and illness Following his service as Minister of Science and Technology in the mid-1990s, Haji Muhammad Nawaz Khokhar reduced his involvement in national-level politics, directing attention toward family and clan interests in the Islamabad-Rawalpindi vicinity. He retained local prominence, as evidenced by his participation in a 2010 land dispute with cousin Abdul Rasheed over approximately 10 kanals in Alipur Farash, reflecting ongoing engagement in property and tribal matters typical of Potohar region dynamics.Khokhar's health began to decline in advanced age, culminating in a prolonged illness that necessitated medical care. Family sources reported he had been unwell for an extended period prior to hospitalization in Islamabad.[2][27] No public details emerged on the precise nature of the condition, though it involved sustained treatment at a local facility.[28] Funeral and public response Haji Muhammad Nawaz Khokhar passed away on January 9, 2021, at the age of 74 in Islamabad, succumbing to complications from a recent COVID-19 infection that had damaged his lungs despite subsequent negative tests.[9][7] His funeral prayers were held the following day, January 10, in Rawalpindi at Khokhar House, drawing a large crowd including family members and reflecting the stature of the influential Khokhar clan in local politics.[29][27]The proceedings were attended by prominent political figures from the Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP), such as Chairman Bilawal Bhutto Zardari, former President Asif Ali Zardari, Senators Farhatullah Babar and Quratul Ain Marri, as well as cross-party leaders including Senate Chairman Sadiq Sanjrani, opposition leader Raja Zafarul Haq, and Information Minister Shibli Faraz.[27] Interior Minister Sheikh Rashid Ahmed visited the family to offer condolences, while Punjab Chief Minister Sardar Usman Buzdar expressed grief over the loss.[30][27] A Senate delegation led by Chairman Sanjrani also called on Khokhar's son, Senator Mustafa Nawaz Khokhar, to condole the death.[31]Public and media responses emphasized Khokhar's enduring political influence, with PPP leadership and workers expressing deep sorrow.[27]Bilawal Bhutto Zardari highlighted Khokhar's steadfast loyalty to Benazir Bhutto, while Sanjrani described him as a sagacious politician.[27] An obituary in The News International portrayed him as a "larger-than-life" figure who led the politically active Khokhar clan through decades of maneuvering in Islamabad's power dynamics.[7] Legacy and influence Contributions to Pakistani politics Khokhar's repeated elections to the National Assembly from Islamabad's constituency—serving terms from 1985 to 1988, 1990 to 1993, and 1993 to 1996—reflected his capacity to deliver targeted development initiatives, including infrastructure enhancements and housing support in the capital's peripheral rural areas, where empirical growth in urban sprawl correlated with MNA-led advocacy for federal allocations during the post-Zia democratization phase.[2][7] His focus on constituency needs indirectly bolstered Islamabad's expansion by leveraging political networks to prioritize land regularization and basic amenities, contributing to the city's population influx from 300,000 in 1981 to over 800,000 by 1998 as per census data.[9]As Deputy Speaker of the National Assembly from November 4, 1990, to July 18, 1993, Khokhar upheld procedural discipline during a volatile era of civilian-military tensions and governmental dissolutions, facilitating smoother legislative deliberations in the assembly's transitional framework following the restoration of democracy in 1988.[1] His tenure, under Prime Minister Nawaz Sharif's administration, emphasized cross-party neutrality, enabling the passage of key bills amid opposition challenges and presidential interventions, which stabilized parliamentary operations in the early 1990s.[7]Khokhar further advanced Pakistan's diplomatic outreach by leading an eight-member parliamentary delegation to Kuwait in early 1991, amid the Gulf War's aftermath, to strengthen bilateral ties and parliamentary exchanges during a critical juncture for regional stability.[32] These efforts underscored his role in bridging domestic governance with international engagement, enhancing the National Assembly's institutional credibility at a time when Pakistan navigated post-Cold War realignments.[9] Criticisms and controversies Khokhar faced multiple legal challenges, including an arrest on fraud charges in January 1995 while serving as a critic of Prime Minister Benazir Bhutto's Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP) administration.[33] In November 1999, following the military coup, he was among 21 prominent figures detained for alleged loan defaults accumulated during his prior ministerial role under Bhutto's PPP government.[34] Additionally, the National Accountability Bureau (NAB) pursued a reference against him in the early 2000s for allegedly acquiring 150 kanals of shamlat deh (village common land) in Punjab through undue influence, prompting him to opt for a plea bargain to settle the matter.[35][36]His political trajectory involved switching allegiances from the PPP—where he held ministerial positions—to the Pakistan Muslim League (Nawaz) faction via the Islami Jamhoori Ittehad alliance by the early 1990s, culminating in his election as deputy speaker of the National Assembly in 1997 under Nawaz Sharif's PML-N government. Such shifts, common in Pakistan's patronage-driven system, drew perceptions of opportunism, as they prioritized access to power over ideological fidelity amid the era's PPP-PML rivalry.[34]Khokhar's stint as Minister of Science and Technology (1997–1999) attracted minimal contemporaneous critique, though broader analyses of Sharif's tenure highlight stagnant technological progress despite allocated budgets, attributing delays to bureaucratic inefficiencies and political instability rather than individual malfeasance.
