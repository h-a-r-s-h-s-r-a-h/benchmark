--- [Oliver Heaviside - Wikipedia] ---
Oliver Heaviside ( HEV-ee-syde; 18 May 1850 – 3 February 1925) was a British mathematician and electrical engineer who invented a new technique for solving differential equations (equivalent to the Laplace transform), independently developed vector calculus, and rewrote Maxwell's equations in the form commonly used today. He significantly shaped the way Maxwell's equations were understood and applied in the decades following Maxwell's death. Also, in 1893, he extended them to gravitoelectromagnetism, which was confirmed by Gravity Probe B in 2005. His formulation of the telegrapher's equations became commercially important during his own lifetime, after their significance went unremarked for a long while, as few others were versed at the time in his novel methodology. Although at odds with the scientific establishment for most of his life, Heaviside changed the face of telecommunications, mathematics, and science.

Oliver Heaviside (18 May 1850 – 3 February 1925) was a self-taught British mathematician, physicist, and electrical engineer renowned for his pioneering work in electromagnetic theory and applied mathematics.[1] Born in Camden Town, London, to a wood engraver father, Heaviside suffered from partial deafness due to childhood scarlet fever and left formal schooling at age 16, instead educating himself in languages, electricity, and Morse code.[1] He briefly worked as a telegrapher in Denmark (1868) and Newcastle upon Tyne (1871) before abandoning the profession in 1874 to devote himself to independent research on electromagnetism, supported by his brother Arthur, also an engineer.[1][2]Heaviside's most influential contribution to physics was his reformulation of James Clerk Maxwell's cumbersome 20 equations in 20 variables into the four concise vector-based equations now known as Maxwell's equations, accomplished independently of the concurrent work by J. Willard Gibbs around 1884 and published in his multivolume Electromagnetic Theory (1893–1912).[3][4] This simplification, using modern vector notation that Heaviside helped develop alongside Gibbs, provided a clearer framework for understanding electromagnetic waves and fields, profoundly influencing electrical engineering and relativity theory.[5][6] In applied mathematics, he invented operational calculus in the 1880s, a method treating differentiation and integration as algebraic operations (e.g., p=d/dt) to solve linear differential equations arising in electrical circuits and transmission lines, which laid groundwork for later Laplace transform techniques despite initial criticism for its lack of rigor.[7][8]Heaviside also predicted the existence of a conductive atmospheric layer—now called the ionosphere or Kennelly–Heaviside layer—in 1902 to explain long-distance radio wave propagation, a hypothesis experimentally confirmed in 1924 by Edward Appleton.[9][10][11] His work extended to telegraphy, where he derived the telegrapher's equations modeling signal distortion in cables, and to broader electromagnetic phenomena, including Poynting's theorem (independently derived). Elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1891, Heaviside lived reclusively in Torquay from 1909 onward, exhibiting signs of paranoia, and died in relative obscurity despite his transformative impact on modern physics and engineering.[1][5][10] Biography Early Life Oliver Heaviside was born on May 18, 1850, in Camden Town, a working-class district of north London, England. He was the youngest of four sons born to Thomas Heaviside, a wood engraver and watercolor artist who worked as a small tradesman, and Rachel (née West) Heaviside, a former governess who ran a small school for neighborhood children from their modest home. The family lived in straitened circumstances, with Thomas's irregular income contributing to financial instability that would later affect the children's opportunities.[1][12][13]Heaviside's early years were marked by significant health challenges that shaped his personality and social interactions. At around age nine, he contracted scarlet fever, which left him with a profound hearing impairment—described as near-deafness—that persisted lifelong and contributed to his withdrawal from peers and preference for solitary pursuits. Despite this, he showed intellectual promise, influenced by his family's environment; his older brothers, Arthur West and Charles, pursued careers in engineering and the musical industry, respectively, providing early exposure to technical and scientific ideas through shared resources and discussions in the household. Their successes, particularly Arthur's role as a telegraph engineer with the General Post Office, subtly inspired Heaviside's later interests without direct involvement at this stage.[1][12][14][15]Heaviside received limited formal education, attending his mother's private elementary school until age twelve and then a local grammar school, where he performed well academically—ranking fifth out of five hundred pupils in 1865—but left at sixteen in 1866 due to the family's financial constraints, without pursuing higher qualifications. Undeterred, he turned to self-study, drawing on the family library and his brothers' professional materials to explore mathematics, physics, and languages such as German and Danish. By age fifteen, Heaviside developed a keen fascination with electricity, conducting simple experiments with homemade batteries and rudimentary telegraphic equipment, which honed his practical skills and laid the groundwork for his independent learning in scientific principles.[1][12][13][14] Professional Career Heaviside began his professional career in 1868 at the age of 18, joining the Great Northern Telegraph Company as a telegraph operator, first in Denmark and then, from 1871, in Newcastle upon Tyne, England, where he worked under the supervision of his older brother Arthur, a superintendent at the company.[1] His daily duties included operating submarine telegraph cables for trans-Scandinavian communications, diagnosing and repairing signal faults, and managing high-speed Morse code transmissions, which exposed him to practical challenges in long-distance electrical signaling such as attenuation and distortion. This role provided early familiarity with James Clerk Maxwell's electromagnetic theories through company resources. In May 1874, citing increasing deafness and a desire for deeper study, Heaviside resigned to pursue independent research in electricity and mathematics full-time.[16]From 1874 through the 1890s, Heaviside supported himself through independent research, initially living with his parents in London on a modest income from savings and family assistance. After his father's death in 1894 and his mother's in 1896, he inherited a small sum from her estate but relied primarily on financial support from his brother Arthur, an engineer, with family assistance including from his brother Charles, who operated a successful music shop.[12][17]In 1889, Heaviside relocated with his aging parents from London to Paignton in Devon for greater solitude conducive to his theoretical pursuits, settling above Charles's music shop near the coast. This mid-career move to a quieter rural setting allowed him to immerse himself in mathematical and electromagnetic studies away from urban distractions.Heaviside maintained key correspondences during his productive years, including a brief exchange with James Clerk Maxwell in 1873 regarding vector analysis and more sustained discussions with Lord Kelvin (William Thomson) from the late 1870s onward about wave propagation and cable theory.[1] His interactions with William Henry Preece, engineer-in-chief of the General Post Office, were marked by conflict; Heaviside's pointed critiques in The Electrician of Preece's inductive loading experiments for telegraph lines—arguing they worsened signal distortion—escalated tensions, leading Preece to block publication of a joint paper by Heaviside and Arthur in 1887 and contributing to Heaviside's marginalization in official British telegraphy circles.[16]Heaviside's engagement with scientific societies remained minimal, reflecting his preference for solitary work over communal activities. Elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1891 for his advancements in electromagnetic mathematics, he seldom attended meetings or participated in society affairs during his tenure.[18] Later Years In the early 1900s, Oliver Heaviside's health deteriorated significantly, exacerbating his partial deafness from childhood scarlet fever and leading to bouts of what he called his "hot and cold disease," likely rheumatism or gout, which confined him increasingly to his home.[12] By 1908, he relocated from Newton Abbot to Torquay in Devon, where he lived reclusively at Homefield, supported only by meager civil list pension of £120 annually (increased to £220 in 1914), amid ongoing financial precarity that forced a simple lifestyle without regular employment.[12][13][19]Heaviside's isolation deepened after 1916, when his longtime housekeeper Mary Way departed, leaving him to manage alone until entering a nursing home; he became known for his prickly demeanor and avoidance of social contact, though he maintained personal routines like pipe-smoking and vegetarianism, along with a fondness for adopting cats.[12] Philosophically, he advocated for absolute space and time, rooted in Newtonian concepts and the electromagnetic ether, and expressed skepticism toward Einstein's theory of relativity, viewing it as incompatible with his understanding of wave propagation in an absolute medium. He also critiqued modern science for its academic formalism, preferring intuitive, practical approaches, and rejected traditional religious notions of immortality in favor of a legacy enduring like the "stars" of great minds such as Shakespeare or Newton.[12]Heaviside died on February 3, 1925, at age 74 in a Torquay nursing home following a fall, after which a modest funeral was held, and he was buried in an unmarked plot beside his parents in Paignton Cemetery; his name and "F.R.S." were added to the gravestone years later by admirers.[12][13] Posthumously, efforts to honor him included the 2014 Heaviside Memorial Project, initiated by Newcastle University academics, which raised funds through public subscription to restore his family grave in Paignton, revealing and preserving the weathered monument.[20] In 2022, English Heritage unveiled a blue plaque at his childhood home in Camden, London, recognizing his telecommunications theories.[13] Mathematical Contributions Operational Calculus In the 1880s and 1890s, Oliver Heaviside developed operational calculus as a practical method for solving differential equations arising in electrical engineering, allowing engineers to treat derivatives as algebraic operations rather than relying on the full apparatus of traditional calculus.[21] This approach was particularly suited to problems in circuit analysis and electromagnetic phenomena, where Heaviside worked as a self-taught consultant after leaving formal employment in 1874.[22] He first systematically outlined the method in his 1892 collection Electrical Papers, with further elaboration in Electromagnetic Theory (Volume II, 1899).[23] Heaviside developed his method independently of Pierre-Simon Laplace's earlier transform (late 18th century), of which he was unaware, providing a practical engineering tool decades before its rigorous reapplication in similar contexts.[1]Central to Heaviside's system is the p-operator, defined as p=dtd, which replaces differentiation with respect to time by multiplication by p, effectively transforming linear differential equations into algebraic equations.[23] For instance, the equation Ldtdi+Ri=E(t) for current i in an inductive-resistive circuit becomes i=Lp+RE(t), where the solution is obtained by manipulating the operator expression as if it were a rational function.[21] To find the time-domain solution, Heaviside applied the expansion theorem, which decomposes the operator into partial fractions corresponding to the roots of the characteristic equation, yielding a series of exponential terms.[23]The Heaviside expansion formula provides the inverse operation for these rational functions of p, stating that if f(p)=∑p+αkAk, then the solution is f(t)=∑Ake−αkt for t > 0, assuming the system starts from rest.[21] This method naturally accommodates discontinuous forcing functions, such as the unit step functionH(t), which Heaviside introduced to model sudden impulses like voltage switches in circuits; the operator handles such jumps by implicitly assuming zero initial conditions before t = 0.[23]Compared to contemporary methods, Heaviside's operational calculus offered engineers a streamlined, intuitive tool that avoided the need for explicit integration or series solutions, prioritizing results over proofs.[21] Heaviside's heuristic approach paralleled Laplace's transform by treating it without contour integrals, though later mathematicians like Thomas Bromwich (1916) and John Carson (1926) formalized it using Laplace's integral representation L{f(t)}(s)=∫0∞f(t)e−stdt.[5]Heaviside's work faced criticism from academic mathematicians, such as those at Cambridge, for its lack of foundational rigor and reliance on formal manipulations without convergence proofs, as noted in reviews of his publications.[21] Despite this, the method proved invaluable in engineering practice and was eventually rigorized in the 20th century through operator algebras and transform theory, influencing fields like control systems.[23] Vector Methods and Functions In the 1880s, Oliver Heaviside independently developed a system of vector calculus tailored for physical applications, particularly in electromagnetism, without relying on the quaternion framework promoted by earlier mathematicians like William Rowan Hamilton.[24] His approach emphasized three-dimensional Euclidean space and introduced key operations such as the dot product (denoted as a scalar product) and cross product (as a vector product perpendicular to the operands), along with the gradient (rate of change in a scalar field) and curl (circulation around a point in a vector field). Heaviside's journal publications began in 1882, following Gibbs' private circulation of notes in 1881 (formally published in 1901), though both developed their ideas independently in the early 1880s.[25] Heaviside's innovations streamlined the representation of physical laws, making them more intuitive and less cumbersome than quaternion-based alternatives.[24]Heaviside also pioneered notational conventions that enhanced clarity in vector analysis. He advocated for boldface type to denote vectors, distinguishing them from scalars, a practice he proposed in 1891 to replace inconsistent uses of Greek or Gothic letters in prior works.[26] Additionally, he adopted and popularized the del operator, symbolized by ∇ (nabla), originally introduced by Hamilton for quaternions but repurposed by Heaviside as a vector differential operator for computing gradients, divergences, and curls in Cartesian coordinates.[27] These notations allowed for compact expressions of physical equations, such as ∇ · E for the divergence of the electric field, simplifying derivations in fields like electrostatics.Among Heaviside's contributions to special functions, the Heaviside step function stands out for its role in modeling discontinuous phenomena. Defined as0 & t < 0 \\ 1 & t \geq 0 \end{cases} $$ this function captures abrupt transitions, such as sudden voltage changes in electrical circuits or signal onsets in transmission lines, proving invaluable in signal processing and control theory.[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2017.0459) Heaviside introduced it within his broader mathematical toolkit to handle impulsive forces and step-like inputs, where its derivative represents a unit impulse function for instantaneous changes.[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2017.0459) Heaviside further advanced functions in potential theory and spherical harmonics, applying vector methods to decompose potentials into harmonic expansions for solving boundary-value problems in electrostatics. His treatments integrated vector operators with spherical harmonics to analyze field distributions around charged spheres or conductors, providing efficient computational tools for electromagnetic potentials without quaternion complications. These vector methods and functions were systematically detailed across Heaviside's three-volume *Electromagnetic Theory* (Volume I, 1893; Volume II, 1899; Volume III, 1912), where they formed the foundation for reformulating physical laws.[](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/electromagnetic-theory/53D326419EBF3D1DC3187B0D823BBE84) His work influenced subsequent developments in tensor analysis, bridging vector calculus to higher-dimensional generalizations used in relativity and continuum mechanics by providing a precedent for coordinate-independent formulations.[](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.09679) ## Electromagnetic Theory ### Reformulation of Maxwell's Equations In the 1860s, James Clerk Maxwell formulated his theory of electromagnetism using approximately 20 scalar equations expressed in component form, primarily relying on potentials to describe electromagnetic phenomena.[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2017.0457) Oliver Heaviside, working independently, dramatically simplified this framework in 1884–1885 by recasting it into four compact vector equations that directly relate the fundamental electromagnetic fields and sources.[](https://physicstoday.scitation.org/doi/10.1063/PT.3.1788) These equations, now known as Maxwell's equations in their modern form, are: \begin{align} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} &= \rho, \ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} &= 0, \ \nabla \times \mathbf{E} &= -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}, \ \nabla \times \mathbf{H} &= \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}. \end{align} Here, $\mathbf{E}$ is the electric field, $\mathbf{H}$ the magnetic field strength, $\mathbf{D} = \epsilon \mathbf{E}$ the electric displacement (with $\epsilon$ the permittivity), $\mathbf{B} = \mu \mathbf{H}$ the magnetic flux density (with $\mu$ the permeability), $\rho$ the charge density, and $\mathbf{J}$ the current density.[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2017.0457) Heaviside achieved this reformulation by introducing vector notation for the fields $\mathbf{E}$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{D}$, and $\mathbf{H}$, drawing on his earlier development of vector methods to eliminate redundancies in Maxwell's original system.[](https://physicstoday.scitation.org/doi/10.1063/PT.3.1788) He discarded Maxwell's reliance on scalar and vector potentials, instead deriving the equations through vector identities that directly connect curls and divergences to physical sources and time derivatives, thereby streamlining the mathematical structure without altering the underlying physics.[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2017.0457) This process emphasized the intrinsic relationships between electric and magnetic fields, making the equations more intuitive for practical computations. A key insight from Heaviside's vector form was the clear prediction of electromagnetic waves propagating through vacuum at a finite speed $c = 1 / \sqrt{\epsilon \mu}$, where $\epsilon$ and $\mu$ are the [permittivity](/page/Permittivity) and permeability of free space, respectively—aligning with the speed of light and underscoring the unified nature of electricity, magnetism, and optics.[](https://physicstoday.scitation.org/doi/10.1063/PT.3.1788) Unlike Maxwell's more theoretical emphasis on the luminiferous ether as a medium for propagation, Heaviside adopted an engineering-oriented perspective, prioritizing applications to transmission lines and practical field behaviors over speculative ether models.[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2017.0457) Heaviside's reformulation first appeared in his serial publication "Electromagnetic Induction and its Propagation" in *The Electrician* from 1885 to 1887, and it quickly gained traction, becoming the standard presentation of Maxwell's equations by the early 1900s due to its elegance and utility in both theoretical and applied electromagnetism.[](https://physicstoday.scitation.org/doi/10.1063/PT.3.1788) This work laid the groundwork for subsequent advances in electrical engineering and radio technology.[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2017.0457) ### Wave Propagation and the Heaviside Layer In the 1870s and 1880s, Oliver Heaviside investigated the propagation of transverse electromagnetic (TEM) waves along wires and cables, demonstrating that signals did not simply diffuse but instead oscillated as waves influenced by the interplay of [inductance](/page/Inductance) and [capacitance](/page/Capacitance).[](https://jontalle.web.engr.illinois.edu/uploads/493/PDFs/HeavisideOliver-Hunt.12.pdf) This work revealed how these parameters caused signal distortion over long distances in submarine and land cables, with higher frequencies attenuating more rapidly due to capacitive effects dominating in low-inductance lines.[](https://jontalle.web.engr.illinois.edu/uploads/493/PDFs/HeavisideOliver-Hunt.12.pdf) Heaviside's analyses, published in journals such as the *Philosophical Magazine* and the *Journal of the Society of Telegraph Engineers*, provided a theoretical foundation for understanding these phenomena through extensions of earlier telegraph theories.[](https://jontalle.web.engr.illinois.edu/uploads/493/PDFs/HeavisideOliver-Hunt.12.pdf) Building on his reformulation of Maxwell's equations, Heaviside applied the wave equation to electromagnetic propagation, expressed as $\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = \mu \epsilon \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}$, where $\mathbf{E}$ is the electric field, $\mu$ is permeability, and $\epsilon$ is permittivity. This equation described wave speed and behavior in various media, with attenuation factors differing significantly between ground-based paths, which suffered higher losses due to earth conductivity, and aerial paths, which allowed lower attenuation for longer-range transmission.[](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/jres/56/jresv56n4p237_A1b.pdf) Heaviside's calculations highlighted how ground reflection and absorption reduced signal strength over curved earth surfaces compared to direct skyward propagation.[](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/jres/56/jresv56n4p237_A1b.pdf) In 1902, Heaviside hypothesized the existence of a reflective conducting stratum in the upper atmosphere around 80 km (50 miles) altitude, as he estimated, to explain the beyond-line-of-sight propagation of radio waves observed in experiments.[](https://ethw.org/Kennelly-Heaviside_Layer) This layer, proposed independently alongside Arthur Kennelly, would enable skywave reflection, bending signals around the Earth's curvature and facilitating long-distance radio communication.[](https://ethw.org/Kennelly-Heaviside_Layer) The prediction accounted for [Guglielmo Marconi](/page/Guglielmo_Marconi)'s puzzling 1901 transatlantic signal reception, suggesting ionized gases in the stratosphere acted as a mirror for electromagnetic waves.[](https://ethw.org/Kennelly-Heaviside_Layer) Later confirmed experimentally and named the Kennelly-Heaviside layer, it marked a key advance in ionospheric theory.[](https://space.engin.umich.edu/outreach/timeline-of-space-physics/) Heaviside's early contributions to radio theory included conceptualizing vertical antennas for efficient ground-to-sky wave launch and considering polarization effects, where vertical polarization minimized ground losses for low-angle radiation.[](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/39172/60412278-MIT.pdf?sequence=2&isAllowed=y) These ideas influenced Marconi's designs for transatlantic transmissions, as Heaviside's models of wave reflection and aerial paths provided the theoretical rationale for using tall masts to exploit skywave modes over direct ground waves.[](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/39172/60412278-MIT.pdf?sequence=2&isAllowed=y) In the third volume of *Electromagnetic Theory* (1912), Heaviside refined his propagation theories, discussing ether models for wave guidance and critiquing emerging relativity concepts as incompatible with absolute ether-based electromagnetism.[](https://archive.org/details/electromagnetict03heavuoft) He emphasized aerial transmission's superiority for global signaling, integrating attenuation analyses to predict minimal losses in vacuum-like upper atmospheres versus terrestrial paths.[](https://archive.org/details/electromagnetict03heavuoft) ## Engineering Innovations ### Telephony and Loading Coils In the 1880s, Oliver Heaviside conducted pioneering analysis of signal distortion in long-distance telephone and telegraph lines, identifying that attenuation and dispersion arose from imbalances in the line's distributed parameters: resistance $R$, inductance $L$, conductance $G$, and capacitance $C$ per unit length.[](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/oliver-heaviside-a-first-rate-oddity) He demonstrated that these effects caused signals to smear over distance, with attenuation approximated by factors involving $\sqrt{(R + j\omega L)(G + j\omega C)}$, where higher-frequency components suffered greater distortion due to the skin effect and capacitive leakage.[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2017.0457) To achieve distortionless transmission, Heaviside emphasized balancing $L/C$ to maintain a constant phase velocity $v = 1/\sqrt{LC}$, ideally approaching the speed of light $c$ in vacuum for optimal performance.[](https://www.ittc.ku.edu/~jstiles/723/handouts/The%20Distortionless%20Line.pdf) Heaviside's key insight, published in 1887, was the condition for distortionless propagation: $R/L = G/C$, which ensures the propagation constant $\gamma = \alpha + j\beta$ has a frequency-independent $\beta = \omega \sqrt{LC}$ and constant attenuation $\alpha = \sqrt{RG}$.[](https://www.ittc.ku.edu/~jstiles/723/handouts/The%20Distortionless%20Line.pdf)[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2017.0457) In typical lines, low natural inductance made $R/L > G/C$, exacerbating [distortion](/page/Distortion); Heaviside proposed compensating by artificially increasing $L$ through periodic insertion of inductors, known as loading coils, spaced along the line to approximate the continuous condition without introducing excessive phase shifts.[](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/oliver-heaviside-a-first-rate-oddity) This approach, first detailed in his 1887 work in *The Electrician*, enabled signals to propagate with minimal [waveform](/page/Waveform) deformation, transforming [telephony](/page/Telephony) from local to transcontinental feasibility.[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2017.0457) The [loading coil](/page/Loading_coil) innovation, conceptualized by Heaviside in 1887 and refined in 1893 suggestions, involved discrete series inductors to boost effective $L$, satisfying the distortionless condition and reducing [attenuation](/page/Attenuation) by approximately 50% on long lines, as the balanced $\alpha = R \sqrt{C/L}$ minimized losses compared to unbalanced cases. For instance, implementation allowed voice signals to travel twice the distance—such as from [Boston](/page/Boston) to [Chicago](/page/Chicago) (about 1,200 miles)—with acceptable clarity, saving [AT&T](/page/AT&T) an estimated $100 million in infrastructure by 1925 through extended line reuse. Adopted widely by [AT&T](/page/AT&T) in the early 1900s following practical tests, these coils were installed at intervals of 1–2 miles, enabling the first transcontinental [telephone](/page/Telephone) calls in 1915. A notable priority dispute arose when Michael Idvorsky Pupin secured a patent for loading coils in 1900 (U.S. Patent 761,995, issued 1904 based on 1899 filing), earning royalties from AT&T despite Heaviside's earlier theoretical priority; Pupin acknowledged Heaviside's influence but claimed independent invention of the discrete implementation, while George A. Campbell at AT&T developed a competing design in 1903, leading to legal settlements favoring Pupin. Heaviside, lacking patents, received no compensation and expressed resentment toward cable companies for ignoring his ideas, though modern accounts credit him as the foundational theorist.[](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/oliver-heaviside-a-first-rate-oddity)[](https://patents.google.com/patent/US761995A/en) Beyond coils, Heaviside's telephony predictions included early analyses of crosstalk, attributing interference between adjacent lines to mutual inductance and capacitance, and recommending twisted-pair configurations to mitigate it through balanced impedance.[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2017.0454) He also advanced impedance matching concepts, coining the term "impedance" in 1886 to describe line characteristics $\sqrt{L/C}$, ensuring minimal reflections at junctions for efficient power transfer in telephone networks.[](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/oliver-heaviside-a-first-rate-oddity) These insights, grounded in his operational calculus for modeling transient responses, laid groundwork for modern wired communications.[](https://www.ittc.ku.edu/~jstiles/723/handouts/The%20Distortionless%20Line.pdf) ### Other Electrical Engineering Advances In the 1880s, Oliver Heaviside developed the concept of the coaxial cable, a self-shielding design optimized for transmitting high-frequency electrical signals with minimal losses and interference. The structure consisted of a central conductor surrounded by a dielectric insulator, enclosed within an outer conducting sheath that prevented external electromagnetic fields from penetrating while confining the signal within the cable. Heaviside patented this invention in Britain in 1880 under Patent No. 1407, recognizing its potential to address signal attenuation issues in early electrical transmission systems.[](https://www.waveform.com/blogs/main/146807175-oliver-heaviside-and-the-coaxial-cable)[](https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/news/oliver-heaviside-self-taught-pioneer-electromagnetism-vector-calculus/) Heaviside also advanced theories on insulation and [dielectrics](/page/Dielectric), particularly their impact on [permittivity](/page/Permittivity) in cables used for [submarine](/page/Submarine) [telegraphy](/page/Telegraphy). His analyses demonstrated how [dielectric](/page/Dielectric) materials, such as [gutta-percha](/page/Gutta-percha), could be optimized to reduce [capacitance](/page/Capacitance) and signal distortion in underwater environments, where moisture and pressure exacerbated electrical losses. These insights, detailed in his [1889](/page/1889) paper "On the Electromagnetic Effects due to the Motion of [Electrification](/page/Electrification) through a [Dielectric](/page/Dielectric)," informed improvements in cable construction, enabling more reliable long-distance [telegraphy](/page/Telegraphy) by balancing inductive and capacitive effects.[](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Motion_of_Electrification_through_a_Dielectric)[](https://hackaday.com/2020/06/19/loading-coils-the-heaviside-condition-and-pupin-coils/) In measurement techniques, Heaviside innovated adaptations of the [Wheatstone bridge](/page/Wheatstone_bridge) for accurately assessing resistance in electrical circuits. In a [1873](/page/1873) publication, he described configurations that maximized sensitivity for resistance measurements, building on the bridge's resistance-balancing principle. These methods provided precursors to more advanced diagnostic tools, laying groundwork for visualizing transient waveforms in later oscillograph designs. His limited [patent](/page/Patent) filings, constrained by chronic poverty, nonetheless influenced industry standards; for instance, the [Bell System](/page/Bell_System) adopted his distortionless transmission principles after a [1897](/page/1897) review, integrating them into cable practices that enhanced network efficiency.[](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/oliver-heaviside-a-first-rate-oddity)[](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/978-3-0348-0177-5_4.pdf)[](https://www.fourmilab.ch/fourmilog/archives/2018-11/001795.html) Heaviside's engineering philosophy integrated rigorous mathematical theory with empirical validation, insisting that theoretical models be tested against practical observations in real-world systems like cables and circuits. This approach, evident in his self-funded experiments and publications, prioritized physical [intuition](/page/Intuition) to ensure innovations were viable beyond abstract analysis.[](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/oliver-heaviside-a-first-rate-oddity) ## Publications and Legacy ### Major Publications Heaviside began his publishing career with a series of articles in the journal *The Electrician*, starting in the early [1870s](/page/1870s) and continuing through the [1890s](/page/1890s), covering topics in vectors, operational methods, and [electromagnetism](/page/Electromagnetism); these writings, numbering numerous pieces, laid the groundwork for his subsequent books.[](https://www.theiet.org/membership/library-and-archives/the-iet-archives/biographies/oliver-heaviside-1850-1925) His contributions to *The Electrician* included over 100 articles by some accounts, often appearing at a rate of 2–3 per month between 1883 and 1887, and they addressed practical electrical problems alongside theoretical developments.[](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/361410548_Heaviside_Equation_A_History) In 1892, Heaviside compiled and expanded many of these articles into *Electrical Papers*, published in two volumes by Macmillan and Company; the work established his authority in electromagnetic theory, [telegraphy](/page/Telegraphy), and [telephony](/page/Telephony), including mathematical treatments of telegraph equations and critical analyses of contemporary electrical practices.[](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/electrical-papers/E8DA42E7CD2341B67DFAF486F4027EE0) The volumes totaled approximately 1,100 pages, integrating his earlier periodical contributions with new material on circuit theory and inductive effects in wires.[](https://archive.org/details/electricalpapers01heavuoft) Heaviside's most comprehensive work, *Electromagnetic Theory*, appeared in three volumes: Volume 1 in 1893, focusing on electrostatics, magnetostatics, and vector methods; Volume 2 in 1899, addressing electromagnetic dynamics and wave propagation; and Volume 3 in 1912, exploring advanced topics such as the electromagnetic ether and further theoretical extensions.[](https://old.maa.org/press/periodicals/convergence/mathematical-treasure-heavisides-electromagnetic-theory)[](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/electromagnetic-theory/669307411FDC25371647BB72611D65E3) Published by "The Electrician" Printing and Publishing Company, the series synthesized his prior articles into a unified treatise, spanning over 1,500 pages in total and incorporating unpublished notes alongside revised essays.[](https://archive.org/details/electromagnetict03heavuoft) Heaviside's prose was notably dense and idiosyncratic, characterized by concise yet unconventional phrasing, neologisms like "impulse" for the [Dirac delta function](/page/Dirac_delta_function), and a preference for intuitive derivations over formal proofs, which often alienated academic readers seeking rigorous justifications.[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2018.0229) This stylistic approach, while innovative for engineers, contributed to limited accessibility among mathematicians, as his total published output—approaching 3,000 pages across articles and books—prioritized practical insight over exhaustive demonstrations.[](https://www.aps.org/archives/publications/apsnews/201002/physicshistory.cfm) ### Recognition and Influence Despite his groundbreaking contributions to electrical engineering and mathematics, Oliver Heaviside experienced significant initial neglect following his death in 1925, largely attributable to his reclusive lifestyle and lack of formal academic affiliations, which limited his visibility in professional circles. Heaviside's prickly personality and self-imposed isolation in [Torquay](/page/Torquay) further contributed to his obscurity, as he avoided conferences and collaborations that might have amplified his reputation during his lifetime.[](https://ethw.org/Oliver_Heaviside) In recognition of his work on conductance, Heaviside coined the term "mho" ([ohm](/page/Ohm) spelled backward) as a unit for electrical conductance in [1885](/page/1885), though it was rarely adopted and later supplanted by the [siemens](/page/Siemens) in the [International System of Units](/page/International_System_of_Units). Modern recognition of Heaviside's legacy has grown substantially in recent decades. In 1970, the [International Astronomical Union](/page/International_Astronomical_Union) named a lunar [crater](/page/Crater) on the Moon's [far side](/page/The_Far_Side) after him, honoring his predictions about electromagnetic wave propagation.[](https://planetarynames.wr.usgs.gov/Feature/2400) Biographies such as Basil Mahon's *The Forgotten Genius of Oliver Heaviside: A Maverick of Electrical Science* (2017) have brought renewed attention to his life and innovations, portraying him as an underappreciated pioneer. To mark the centenary of his death in 2025, events including a commemorative talk at the [University of Cambridge](/page/University_of_Cambridge) on October 28 and an illustrated lecture by the Camden History Society on January 16 have highlighted his enduring impact on [telephony](/page/Telephony) and electromagnetic theory.[](https://talks.cam.ac.uk/talk/index/240058)[](http://www.camdenhistorysociety.org/events/2025/1/16/oliver-heaviside-an-electrical-genius-of-the-victorian-age-an-illustrated-zoom-talk-by-hugh-griffiths) Heaviside's influence permeates modern [electrical engineering](/page/Electrical_engineering) and [physics education](/page/Physics_education). His [operational calculus](/page/Operational_calculus) laid foundational groundwork for the Laplace and Fourier transforms widely used in [signal processing](/page/Signal_processing) and control systems, forming a core component of electrical engineering curricula worldwide. His reformulation of [Maxwell's equations](/page/Maxwell's_equations) using vector notation established the standard framework still taught in physics textbooks, enabling clearer analysis of electromagnetic phenomena.[](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2018.0229) These advancements underpin contemporary [signal processing](/page/Signal_processing) techniques essential for [telecommunications](/page/Telecommunications) and digital systems. Prior encyclopedic coverage often underemphasizes Heaviside's relevance to 21st-century technologies. His mathematical models of wave propagation and the [ionosphere](/page/Ionosphere)—particularly the Kennelly-Heaviside layer—inform modern ionospheric research, including corrections for signal delays in [Global Positioning System](/page/Global_Positioning_System) (GPS) operations, where ionospheric effects can introduce errors of up to several meters in positioning accuracy. Recent digital archives, such as the Engineering and Technology History Wiki's comprehensive entry on Heaviside (updated in the [2020s](/page/2020s)), provide accessible resources for exploring his archival papers and influence on engineering history.[](https://ethw.org/Oliver_Heaviside) Heaviside's persona as a reclusive maverick [scientist](/page/Scientist) has inspired cultural depictions in [literature](/page/Literature) and media, often drawing parallels to figures like [Nikola Tesla](/page/Nikola_Tesla) in narratives about eccentric geniuses challenging established norms.[](https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/news/oliver-heaviside-self-taught-pioneer-electromagnetism-vector-calculus/) Such portrayals, including in biographical works emphasizing his self-taught brilliance, underscore his role as a symbol of independent innovation in stories of scientific outsiders.[](https://www.amazon.com/Forgotten-Genius-Oliver-Heaviside-Electrical/dp/1633883310)


--- [Twenty Three Years: The Acceptance of Maxwell's Theory] ---
On October 21, 1805, flying splinters of wood wreak carnage in the battle of Trafalgar as the final and perhaps greatest sailing ship battle of all time is fought to its gory end. Twenty-seven of Lord Nelson’s British battle ships with 2150 cannon face Napoleon’s fleet of 33 French and Spanish ships carrying 2640 cannon. At the height of battle, Lord Nelson’s flag ship, the HMS Victory, becomes locked, side-by-side with the French ship Redoubtable, their rigging entangled. The British crew gains the upper hand by reducing the gun powder in each charge, allowing the cannon balls to bounce around inside, which wrecks even more carnage against the French ship (see Figure 1). While Lord Nelson himself does not survive the battle, the British navy is victorious due to the extensive damage inflicted by its radical gun powder tactic and a major storm which soon scatters the remaining survivors. Today you can view a statue of Nelson on a hundred foot column in the exact center of London’s Trafalgar Square, signifying the importance of this battle to Britain’s history. Little known is the fact that the radical battle-winning strategy Nelson used was first suggested by John Clerk of Eldin, great-great uncle of James Clerk Maxwell.1 Britain’s victory assures British dominance of the oceans for the remainder of the 19th century. As part of this dominance, Britain will discover and secure a monopoly on the production of “gutta percha,” a natural plastic made from the sap of a tropical tree. Gutta percha will become the perfect, and for many years, the only practical insulator for the undersea cables used by the early communication networks, which will link the far-flung dominions of the Empire. Flash forward 60 years to 1865. The American Civil War is ending. Maxwell publishes “A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field” in the Royal Society Transactions, Vol. CLV (he actually presented the paper orally in December 1864). Comparing several measurements of the speed of light to that calculated by his new electromagnetic theory, he notes, “The agreement of the results seems to show that light and magnetism are affections of the same substance, and that light is an electromagnetic disturbance propagated through the field according to electromagnetic laws.” The directly measured values for the speed of light that Maxwell quotes are 314,858,000 m/s (M. Fizeau), 298,000,000 m/s (M. Foucault) and 308,000,000 m/s (by stellar aberration). Measurements of a capacitor discharge applied to Maxwell’s theory yields 310,740,000 m/s (M.M. Weber and Kohlrausch). Given our modern knowledge of the speed of light, we know which results are presented to appropriate precision and with minimum error, and even with that knowledge, Maxwell’s conclusion is strong. Thus, one of the greatest problems of physics is now solved. Or is it? It is actually too early to break out the champagne, for we must patiently wait 23 years. One problem is that Maxwell offers no mechanical model for the “luminiferous ether,” the medium in which this supposed wave travels. Maxwell and friends all know that light is a transverse wave (assuming that it is actually a wave, not a particle as Newton had insisted). So whatever medium you propose, it must not allow a longitudinal wave. The medium cannot have translational stiffness. The earth plows through this medium without spiraling into the sun, so either it has no mechanical effect on matter or it has no shear strength. But without shear strength, it can not support a transverse wave. And if there is no interaction with matter, how can any wave get started? And so, Maxwell’s theory is just a bunch of equations with no model whatsoever. And what a bunch of equations—there are 20 of them, simultaneous differential equations, rather than the four equations we know today. Maxwell has the concepts of divergence (he uses the opposite sign and calls it convergence) and curl. But vector calculus is not yet formalized, so he must write out all 20 equations. To do so, he sometimes relies on the use of “quaternions.” Quaternions are a combination of a scalar and a vector, which require the use of a squared magnitude of –1, further complicating the equations. Wait, there’s more. Those 20 equations are not easily recognized today. Maxwell places as primary something he calls “electromagnetic momentum” (because its time derivative is force). Electric and magnetic fields are secondary. His friend, Michael Faraday, who originated the field concept as an alternative to the then popular “action at a distance,” called it the “electrotonic state.” It is, Faraday said, changes in the electrotonic state surrounding magnets that cause magnetic induction. Maxwell formalized Faraday’s field concept. The electrotonic state is today called the magnetic vector potential, usually introduced only in graduate level EM courses as a side-effect of a cute little vector identity (primacy of the vector potential is returning to popularity in physics). Maxwell viewed magnetic vector potential as primary (presumably why he gave it the symbol A) and magnetic field as secondary (presumably why he gave it the symbol B). However, by making the vector (and scalar) potentials primary, Maxwell’s equations became complicated. And so, very few took the time to learn them. Finally, recognition of his work would take longer because of Maxwell himself. Unlike Newton, Maxwell was not an aggressive self-promoter. For example, while president of Section A of the British Association, he gave a presidential address (published in Vol. 2 of the new British journal Nature) at the 1870 annual meeting with high praise for a vortex theory of molecules due to his good friend William Thomson (later Lord Kelvin). Rather than wave the flag to the scientific world about his electromagnetic theory, he only mentioned briefly at the end, “Another theory of electricity which I prefer…,” not even taking credit for his own work.2 Maxwellian: FitzGerald Maxwell died in 1879. There was no one, no students or colleagues, to carry on his work in electromagnetics. Well, almost no one. Two days after Maxwell’s death, the Royal Society mailed a paper review that had been written months earlier by Maxwell back to George Francis FitzGerald (see Figure 2). FitzGerald was a fellow and soon to be professor of Trinity College Dublin.3 The future of electromagnetics would now lie in the hands of FitzGerald. FitzGerald was a brilliant idea man, although self-described as “lazy” when it came to follow up experimental work. Typically, he would do the initial work and then rely on others (usually friends or students) to continue the effort. In the paper that Maxwell reviewed, FitzGerald had linked Maxwell’s electrodynamic theory to an earlier theory of Prof. James MacCullagh also of Trinity College. This theory modeled the luminiferous ether required by Fresnel’s wave theory of light and required a purely rotational elasticity, i.e., no translational stress was allowed to form. MacCullagh had shown that given this form for the ether, one could model refraction, reflection and polarization perfectly. Quite the coincidence. However, there were a couple of problems. First, MacCullagh did not suggest a physical form for this mysterious ether (it is certainly unknown in normal matter). Second, in 1862, G.G. Stokes, in reviewing a number of proposed ethereal models, pointed out that MacCullagh’s ether violates conservation of angular momentum. A nice idea while it lasted. Maxwell published his treatise on electromagnetics (the founding document of our field) in 1873. However, in this, and in all of Maxwell’s EM theory publications, there is no electromagnetic treatment of reflection or refraction. FitzGerald is one of the very few people who read and learned the treatise in detail. FitzGerald also extensively studied MacCullagh’s ether model while preparing for his fellowship exam. His well annotated copy of Maxwell’s treatise includes a long note dated 7 September 1878 where he first mentions that it might be possible to connect Maxwell’s theory to MacCullagh’s. Over-simplifying just a bit, FitzGerald found a mapping of variables from Maxwell’s theory to MacCullagh’s. In his paper he describes the mapping and points out that MacCullagh’s work now brings reflection and refraction to Maxwell’s theory. However, Stokes’s objections still hold; MacCullagh’s ether does not conserve angular momentum. If MacCullagh’s ether is the same as Maxwell’s, then Maxwell cannot be correct either. FitzGerald, a life long believer in some kind of ether, optimistically announces that perhaps we should “emancipate our minds from the thralldom of a material ether.” Maxwellian: Lodge Oliver Lodge (see Figure 3) was the son of a pottery clay merchant. His path takes a higher road when he wins a scholarship and completes a University of London external degree. He completes a doctorate at University College in London and later becomes the first professor of physics at the new University College in Liverpool in 1881. Lodge had acquired a copy of Maxwell’s treatise in 1873, right after it was published. He started studying it in 1876. He first met FitzGerald at the August 1878 Dublin meeting of the British Association. Sharing a strong interest in Maxwell’s treatise, they quickly became fast friends. A great unsolved mystery is why Maxwell never tried to produce electromagnetic waves. Historians suggest that Maxwell himself simply missed the implication of his theory. The possibility that changing currents could make light and that radiation could happen is not obvious from Maxwell’s equations, especially in the form Maxwell used. Maxwell appears to have believed that light was made by mechanically vibrating molecules somehow coupling to and vibrating the ether. And EM waves of long wavelength? At that time, such “dark light” was outside human experience. Lodge was more comfortable working with mechanical models, rather than directly with equations. In 1879, one of his models suggested that it might be possible to create light electromagnetically. But how? Lodge proposed applying a voltage source through a switch that switches (“breaks”) 400 times a second. The square wave is applied to a coil that somehow doubles the frequency. A cascade of 40 such coils should yield light. FitzGerald gently points out that the square wave would be smoothed into a sine wave after only several coils and thus would not work. Lodge, not one to give up easily, suggests that a discharging condenser (i.e., capacitor, in the form of a Leyden jar) is oscillatory. FitzGerald’s response was not preserved, but he likely told Lodge that the frequency would be too low for light. So close, yet they missed the idea that the experiment would generate long wavelengths. If only they could find a way to “see” such wavelengths. FitzGerald then pursued the problem mathematically, using Maxwell’s treatise and making several errors along the way. First he reads Maxwell too literally, as though the treatise is an electromagnetic bible. For example, Maxwell states repeatedly that his theory gives results equivalent to the old “action at a distance” concept. In this case, there can be no radiation. However, it appears that Maxwell’s comment was limited to the non-time varying situation, something FitzGerald did not realize. A second very serious error concerns what is now called the gauge condition. Maxwell selected what we call the Colomb gauge (divergence of A is zero) and then incorrectly specified the scalar potential to be independent of time, yielding a static solution. Eventually FitzGerald realizes the problem, in part by toying with mechanical models of the ether that he had built. Then likely inspired by Lord Rayleigh’s Theory of Sound, and by electromagnetic research published by Lorenz, he introduces retarded potentials to Maxwell’s theory. FitzGerald, with considerable effort, does find a solution to Maxwell’s equations for a time varying current, but it is a non-radiating solution, “like the nodes and loops in an organ pipe.” He thus concludes that generating EM waves electrically is impossible. What FitzGerald did not realize at the time, is that he had unwittingly found the solution assuming a conducting wall boundary condition. This illustrates a problem of the vector potential; boundary conditions are difficult to see. Years later FitzGerald realizes that an alternative solution (assuming a different boundary condition) takes the form of traveling waves. Regardless, the damage is done. FitzGerald and Lodge continue enthusiastically working with Maxwell’s theory, but the search for EM wave generation is terminated... until 1888. Maxwellian: Heaviside Described by a friend4 as a “first rate oddity,” Oliver Heaviside (see Figure 4) was also an exceptionally prolific writer, albeit difficult to read, and an absolute mathematical genius. He was raised in poverty in Camden Town, London around the corner from where Dickens himself had lived. In addition to the harsh treatment, he was often ill and never attended a university. Beyond a reasonable early education (partially provided by his mother), he was self-taught in science and mathematics. He learned by reading books from the library. It appears he passionately avoided books on theology and metaphysics (unlike Maxwell), but he adored books dealing with Newton and Laplace, to name a few. His started his first and only job in 1867 as a telegraph clerk. His uncle by marriage, Charles Whetstone (of bridge fame), helped him obtain the position. Because of its importance to the Empire, much of British science was focused on problems relating to undersea telegraph cables. The first undersea cable (Dover to Calais) was laid in 1851. By 1885, there are nearly 100,000 miles of cable under the ocean, mostly laid by Britain due to their dominance on the ocean and monopoly in the only viable undersea cable insulator, gutta percha. Heaviside obtained a copy of Maxwell’s treatise in 1873. “I browsed through it and I was astonished! I read the preface and the last chapter, and several bits here and there; I saw that it was great, greater and greatest… I was determined to master the book and set to work.” Heaviside left his job the next year and moved in with his parents to pursue the mathematics of Maxwell, especially with regard to telegraphic (and soon, telephonic) cable problems. Heaviside had no patience with perceived stupidity and one person in particular, William Henry Preece, the Engineer-in-Chief of the British General Post Office (which controlled all British telegraph and telephone lines). In turn, Preece, who considered himself to be an especially intelligent, “practical man,” had no need for theoretical mathematicians. In one case, Heaviside derived the transmission line “telegraphers” equation directly from Maxwell’s theory. William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) had successfully analyzed undersea cables based on the diffusion equation, i.e., just resistance and capacitance, but no inductance. In this case, a pulse effectively diffuses into the cable and Thomson’s model provided reasonable results for most undersea cables but failed miserably for overhead lines. When Heaviside derived the full telegrapher’s equation, he determined that if the ratio of L/R is equal to C/G, distortion (i.e., pulse spreading) could be eliminated. Since G is very small, thanks to gutta percha, decreasing R to the required value would be prohibitively expensive. So, the solution to addressing distortion was simply increase L. However, his work is effectively suppressed over a long period by Preece, who was vehement that the inductance of a transmission line is zero and increasing it would only lead to disaster. As a “practical man,” he could not be convinced otherwise by these silly mathematicians. Later engineers in the United States successfully make, apply and patent the same discovery, to which Heaviside received no credit. In the summer of 1884, Heaviside starts working on energy flow in the electromagnetic field. The derivation is complicated, but the result is simple: S=ExH. Heaviside, being reclusive and not well connected with the rest of the scientific community, is later only a little disappointed to find that Prof. Poynting of the new Mason College of Science in Birmingham had published the same result a few months earlier. As an example of energy flow, take the field around a straight copper wire at DC. ExH points radially into the wire. Energy does not flow along the wire as had been thought. It flows from the field surrounding the wire and dissipates as heat as it enters the wire. This is the big clue. In sharp contrast to action at a distance, where energy is viewed as flowing along the wire with the current like water in a pipe, Maxwell’s equations suggest that energy is in the field and flows from the field into the resistance of the conductor. In fact, the Maxwellian view at this time is that charge and current are not physical. Rather they are changes in the stresses and strains of the ether. The conductor of the wire relieves the stress of the field and dissipates the energy as heat. This view fades with the advent of a new concept a few years later, the electron. As for the modern view, electromagnetic energy is calculated in terms of either the field or the current. It’s hard to say precisely “where” it is. Heaviside went further than Poynting. As Heaviside was working with his energy concept, he came upon a new form for Maxwell’s equations, the “duplex” form, the four equations with which we are familiar today. These differential curl equations involve E, H, D and B. The potentials are gleefully “murdered” according to Heaviside. “I never made any progress until I threw all the potentials overboard,” he later wrote to FitzGerald. With the duplex form, the symmetries in Maxwell’s equations are beautifully seen, but something was missing. Heaviside adds the fictitious magnetic current to complete the symmetry. If Heaviside modified Maxwell’s equations to this degree, why don’t we call them Heaviside’s equations? Heaviside answered this question in the preface to Vol. 1 of his three-volume, lifetime culminating work, Electromagnetic Theory, stating that if we have good reason, “to believe that he [Maxwell] would have admitted the necessity of change when pointed out to him, then I think the resulting modified theory may well be called Maxwell’s.” In 1888, Lodge is requested to give two lectures on lightning protection and he conducts experiments by discharging condensers in the vicinity of models of various structures. The lectures stimulate major controversy with Preece as Lodge’s results contradict standard practice. But all that is minor. In the course of his experiments, Lodge notices arcs being induced in nearby circuits. In one experiment, he sees an arc at the end of two parallel wires. In the dark, he can even see a distinct standing wave glowing in the air around the wires. Lodge has generated and detected electromagnetic waves. The British Association is meeting in Bath in September. In July, Lodge plans to report his astounding results at that meeting, right after he returns from vacation in the Alps. On the train out of Liverpool, he picks up the July issue of Annalen der Physik. He immediately notices an article, “Ueber elektrodynamische Wellen in Luftraume und deren Reflexion” (“On Electromagnetic Waves in Air and Their Reflection”). Maxwellian: Hertz Heinrich Hertz (see Figure 5) studied in Berlin under Hermann von Helmholtz. Helmholtz had incorporated the electromagnetic theories of Maxwell, Weber and Neumann into a single theory with a parameter (k), whose value selected the theory to be used (later it would be realized that Maxwell’s theories were not correctly incorporated fully). Helmholtz had encouraged Hertz to perform experiments to test and differentiate the theories. Hertz at first declined, having determined that the experiments would be difficult to perform. However, a few years later, while teaching at Technische Hochschule in Karlsruhe, he noticed while discharging a condenser through a loop, that an identical loop some distance away developed arcs. He instantly recognized a resonance condition and suspected electromagnetic waves. The experiments he subsequently conducted would verify reflection, refraction, diffraction and polarization for both free space waves and wire guided waves. It is Hertz’s experimental results that Lodge is reading in Annalen der Physik as he leaves for vacation. As Lodge read the paper, he realized that his own results were now superfluous. However, his disappointment is more than compensated by the beauty and completeness of Hertz’s work. Hertz presents at the September 1888 British Association meeting in Bath and is hailed a hero. His results provide full confirmation of Maxwell’s electromagnetic theory. The British Maxwellians, after 15 years of careful theoretical preparation of Maxwell’s theory, are catapulted to the top of British science thanks to Hertz’s timely experimental validation. Hertz is warmly welcomed into the small Maxwellian group and takes an active role, but, unfortunately, for far to short a period of time. In Germany, the importance of Hertz’s results is not at first fully recognized. Some will say (only partly in jest), that word of Hertz’s experiments reached Germany by way of England. However, once recognized, German researchers likewise embrace Maxwell’s theory as well. Hertz had, independently of Heaviside, discarded Maxwell’s potentials and developed the modern duplex form of Maxwell’s equations. When Hertz becomes aware of Heaviside’s work, he graciously yields priority to Heaviside and likewise chooses to call them Maxwell’s equations. As a tribute to Hertz, they are for a few years also sometimes called the Hertz-Maxwell equations. Epilog Hertz returned to England for one final visit to receive the prestigious Rumford Medal from the Royal Society in 1890. The year before, Hertz had received a major promotion and moved to the chair of physics at the University of Bonn. Unfortunately, the move also deprived him of experimental facilities. In close collaboration with the British Maxwellians, Hertz continued to make significant theoretical contributions, although work becomes difficult as he starts suffering from an infection that spreads to his jaw and sinuses. He writes to his parents in August 1892, “At present my nose is my universe.” He dies tragically and painfully at the age of 36 from blood poisoning after an operation. Michelson and Morley performed their interferometer experiment in 1887, which casts doubt on the existence of an ether. British researchers continue searching for mechanical models of the ether well into the 20th century before the effort is gradually dropped as pointless. FitzGerald passes away after a particularly difficult bout of indigestion at the age of 49. To Heaviside and Lodge his death is a great shock. Lodge does not contribute any further work to Maxwell’s theory after 1900, shifting his efforts toward researching communication with the dead. He died on August 22, 1940, promising to make public appearances after his death. No such appearances have been recorded. In 1896, Heaviside’s father dies, leaving him on his own for the first time in his life. FitzGerald and John Perry arrange a Civil List pension for him. In a more difficult task, they convince him to accept it, offering it as recognition of service to his country. Heaviside became senile in his old age (“I am as stupid as an owl”). He dies on February 3, 1925 after falling off a ladder and landing on his back. He is 74. His ride to the hospital is his first and final ride in an automobile. I close with a paragraph written January 30, 1891 in Heaviside’s Electromagnetic Theory: “Lastly, from millions of vibrations per second, proceed to billions, and we come to light (and heat) radiation, which are, in Maxwell’s theory, identified with electromagnetic disturbances. The great gap between Hertzian waves and waves of light has not yet been bridged, but I do not doubt that it will be done by the discovery of improved methods of generating and observing very short waves.” We truly do stand on the shoulders of giants. A small legacy enabled the family to move to a better part of Camden when he was thirteen and he was sent to Camden House Grammar School. He was a good student, placing fifth out of five hundred students in 1865, but his parents could not keep him at school after he was 16, so he continued studying for a year by himself and had no further formal education. Heaviside's uncle by marriage was Sir Charles Wheatstone (1802–1875), an internationally celebrated expert in telegraphy and electromagnetism, and the original co-inventor of the first commercially successful telegraph in the mid-1830s. Wheatstone took a strong interest in his nephew's education and in 1867 sent him north to work with his own, older brother Arthur, who was managing one of Wheatstone's telegraph companies in Newcastle-upon-Tyne. Two years later he took a job as a telegraph operator with the Danish Great Northern Telegraph Company laying a cable from Newcastle to Denmark using British contractors. He soon became an electrician. Heaviside continued to study while working, and by the age of 22 he published an article in the prestigious Philosophical Magazine on 'The Best Arrangement of Wheatstone's Bridge for measuring a Given Resistance with a Given Galvanometer and Battery' which received positive comments from physicists who had unsuccessfully tried to solve this algebraic problem, including Sir William Thomson, to whom he gave a copy of the paper, and James Clerk Maxwell. When he published an article on the duplex method of using a telegraph cable, he poked fun at R. S. Culley, the engineer in chief of the Post Office telegraph system, who had been dismissing duplex as impractical. Later in 1873 his application to join the Society of Telegraph Engineers was turned down with the comment that "they didn't want telegraph clerks". This riled Heaviside, who asked Thomson to sponsor him, and along with support of the society's president he was admitted "despite the P.O. snobs". In 1873 Heaviside had encountered Maxwell's newly published, and later famous, two-volume Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism. In his old age Heaviside recalled: I remember my first look at the great treatise of Maxwell's when I was a young man… I saw that it was great, greater and greatest, with prodigious possibilities in its power… I was determined to master the book and set to work. I was very ignorant. I had no knowledge of mathematical analysis (having learned only school algebra and trigonometry which I had largely forgotten) and thus my work was laid out for me. It took me several years before I could understand as much as I possibly could. Then I set Maxwell aside and followed my own course. And I progressed much more quickly… It will be understood that I preach the gospel according to my interpretation of Maxwell. Undertaking research from home, he helped develop transmission line theory (also known as the "telegrapher's equations"). Heaviside showed mathematically that uniformly distributed inductance in a telegraph line would diminish both attenuation and distortion, and that, if the inductance were great enough and the insulation resistance not too high, the circuit would be distortionless while currents of all frequencies would have equal speeds of propagation. Heaviside's equations helped further the implementation of the telegraph. Middle years From 1882 to 1902, except for three years, he contributed regular articles to the trade paper The Electrician, which wished to improve its standing, for which he was paid £40 per year. This was hardly enough to live on, but his demands were very small and he was doing what he most wanted to. Between 1883 and 1887 these averaged 2–3 articles per month and these articles later formed the bulk of his Electromagnetic Theory and Electrical Papers. In 1880, Heaviside researched the skin effect in telegraph transmission lines. That same year he patented, in England, the coaxial cable. In 1884 he recast Maxwell's mathematical analysis from its original cumbersome form (they had already been recast as quaternions) to its modern vector terminology, thereby reducing twelve of the original twenty equations in twenty unknowns down to the four differential equations in two unknowns we now know as Maxwell's equations. The four re-formulated Maxwell's equations describe the nature of electric charges (both static and moving), magnetic fields, and the relationship between the two, namely electromagnetic fields. Between 1880 and 1887, Heaviside developed the operational calculus using p for the differential operator, (which Boole had previously denoted by D), giving a method of solving differential equations by direct solution as algebraic equations. This later caused a great deal of controversy, owing to its lack of rigour. He famously said, "Mathematics is an experimental science, and definitions do not come first, but later on." On another occasion he asked somewhat more defensively, "Shall I refuse my dinner because I do not fully understand the process of digestion?" In 1887, Heaviside worked with his brother Arthur on a paper entitled "The Bridge System of Telephony". However the paper was blocked by Arthur's superior, William Henry Preece of the Post Office, because part of the proposal was that loading coils (inductors) should be added to telephone and telegraph lines to increase their self-induction and correct the distortion which they suffered. Preece had recently declared self-inductance to be the great enemy of clear transmission. Heaviside was also convinced that Preece was behind the sacking of the editor of The Electrician which brought his long-running series of articles to a halt (until 1891). There was a long history of animosity between Preece and Heaviside. Heaviside considered Preece to be mathematically incompetent; an assessment supported by the biographer Paul J. Nahin: "Preece was a powerful government official, enormously ambitious, and in some remarkable ways, an utter blockhead." Preece's motivations in suppressing Heaviside's work were more to do with protecting Preece's own reputation and avoiding having to admit error than any perceived faults in Heaviside's work. The importance of Heaviside's work remained undiscovered for some time after publication in The Electrician, and so its rights lay in the public domain. In 1897, AT&T employed one of its own scientists, George A. Campbell, and an external investigator Michael I. Pupin to find some respect in which Heaviside's work was incomplete or incorrect. Campbell and Pupin extended Heaviside's work, and AT&T filed for patents covering not only their research, but also the technical method of constructing the coils previously invented by Heaviside. AT&T later offered Heaviside money in exchange for his rights; it is possible that the Bell engineers' respect for Heaviside influenced this offer. However, Heaviside refused the offer, declining to accept any money unless the company were to give him full recognition. Heaviside was chronically poor, making his refusal of the offer even more striking. But this setback had the effect of turning Heaviside's attention towards electromagnetic radiation, and in two papers of 1888 and 1889, he calculated the deformations of electric and magnetic fields surrounding a moving charge, as well as the effects of it entering a denser medium. This included a prediction of what is now known as Cherenkov radiation, and inspired his friend George FitzGerald to suggest what now is known as the Lorentz–FitzGerald contraction. In 1889, Heaviside first published a correct derivation of the magnetic force on a moving charged particle, which is now called the Lorentz force. In the late 1880s and early 1890s, Heaviside worked on the concept of electromagnetic mass. Heaviside treated this as material mass, capable of producing the same effects. Wilhelm Wien later verified Heaviside's expression (for low velocities). In 1891 the British Royal Society recognized Heaviside's contributions to the mathematical description of electromagnetic phenomena by naming him a Fellow of the Royal Society, and the following year devoting more than fifty pages of the Philosophical Transactions of the Society to his vector methods and electromagnetic theory. In 1905 Heaviside was given an honorary doctorate by the University of Göttingen. Later years and views In 1896, FitzGerald and John Perry obtained a civil list pension of £120 per year for Heaviside, who was now living in Devon, and persuaded him to accept it, after he had rejected other charitable offers from the Royal Society. In 1902, Heaviside proposed the existence of what is now known as the Kennelly–Heaviside layer of the ionosphere. Heaviside's proposal included means by which radio signals are transmitted around the Earth's curvature. The existence of the ionosphere was confirmed in 1923. The predictions by Heaviside, combined with Planck's radiation theory, probably discouraged further attempts to detect radio waves from the Sun and other astronomical objects. For whatever reason, there seem to have been no attempts for 30 years, until Jansky's development of radio astronomy in 1932. In later years his behavior became quite eccentric. According to associate B. A. Behrend, he became a recluse who was so averse to meeting people that he delivered the manuscripts of his Electrician papers to a grocery store, where the editors picked them up. Though he had been an active cyclist in his youth, his health seriously declined in his sixth decade. During this time Heaviside would sign letters with the initials "W.O.R.M." after his name. Heaviside also reportedly started painting his fingernails pink and had granite blocks moved into his house for furniture. In 1922, he became the first recipient of the Faraday Medal, which was established that year. On Heaviside's religious views, he was a Unitarian, but not a religious one. He was even said to have made fun of people who put their faith in a supreme being. Heaviside died on 3 February 1925, at Torquay in Devon, and is buried near the eastern corner of Paignton cemetery. He is buried with his father, Thomas Heaviside (1813–1896) and his mother, Rachel Elizabeth Heaviside. The gravestone was cleaned thanks to an anonymous donor sometime in 2005. Most of his recognition was gained posthumously. Heaviside Memorial Project In July 2014, academics at Newcastle University, UK and the Newcastle Electromagnetics Interest Group founded the Heaviside Memorial Project in a bid to fully restore the monument through public subscription. The restored memorial was ceremonially unveiled on 30 August 2014 by Alan Heather, a distant relative of Heaviside. The unveiling was attended by the Mayor of Torbay, the MP for Torbay, an ex-curator of the Science Museum (representing the Institution of Engineering and Technology), the Chairman of the Torbay Civic Society, and delegates from Newcastle University. The Heaviside Collection 1872–1923 A collection of Heaviside's notebooks, papers, correspondence, notes and annotated pamphlets on telegraphy is held at the Institution of Engineering and Technology (IET) Archive Centre. Innovations and discoveries Heaviside did much to develop and advocate vector methods and the vector calculus. Maxwell's formulation of electromagnetism consisted of 20 equations in 20 variables. Heaviside employed the curl and divergence operators of the vector calculus to reformulate 12 of these 20 equations into four equations in four variables (B, E, J, and ρ), the form by which they have been known ever since (see Maxwell's equations). Less well known is that Heaviside's equations and Maxwell's are not exactly the same, and in fact it is easier to modify the latter to make them compatible with quantum physics. The possibility of gravitational waves was also discussed by Heaviside using the analogy between the inverse-square law in gravitation and electricity He invented the Heaviside step function and employed it to model the current in an electric circuit. He was the first to use the unit impulse function now usually known as the Dirac delta function. He invented his Operational calculus method for solving linear differential equations. This resembles the currently used Laplace transform method based on the "Bromwich integral" named after Bromwich who devised a rigorous mathematical justification for Heaviside's operator method using contour integration. Heaviside was familiar with the Laplace transform method but considered his own method more direct. Heaviside developed the transmission line theory (also known as the "telegrapher's equations"), which had the effect of increasing the transmission rate over transatlantic cables by a factor of ten. It originally took ten minutes to transmit each character, and this immediately improved to one character per minute. Closely related to this was his discovery that telephone transmission could be greatly improved by placing electrical inductance in series with the cable. Heaviside also independently discovered the Poynting vector. Heaviside advanced the idea that the Earth's uppermost atmosphere contained an ionized layer known as the ionosphere; in this regard, he predicted the existence of what later was dubbed the Kennelly–Heaviside layer. In 1947 Edward Victor Appleton received the Nobel Prize in Physics for proving that this layer really existed.
