--- [Elmina Castle - Wikipedia] ---
Elmina Castle was erected by the Portuguese in 1482 as Castelo de São Jorge da Mina (St. George of the Mine Castle), also known as Castelo da Mina or simply Mina (or Feitoria da Mina), in present-day Elmina, Ghana, formerly the Gold Coast. It holds several profound distinctions: it was the first trading post built on the Gulf of Guinea and is the oldest extant European building in Sub-Saharan Africa. 
First established as a trade settlement for indigenous people of the region, the castle later became one of the most important stops of the Atlantic slave trade. The Dutch seized the fort from the Portuguese in 1637, after an unsuccessful attempt in 1596; they  took over all of the Portuguese Gold Coast in 1642. The slave trade continued under the Dutch until 1814. 
In 1872, the Dutch Gold Coast, including the fort, became a possession of the United Kingdom in its treaty with the Dutch that divided dominance and forts in colonial territories.
The Gold Coast gained its independence as Ghana from the United Kingdom in 1957. It controls the castle.
Elmina Castle is a historical site and destination. The castle is recognized by UNESCO as a World Heritage Site, along with other castles and forts in Ghana, because of its place in the Atlantic slave trade. It is a major tourist attraction in Ghana. German director Werner Herzog used it as a major filming location for his drama Cobra Verde (1987).

Elmina Castle, known in Portuguese as Castelo de São Jorge da Mina and also referred to as St. George's Castle, is a fortress erected by Portuguese traders in 1482 on the coast of what is now Elmina, Ghana, marking it as the oldest surviving European-built structure in sub-Saharan Africa.[1] Constructed to safeguard Portugal's monopoly on the lucrative gold trade with inland African kingdoms and to serve as a secure base for European merchants amid local rivalries, the castle featured robust stone fortifications, cannon emplacements, and living quarters for up to 500 personnel.[2] By the early 16th century, its function shifted as the demand for labor in New World plantations grew, transforming it into a primary depot for the transatlantic slave trade where captured Africans from the interior were imprisoned in subterranean dungeons before being marched through the "Door of No Return" to waiting ships bound for the Americas.[1][2]The castle changed hands multiple times, reflecting the intense European competition for West African commerce: the Dutch West India Company seized it in 1637 after a prolonged siege, expanding its slave-trading operations until ceding control to the British in 1872 following the abolition of the slave trade in their empire decades earlier.[2] As part of Ghana's Forts and Castles, inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 1979, Elmina stands as a tangible testament to over four centuries of colonial economic extraction, military fortification, and the commodification of human beings, with its architecture—blending Renaissance defensive design and tropical adaptations—preserved amid ongoing restoration efforts to counter decay from humidity and neglect.[1] Its historical significance lies not only in pioneering sustained European presence in tropical Africa but also in embodying the causal chain of resource-driven imperialism that fueled global mercantilism, though interpretations vary on the relative agency of local African intermediaries in supplying captives versus European demand as the primary driver.[2] Location and Physical Setting Geographical Context Elmina Castle is positioned on the Atlantic coastline of Ghana in the Central Region, at coordinates 5° 4' 55.7" N, 1° 20' 52.5" W, overlooking the Gulf of Guinea.[3] This site lies approximately 150 kilometers west of Accra, the national capital, within a coastal plain characterized by sandy beaches and lagoons.[4] The castle occupies a narrow rocky peninsula jutting into the ocean at the mouth of the Benya River, which creates a sheltered lagoon to the east, offering natural protection from Atlantic swells and facilitating secure anchoring for trading vessels.[5][6] This topographic feature, combining elevated stone outcrops with adjacent riverine access, enabled efficient maritime commerce while providing defensive advantages against both environmental hazards and potential assailants. The surrounding environment includes tropical coastal vegetation and low-lying mangroves along the river, transitioning inland to humid savanna woodlands.[7] The region's tropical climate features high temperatures averaging 25–30°C year-round, with two rainy seasons peaking in May–June and October–November, supporting the area's historical role in resource extraction and trade networks extending into the hinterlands.[7] Site Layout and Environment Elmina Castle is positioned on a rocky promontory projecting into the Gulf of Guinea, offering a commanding view of the Atlantic Ocean and natural defense against naval approaches. The surrounding coastal environment consists of discontinuous sandy and rocky beaches, with a tropical climate marked by high humidity, heavy rainfall, and persistent wave action that contributes to ongoing shoreline erosion. This location, adjacent to Elmina's fishing harbor, facilitated direct access for trade vessels while exposing the structure to corrosive marine conditions.[8][1]The fortress follows a basic rectangular layout typical of 15th-century European militaryarchitecture, forming a large enclosure with high whitewashed walls built from local sandstone using mud and lime mortar. Four corner bastions equipped for cannon placement provided defensive coverage, while an inner courtyard was surrounded by multi-story buildings including the governor's quarters, administrative offices, a chapel (later repurposed as an auction room), and warehouses. Subterranean dungeons, accessed via steep stairs, featured cramped, dimly lit cells with minimal ventilation and drainage, intended to confine enslaved individuals under harsh conditions prior to embarkation. A seaward-facing portal known as the Door of No Return allowed direct passage from these holding areas to ships docked at the castle's base.[1][9][10]Landward defenses were supplemented by Fort Saint Jago, situated on an adjacent hill to guard against inland assaults, underscoring the site's dual maritime and terrestrial vulnerabilities. Over centuries of occupation by successive European powers, additions and modifications—such as reinforced walls and expanded storage—altered the original Portuguese design without fundamentally changing its core fortified enclosure.[10][1] Pre-Portuguese Era Indigenous Settlements and Trade Networks The region of modern Elmina fell under the jurisdiction of the Eguafo kingdom, an Akan polity that exerted influence over coastal territories by the thirteenth century. Elmina, locally termed Edina or Amankwaakrom after its purported founding ancestor Kwaa Amankwaa, comprised two small villages situated on opposite banks of the Benya River, supporting primarily fishing and subsistence activities. Archaeological surveys at Elmina and nearby Eguafo sites have uncovered limited pre-fifteenth-century artifacts, such as pottery and iron tools, pointing to modest, decentralized settlements rather than fortified urban complexes.[11][12]Pre-Portuguese trade networks along the Gold Coast integrated Elmina as a coastal node linking inland resource extraction to maritime exchange. Gold, primarily alluvial deposits panned from rivers in Akan interior regions like Denkyira and Asante territories, was conveyed southward by porters along footpaths to coastal aggregators, who bartered it for fish, salt, and cowries obtained via regional canoe traffic. The Benya Lagoon's natural shelter enhanced Elmina's utility for inter-coastal voyages, connecting it to ports such as Shama and Axim for commodities like ivory, kola nuts, and cloth from northern overland routes indirectly tied to Sahelian caravans. These exchanges, regulated by local chiefs under Eguafo oversight, emphasized reciprocity and kinship alliances rather than coercion, yielding an estimated 15-20 tons of gold annually across the coast by the late fifteenth century.[13][14]Eguafo governance, characterized by matrilineal royal succession and advisory councils of elders, mediated these networks to secure tribute from inland suppliers while defending against raids from neighboring states like Fetu. This structure positioned Elmina as a peripheral yet vital entrepôt, where gold weighed in ackies (approximately 17 grams each) was stored in antelope-skin pouches for trade. Oral traditions and early Portuguese accounts corroborate the site's commercial vibrancy, with local traders leveraging geographic advantages to sustain economic ties predating European intervention in the 1470s.[15][13] Local Political Structures Prior to the arrival of the Portuguese in 1482, the site of Elmina, known locally as Edina, functioned as a small fishing and trading hamlet within a network of autonomous Akan-speaking chiefdoms along the Gold Coast. These polities, including Edina, Eguafo, Fetu, and Asebu, lacked centralized overlordship and operated as independent entities focused on coastal resource management and inland trade connections for gold, kola nuts, and fish.[15]Governance in these chiefdoms followed matrilineal Akan principles, centered on a paramount chief (omanhene) who embodied spiritual, judicial, and military authority through possession of a sacred stool symbolizing lineage continuity and communal legitimacy. The chief was advised by a council of elders representing kinship lineages and, where formalized, a queen mother who influenced succession and mediated disputes, ensuring consensus-based decision-making over unilateral rule.[16][17]In Edina specifically, authority rested with the stool-holder Caramansa (also recorded as Kwamina Ansa), who led the settlement's approximately 1,000–2,000 inhabitants in the late 15th century and demonstrated sovereign capacity by granting land rights and tribute arrangements to Portuguese traders upon their initial contact.[18][12] This structure emphasized localized control without divisional sub-chiefs, distinguishing it from later militarized forms influenced by European fort-based economies.[19]Inter-chiefdom relations involved alliances for defense and trade rather than hierarchical subordination, with conflicts resolved through diplomacy or raids over resources like coastal lagoons and gold-bearing hinterlands, reflecting a decentralized political landscape vulnerable to external disruptions.[15] Portuguese Foundation and Early Operations (1482–1637) Construction of the Fortress In 1481, King John II of Portugal commissioned the construction of a fortress at the Gold Coast to secure Portuguese dominance in the regional goldtrade, dispatching Diogo de Azambuja to lead the expedition.[2] Azambuja's fleet, consisting of ten caravels and two transport ships, departed Lisbon carrying approximately 500 to 600 soldiers, servants, and skilled workers, including 100 masons and carpenters tasked with the build.[20][2] The expedition arrived at the chosen site—a strategic headland at the mouth of the Benya Lagoon—in January 1482, selected for its natural defensibility protruding into the Gulf of Guinea.[2][21]On January 20, 1482, Azambuja negotiated with the local Fante chief Kwamena Ansa (also known as Caramansa or Kwamin Ansah) for permission to construct the fortress, offering gifts such as brass basins, mirrors, and cloth while promising ongoing tribute and peaceful trade relations.[20][2] The site held sacred significance for locals, leading to initial reluctance and minor resistance, which the Portuguese countered by demonstrating cannon fire and, in one account, burning nearby structures to assert dominance before securing agreement.[21] The foundation stone for São Jorge da Mina (St. George of the Mine) was laid the following day, January 21, 1482, marking the start of rapid assembly using prefabricated materials shipped from Europe, including foundation stones and roof tiles, supplemented by local resources.[20][22]The initial fortress featured high, cannon-resistant walls of dark brown stone, corner towers, and an inner courtyard with a cistern for water storage, designed as a self-contained trading post rather than a full residential castle.[2] Construction progressed swiftly, with the first story of a tower completed in approximately 20 days through coordinated labor of the Portuguese specialists, enabling quick fortification against potential local or rival threats.[22] The core structure, including a church for the Catholic settlers, was finished by 1486, establishing it as the first permanent European-built fortress south of the Sahara and a hub for exporting gold dust acquired via barter with inland Akan traders.[2][21] Gold Trade Dominance and Economic Mechanisms The Portuguese fortress of São Jorge da Mina, established in 1482 under the command of Diogo de Azambuja, was constructed specifically to assert dominance over the Gold Coast's gold trade by creating a fortified coastal enclave that controlled access to inland gold supplies transported by Akan merchants.[2] This strategic positioning monopolized barter exchanges, preventing rival European traders from bypassing Portuguese intermediaries and compelling local Fante and other coastal groups to route gold through the fort via enforced treaties that restricted direct trade with interlopers.[2][23] The crown-appointed captain-major oversaw operations, using the fort's armaments to deter local resistance and enforce exclusivity, as seen in initial negotiations with ruler Kwamena Ansa, who initially opposed construction but later cooperated under pressure.[2]Economic mechanisms relied on a state-regulated feitoria system, where gold dust and nuggets—sourced from hinterland mines via established African networks—were exchanged for imported European and Asian goods including linen cloth, brass manilhas (bracelet currency), glass beads, iron tools, and liquor.[23][2] Transactions occurred in a depot within the castle walls, with gold weighed using standardized balances to ensure consistent valuation, while diplomatic gifts to local elites like cloth and livestock secured ongoing supply flows without territorial conquest.[23] Violations of trade exclusivity by locals were punished through imprisonment or flogging, reinforcing Portuguese leverage despite dependence on African porters and intermediaries for inland procurement.[2]Export volumes underscored this dominance: from 1490 to 1560, approximately 1,000 kilograms of gold were shipped to Portugal, accounting for roughly 10% of the era's global known supply and fueling the kingdom's minting of cruzados for European commerce.[2] Peak annual outflows in the mid-16th century reached 24,000 ounces (about 750 kilograms), reflecting efficient mechanisms that integrated the fort as a secure transshipment hub amid fluctuating local production and competition from overland routes.[21] This system sustained profitability until the late 16th century, when rising slave exports began diverting resources, though gold remained central until the Dutch capture in 1637.[2] Dutch Occupation and Expansion (1637–1872) Military Conquest and Fortifications The Dutch West India Company launched a concerted assault on Elmina Castle in August 1637, deploying a fleet of nine ships carrying approximately 400 sailors, 800 soldiers, and numerous cannons to overwhelm the Portuguese defenders.[24] Positioned on a hill north of the castle, Dutch forces bombarded the structure over several days, exploiting its vulnerability to land-based artillery after the Portuguese had repelled prior naval assaults from European rivals.[25] The siege culminated in the castle's capture between August 25 and 28, marking a pivotal victory that shifted control of the Gold Coast's primary European stronghold from Portugal to the Netherlands and facilitated subsequent Dutch dominance in regional trade.[24]Following the conquest, the Dutch promptly repaired bombardment-induced damage to the castle's walls and structures, while initiating broader defensive enhancements to secure their hold against potential Portuguese counterattacks or inland threats from African polities.[6] To safeguard against recurrence of the landward vulnerability exploited in 1637, they constructed Fort St. Jago (also known as Coenraadsburg or Fort Coenraadsburg) atop the same northern hill in the 1660s, equipping it with heavy guns for overlapping fields of fire with the main castle.[26] This auxiliary bastion, positioned strategically opposite Elmina Castle, formed part of a ring of supporting fortifications, including outlying posts that extended defensive coverage and deterred sieges or raids.[27]Over the subsequent decades, the Dutch methodically expanded the castle's core fortifications, enlarging bastions, reinforcing ramparts with additional stonework, and integrating the site into a fortified complex that prioritized both maritime and terrestrial defense amid intensifying competition with British and other European powers.[25] These modifications, spanning from initial post-1637 rebuilds through major reconstructions by 1774, transformed the original Portuguese design into a more robust Dutch stronghold capable of sustaining prolonged operations in a contested coastal environment.[25] Such engineering adaptations underscored the causal imperative of adapting fortifications to empirical threats, as evidenced by the reduced incidence of successful assaults on Elmina under Dutch administration compared to the Portuguese era.[28] Transition to Atlantic Slave Trade The Dutch West India Company (WIC) captured Elmina Castle from the Portuguese on August 29, 1637, following a siege supported by forces dispatched from Dutch-controlled Brazil, where expanding sugar plantations created acute demand for enslaved labor.[6] Initially, the WIC preserved the site's role as a gold trading hub, securing control over exports from the Gold Coast interior, but the strategic acquisition aligned with broader ambitions to establish a steady transatlantic slave supply chain for plantation colonies.[29] This integration of slave trading reflected causal pressures from European colonial economics: the collapse of indigenous American populations due to disease and overwork necessitated African labor imports, rendering slave procurement more lucrative than gold amid rising competition and finite mineral resources.By 1636–1654, Elmina served as a critical WIC base for exporting enslaved Africans primarily to Brazil, with the trade's onset coinciding with the company's monopoly on Dutch Atlantic commerce. After Portuguese reconquest of Brazil in 1654, shipments redirected to Caribbean entrepôts like Curaçao and later Suriname, sustaining Elmina's function as a holding and embarkation point where coastal African polities supplied captives—often war prisoners or judicially condemned individuals—via established inland networks.[30] The WIC fortified and adapted the castle's infrastructure, including storage vaults and defensive positions, to accommodate slave warehousing, though gold transactions persisted alongside, with slaves sometimes bartered for commodities like guns and textiles.[28]The mid-17th century marked a decisive pivot, as Atlantic slave demand surged post-1650, outpacing gold's volatility; WIC records indicate Dutch vessels embarked over 85,000 enslaved individuals from West Africa before 1674, with Elmina as the administrative headquarters facilitating procurement from the Gold Coast's estimated 1.2 million total exports by all Europeans through the 18th century.[31][28] Monopoly erosion after 1730 intensified private Dutch trading, peaking slave dispatches and prompting further expansions, such as reinforced dungeons, by the late 18th century when slave auctions supplanted earlier chapel uses. This evolution underscored Elmina's transformation into a linchpin of the Dutch segment of the Atlantic system, which accounted for 5–6% of total transatlantic embarkations, driven by empirical profitability over alternative commodities.[30] British Administration (1872–1957) Administrative Changes Following the cession of the Dutch Gold Coast possessions to Britain under the terms of the 6 April 1872 treaty, Elmina Castle transitioned from Dutch to British administrative control as part of the newly consolidated British Gold Coast colony.[32] This handover marked a shift from the castle's prior role as a Dutch trading headquarters to integration within Britain's colonial governance structure, centered initially at Cape Coast before relocation to Accra in 1877.[33]Under British rule, the castle was repurposed primarily as a prison, or gaol, to detain local offenders and political prisoners, reflecting the colony's emphasis on maintaining order amid post-slave trade economic shifts and resistance from inland kingdoms like Ashanti.[2][34] Quarterly reports documented its operations as a penal facility, with cells adapted from former slave holding areas for incarceration purposes.[34] Notably, in 1896, following the Anglo-Ashanti War, British forces imprisoned Ashanti king Prempeh I at the castle for four years prior to his exile to the Seychelles, underscoring its use in suppressing regional opposition.[35]By the mid-20th century, amid decolonization pressures, the British repurposed the castle further in 1948 into a police training school to train colonial law enforcement personnel, a function it retained until Ghana's independence in 1957 transferred full authority to the new sovereign state.[2] This late administrative adaptation aligned with Britain's winding down of direct territorial control, prioritizing institutional continuity over the site's historical trading or punitive roles.[36] Declining Slave Trade and Shift to Other Uses The transatlantic slave trade at Elmina Castle, which had been a central hub under Dutch control with annual exports peaking at approximately 30,000 enslaved individuals, declined sharply in the early 19th century amid mounting international abolitionist pressures. The Kingdom of the Netherlands formally prohibited the slave trade in 1814 as part of post-Napoleonic agreements, effectively curtailing legal exports from the castle, though sporadic illicit activities persisted until broader enforcement in the 1830s and 1840s. By the mid-19th century, the fort's role in human trafficking had diminished to negligible levels, supplanted by localized commerce in gold, ivory, and palm oil, rendering the aging structure economically redundant.British acquisition of Elmina Castle occurred in 1872 through the Anglo-Dutch Gold Coast Cession Treaty, which exchanged Dutch coastal forts for British relinquishment of claims in Sumatra. At this juncture, the castle held no viable commercial function for the new administrators, as the slave trade was long suppressed under British naval patrols and diplomatic initiatives since the 1807 Slave Trade Act. Local resistance to the transfer manifested in the 1873 Elmina riots, prompting a British bombardment that damaged surrounding areas but spared the castle itself, underscoring its transitional status.[37]Under British colonial rule, Elmina Castle was repurposed as a training facility for the Gold Coast Police, accommodating recruits and serving administrative needs until Ghana's independence in 1957. This shift reflected broader colonial strategies to repurpose obsolete fortifications for security and governance, with the castle's dungeons and barracks adapted for lodging and instruction rather than incarceration or trade. Maintenance was minimal, focusing on structural integrity amid coastal erosion, as the site transitioned from a symbol of exploitation to a utilitarian outpost.[2][38] Architectural and Engineering Features Defensive Structures and Materials Elmina Castle's foundational defensive architecture, established by the Portuguese in 1482, consists of a rectangular enclosure fortified with four corner bastions designed to mount artillery for enfilading fire against approaching threats from land and sea.[1] These bastions, along with high perimeter walls, formed the primary barriers, leveraging the site's rocky promontory for natural seaward protection while emphasizing landward defenses via the reinforced western curtain wall.[39] The structure's military design prioritized deterrence of rival European powers and local resistance, with cannon emplacements enabling overlapping fields of fire.[1]Construction materials centered on locally sourced rough-hewed sandstone blocks, bonded with mud-and-lime mortar for durability against tropical weathering and bombardment.[39] The walls were often plastered over for added cohesion and maintenance, while upper-level elements incorporated imported Dutch brick during later modifications.[6] Following the Dutch capture in 1637, fortifications were enhanced with more robust stonework and expanded sea defenses to counter erosion and naval assaults, reflecting iterative adaptations to evolving threats.[40]Armament included heavy cannons positioned on bastion platforms and wall batteries, capable of firing towards the harbor and inland approaches, underscoring the castle's role as a self-sustaining military outpost.[1] These features, combined with gated entrances and internal redoubts, ensured layered defense, though vulnerabilities to siege and betrayal persisted, as evidenced by the 1637 Dutch conquest via internal uprising.[6] Internal Layout and Functional Spaces Elmina Castle's internal layout comprised a multi-story rectangular fortress enclosing a central courtyard, with ground-level spaces dedicated to storage and later confinement, and upper levels reserved for administrative and residential use by European occupants.[10] The Portuguese, who erected the structure in 1482 as São Jorge da Mina, incorporated warehouses for gold and trade goods, soldiers' quarters, and a Catholic chapel positioned above the courtyard to support missionary activities alongside commerce.[37]Following Dutch seizure in 1637, ground-floor storerooms were adapted into dungeons for holding enslaved Africans, including separate areas for males, females, and those deemed rebellious, marked by narrow ventilation slits, damp conditions, and minimal sanitation that contributed to high mortality rates prior to shipment.[2][10] Upper-story governor's quarters offered spacious, well-ventilated rooms with sea views and balconies overlooking the female dungeon courtyard, enabling oversight and selection of captives.[10] The Dutch repurposed the original chapel as a trading marketplace before constructing a new Protestant one, maintaining religious functions amid the shift to slave exports.[2]Key functional transitions reflected economic priorities: initial gold-focused storage gave way to expansive slave-holding capacities, with an estimated 30,000 captives processed annually by the 18th century via the "Door of No Return"—a seaward exit from the female dungeon—facilitating direct loading onto ships.[2][37] Under British rule after 1872, spaces served administrative and penal purposes, including as a prison for local leaders like Asantehene Prempeh I from 1896 to 1900, before repurposing as a police training facility in 1948.[2] This vertical stratification underscored the site's operational hierarchy, prioritizing European comfort and control over captive welfare.[10] Role in Broader Historical Trade Systems Contributions to European Exploration and Commerce Elmina Castle, known initially as São Jorge da Mina, was erected by Portuguese forces in 1482 as the first permanent European fortification south of the Sahara, establishing a secure base for maritime commerce along the West African coast. This outpost enabled Portugal to monopolize the trans-Saharan gold trade previously dominated by Arab intermediaries, allowing direct exchanges of European manufactured goods—such as copper bracelets, brass basins, and textiles—for gold dust and ivory from Akan polities inland. The influx of gold, peaking at thousands of ounces annually in the late 15th century, reinvigorated Portugal's economy and provided fiscal resources critical for sustaining the Age of Discovery's expansive naval expeditions.[2][21][41]As a resupply depot offering fresh water, provisions, and repairs, the castle functioned as a logistical hub for Portuguese caravels probing southward, contributing to the mapping of the African coastline and the eventual circumnavigation of the continent by Bartolomeu Dias in 1488. Its strategic defenses, including artillery emplacements, deterred rival European incursions, preserving Portuguese commercial primacy until the Dutch capture in 1637 and fostering an environment where exploratory voyages could extend to the Indian Ocean under Vasco da Gama in 1497–1499. The gold revenues from Mina directly subsidized shipbuilding and armaments, causal links evident in royal accounts allocating African bullion to crown-sponsored fleets.[25][2]Under Dutch control from 1637, the castle expanded its commercial role, integrating into broader European networks by exporting African commodities to Amsterdam markets, which in turn financed colonial ventures in Asia and the Americas. This shift amplified Elmina's contributions to global commerce, with Dutch records documenting shipments of gold, pepper, and later enslaved labor that underpinned mercantile capitalism's growth, though primary emphasis remained on resource extraction over territorial conquest. The fortress's enduring function as a tradenexus underscored its pivotal, if extractive, role in linking African interior economies to European expansionist ambitions.[21][2] Empirical Scale and Logistics of Slave Exports Elmina Castle functioned as a central hub for the transatlantic slave trade after its initial establishment for gold commerce, with significant exports commencing under Portuguese administration from the late 15th century and intensifying under Dutch control following their 1637 capture of the fort. Empirical records from the Dutch West India Company indicate that slave shipments from Elmina contributed substantially to the Netherlands' overall export of approximately 555,000 enslaved Africans across the Atlantic between 1600 and 1800, though precise per-port breakdowns remain estimates derived from shipping manifests and company ledgers.[42] Peak activity occurred in the 18th century, when historical accounts estimate up to 30,000 captives were processed and exported annually through the castle's facilities.[43][44]Logistically, enslaved individuals were primarily procured through raids and wars inland by African polities allied with or supplying European traders, then marched in coffles to coastal forts like Elmina, often covering hundreds of kilometers under duress. Upon arrival, captives were inspected, branded, and held in subterranean dungeons designed to accommodate 100 to 200 persons per cell, with provisions limited to minimal rations of maize or yam to sustain viability for sale while minimizing costs.[45] High confinement density—sometimes exceeding capacity during peak seasons—fostered rampant disease transmission, including dysentery and smallpox, resulting in mortality rates of 10-20% during holding periods based on surviving trader journals.[46]Export operations relied on a barter system where European imports like gunpowder, iron bars, and rum were exchanged for human cargo, with Dutch factors negotiating prices per head according to age, sex, and health. Selected slaves were funneled through the fortified "Door of No Return"—a narrow gateway leading from the castle to the sea—onto canoes or small boats rowed to anchored slave ships, which typically loaded 200-400 individuals per vessel for the Middle Passage to destinations including Suriname, Brazil, and the Caribbean.[2] The castle's wharf and offshore anchoring mitigated risks of rebellion during transfer, while armaments deterred inter-European competition or local resistance. British oversight after 1872 marked the trade's decline, as abolitionist pressures and patrols curtailed operations by the 1810s.[45] Post-Independence Ghanaian Era (1957–Present) Nationalization and Initial Preservation Following Ghana's attainment of independence from British colonial rule on March 6, 1957, Elmina Castle transitioned from colonial administration to state ownership, with management responsibilities assigned to the newly formed Ghana Museums and Monuments Board (GMMB). The GMMB, established by Ordinance No. 20 on March 5, 1957, through the merger of the National Museum and the Monuments and Relics Commission, assumed oversight of national heritage sites, including the castle, to safeguard them as symbols of historical significance.[47][48] This nationalization aligned with the post-independence government's emphasis on cultural patrimony, repurposing the site from its prior utilitarian roles—such as a police training facility under late British rule—to a preserved monument.[45]Initial preservation initiatives under the GMMB prioritized structural stabilization and public accessibility, recognizing the castle's role in illustrating European-African trade dynamics. By 1957, the site was opened to visitors, facilitating early interpretive tours that highlighted its architectural integrity and historical functions, though systematic conservation remained limited by resource constraints in the nascent republic.[49] These efforts built on pre-independence maintenance but shifted focus toward national identity formation, with the GMMB documenting and protecting key features like the defensive walls and internal chambers to prevent decay from tropical climate exposure.[37]Through the 1960s and 1970s, GMMB-led activities included basic repairs to masonry and roofing, funded modestly by state allocations, while avoiding major alterations to retain authenticity. This period laid groundwork for later international recognition, emphasizing empirical documentation of the site's material condition over interpretive embellishments, amid Ghana's broader nation-building priorities under President Kwame Nkrumah.[50] By the late 1970s, accumulating evidence of deterioration—such as erosion in dungeons and bastions—underscored the need for enhanced funding, setting the stage for UNESCO involvement.[51] UNESCO World Heritage Status (1979) Elmina Castle, formally St. George's Castle, was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1979 as part of the serial property "Forts and Castles, Volta, Greater Accra, Central and Western Regions," which encompasses 32 fortifications along Ghana's coastline spanning approximately 500 kilometers from Keta to Beyin.[1] This listing recognizes the structures' role in pre-colonial Afro-European commerce, built between 1482 and 1786 by powers including Portugal, the Netherlands, Denmark, Sweden, and Britain primarily for gold and later slave trade.[1] The serial site includes three major castles—St. George's at Elmina, Cape Coast Castle, and Christiansborg Castle—along with 15 forts in good condition and additional ruins, highlighting their collective architectural and historical integrity despite varying states of preservation.[1]The inscription was granted under Criterion (vi), which applies to sites "directly or tangibly associated with events or living traditions, with ideas, or with beliefs, with artistic and literary works of outstanding universal significance."[1] UNESCO's justification emphasizes how these forts and castles shaped Ghanaian and global history over four centuries, serving as focal points for the gold trade followed by the transatlantic slave trade, and reflecting processes of cultural exchange and acculturation that facilitated the latter.[1] They stand as physical testimonies to the African Diaspora, marking the departure points for millions enslaved and shipped to the Americas, while also symbolizing early sustained European-African encounters in sub-Saharan Africa.[1]Within this context, Elmina Castle holds particular prominence as the oldest of the inscribed structures, constructed by the Portuguese in 1482 as the first European trading post south of the Sahara and one of the earliest European buildings outside Europe.[1] Its inclusion underscores its foundational role in establishing fortified coastal enclaves that enabled direct maritime trade, bypassing trans-Saharan routes, and later became central to the logistics of slave exports, with features like dungeons and export gates preserved as evidence of operational scale.[1] The 1979 designation imposed obligations on Ghana for protection, maintenance, and presentation, though subsequent reports have noted challenges in implementation amid coastal erosion and tourism pressures.[52] Modern Preservation, Tourism, and Developments Conservation Challenges and Efforts Elmina Castle faces significant conservation challenges primarily from its coastal location, including erosion from the Atlantic Ocean, high humidity, and saltwater corrosion that accelerate structural decay of its stone and masonry components.[53][54] These environmental factors have led to partial collapses and ongoing deterioration, compounded by historical neglect and the site's age since its construction in 1482.[55] Insufficient funding remains a persistent issue, with Ghana's government relying on limited international aid despite the site's UNESCO status, resulting in inadequate maintenance and risk of further instability.[56] High tourism volumes, attracting over 100,000 visitors annually, exacerbate wear on interiors and pathways, while past sand and gravel mining nearby threatened foundational integrity until halted.[50][53]Efforts to address these challenges include the 2022 sea defense project, which constructed a protective wall along the shoreline to mitigate wave erosion and eliminate adjacent mining activities, preserving the castle's seaward facade as a UNESCOWorld Heritage site.[53][54] The Ghana Heritage Conservation Trust allocated 77,000 Ghanaian cedis in 2015 for restoration works at Elmina and nearby castles, focusing on structural reinforcements and exhibit upgrades.[57] The Elmina Cultural Heritage & Management Programme's second phase, supported by international partners, targets preventing collapse through targeted repairs, alongside renovations to adjacent sites like Fort St. Jago and the Dutch cemetery.[58]UNESCO's ongoing monitoring via state of conservation reports has guided these initiatives, emphasizing architectural documentation and preliminary assessments completed by 2023, with restoration phases commencing thereafter.[59] Despite these measures, experts note that sustained funding and local capacity-building are essential to counterbalance tourism pressures and climatic threats.[55][60] Digital Documentation and Recent Projects (2010s–2020s) The Digital Elmina Project, a collaborative initiative between the University of Rochester and the University of Ghana, commenced in 2016 to document and preserve Elmina Castle through advanced digital technologies.[61] This effort launched the Elmina Digital Archaeology Field School in summer 2017, conducting intensive surveys over three field seasons from 2016 to 2018, capturing approximately 68,000 ground-level photographs via photogrammetry, around 1,000 drone-based aerial images, and interior laser scans using a Faro scanner that generated about 35 billion data points covering 98% of the structure.[62][63] These techniques, supplemented by total station measurements and traditional surveying, produced high-resolution 3D models processed with Siemens NX software for structural analysis and finite element modeling to assess preservation needs.[62]Key outputs include web-accessible virtual tours emphasizing photogrammetric reconstructions that simulate realistic walkthroughs of the castle's interiors and exteriors, enabling remote educational access without physical travel.[62] The project documented over 120 specific areas within the castle, integrating archaeological, ethnographic, and engineering data to analyze building phases, material degradation, and historical modifications.[64] In 2022, researchers extended this work by reconstructing the castle digitally for mobile virtual reality applications, processing billions of laser-scanned points into detailed meshes optimized for low-resource devices, facilitating immersive heritage experiences.[65]These digital initiatives support UNESCO-mandated conservation by providing baseline records for monitoring erosion, seismic risks, and tourism impacts on the site, while fostering Ghanaian capacity in heritage technologies through joint training.[62] Complementary efforts in the late 2010s aligned with Ghana's Year of Return campaign in 2019, which boosted site visits and underscored the need for non-invasive documentation to balance accessibility with structural integrity.[60] Ongoing analyses from the project's datasets continue to inform restoration priorities, though challenges persist in integrating local stakeholder input and securing sustained funding for updates.[66] Historical Significance and Interpretive Debates Achievements in Trade Infrastructure and Regional Impact Elmina Castle, constructed between 1480 and 1482 by the Portuguese under Diogo de Azambuja, represented a pioneering engineering accomplishment as the first permanent European stone fortress in sub-Saharan Africa, featuring robust masonry walls, bastions, and cannon emplacements designed to safeguard trade operations against local and rival European threats.[37] This infrastructure enabled secure warehousing of commodities and defense of coastal access points, facilitating the transition from seasonal trading voyages to year-round entrepôts that integrated Atlantic shipping with inland African supply chains.[29]The castle's strategic location and fortifications supported substantial gold exports from the Gold Coast's Akan hinterlands, with annual shipments reaching approximately 24,000 ounces by the early 16th century—equivalent to about 10% of Europe's gold supply at the time—and totaling over one metric ton dispatched to Lisbon between 1490 and 1560.[67] Under Portuguese administration, it monopolized gold inflows, exchanging European goods like brassware, cloth, and beads for dust and nuggets transported via established caravan routes, thereby establishing Elmina as the primary conduit for West African mineral wealth into global markets.[37]Dutch capture in 1637 further enhanced the site's trade infrastructure through expansions, including reinforced curtain walls and administrative facilities that centralized operations for the Dutch West India Company, amplifying export logistics and regional connectivity.[68] These developments spurred local economic activity, sustaining a population exceeding 20,000 in Elmina by fostering a canoe-based harbor system for offloading cargoes and attracting inland traders, which in turn stimulated ancillary commerce in provisions and labor services.[37] The castle's role as a defended hub thus catalyzed the Gold Coast's integration into broader Atlantic networks, yielding sustained revenue streams that underpinned European commercial expansion without reliance on transient outposts.[29] Criticisms, Atrocities, and Causal Factors in Slave Trade Elmina Castle's role in the transatlantic slave trade involved holding thousands of captives in cramped dungeons under brutal conditions that contributed to significant mortality before shipment. Historical records indicate that male dungeons could confine up to 400 individuals and female dungeons up to 200, with inadequate ventilation, sanitation limited to central drains, and rations of water and cornmeal insufficient to prevent starvation and disease outbreaks.[69] These facilities, repurposed from initial gold trade storage after the Portuguese shifted focus in the 16th century, exemplified the commodification of human lives, where captives endured beatings, chaining, and separation by sex to minimize resistance.[2]Atrocities extended to systematic sexual violence, as fort governors and traders accessed female dungeons via stairways from upper quarters, selecting women for abuse—a practice documented in European accounts and later testimonies. Rebellious or ill slaves faced further punishment in solitary cells devoid of light, exacerbating psychological torment. The "Door of No Return," a narrow portal leading to waiting ships, facilitated the final march of survivors onto vessels, with estimates suggesting high pre-embarkation death rates due to these conditions, though precise figures for Elmina remain elusive amid broader Gold Coast exports of hundreds of thousands over two centuries.[29][70]Criticisms of Elmina's operations, voiced by 18th- and 19th-century European abolitionists like Olaudah Equiano in his 1789 narrative, condemned the fort's architecture and routines as engines of dehumanization, prioritizing profit over humanity and enabling unchecked abuses by isolated traders. Modern scholarly critiques, drawing on primary logs from Dutch and British administrations, highlight how the castle's design—fortified against African resistance yet vulnerable internally—perpetuated a cycle of violence, though some analyses caution against overemphasizing European agency while understating local complicity, given systemic biases in post-colonial historiography that amplify victim narratives at the expense of evidentiary balance.[71]Causal factors in the slave trade at Elmina stemmed from intertwined economic pressures and regional dynamics. European demand surged post-1500 due to labor shortages on American sugar, tobacco, and cotton plantations, where indigenous populations collapsed from introduced diseases and overwork, necessitating an imported workforce estimated at 12.5 million Africans overall.[72] At Elmina, Portuguese and later Dutch traders exchanged firearms, textiles, and rum for captives, fueling a supply chain rooted in pre-existing African warfare and enslavement practices, where coastal kingdoms like the Fante captured inland rivals or debtors for sale, amplifying conflicts via imported guns that escalated prisoner yields.[29] This reciprocal system, rather than unilateral European imposition, drove exports, as African elites profited from the trade's incentives, with Elmina's strategic location enabling efficient logistics until British abolition in 1807 curtailed operations.[2][73] Balanced Perspectives on African Agency and European Motivations Local African polities, including the Fante Confederacy near Elmina, actively participated in the supply chain of the transatlantic slave trade by capturing individuals through intertribal warfare, judicial punishments, and raids, then selling them to European traders at coastal forts like Elmina Castle for European-manufactured goods such as firearms, textiles, and alcohol.[74][75] This agency enabled African intermediaries to amass wealth and military power; for instance, the Fante leveraged their coastal position to control the flow of slaves from inland suppliers like the Asante to European buyers, exchanging captives for guns that fueled further conflicts and captures.[74] European demand stimulated an expansion of internal African enslavement practices, which predated European arrival but intensified with the influx of firearms—estimated to have armed African states sufficiently to export around 12 million people over four centuries, with suppliers retaining economic and political benefits from the transactions.[76][77]European construction of Elmina Castle in 1482 by the Portuguese was driven primarily by economic imperatives to secure and monopolize the gold trade from the interior, rather than an initial focus on human trafficking; the fortress served as a fortified trading post to defend against rival European powers and local competitors while facilitating barter for gold dust transported via inland routes.[2] By the early 16th century, as Spanish and Portuguese colonial plantations in the Americas generated demand for labor-intensive crops like sugar, Europeans shifted toward slave procurement at Elmina, motivated by the high profitability of coerced labor over indentured alternatives—yields from slave-based sugar production in Brazil and the Caribbean reportedly exceeded 300% returns on investment for traders.[2][78] This transition reflected causal economic incentives, including labor shortages in the New World and the comparative advantage of African coastal supply networks, rather than isolated ideological racism; subsequent Dutch capture of the castle in 1637 further entrenched its role in slave exports to sustain mercantile profits for the Dutch West India Company.[2]Historians emphasizing African agency argue that portraying the trade solely as European imposition overlooks empirical evidence of mutual economic interdependence, where African rulers negotiated terms, rejected unfavorable deals, and sometimes protected their own subjects from enslavement, as seen in Fante alliances with Europeans against inland threats.[75] Conversely, European motivations were rooted in mercantilist competition among Portugal, the Netherlands, and Britain to dominate Atlantic commerce, with slave trading at Elmina yielding direct fiscal gains—such as the Portuguese crown's collection of duties on gold and later slaves—that funded further expeditions.[2] This bilateral dynamic underscores that while Europeans innovated ocean-going transport and auction systems, the trade's scale depended on African willingness to supply captives, often prioritizing short-term gains in arms and luxury imports over long-term societal costs.[76] Academic analyses, drawing from trade logs and archaeological data, estimate that over 1 million slaves passed through Elmina between the 17th and 19th centuries, with African suppliers capturing the majority inland and delivering them to the castle gates, highlighting complicit local decision-making amid broader causal pressures from global demand.[78]

Ghana, officially the Republic of Ghana, is a country in West Africa. It is situated with the Gulf of Guinea and the Atlantic Ocean to the south, and shares borders with Côte d'Ivoire to the west, Burkina Faso to the north, and Togo to the east. Ghana covers an area of 239,567 km2 (92,497 sq mi), spanning diverse ecologies, from coastal savannas to tropical rainforests. With over 35 million inhabitants, Ghana is the thirteenth-most populous country in Africa, and the second-most populous country in West Africa specifically. The capital and largest city is Accra.
The earliest kingdoms to emerge in Ghana were Bonoman in the south and the Kingdom of Dagbon in the north, with Bonoman existing in the area during the 11th century. The Asante Empire and other Akan kingdoms in the south emerged over the centuries. Beginning in the 15th century, the Portuguese Empire, followed by other European powers, contested the area for trading rights, until the British ultimately established control of the coast by the 19th century. Following more than a century of colonial resistance, the current borders of the country took shape, encompassing four separate British colonial territories: Gold Coast, Ashanti, the Northern Territories, and British Togoland. These were unified as an independent dominion within the Commonwealth of Nations. On 6 March 1957 Ghana became the first colony in Sub-Saharan Africa to achieve sovereignty. Under President Kwame Nkrumah, it became influential in decolonisation efforts and the Pan-African movement.
Ghana is a multi-ethnic country with diverse linguistic and religious groups; while the Akan are the largest ethnic group, they constitute a plurality. Most Ghanaians are Christians (71.3%); almost a fifth are Muslims; a tenth practise traditional faiths or report no religion. Ghana is a unitary constitutional democracy led by a president who is head of state and head of government. For  political stability in Africa, Ghana ranked seventh in the 2022 Ibrahim Index of African Governance and fifth in the 2024 Fragile States Index. Since 1993, it has maintained one of the freest and most stable governments on the continent, and performs relatively well in healthcare, economic growth, and human development. It has a significant influence in West Africa and Africa as a whole. Ghana is highly integrated in international affairs, being a founding member of the Non-Aligned Movement and the African Union, and a member of the United Nations, South Atlantic Peace and Cooperation Zone, Economic Community of West African States, the Group of 24 and the Commonwealth of Nations.

Ghana is thought to originate from the title Kaya Maghan of the rulers of Wagadu, which translates as ruler of gold. As the Gold Coast colony prepared for independence, the nation's leader and first prime minister later first president Kwame Nkrumah the one who led Ghana to independence, settled on Ghana, aiming to evoke a sense of unity and liberation among the Ghanaian people. The name was a powerful reminder of their shared heritage and the legacy of the ancient empire that once thrived in the wider region. It encapsulated the aspirations of the Ghanaian people for self-governance, progress, and a future marked by dignity and resilience.[20][21] History[] Medieval kingdoms[] The earliest recorded kingdoms to emerge in modern Ghana were the Mole-Dagbon states.[22] Before the unification of Dagbon, societies were decentralised, and headed by the Tindaamba (singular: tindana).[23] These decentralised states were unified by King Gbewaa, who lived a long life, and formed a stable, peaceful society.[24] Dagbon extended beyond the boundaries of present-day Ghana.[25][26][27] Kingdoms that emerged from Dagbon include the Mossi Kingdoms of Burkina Faso,[28] and Bouna Kingdom of Ivory Coast.[29] The kingdom enjoyed great prosperity, establishing Ghana's earliest educational systems,[30] and using a writing script[31][32] prior to European invasion. Female chiefs who rule over male subjects are present in the kingdom,[33] and inheritance is both patrilineal and matrilineal.[34] The Yaa Naa is the King of Dagbon and the Gundo Naa is the Queen.[35][36] The kingdom remained uncolonised. In 1896, Germany invaded Eastern Dagbon (Naya) and burnt down its capital, Yendi,[37][38] during the Battle of Adibo.[39][40] The Akan-speaking peoples began to move into what later became Ghana toward the 15th century.[22][41] By the 16th century, the Akans were established in the Akan state called Bonoman, for which the Brong-Ahafo region was named.[22][42] From the 17th century, Akans emerged from what is believed to have been the Bonoman area, to create Akan states, mainly based on gold trading.[43] These states included Bonoman (Brong-Ahafo region), Ashanti (Ashanti Region), Denkyira (Western North region), Mankessim Kingdom (Central region), and Akwamu (Eastern region).[22] By the 19th century, the territory of the southern part of Ghana was included in the Kingdom of Ashanti.[22] The government of the Ashanti Empire operated first as a loose network and eventually as a centralised kingdom with a specialised bureaucracy centred in the capital city of Kumasi.[22] Prior to Akan contact with Europeans, the Akan people created an economy based on principally gold and gold bar precious metals, which were traded with other states in Africa.[22][44] The Ga-Dangme and Ewe migrated westward from south-western Nigeria. The Ewe – formerly known as Dogbo- migrated from Oyo area with their Gbe-speaking kinsmen (Adja, Fon, Phla/Phera and Ogun/Gun) and in transition, settled at Ketou in Benin Republic, Tado in Togo, Dogbo Nyigbo in Benin Republic and with Nortsie (a walled town in present-day Togo) as their final dispersal point. Their dispersal from Nortsie was necessitated by the high-handed rule of King Agorkorli (Agɔ Akɔli) who was the reigning monarch of the tribe at that time. The Ewe in Ghana speak three principal dialects: Anlo(along the coast),Tongu(along the Volta river) and Ewedome (in the hill country side).The Ga-Dangme occupy the Greater Accra Region and parts of the Eastern Region, while the Ewe are found in the Volta Region as well as the neighbouring Togo, Benin Republic and Nigeria (around Badagry area). European contact and colonialism[] Akan trade with European states began after contact with the Portuguese in the 15th century.[45] European contact was by the Portuguese people, who came to the Gold Coast region in the 15th century to trade. The Portuguese then established the Portuguese Gold Coast (Costa do Ouro), focused on the availability of gold.[46] The Portuguese built a trading lodge at a coastal settlement called Anomansah (the perpetual drink), which they renamed São Jorge da Mina.[46] In 1481, King John II of Portugal commissioned Diogo de Azambuja to build the Elmina Castle, which was completed in three years.[46] By 1598, the Dutch had joined the Portuguese in the gold trade, establishing the Dutch Gold Coast (Nederlandse Bezittingen ter Kuste van Guinea – 'Dutch properties at the Guinea coast') and building forts at Fort Komenda and Kormantsi.[47] In 1617, the Dutch captured the Elmina Castle from the Portuguese and Axim in 1642 (Fort St Anthony).[47] European traders had joined in gold trading by the 17th century, including the Swedes, establishing the Swedish Gold Coast (Svenska Guldkusten), and Denmark–Norway, establishing the Danish Gold Coast (Danske Guldkyst or Dansk Guinea).[48] European traders participated in the Atlantic slave trade in this area.[49] More than 30 forts and castles were built by the merchants. The Germans established the Brandenburger Gold Coast or Groß Friedrichsburg.[50] In 1874, Great Britain established control over some parts of the country, assigning these areas the status of the British Gold Coast.[51] Military engagements occurred between British colonial powers and Akan nation-states. The Kingdom of Ashanti defeated the British some times in the 100-year-long Anglo-Ashanti wars and eventually lost with the War of the Golden Stool in 1900.[52][53][54] Transition to independence[] In 1947, the newly formed United Gold Coast Convention led by "The Big Six" called for "self-government within the shortest possible time" following the 1946 Gold Coast legislative election.[48][55] Kwame Nkrumah, a Ghanaian nationalist who led Ghana from 1957 to 1966 as the country's first prime minister and president, formed the Convention People's Party in 1949 with the motto "self-government now".[48] The party initiated a "positive action" campaign involving non-violent protests, strikes and non-cooperation with the British authorities. Nkrumah was arrested and sentenced to one year imprisonment during this time. In the Gold Coast's 1951 general election, he was elected to Parliament and was released from prison.[48] He became prime minister in 1952 and began a policy of Africanization.[citation needed] At midnight of March 6, 1957, the Gold Coast, Ashanti, the Northern Territories, and British Togoland were unified as one single independent dominion within the British Commonwealth under the name Ghana. This was done under the Ghana Independence Act 1957. The current flag of Ghana, consisting of the colours red, gold, green, and a black star, dates back to this unification.[56] On 1 July 1960, following the Ghanaian constitutional referendum and Ghanaian presidential election, Nkrumah declared Ghana a republic and assumed the presidency.[7][8][9][48] 6 March is the nation's Independence Day, and 1 July is celebrated as Republic Day.[57][58] Nkrumah led an authoritarian regime in Ghana, as he repressed political opposition and conducted elections that were not free and fair.[59][60][61][62][63] In 1964, a constitutional amendment made Ghana a one-party state, with Nkrumah as president for life of both the nation and its party.[64] Nkrumah was the first African head of state to promote the concept of Pan-Africanism, which he had been introduced to during his studies at Lincoln University, Pennsylvania in the United States, at the time when Marcus Garvey was known for his "Back to Africa Movement".[48] He merged the teachings of Garvey, Martin Luther King Jr. and the naturalised Ghanaian scholar W. E. B. Du Bois into the formation of 1960s Ghana.[48] Osagyefo Dr. Kwame Nkrumah, as he became known, played an instrumental part in the founding of the Non-Aligned Movement, and in establishing the Kwame Nkrumah Ideological Institute to teach his ideologies of communism and socialism.[65] His life achievements were recognised by Ghanaians during his centenary birthday celebration, and the day was instituted as a public holiday in Ghana (Founders' Day).[66] Operation Cold Chop and aftermath[] The government of Nkrumah was subsequently overthrown in a coup by the Ghana Armed Forces, codenamed "Operation Cold Chop". This occurred while Nkrumah was abroad with Zhou Enlai in the People's Republic of China, on a mission to Hanoi, Vietnam, to help end the Vietnam War. The coup took place on 24 February 1966, led by Colonel Emmanuel Kwasi Kotoka and Brigadier Akwasi Afrifa. The National Liberation Council was formed, chaired by Lieutenant General Joseph A. Ankrah.[67][68] A series of alternating military and civilian governments, often affected by economic instabilities,[69] ruled Ghana from 1966, ending with the ascent to power of Flight Lieutenant Jerry John Rawlings of the Provisional National Defence Council in 1981.[70] These changes resulted in the suspension of the constitution in 1981 and the banning of political parties.[71] The economy soon declined, so Rawlings negotiated a structural adjustment plan, changing many old economic policies, and growth recovered during the mid-1980s.[71] A new constitution restoring multi-party system politics was promulgated in the presidential election of 1992, in which Rawlings was elected, and again in the general election of 1996.[72] In a tribal war in Northern Ghana in 1994, between the Konkomba and other ethnic groups, including the Nanumba, Dagomba and Gonja, between 1,000 and 2,000 people were killed and 150,000 people were displaced.[73] After the 2000 general election, John Kufuor of the New Patriotic Party became president of Ghana on 7 January 2001 and was re-elected in 2004, thus also serving two terms (the term limit) as president of Ghana and marking the first time under the fourth republic that power was transferred from one legitimately elected head of state and head of government to another.[72] Nana Akufo-Addo, the ruling party candidate, was defeated in a very close 2008 general election by John Atta Mills of the National Democratic Congress.[74][75] Mills died of natural causes and was succeeded by Vice President John Mahama on 24 July 2012.[76] Politics[] Ghana is a unitary presidential constitutional democracy with a parliamentary multi-party system that is dominated by two parties—the National Democratic Congress (NDC) and the New Patriotic Party (NPP). Ghana alternated between civilian and military governments until January 1993, when the military government gave way to the Fourth Republic of Ghana after presidential and parliamentary elections in late 1992. The 1992 constitution of Ghana divides powers among a commander-in-chief of the Ghana Armed Forces (President of Ghana), parliament (Parliament of Ghana), cabinet (Cabinet of Ghana), council of state (Ghanaian Council of State), and an independent judiciary (Judiciary of Ghana). The government is elected by universal suffrage after every four years.[95] Nana Akufo-Addo won the presidency in the general election in 2016, defeating incumbent John Mahama. He also won the 2020 election after the presidential election results were challenged at the Supreme Court by flagbearer of the NDC, John Mahama. Presidents are limited to two four-year terms in office. The president can serve a second term only upon re-election. Ghana favours international and regional political and economic co-operation, and is an active member of the United Nations and the African Union.[101] Ghana has a strong relationship with the United States. Bush, and Barack Obama—and a Vice President—Kamala Harris—have made diplomatic trips to Ghana.[102] Many Ghanaian diplomats and politicians hold positions in international organisations, including Ghanaian diplomat and former Secretary-General of the United Nations Kofi Annan, International Criminal Court Judge Akua Kuenyehia, as well as former President Jerry John Rawlings and former President John Agyekum Kufuor, who both served as diplomats of the United Nations.[95] In September 2010, President John Atta Mills visited China on an official visit. Mills and China's former President Hu Jintao marked the 50th anniversary of diplomatic ties between the two nations, at the Great Hall of the People.[103] China reciprocated with an official visit in November 2011, by the vice-chairman of the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress of China, Zhou Tienong who visited Ghana and met with Ghana's President John Mahama.[104] China recently became one of the top investing countries of Ghana, which predominantly focus on infrastructure, natural resources, and manufacturing sectors, have promoted economic growth, job creation, and technology transfer in Ghana. However, concerns regarding the sustainability of Chinese-financed projects, environmental impacts, and the lack of transparency in their investments call for a careful assessment of these collaborations.[105] Iranian President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad met with Mahama in 2013 to hold discussions on strengthening the Non-Aligned Movement and also co–chair a bilateral meeting between Ghana and Iran at the Ghanaian presidential palace Flagstaff House.[106][107][108][109][110] The Sustainable Development Goals (SDG) were integrated into Ghana's development agenda and the budget. According to reports, the SDGs were implemented through a decentralized planning approach. This allows for stakeholders' participation, such as in UN agencies, traditional leaders, civil society organizations, academia, and others.[111] The 17 SDGs are a global call to action to end poverty among others, and the UN and its partners in the country are working towards achieving them.[112] According to the President Nana Akufo-Addo, Ghana was "the first sub-Saharan African country to achieve the goal of halving poverty, as contained in Goal 1 of the Millennium Development Goals".[113] Military[] In 1957, the Ghana Armed Forces (GAF) consisted of its headquarters, support services, three battalions of infantry and a reconnaissance squadron with armoured vehicles.[114] President Nkrumah aimed at rapidly expanding the GAF to support the United States of Africa ambitions. Thus, in 1961, 4th and 5th Battalions were established, and in 1964 6th Battalion was established, from a parachute airborne unit originally raised in 1963.[115] Today, Ghana is a regional power and regional hegemon.[18] In his book Shake Hands with the Devil, Canadian Forces commander Roméo Dallaire highly rated the GAF soldiers and military personnel.[114] The military operations and military doctrine of the GAF are conceptualised in the constitution, Ghana's Law on Armed Force Military Strategy, and Kofi Annan International Peacekeeping Training Centre agreements to which GAF is attestator.[116][117][118] GAF military operations are executed under the auspices and imperium of the Ministry of Defence.[116][119] Although Ghana is relatively peaceful and is often considered being one of the least violent countries in the region, Ghana has experienced political violence in the past and 2017 has thus far seen an upward trend in incidents motivated by political grievances.[120] Law enforcement[] The Ghana Police Service and the Criminal Investigation Department are the main law enforcement agencies, responsible for the detection of crime, maintenance of law and order and the maintenance of internal peace and security.[121] The Ghana Police Service has eleven specialised police units, including a Militarized police Rapid deployment force and Marine Police Unit.[122][123] The Ghana Police Service operates in 12 divisions: ten covering the regions of Ghana, one assigned specifically to the seaport and industrial hub of Tema, and the twelfth being the Railways, Ports and Harbours Division.[123] The Ghana Police Service's Marine Police Unit and Division handles issues that arise from the country's offshore oil and gas industry.[123] The Ghana Prisons Service and the sub-division Borstal Institute for Juveniles administers incarceration.[124] Ghana retains and exercises the death penalty for treason, corruption, robbery, piracy, drug trafficking, rape, and homicide.[125][126] The new sustainable development goals adopted by the United Nations call for the international community to come together to promote the rule of law; support equal access to justice for all; reduce corruption; and develop effective, accountable, and transparent institutions at all levels.[127] Ghana is used as a key narcotics industry transshipment point by traffickers, usually from South America as well as some from other African nations.[128] In 2013, the UN chief of the Office on Drugs and Crime stated that "West Africa is completely weak in terms of border control and the big drug cartels from Colombia and Latin America have chosen Africa as a way to reach Europe."[129] There is not a wide or popular knowledge about the narcotics industry and intercepted narcotics within Ghana, since it is an underground economy. The social context within which narcotic trafficking, storage, transportation, and repacking systems exist in Ghana and the state's location along the Gulf of Guinea makes Ghana an attractive country for the narcotics business.[128][130] The Narcotics Control Board has impounded container ships at the Sekondi Naval Base in the Takoradi Harbour. These ships were carrying thousands of kilograms of cocaine, with a street value running into billions of Ghana cedis. However, drug seizures saw a decline in 2011.[128][130] Drug cartels are using new methods in narcotics production and narcotics exportation, to avoid Ghanaian security agencies.[128][130] Underdeveloped institutions, porous open borders, and the existence of established smuggling organisations contribute to Ghana's position in the narcotics industry.[128][130] President Mills initiated ongoing efforts to reduce the role of airports in Ghana's drug trade.[128] Human rights[] Homosexual acts are prohibited by law in Ghana.[131] According to a 2013 survey by the Pew Research Center, 96% of Ghanaians believe that homosexuality should not be accepted by society.[132] Sometimes elderly women in Ghana are accused of witchcraft, particularly in rural Ghana. Issues of witchcraft mainly remain as speculations based on superstitions within families. In some parts of northern Ghana, there exist what are called witch camps. These are said to house a total of around 1,000 people accused of witchcraft.[133] The Ghanaian government has announced that it intends to close the camps.[133] Economy[] Ghana possesses industrial minerals, hydrocarbons and precious metals. It is an emerging designated digital economy with mixed economy hybridisation and an emerging market. It has an economic plan target known as the "Ghana Vision 2020". This plan envisions Ghana as the first African country to become a developed country between 2020 and 2029 and a newly industrialised country between 2030 and 2039.[134] This excludes fellow Group of 24 member and Sub-Saharan African country South Africa, which is a newly industrialised country.[135] Ghana's economy has ties to the Chinese yuan renminbi along with Ghana's vast gold reserves. In 2013, the Bank of Ghana began circulating the renminbi throughout Ghanaian state-owned banks and to the Ghana public as hard currency along with the national Ghanaian cedi for second national trade currency.[136] Between 2012 and 2013, 38% of rural dwellers were experiencing poverty whereas only 11% of urban dwellers were.[137] Urban areas hold greater opportunity for employment, particularly in informal trade, while nearly all (94 percent) of "rural poor households" participate in the agricultural sector.[138] The Volta River Authority and the Ghana National Petroleum Corporation, both state-owned, are the two major electricity producers.[139] The Akosombo Dam, built on the Volta River in 1965, along with the Bui Dam, the Kpong Dam and several other hydroelectric dams, provide hydropower.[140][141] In addition, the government sought to build the second nuclear power plant in Africa. The Ghana Stock Exchange is the fifth largest on continental Africa and 3rd largest in sub-saharan Africa with a market capitalisation of GH¢ 57.2 billion or CN¥180.4 billion in 2012 with the South Africa JSE Limited as first.[142] The Ghana Stock Exchange was the second best performing stock exchange in sub-saharan Africa in 2013.[143] Ghana produces high-quality cocoa.[144] It is the second largest producer of cocoa globally and its ICCO membership helps in its international cocoa trade.[145] Ghana is classified as a middle income country.[4][146] Services account for 50% of GDP, followed by manufacturing (24.1%), extractive industries (5%), and taxes (20.9%).[139] Ghana has an increasing primary manufacturing economy and export of digital technology goods along with assembling and exporting automobiles and ships, diverse resource rich exportation of industrial minerals, agricultural products primarily cocoa, petroleum and natural gas,[147] and industries such as information and communications technology primarily via Ghana's state digital technology corporation Rlg Communications which manufactures tablet computers with smartphones and various consumer electronics.[139][148] Urban electric cars have been manufactured in Ghana since 2014.[149][150] It announced plans to issue government debt by way of social and green bonds in Autumn 2021, making it the first African country to do so.[151][152] The country, which was planning to borrow up to $5 billion in international markets,[when?] would use the proceeds from these sustainable bonds to refinance debt used for social and environmental projects and pay for educational or health. Only a few other nations have sold them so far, including Chile and Ecuador. The country will use the proceeds to forge ahead with a free secondary-school initiative started in 2017 among other programs, despite having recorded its lowest economic growth rate in 37 years in 2020.[153] It produces and exports hydrocarbons such as sweet crude oil and natural gas.[154][155] The 100%-state-owned filling station company, Ghana Oil Company, is the number 1 petroleum and gas filling station, and the 100%-state-owned state oil company Ghana National Petroleum Corporation oversees hydrocarbon exploration and production of petroleum and natural gas reserves. Ghana aims to further increase the output of oil to 2.2 million barrels (350,000 m3) per day and gas to 34,000,000 cubic metres (1.2×10 9 cu ft) per day.[156] The Jubilee Oil Field, which contains up to 3 billion barrels (480,000,000 m3) of sweet crude oil, was discovered in 2007.[157] Ghana is believed to have up to 5 billion barrels (790,000,000 m3) to 7 billion barrels (1.1×109 m3) of petroleum in reserves,[158] which is the fifth-largest in Africa and the 21st-to-25th-largest proven reserves in the world. It also has up to 1.7×1011 cubic metres (6×10 12 cu ft) of natural gas in reserves.[159] The government has drawn up plans to nationalise petroleum and natural gas reserves to increase government revenue.[160] In 2015, Ghana produced 88 metric tonnes of gold as per the our world in data report.[161] As of 2019, Ghana was the 7th largest producer of gold in the world, producing ~140 tonnes that year.[162] This record saw Ghana surpass South Africa in output for the first time, making Ghana the largest gold producer in Africa.[163] In addition to gold, Ghana exports silver, timber, diamonds, bauxite, and manganese, and has other mineral deposits.[164] Ghana ranks 9th in the world in diamond export and reserve size.[165] The government has drawn up plans to nationalize mining industry to increase government revenue.[166][167] "Shortages" of electricity in 2015 and 2016 led to dumsor ("persistent, irregular and unpredictable" electric power outages),[168] increasing the interest in renewables.[169] As of 2019, there is a surplus of electricity.[170] The judicial system of Ghana deals with corruption, economic malpractice and lack of economic transparency.[171] According to Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index of 2018, out of 180 countries, Ghana was ranked 78th, with a score of 41 on a scale where a 0–9 score means highly corrupt, and a 90–100 score means very clean. This was based on perceived levels of public sector corruption.[172] Science and technology[] Ghana launched a cellular mobile network in 1992. It was later connected to the Internet and introduced ADSL broadband services.[173] It was ranked 99th in the Global Innovation Index in 2024.[174][175][176] The Ghana Space Science and Technology Centre (GSSTC) and Ghana Space Agency (GhsA) oversee space exploration and space programmes. GSSTC and GhsA worked to have a national security observational satellite launched into orbit in 2015.[177][178] Ghana's annual space exploration expenditure has been 1% of its GDP, to support research in science and technology. In 2012, Ghana was elected to chair the Commission on Science and Technology for Sustainable Development in the South (Comsats); Ghana has a joint effort in space exploration with the South African National Space Agency.[177] Tourism[] In 2011, tourists visiting Ghana numbered 1,087,000,[179] with arrivals including South Americans, Asians, Europeans, and North Americans.[180] Among the attractions and tourist destinations are waterfalls such as Kintampo waterfalls and the largest waterfall in west Africa, Wli waterfalls, the coastal palm-lined sandy beaches, caves, mountains, rivers, and reservoirs and lakes such as Lake Bosumtwi and the largest human-made lake in the world by surface area, Lake Volta, dozens of forts and castles, World Heritage Sites, nature reserves and national parks.[180] Notable castles are Cape Coast Castle and the Elmina Castle.[181] Castles mark where blood was shed in the slave trade and preserve and promote the African heritage stolen and destroyed through the slave trade.[182] The World Heritage Convention of UNESCO named Ghana's castles and forts as World Heritage Monuments, based on the criterion: "The Castles and Forts of Ghana shaped not only Ghana’s history but that of the world over four centuries as the focus of first the gold trade and then the slave trade. They are a significant and emotive symbol of European-African encounters and of the starting point of the African Diaspora."[182] The World Economic Forum statistics in 2010 showed that out of the world's favourite tourist destinations, Ghana was ranked 108th out of 139 countries.[183] The country had moved two places up from the 2009 rankings. In 2011, Forbes magazine published that Ghana was ranked the 11th most friendly country in the world. The assertion was based on a survey in 2010 of a cross-section of travellers. Of all the African countries that were included in the survey, Ghana ranked highest.[183] Tourism is the fourth highest earner of foreign exchange for the country.[183] In 2024, Ghana ranked as the 55th most peaceful country in the world.[184] Up and down the coastline, surfing spots have been identified and cultivated by locals and internationals. Surfers have made trips to the country to sample the waves. Surfers carried their boards amid traditional fishing vessels.[185] According to Destination Pride[186]—a data-driven search platform used to visualize the world's LGBTQ+ laws, rights and social sentiment—Ghana's Pride score is 22 (out of 100).[187] Demographics[] As of 2024[update] United Nations' reports, Ghana has a population of 34,581,288.[188] As of 2018[update], around 29% of the population is under the age of 15, while persons aged 15–64 make up 57.8% of the population.[189] The 2010 census reported that the largest ethnic groups are the Akan (47.3%), the Mole-Dagbani (18.5%), the Ewe (13.9%), the Ga-Dangme (7.4%), the Gurma (5.7%) and the Guan (3.7%).[190]As of 2024[update] United Nations' reports, the median age of Ghanaian citizens is 21 years old.[191] Ghana contributes 0.42% to the total world population.[188] With recent legal immigration of skilled workers who possess Ghana Cards, there is a small population of Chinese, Malaysian, Indian, Middle Eastern and European nationals. In 2010, the Ghana Immigration Service reported many economic migrants and Illegal immigrants inhabiting Ghana: 14.6% (or 3.1 million) of Ghana's 2010 population (predominantly Nigerians, Burkinabe citizens, Togolese citizens, and Malian citizens). In 1969, under the "Ghana Aliens Compliance Order" enacted by then Prime Minister Kofi Abrefa Busia,[192] the Border Guard Unit deported more than 3,000,000 aliens and illegal immigrants in three months as they made up 20% of the population at the time.[192][193][194] In 2013, there was a mass deportation of illegal miners, more than 4,000 of whom were Chinese nationals.[195][196] Template:Largest cities Languages[] English is the official language of Ghana.[197][198] Additionally, there are eleven languages that have the status of government-sponsored languages: - Akan languages (Asante Twi, Akuapem Twi, Fante, Bono which have a high degree of mutual intelligibility, and Nzema, which is less intelligible with the above) - Dangme - Ewe - Ga - Guan - Kasem - Mole–Dagbani languages (Dagaare and Dagbanli)[199][200] Of these, Asante Twi is the most widely spoken.[201] Because Ghana is surrounded by French-speaking countries, French is widely taught in schools and used for commercial and international economic exchanges. Since 2005, Ghana has been an associate member of the Organisation internationale de la Francophonie,[202] the global organisation that unites French-speaking countries (84 nations on six continents). In 2005, more than 350,000 Ghanaian children studied French in schools. Since then, its status has been progressively updated to a mandatory language in every junior high school,[203] and it is in the process of becoming an official language.[204][205] Ghanaian Pidgin English, also known as Kru English (or in Akan, kroo brofo), is a variety of West African Pidgin English spoken in Accra and in the southern towns.[206] It can be divided into two varieties, referred to as "uneducated" or "non-institutionalized" pidgin and "educated" or "institutionalized" pidgin, the former associated with uneducated or illiterate people and the latter acquired and used in institutions such as universities.[207] Religion[] Christianity is the largest religion in Ghana, with 71.3% of the population being members of various Christian denominations as of the 2021 census.[208] Islam is practised by 20% of the total population. According to a 2012 report by Pew Research, 51% of Muslims are followers of Sunni Islam, while approximately 16% belong to the Ahmadiyya movement and around 8% identify with Shia Islam, while the remainder are non-denominational Muslims.[209][210] There is "no significant link between ethnicity and religion in Ghana".[211] Ghana has around 150,000 Jehovah's Witnesses.[212] Universal health care and life expectancy[] Ghana has a universal health care system, National Health Insurance Scheme (NHIS), which is strictly designated for Ghanaian nationals.[213] Health care is variable throughout Ghana and in 2012, more than 12 million Ghanaian nationals were covered by the NHIS.[214] Urban centres are well served and contain most of the hospitals, clinics, and pharmacies. There are more than 200 hospitals, and Ghana is a destination for medical tourism.[215] In 2010, there were 0.1 physicians per 1,000 people and as of 2011[update], 0.9 hospital beds per 1,000 people.[189] In 2010, 5.2% of Ghana's GDP was spent on health.[216] In 2020, the WHO announced Ghana became the second country in the WHO African Region to attain regulatory system "maturity level 3", the second-highest in the four-tiered WHO classification of National medicines regulatory systems.[217] Life expectancy at birth in 2024 was 69 for a female and 64 for a male.[citation needed] In 2013, infant mortality was to 39 per 1,000 live births.[218] Sources vary on life expectancy at birth; the World Health Organization (WHO) estimated 62 years for men and 64 years for women born in 2016.[219] The fertility rate declined from 3.99 (2000) to 3.28 (2010) with 2.78 in urban region and 3.94 in rural region.[190] The United Nations reports a fertility decline from 6.95 (1970) to 4.82 (2000) to 3.93 live births per woman in 2017.[220] As of 2012[update], the HIV/AIDS prevalence was estimated at 1.40% among adults aged 15–49.[221] Education[] The education system is divided into three parts: basic education, secondary cycle, and tertiary education. "Basic education" lasts 11 years (ages 4‒15).[222] It is divided into kindergarten (two years), primary school (two modules of three years) and junior high (three years). Junior high school ends with the Basic Education Certificate Examination.[222][223] Once certified, the pupil can proceed to the secondary cycle.[224] Hence, the pupil has the choice between general education (offered by the senior high school) and vocational education (offered by the technical senior high school or the technical and vocational institutes). Senior high school lasts 3 years and leads to the West African Senior School Certificate Examination, which is a prerequisite for enrollment in a university bachelor's degree programme.[225]: 7 Polytechnics are open to vocational students.[226] A bachelor's degree requires four years of study. It can be followed by a one- or two-year master's degree programme, which can be followed by a PhD programme of at least three years.[225]: 9 A polytechnic programme lasts two or three years.[226] Ghana possesses colleges of education.[227] Some of the universities are the University of Ghana, Kwame Nkrumah University of Science and Technology, and University of Cape Coast.[228] There are more than 95% of children in school.[229][230] The female and male ages 15–24 years literacy rate was 81% in 2010, with males at 82%,[231] and females at 80%.[232] An education system annually attracts foreign students particularly in the university sector.[233][234] Ghana has a free education six-year primary school education system beginning at age 6.[235] The government largely funds basic education comprising public primary schools and public junior high schools. Senior high schools were subsidised by the government until September 2017/2018 academic year that senior high education became free.[236] At the higher education level, the government funds more than 80% of resources provided to public universities, polytechnics and teacher training colleges. As part of the Free Compulsory Universal Basic Education, Fcube, the government supplies all basic education schools with all their textbooks and other educational supplies, like exercise books. Senior high schools are provided with all their textbook requirements by the government. Private schools acquire their educational material from private suppliers.[237] Culture[] Food and drink[] Ghanaian cuisine includes an assortment of soups and stews with varied seafoods; most Ghanaian soups are prepared with vegetables, meat, poultry or fish.[238] Fish is important in the diet, with tilapia, roasted and fried whitebait, smoked fish and crayfish, all being common components of Ghanaian dishes.[238] Banku (akple) is a common starchy food made from ground corn (maize),[238] and cornmeal based staples kɔmi (kenkey) and banku (akple) are usually accompanied by some form of fried fish (chinam) or grilled tilapia and a very spicy condiment made from raw red and green chillies, onions and tomatoes (pepper sauce).[238] Banku and tilapia is a combo served in most restaurants.[238] Fufu is the most common exported Ghanaian dish and is a delicacy across the African diaspora.[238] Rice is an established staple meal across the country, with various rice-based dishes serving as breakfast, lunch and dinner, the main variants are waakye, plain rice and stew (eight kontomire or tomato gravy), fried rice and jollof rice.[239] Literature[] Clothing[] During the 13th century, Ghanaians developed their unique art of adinkra printing. Hand-printed and hand-embroidered adinkra clothes were made and used exclusively by royalty for devotional ceremonies. Each of the motifs that make up the corpus of adinkra symbolism has a name and meaning derived from a proverb, a historical event, human attitude, ethology, plant life-form, or shapes of inanimate and man-made objects. The meanings of the motifs may be categorised into aesthetics, ethics, human relations, and concepts.[240] The Adinkra symbols have a decorative function as tattoos but also represent objects that encapsulate evocative messages that convey traditional wisdom, aspects of life, or the environment. There are many symbols with distinct meanings, often linked with proverbs. In the words of Anthony Appiah, they were one of the means in a pre-literate society for "supporting the transmission of a complex and nuanced body of practice and belief".[241] Along with the adinkra cloth, Ghanaians use many cloth fabrics for their traditional attire.[242] The different ethnic groups have their own individual cloth. The most well known is the Kente cloth.[242] Kente is a very important national costume and clothing, and these clothes are used to make traditional and modern Kente attire.[242] Different symbols and different colours mean different things.[242] Kente is the most famous of all the Ghanaian clothes.[242] Kente is a ceremonial cloth hand-woven on a horizontal treadle loom and strips measuring about 4 inches wide are sewn together into larger pieces of cloths.[242] Cloths come in various colours, sizes and designs and are worn during very important social and religious occasions.[242] In a cultural context, kente is more important than just a cloth as it is a visual representation of history and also a form of written language through weaving.[242] The term kente has its roots in the Akan word kɛntɛn which means a basket and the first kente weavers used raffia fibres to weave cloths that looked like kenten (a basket); and thus were referred to as kenten ntoma; meaning basket cloth.[242] The original Akan name of the cloth was nsaduaso or nwontoma, meaning "a cloth hand-woven on a loom"; however, "kente" is the most frequently used term today. Kente is also woven by the Ewe people (Ewe Kente) in the Volta Region. The main weaving centres are Agortime area and Agbozume. Agbozume has a vibrant kente market attracting patrons from all over west Africa and the diaspora.[242] Contemporary Ghanaian fashion includes traditional and modern styles and fabrics and has made its way into the African and global fashion scene. The cloth known as African print fabric was created out of Dutch wax textiles. It is believed that in the late 19th century, Dutch ships on their way to Asia stocked with machine-made textiles that mimicked Indonesian batik stopped at many West African ports on the way. The fabrics did not do well in Asia. However, in West Africa—mainly Ghana where there was an already established market for cloths and textiles—the client base grew and it was changed to include local and traditional designs, colours and patterns to cater to the taste of the new consumers.[243] Today outside of Africa it is called "Ankara", and it has a client base well beyond Ghana and Africa as a whole. It is popular among Caribbean peoples and African Americans; celebrities such as Solange Knowles and her sister Beyoncé have been seen wearing African print attire.[244] Many designers from countries in North America and Europe are now using African prints, and they have gained a global interest.[245] British luxury fashion house Burberry created a collection around Ghanaian styles.[246] American musician Gwen Stefani has repeatedly incorporated African prints into her clothing line and can often be seen wearing it.[247] Internationally acclaimed Ghanaian-British designer Ozwald Boateng introduced African print suits in his 2012 collection.[248] Music and dance[] Music incorporates types of musical instruments such as the talking drum ensembles, Akan Drum, goje fiddle and koloko lute, court music, including the Akan Seperewa, the Akan atumpan, the Ga kpanlogo styles, and log xylophones used in asonko music.[249] African jazz was created by Kofi Ghanaba.[250] A form of secular music is highlife.[249] Highlife originated in the 19th and 20th centuries and spread throughout West Africa.[249] In the 1990s, a genre of music was created incorporating the influences of highlife, Afro-reggae, dancehall and hip hop.[249] This hybrid was called hiplife.[249] There are dances for occasions.[251] Dances for celebrations include the Adowa, Kpanlogo, Azonto, Klama, Agbadza, Borborbor and Bamaya.[251] The Nana Otafrija Pallbearing Services, also known as the Dancing Pallbearers, come from the coastal town of Prampram. The group was featured in a BBC feature story in 2017, and footage from the story became part of an Internet meme in the wake of the COVID-19 world pandemic.[252] Media[] Chapter 12 of the 1992 Constitution of Ghana guarantees freedom of the press and independence of the media, while Chapter 2 prohibits censorship.[253] Post-independence, private outlets closed during the military governments, and media laws prevented criticism of government.[254] Press freedoms were restored in 1992, and after the election in 2000 of Kufuor, the tensions between the private media and government decreased. Kufuor supported press freedom and repealed a libel law, and maintained that the media had to act responsibly.[255] The media have been described as "one of the most unfettered" in Africa.[256] In 1948, the Gold Coast Film Unit was set up in the Information Services Department.[257] Architecture[] There are two types of construction: the series of adjacent buildings in an enclosure around a common, and the round huts with grass roof.[258] The round huts with grass roof architecture are situated in the northern regions, while the series of adjacent buildings are in the southern regions. Postmodern architecture and high-tech architecture buildings are in the southern regions, while heritage sites are evident in the more than 30 forts and castles in the country, such as Fort William and Fort Amsterdam. Ghana has museums that are situated inside castles, and two are situated inside a fort.[259] The Military Museum and the National Museum organise temporary exhibitions.[259] Ghana has museums that allow an in-depth look at specific regions, with a number of museums providing insight into the traditions and history of the geographical areas.[259] The Cape Coast Castle Museum and St. Georges Castle (Elmina Castle) Museum offer guided tours. The Museum of Science and Technology provides its visitors with a look into the domain of scientific development, through exhibits of objects of scientific and technological interest.[259] Sports[] Association football is the top spectator sport in Ghana.[260] Ghana has won the Africa Cup of Nations four times, the FIFA U-20 World Cup once, and has participated in four consecutive FIFA World Cups (2006, 2010, 2014 and 2022) and has also won the FIFA U-17 World Cup twice.[260] The International Federation of Football History and Statistics crowned Asante Kotoko SC as the African club of the 20th century.[261] Ghana competes in the Commonwealth Games, sending athletes in every edition since 1954 (except for the 1986 games). Ghana has won 57 medals at the Commonwealth Games, including 15 gold, with all but one of their medals coming in athletics and boxing. # What&#39;s the difference between &#39;a year&#39; and &#39;the year&#39;?

When I should use "a year" and when I should use "the year."


## Accepted Answer

'A year' can be any year without any specification. But 'the year' means a particular/specified year or the one which is already mentioned and thereby known. 
E.g: In a year there are twelve months. (means any year or all years)
     I was born in the year 2000. (in that particular year)

Grammatically 'a/an' is known as indefinite article and 'the' is definite article.

The indefinite article(a/an) refers to things in general and the definite article(the) refers to a particular person or thing. This is the very basic rule pertaining to the use of articles. Actually, in English grammar, perhaps the uses of the articles are the most difficult/confusing topic.
For details on this subject, it is better to consult a good grammar like 'PRACTICAL ENGLISH USAGE' by Michael Swan.

'January is the first month of the year'- here 'the' is used in a sense of 'general plus known', as everybody knows what a year is and how many months are there in a year and what they are.
Sometimes 'the' is used with a singular countable noun to talk about things in general.
See a couple of examples from the above-mentioned grammar:
E.g: 'Schools should pay less attention to examination success, and more attention to the child.' (means all school children, not a particular child)
'The tiger is in danger of becoming extinct.'( means the whole tiger family, not any specific tiger.
