--- [Vehicle registration plates of Japan - Wikipedia] ---
In Japan, the national government issues vehicle registration plates for motor vehicles through the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism Land Transportation Offices nationwide. However, the local municipality rather than the national government registers certain vehicles with small engine displacements.
The number on the top line is a vehicle class code which begins with a 0 through 9 to indicate specific vehicle classification. This is signified by the length, width and height of the vehicle as well as engine displacement. Broadly speaking, passenger automobiles with engine displacements at or smaller than 2000 cc receive 5-series plates, while passenger automobiles with engine displacements larger than 2,000 cc (120 cu in) or more receive 3-series license plates.

Official vehicles of the Imperial household are exempt from the requirement to display such plates. Official vehicles of the Self-Defense Forces and foreign diplomats are required to display other plates.

The plates are installed on both the front and rear of the vehicle, with the rear plate permanently attached to the vehicle with a prefecture seal completely covering one of the attaching plate bolts. The plate is only removed when the vehicle is sold secondhand to someone from a different prefecture, has reached the end of service and has been sold for scrap, or exported. New vehicles are not delivered to the purchaser until the plates have been attached at the dealership.
Since November 1, 1970, a "jikō-shiki" (字光式) plate has been offered for private vehicles at the owner's request. The green characters on this type of plate are replaced with molded green plastic that can be illuminated from behind the plate. From May 19, 1998, specific numbers can also be requested if the numbers are not already in use. From 2010, these are also available in blue version of vehicle registration plates started in 1973.
The international vehicle registration code for Japan is J.

Vehicle registration plates of Japan History Origins and early formats In the late 19th century, automobiles were first imported to Japan, with the initial recorded vehicle—a Benz Velo—arriving in 1898 for use by a Tokyo physician, but no formal registration or numbering system existed at that time. Systematic vehicle identification emerged in response to growing traffic incidents on public roads, leading to the first mandatory requirement for number plates in 1907 under Tokyo Prefecture regulations. These early plates consisted solely of four-digit Arabic numerals, displayed on black backgrounds with white characters, either painted directly onto the vehicle body or affixed as rectangular metal or wooden tags at prominent front and rear positions as directed by the Tokyo Metropolitan Police.[4][5][6] Initially limited to Tokyo, this format prioritized basic identifiability for a small number of affluent owners, as automobiles numbered fewer than 1,000 nationwide by 1910. Other prefectures adopted similar numeric systems independently in the ensuing years, without unified national standards, reflecting decentralized authority under the Home Ministry. By 1927, a more standardized approach incorporated prefecture names alongside the numeric identifier—such as "Tokyo 1234"—to distinguish regional registrations amid rising vehicle ownership, which exceeded 20,000 by the early 1930s. Plates remained simple, often hand-painted or embossed on thin metal sheets, with dimensions approximately 155 mm high by 310 mm wide, lacking color coding or reflective materials.[7][5] Pre-World War II formats evolved minimally, adding occasional vehicle type indicators for commercial or official use, but retained the core numeric-prefecture structure to facilitate taxation and enforcement under the Road Act amendments. Wartime shortages shifted production to rudimentary stamped or wooden plates, prioritizing durability over aesthetics, as civilian vehicle numbers stagnated around 100,000 amid resource constraints.[4][8]Post-war developments and standardization Following the end of World War II and the onset of the Allied occupation in 1945, the Supreme Commander for the Allied Powers (SCAP) mandated the registration and plating of motor vehicles to regulate traffic and ensure accountability on public roads. A directive issued on March 15, 1946, required all motor vehicles and trailers to display license plates at all times, marking an early post-war effort to restore order amid Japan's devastated infrastructure and limited vehicle fleet. During the occupation period (1945–1952), plates were issued starting in 1948, often featuring simple numeric or alphanumeric formats on metal backgrounds, with examples from 1948–1949 showing black bases with painted markings, reflecting resource constraints and administrative control by occupation authorities.[9][10] With the restoration of Japanese sovereignty under the Treaty of San Francisco in 1952, vehicle registration reverted to national oversight, though plates remained largely decentralized, managed by prefectural authorities with varying formats influenced by pre-war traditions and local needs. Diplomatic plates, for instance, were standardized in white-on-blue design from 1952, featuring a hiragana character followed by numerals to denote embassies or consulates. Commercial and private vehicles often used embossed aluminum or steel plates with prefecture-specific identifiers, such as kanji or hiragana for regions, combined with serial numbers, but lacked uniform national coding for vehicle classes or dimensions. This era saw rapid growth in vehicle ownership—from approximately 1.4 million registered vehicles in 1955 to 3.7 million by 1965—driven by economic recovery, necessitating more systematic administration to handle increasing numbers and types of automobiles, trucks, and motorcycles.[11][12] Standardization accelerated in the early 1960s amid booming industrialization and urbanization, with the Ministry of Transport (predecessor to the modern Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism) implementing a national format by 1961 that included a classification number for vehicle type, followed by the prefecture's name or abbreviation, a hiragana character, and a serial number. This culminated in August 1962 with the introduction of the "normal series" for passenger vehicles over 660 cc, featuring reflective green characters on a white background, sized at 330 mm by 165 mm, alongside trade and foreign travel variants in similar green-on-white schemes using Western numerals where applicable. Smaller vehicles received black-on-yellow plates, while commercial uses adopted white-on-green reverses, establishing color coding by ownership type to enhance visibility and enforcement. These reforms, including vehicle class indicators (initially implicit, formalized with two-digit codes by 1967), addressed inconsistencies in pre-1962 local designs, improving interoperability across prefectures and supporting Japan's expanding road network. Foreign travel plates with Latin characters were officially issued from 1964 to facilitate international tourism and exports.[3][11][13]Key reforms from 1970 onward In November 1970, optional illuminated license plates, known as jikō-shiki (字光式), were introduced for private passenger vehicles to enhance nighttime visibility, particularly in snowy regions like Hokkaido where snow accumulation obscured standard plates. These plates feature translucent green characters on a white background, backlit by internal lamps powered from the vehicle's electrical system, and were developed by the automotive parts company World Auto Plate before nationwide availability.[14][15] From May 1998, the vehicle classification code on the upper line of standard plates expanded from two to three digits for certain categories, allowing for increased capacity to assign unique identifiers as vehicle registrations grew. This adjustment, applied to codes for larger-engine cars and other classes, addressed the nearing exhaustion of two-digit combinations without altering the overall hiragana-based class system or serial numbering.[11] In August 2013, the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism approved local license plate numbers for specific areas, such as Morioka and Hiraizumi, marking the second phase of regional introductions to support local identity and tourism. By October 2018, this evolved into special plates featuring designs highlighting 41 regional motifs, including local scenery and symbols, issued upon municipal proposals to promote revitalization and foster community pride.[16] As of January 2025, to accommodate the depletion of available numeric combinations for custom (wishes) plates amid rising demand for personalized registrations, the serial number format began incorporating Roman alphabetic letters alongside digits and hiragana, marking the first major encoding expansion since 1998. This change applies primarily to optional vanity plates while maintaining backward compatibility for standard issuances.[17]Physical Design Dimensions, materials, and reflectivity Standard vehicle registration plates in Japan measure 330 mm in width by 165 mm in height for passenger cars, light trucks, and similar medium-sized vehicles.[1] Larger plates, used on heavy trucks, buses, and certain specialized vehicles, measure 440 mm in width by 220 mm in height.[18]Motorcycle plates are smaller, typically 250 mm in width by 125 mm in height, while kei vehicle plates conform to the medium standard of 330 mm by 165 mm. These dimensions ensure compatibility with vehicle mounting points and compliance with visibility requirements under the Road Transport Vehicle Act.[19] Plates are constructed from aluminum or other metal bases, often combined with transparent protective layers, to provide durability against environmental wear, corrosion, and mechanical stress.[20] The metal substrate must possess sufficient thickness—typically around 1-2 mm—and strength to withstand normal use without deformation or peeling.[21] Characters and borders are embossed into the metal surface, then filled with paint or enamel for contrast, adhering to color standards specified in national regulations.[1] Reflectivity is achieved through the application of retro-reflective sheeting or coatings on the plate surface, mandated to enhance nighttime visibility for law enforcement and other drivers.[20] This material reflects incident light back toward its source, such as vehicle headlights, at angles up to 0.2 degrees deviation, meeting performance criteria outlined in quality standards from the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism.[21] Plates must maintain reflectivity over their service life, with testing protocols verifying resistance to fading, abrasion, and chemical exposure.[22] Non-compliant alterations, such as covers that absorb or distort reflection, are prohibited under vehicle inspection rules.[23]Color coding by vehicle type Japanese vehicle registration plates employ a color coding system to distinguish between private and commercial use, as well as between standard and lightweight (kei) vehicles. Special use plates Vehicles owned by personnel with the United States military in Japan under the Status of Forces Agreement (SOFA) have a "Y" on white plates, or an "A" on yellow plates, where the hiragana character is normally displayed. Earlier versions of the SOFA license plate displayed the letters "K", "M", "G", "H" and "E". These letters indicated the car was imported into Japan under SOFA and was left-hand drive. Today, cars with an "E" indicate that Japanese sales tax has not been paid and the vehicle will not remain in Japan when the military member departs. Since the 1980s, military commands have discouraged servicemembers assigned to Japan from shipping their vehicles from the U.S. into the country, so this is rarely seen. Military members who retire in Japan use the hiragana "よ". Many opt to purchase second-hand domestic vehicles through used car dealers off-base, and from other servicemembers departing Japan at on-base "lemon lots".[2] The official imperial cars have a special number plate with the kanji 皇 and a one-digit number below. Out of country plates Because the Japanese writing system, particularly the kana, is unique to Japan, standard-issue Japanese license plates are considered unacceptable for international travel. If motorists wish to take their vehicles abroad with them, the Ministry of Transport will issue them with plates with the hiragana and kanji scripts replaced by Roman letters. The hiragana prefix is replaced by a Kunrei-shiki romanization of that character. The kanji prefecture/office code is replaced by a two- or three-letter abbreviation, the first two letters representing the prefecture, the third (if present) representing the office within the prefecture. All the numerical portions of the plate remain the same. Using the example given above, the plate (足立50Kき21-41) would then read TKA 50K KI 21-41 (TKA for Tokyo Adachi). Vehicle class code system In addition to plate size and color, Japanese plates since 1962 have identified the vehicle type (signified by length, width and height as well as engine displacement) by use of a vehicle class code signified by a number on the top line of the plate for all vehicles with three or more wheels.[3] The vehicle class code system is not widely understood outside of Japan, and as a result, Japanese vehicles displaying "vanity" Japanese license plates at overseas shows and events are often unwittingly misrepresented. Motorbikes and other two wheeled vehicles do not use this system. In 1967, double digit vehicle codes were introduced for the first time, once all previous possible combinations were used. For example, 3 would become 33, and then 34, 35, etc. This began in October 1967 in the more populous prefectures initially for the most common vehicle classes such as 3 and 5. In April 1971 all vehicle codes become double digit across the country. Double digit codes finally stopped being issued in 1999. By the early 1970s, three-wheeled passenger cars were no longer in production and some prefectures began to issue the double digit codes 77, 78 and 79 as an 'overflow series' for passenger cars 2000cc and under. This practice stopped in 1999.
