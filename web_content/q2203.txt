--- [Murder of Doski Azad - Wikipedia] ---
Doski Azad was a 23-year-old transgender woman living in Duhok, Iraqi Kurdistan. She was a make-up artist and internet personality who was open about her transition on social media. On 28 January 2022, she was shot and killed by her estranged brother, Chakdar Azad, in what has been described as a transphobic honour killing; her murder was discovered three days later, on 31 January 2022.
It is reported that Doski had left her family five years prior to the incident, but her family continued to pursue and intimidate her. The incident received wide coverage in international media and was met with condemnation by various organisations, including human rights groups, LGBT organisations, and foreign diplomatic missions. While the Kurdish authorities said that they issued an arrest warrant and worked with Interpol to find the perpetrator, her murderer has still not been found to date.
The murder of Doski Azad has brought attention to the issue of transphobic violence and discrimination faced by transgender individuals in Iraq and Kurdistan, as well as the need for better protection and support for transgender individuals who may be at risk of honour killings. Various advocacy groups have called for urgent action to address transphobic violence and discrimination and to provide support and resources for transgender individuals.

The murder of Doski Azad was the January 2022 killing of a 23-year-old Kurdish makeup artist and social media personality who identified as a transgender woman, found bound and shot dead in a ditch near Duhok in Iraq's Kurdistan Region.[1][2] Azad, originally from a conservative family, had left home around 2018 after beginning to live openly in her identified gender, facing repeated death threats from male relatives who viewed her choices as dishonorable.[3][4] Kurdish police investigations identified her brother as the perpetrator in an apparent honor killing, a practice criminalized by regional law since 2004 but often inadequately prosecuted due to cultural pressures and weak enforcement.[2][5] The brother fled Iraq shortly after the crime, and as of 2024, no arrest or trial had occurred, underscoring persistent risks to individuals defying familial and societal norms on gender in the area.[6][4] The case drew international condemnation and local activism, highlighting how honor-based violence, including against those with non-conforming gender expressions, continues despite formal legal bans, with perpetrators frequently escaping accountability through flight or influence.[7][8] Azad's public persona as a makeup artist on platforms like Instagram amplified awareness of her story post-mortem, fueling debates on cultural clashes between individual autonomy and tribal honor codes in Iraqi Kurdistan.[3] Victim's Background Early Life and Family Doski Azad was born around 1998 or 1999 in the Duhok Governorate of Iraqi Kurdistan, within a conservative Kurdish family adhering to traditional and religiously influenced norms.[3][9] Her upbringing occurred in an environment where deviation from expected gender roles was met with severe familial disapproval, reflecting broader cultural pressures in the region.[9]From a young age, Azad experienced estrangement from her family due to her gender identity, with rejection beginning in her early teenage years; this forced her to achieve independence and support herself financially.[3] Approximately five years prior to her death—around age 18—she permanently left the family home in response to persistent threats, primarily from male relatives, relocating to central Duhok to live autonomously.[9][3]Azad's family included multiple siblings, notably at least two brothers: her estranged brother Chakdar Azad, who had resided in Western Europe for eight years before returning to commit her murder, and another brother who reported the killing to authorities.[3][9] No public details exist regarding her parents' identities or specific roles in her early life, though the household's conservative dynamics contributed to the threats she received from male family members.[3]During her teenage years post-rejection, Azad worked as a makeup artist in Duhok, serving clients at salons and brides' homes, which allowed her to build a local reputation and sustain herself amid familial abandonment.[3] Transition and Public Identity Doski Azad, born male, socially transitioned to live and present publicly as a woman in Duhok, Iraqi Kurdistan, where she identified as transgender.[3] She worked professionally as a makeup artist, specializing in beauty services that complemented her feminine appearance and public persona.[4][2]Azad's visibility as an openly transgender individual was uncommon in the region, contributing to her recognition within limited local circles despite the prevailing cultural conservatism.[6] No public records detail medical aspects of her transition, such as hormone therapy or surgery, but her social presentation as female drew familial opposition and external threats prior to her death.[3] This public embrace of her identity positioned her as a rare example of transgender visibility in Iraqi Kurdistan, though it heightened her vulnerability in a society where gender nonconformity often faces violent backlash.[4] Circumstances of the Murder Prior Threats and Family Estrangement Doski Azad had estranged herself from her family approximately five years prior to her death, having left the family home in Duhok, Iraqi Kurdistan, due to conflicts arising from her transgender identity.[4] She established an independent life as a makeup artist and online personality, no longer residing with relatives despite ongoing familial ties in the region.[3]Friends and associates reported that Azad faced repeated death threats from male family members and distant relatives within her tribe, stemming directly from her public embrace of her transgender identity.[3][10] These threats were continuous and explicit, prompting her to live in hiding within Duhok to evade potential violence from her kin.[11] Local activists noted that such familial in Germany, reportedly maintained estrangement but harbored resentment tied to these familial grievances; he returned to Iraq approximately two weeks before the murder, aligning with the pattern of threats she had endured.[12] Despite her efforts to build autonomy, the persistent hostility from her family underscored the risks transgender individuals face in regions governed by honor-based customs, where reconciliation was unattainable.[13] The Killing and Discovery On January 31, 2022, the body of Doski Azad, a 23-year-old transgender woman, was discovered in the village of Mangesh, approximately 12 miles north of Duhok in the Kurdistan Region of Iraq.[14][15]Duhok police received a call that afternoon reporting the location of the body in a rural area outside the city.[4]Police investigations indicated that Azad had been shot to death by her brother, Chakdar Azad, at a site just outside Duhok prior to the body being left in Mangesh.[3] Chakdar Azad reportedly fled the country immediately after the killing, evading initial capture.[4][13] The incident was classified by authorities as a targeted familial homicide, with the brother identified as the sole suspect based on preliminary evidence and witness reports.[16][8] Perpetrator's Actions Chakdar Azad, the brother of Doski Azad, traveled from Germany to the Kurdistan Region of Iraq with the intent to kill her, Kurdistan Honor killings in Iraqi Kurdistan refer to premeditated murders, typically perpetrated by male family members, targeting women or individuals perceived to have violated familial or tribal honor codes, such as through extramarital relationships, refusal of arranged marriages, or behaviors deemed sexually immoral.[23] These acts are rooted in patriarchal tribal customs that prioritize collective family reputation over individual rights, often resulting in victims being killed by brothers, fathers, or uncles.[24] In the Kurdistan Region of Iraq (KRI), comprising Erbil, Sulaymaniyah, and Duhok governorates, such killings disproportionately affect women and girls, with perpetrators rarely facing severe punishment due to cultural tolerance and inadequate policing.[25]Empirical data indicate a persistent prevalence, with estimates of 50 to 60 women killed annually in the KRI under the pretext of honor between 2010 and 2023, though underreporting likely inflates the true figure due to familial cover-ups and official misclassification as suicides or accidents.[26] Campaigners reported a worrying increase in 2021–2022, including high-profile cases like the March 7, 2022, murder of a young woman in Erbil by relatives over alleged infidelity, and another in Duhok's Khanke camp involving a mother of ten.[27][23] Incidents occur roughly every other week, concentrated in urban areas like Erbil and Sulaymaniyah, where modernization clashes with traditional norms, exacerbating tensions.[24] Human rights monitors attribute the rise to socioeconomic factors, including poverty and unemployment, which reinforce tribal authority over state law.[28]Legally, the KRI amended Iraq's penal code in 2002 to reclassify honor killings as standard murder, removing prior mitigating provisions that treated them as lesser offenses punishable by fines or short sentences.[29] The 2011 Anti-Domestic Violence Law further criminalizes related abuses, mandating shelters and investigations, yet enforcement remains weak, with convictions often resulting in suspended sentences or acquittals via tribal mediation.[30]Amnesty International documented multiple 2023–2024 cases entrenched tribalism in Kurdish society, where "washing shame" through violence restores family standing, particularly in conservative rural areas of Duhok and parts of Sulaymaniyah.[31] Advocacy --- [Kurdish authorities silent as transgender violence increases] ---
Breadcrumb A transgender woman has been killed by her brother in the Iraqi Kurdistan region amid the indifference and impunity of the Kurdish authorities. The murder of the 23-year-old transgender woman in Iraqi Kurdistan has nonetheless provoked an outcry among activists and civil society organisations. Doski Azad, 23, was reportedly killed by her brother, Chakdar Azad, on 28 January. Her body was found three days later in the village of Mangesh, around 12 miles north of the city centre of Duhok, after another brother of the victim called the police. "In a society rooted in heteronormativity, LGBTQ+ people are harmed by their own families and face regular abuse. As sad as it is, ‘honour’ killings are alarmingly common in Kurdish society" Azad, a make-up artist working in a beauty salon in Duhok city, had been living away from her family for over five years before she was killed. She had reportedly received multiple threats from family members over the years and was documenting her transition online at the time of her death. In conservative Kurdish society, being transgender is extremely challenging, with so-called ‘honour killing’ cases on the rise. “Our investigation so far suggests that Doski Azad was killed by her brother at a location just outside the city before he managed to flee the crime scene,” Hemin Suleiman, a spokesman for the Duhok police, told VOA. | Her brother had returned from Germany two weeks before the murder and reportedly escaped Iraqi Kurdistan two days later by driving north to Turkey, Rudaw reported. Suleiman also said that an arrest warrant had been issued for the suspect, but so far, no arrests have been made. The New Arab has repeatedly contacted Suleiman, Romio Hakari, the head of the Kurdistan Parliament’s human rights committee, Gulstan Saeed, deputy head of the committee, Kwestan Mohamad, the minister of Labour and Social Affairs in the Kurdistan Regional Government (KRG), and the spokesperson of the KRG’s human rights board, but none were available or willing to comment. Diplomatic condemnation versus KRG silence The US Consulate General in Erbil denounced the killing and called on the Kurdish authorities to investigate. “We categorically condemn this violence and the discrimination that is undoubtedly at the root of this crime. We ask the authorities to thoroughly investigate this murder and prosecute the perpetrator to the fullest extent of the law,” the US consulate wrote on Twitter. The German Consulate in Erbil on 1 February posted a photo of Doski on Twitter with a caption reading “Human dignity shall be inviolable”. The KRG and Kurdistan Parliament have remained silent on the issue. Moreover, in an online memorandum to the KRG and the Kurdish assembly, hundreds of people - including Muslim clerics, NGOs, university lecturers, and writers - condemned the statements by foreign diplomatic missions on Doski’s case as interference in the KRG’s internal affairs. “The KRI has a majority Muslim population […], but unfortunately, the diplomatic missions of superpower countries…are supporting a phenomenon which is foreign to our religion and our nation’s high values and culture,” reads part of the memo. Legal vacuum In Iraq and the Kurdistan region, there are no specific laws regarding the issue of those who change their gender identity. But legal experts told The New Arab that since the Iraqi constitution stipulates that the principles of Islamic law are a primary source of legislation, the Iraqi council of representatives, as well as the Kurdistan’s parliament, are unlikely to enact laws to protect LGBTQ+ people in the country. Islamic scholars have allowed sex reassignment surgery “for a biological need” but not “at the mere wish to change one’s sex from woman to man, or vice versa”. "In most cases [of transphobic and homophobic violence], the courts are lenient as reactionary tribal and social norms continue to overpower the judiciary" Emad Hameed, Director of the National Centre for Human Rights, a local non-governmental organization, said that Iraqi lawmakers should settle the current legal vacuum regarding transgender people. “The first reason behind such gender-based killings is related to the lack of sovereignty of the law in Iraq and the IKR,” Hameed told The New Arab. “Secondly, there is impunity by the courts towards perpetrators of so-called honour killings. In most cases, the courts are lenient as reactionary tribal and social norms continue to overpower the judiciary.” In 2021, human rights groups documented that Iraqi security forces had arbitrarily arrested LGBTQ+ people based solely on their appearance, subjecting them to “ill-treatment including torture, forced anal exams, and sexual violence, in police custody”, according to Human Rights Watch (HRW). “Security forces also physically, verbally and sexually harass LGBTQ+ people at checkpoints,” a memorandum submitted to the United Nations Human Rights Committee in January 2022 stated. Tanya Kamal Darwesh, director of the Rasan Organisation, a Sulaymaniyah-based human rights group, said that gender-based violence and killings have a history in Iraq and Iraqi Kurdistan, but have recently increased. She also said such killings are normally justified under the guise of defending norms and culture. "Unfortunately almost all crimes are committed against women and girls, Doski has been killed because she has changed her sex into a girl, if she had transformed her sex into a boy she might have been granted a major award,” Darwesh told The New Arab. “The Kurdish executive and legislative authorities should shoulder a big responsibility to consider the danger of repeating such crimes; otherwise, their credibility would be questionable.” Zhiar Ali, a Netherlands-based Kurdish LGBT+ activist and founder of Yeksani, an LGBTQ+ rights network, told The New Arab that threats against the community largely emanate from the tribal values of Kurdish society, which are hard to overcome. “In a society rooted in heteronormativity, LGBTQ+ people are a lot of the time harmed by their own families, facing regular abuse. As sad as it is, ‘honour’ killings are alarmingly common in Kurdish society,” Ali said. “Civil society organisations in Kurdistan do not have a positive impact; there barely create any advocacy campaigns to help educate the wider population,” Ali added. “We are grateful for the alliance of the embassies and consulates, which were all in support of our campaign. However, we need to do more. We need to prevent the murders from happening in the first place, not launch a campaign later.” Dana Taib Menmy is an investigative freelance journalist from the Iraqi Kurdistan region writing on issues of politics, society, human rights, security, and minorities.
