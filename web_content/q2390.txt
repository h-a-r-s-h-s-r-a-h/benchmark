--- [Cindy Sherman - Wikipedia] ---
Cynthia Morris Sherman (born January 19, 1954) is an American artist whose work consists primarily of photographic self-portraits, depicting herself in many different contexts and as various imagined characters.
Her breakthrough work is often considered to be the collection Untitled Film Stills, a series of 70 black-and-white photographs of herself evoking typical female roles in performance media (especially arthouse films and popular B-movies).




--- [Cindy Sherman - Gathering] ---
Cindy Sherman Born in 1954 in Glen Ridge, New Jersey, Cindy Sherman is counted among the most influential artists of the last half-century. Upon graduating from the State University of New York at Buffalo in 1976, Sherman moved to New York City where she began making the seminal photo series Untitled Film Stills. She has gone on to photograph and cast herself in various roles through her masterful use of costume, setting and pose. Her photographs have interrogated themes around representation and identity in contemporary media for over four decades. Solo exhibitions have been held at National Portrait Gallery, London (2020); The Broad, Los Angeles (2016); Van Abbemuseum, Eindhoven (2015); Whitney Museum of American Art, New York (2015); National Museum of Modern Art, Kyoto (2013); Tate Modern, London (2012); and Walker Art Center, Minneapolis (2012) among others. In 2012 the Museum of Modern Art, New York, organised a major retrospective of her work that travelled to San Francisco Museum of Modern Art, Walker Art Center, and Dallas Museum of Art. Sherman's photographs have been included in numerous international exhibitions, notably the Whitney Biennial (1995, 1993, 1991, 1985, 1983); the Biennale of Sydney (1990, 1984); and documenta 7 (1982). In 2013 she co-curated an exhibition for the 55th Venice Biennale.


--- [ART: WHITNEY SHOWS CINDY SHERMAN PHOTOS - The New York Times] ---
Supported by ART: WHITNEY SHOWS CINDY SHERMAN PHOTOS THE Cindy Sherman traveling show has reached the Whitney Museum of American Art. It has exotic characters - sultans, knights, matadors, extraterrestrials, movie actresses - all of them and none of them the artist herself. Ms. Sherman has been billed as a great deconstructor, a simulationist par excellence, a heroine of the post-modern attack on the hero, an originator of the post-modern attack on originality, one of the select few who could have been a great artist in any medium. This astounding performer, still only 33 years old, pulls out all the stops of her camera at the Whitney. The exhibition includes 84 photographs from 1975 to the present. It begins with a selection of pictures from the last few years, some of them larger and more grisly than anything Ms. Sherman has done before. In a recent diptych, a face like a science-fiction creature with fake eyes and a drooling mouth seems vulnerable and monstrous because of the way it is squeezed against the camera. Another work features a figure who looks like something out of ''The Arabian Nights.'' She has fake teeth and fake breasts, and she occupies a fake tent, but the image seems not fake but hyperreal. After the first gallery, the installation is chronological. There are several small black-and-white ''film stills'' evoking stock characters in old melodramas and suspense films that established the artist's reputation in the late 1970's. Her first color photos were done in 1980. By 1982 the dynamics of her work were set. Her figures are close to the camera, and they exist in non-spaces: there is no more than a suggestion of decor; the backgrounds are often dark. Our view is always partial. Figures are less individuals than types. Although we appear to be encountering them in intimate moments, the pictures are detached and distant. There is a fascination with light, which, like some of Ms. Sherman's poses, can suggest the Old Masters. But there is no trace of the faith from which Old Master light often poured. By 1984 the photographs are no longer matted. Ms. Sherman begins using fake body parts, and her figures suggest characters in fairy tales and myths. After a series based on fashion, she has moved in the direction of ugliness and the grotesque. In the last couple of years, she has invented an assortment of freaks and crazies, which is not that surprising given her view that dissociation and multiple personalities are the norm. In a recent work, glutted with frankfurters and ketchup and intimations of sex and blood, we are in a world of ooze and slime in which nothing is firm. Ms. Sherman's photographs are performances. Peter Schjeldahl notes in his appreciation in the catalogue that the artist is ''actress, scenarist, cinematographer, lighting designer, makeup artist and costumer.'' She seems to change roles as easily as changing clothes. She can be as glamorous as Marilyn Monroe or as grisly as the rapist in ''The Virgin Spring.'' Some images fill the frame; in others she is all but absent. Suspension of disbelief is not allowed. The theatricality is so conspicuous that attention is always drawn from what is represented to how it is represented - and to the fierce clarity of the intelligence behind the picture. Despite their apparent appeal to the senses, Ms. Sherman's photographs are heavily Conceptual. In the tradition of Conceptual artists that includes the very different Hans Haacke, Ms. Sherman has no interest in the artistic self and the interior life of the art object. Her touch is absent: the prints are commercially developed. What matters to her are the social and mass media conventions that help determine what the self and work of art are and can be. Her work is not introspective. Its greatest appeal is likely to be to artists and critics who believe everything must be approached in social and political terms. Related Content Advertisement

Cindy Sherman, a prominent figure in contemporary art, was born on January 19, 1954, in Glen Ridge, New Jersey. She grew to become a highly influential artist, known particularly for her photographic self-portraits in various costumes and personas. Sherman's work often challenges ideas about identity, gender, and the role of women in media. She is widely recognized for her series of Untitled Film Stills, which comprises 70 black and white photographs in which she posed in various roles. Cindy Sherman began her famous 'Untitled Film Stills' series in 1977, which she continued until 1980. In this groundbreaking work, she portrayed herself in enigmatic, movie-like stills in various costumes and posed in stereotypical female roles inspired by film noir, B-movies, and European art-house films. This series established her reputation as a significant artist exploring identity and representation in media. In 1981, Cindy Sherman held her first solo exhibition at the Metro Pictures Gallery in New York. This exhibition was crucial in cementing her status as a noteworthy contemporary artist. It featured photographs from her 'Untitled Film Stills' series, showcasing her innovative approach to self-portraiture and her critique of traditional gender roles in society. In 1995, Cindy Sherman was awarded the MacArthur Fellowship, often called the 'Genius Grant.' This prestigious award is given to individuals who have shown exceptional creativity in their work. The grant recognized Sherman's innovative and challenging artworks that explore complex themes including identity, gender, and representation, solidifying her impact on the art world. Cindy Sherman directed her first and only feature film, 'Office Killer,' released in 1997. This dark comedy-horror starring Carol Kane and Molly Ringwald was an extension of Sherman's exploration of female identity and horror. The film's narrative follows a mentally disturbed office worker who turns into a serial killer, showcasing Sherman's signature exploration of identity and transformation. In 1999, the Museum of Modern Art in New York held a major retrospective of Cindy Sherman's work. This exhibition featured pieces from throughout her career, emphasizing her influence and innovation in contemporary art. It brought together works from her various series, including 'Untitled Film Stills,' exploring the depth of her artistic investigation into identity and representation. On February 26, 2011, the Museum of Modern Art in New York City opened a comprehensive exhibition dedicated to the works of Cindy Sherman. This exhibition included 180 of her photographs, examining her role in challenging traditional norms related to gender and identity. It once again showcased her ability to reshape and question how society perceives these aspects. On May 9, 2012, Cindy Sherman's photograph, part of her 'Untitled Film Stills' series, set a record at auction. The piece sold for $3.89 million at a Christie’s auction, becoming the most expensive photograph ever sold at that time. This highlighted not only Sherman's prominence in the art world but also the significant market value of contemporary photography. In 2016, a self-titled monograph was released, detailing Cindy Sherman's extensive career in art. This comprehensive book offered insight into her creative process, her evolution as an artist, and the incredible impact of her work on contemporary photography and gender discourse. It provided both visuals and essays that explored her contributions to the art world. On June 6, 2019, Cindy Sherman had a major exhibition at the National Portrait Gallery in London. This showcased works spanning her entire career, from her early series to more recent works. Focusing on themes like identity and persona, the exhibition critically explored how Sherman has used costume, props, and prosthetics to explore and expand notions of identity and representation. --- [Year - Wikipedia] ---
A year is a unit of time based on how long it takes the Earth to orbit the Sun.  In scientific use, the tropical year (approximately 365 solar days, 5 hours, 48 minutes, 45 seconds) and the sidereal year (about 20 minutes longer) are more exact. The modern calendar year, as reckoned according to the Gregorian calendar, approximates the tropical year by using a system of leap years. 
The term 'year' is also used to indicate other periods of roughly similar duration, such as the lunar year (a roughly 354-day cycle of twelve of the Moon's phases – see lunar calendar), as well as periods loosely associated with the calendar or astronomical year, such as the seasonal year, the fiscal year, the academic year, etc.
Due to the Earth's axial tilt, the course of a year sees the passing of the seasons, marked by changes in weather, the hours of daylight, and, consequently, vegetation and soil fertility. In temperate and subpolar regions around the planet, four seasons are generally recognized: spring, summer, autumn, and winter. In tropical and subtropical regions, several geographical sectors do not present defined seasons; but in the seasonal tropics, the annual wet and dry seasons are recognized and tracked.
By extension, the term 'year' can also be applied to the time taken for the orbit of any astronomical object around its primary –  for example the Martian year of roughly 1.88 Earth years.
The term can also be used in reference to any long period or cycle, such as the Great Year.
