--- [Max Hastings - Wikipedia] ---
Sir Max Hugh Macdonald Hastings (; born 28 December 1945) is a British journalist and military historian who has worked as a foreign correspondent for the BBC, editor-in-chief of The Daily Telegraph, and editor of the Evening Standard. He is also the author of thirty books, most significantly histories, which have won several major awards. Hastings currently writes a bimonthly column for Bloomberg Opinion and contributes to The Times and The Sunday Times.

SIDELIGHTS: In Bomber Command: The British Bombing of Germany in World War II Max Hastings examines the British campaign of "area bombing" against Ger- man cities during World War II. Begun in 1940 when British intelligence saw no other way of defeating the quickly-advancing German forces, the Bomber Command's five-year air offensive resulted not only in the destruction of many German cities and the killing of some 600,000 German civilians, but also left over 50,000 British aircrew dead. Such casualties caused Geoffrey Wheatcroft in a Spectator review of Bomber Command to call the bombings "the greatest war crime of the Second World War." Although C.M. Woodhouse of the Times Literary Supplement faulted Hastings for what he termed "sometimes inadequate and sometimes misleading references," he called Bomber Command "a brilliant tour de force for a man born after the events he describes." Michael Howard in the New Republic offered similar praise, calling the work "careful without being dull, vivid without being overwritten," and "popular history at its best." Hastings's historical account Das Reich: Resistance and the March of the 2nd Panzer Division through France, June 1944 also evoked admiration from Woodhouse, who judged the "well-documented combination of oral history with documentary records" even "maturer than [Bomber Command]" because its author is "less passionately concerned to prove a thesis." In the Spectator, Richard Cobb noted that "some questions do remain unanswered in this crisp and enjoyable narrative," but Hastings displays a "skillful use of a series of personal case histories," by which he reduces "Hitler's corps d'elite" to "feasible persons rather than abstract machines." Sometimes described as the best source to date written about the Falklands War, The Battle for the Falklands brought Hastings and coauthor Jenkins a prestigious award and generally favorable reviews. In Christopher Wain's assessment for the Listener, the account is "outstanding" in that it "pulls together the complex strands of the history of the dispute … and weaves them into a pattern which has few obvious flaws." According to New York Times reviewer Drew Middleton, who called the account "war correspondence in the great tradition of Bill Stoneman and Ernie Pyle," The Battle for the Falklands "probably will endure as the standard history of the campaign because of the happy combination of two authors, each a master in his field." Neal Ascherson's opinion in the Observer was less glowing. He dubbed The Battle for the Falklands "a meticulous war history," but thought the book lacked Hastings's "natural bounce." In the consideration of Charles Carter in the Los Angeles Times, "the book is crammed with facts, coherently told and immensely aided by maps," but "the writing declines to merely competent." Carter noted that an "inherent weakness of such a book is its form, a journalistic effort relying partly on the statements of anonymous interviewees," but also wrote that "the strength of this one is that the narrative is plausible and the conclusions are intelligent." As for the interviews that Carter labeled a weakness, Robert Fox writing in the Times Educational Supplement believed that the Hastings and Simon report represents "the weightiest of the new books [on the Falklands War] in content and reputation." Specifically, Fox observed that Hastings "has carried out a tour de force in interviewing nearly every key commander from all three services" involved in the war. Time reviewer Donald Morrison described The Battle for the Falklands, as "a poignant memorial," while Reid Beddow writing in the Washington Post Book World called it "a small gem of military and naval history." In his review of Overlord: D-Day and the Battle for Normandy, Michael Carver wrote in the Times Literary Supplement that Hastings's book "combines serious historical and critical comment with brilliant reportage," bringing "both the arguments between higher commanders and the fighting on the battlefield itself to life more vividly than any previous books." Middleton, again writing in the New York Times, explained that the central theme of the book is "that whenever Allied troops met Germans on anything like equal terms, the Germans nearly always prevailed." Hastings explains that the German army represented one of the finest military forces the world has ever known. He warns that any future defense of Europe will need to look not to the Allied invasion of Normandy for a model, but to the resourceful defensive tactics of the German forces who fought "in the face of all the odds against them and in spite of their own demented Fuehrer." Such a no-nonsense approach led Detroit News reviewer Al Stark to conclude: "I began to see [Hastings's treatment of Normandy] for what it is, an unvarnished look at one of the greatest military missions of all time…. It is refreshing to find [a book] that describes Normandy straight up, with all the human failure, and still does not diminish the achievement." Hastings told CA: "I am a journalist and military historian, writing mostly about the Second World War. Both my parents and both grandfathers were writers, so I didn't have much choice about how to make a living. As a teenager I thought of being a soldier, but a brief spell with the British Army's Parachute Regiment convinced me that I was too undisciplined to ‘hack it.’ I dropped out of Oxford University when offered a job on the London Evening Standard, and thereafter spent most of my twenties as a foreign correspondent, reporting a lot of wars, including Yom Kippur, Vietnam, the Falklands, Angola, and Cyprus, and living for almost two years, 1967-68, in the United States. "I wrote two pretty bad young reporter's books, then in 1976 I moved to Ireland to start writing seriously. The outcome was my first substantial success, Bomber Command, a study of the Royal Air Force strategic offensive against Germany in World War II. Thereafter I wrote Das Reich about the French Resistance, Overlord about D-Day, and had almost finished The Korean War, when suddenly and very unexpectedly I was invited to edit the British Daily Telegraph newspaper. I stayed a newspaper editor for sixteen years, then got back to proper writing with Armageddon: The Battle for Germany, 1944-45, about the last year of the war in Europe, and its companion volume Nemesis, about the last year of the war against Japan. "I live in the countryside about seventy miles west of London and am very keen on gardening, hunting, and fishing. I still do some journalism for the Daily Mail and Guardian, and review books for the Sunday Times. It has been en exceptionally lucky, happy life!" Sir Max Hugh Macdonald Hastings (born 28 December 1945) is a British journalist, author, militaryhistorian, and former newspaper editor, distinguished for his extensive reporting from war zones including Vietnam and the Falklands War, as well as his critically acclaimed books on 20th-century conflicts.[1][2]Hastings began his career as a foreign correspondent, covering major conflicts and earning awards such as Journalist of the Year and Reporter of the Year for his Falklands coverage in 1982.[3] He served as editor of the Evening Standard from 1976 to 1986, followed by editor-in-chief of The Daily Telegraph from 1986 to 1995, during which he transformed the paper's circulation and editorial stance.[1][4] Later, he edited the Evening Standard again from 1996 to 2002.[5]His authorship spans over 25 books, primarily on military history, with notable works including Bomber Command (1979), which won the Somerset Maugham Prize, and comprehensive accounts of World War II such as Overlord (1984) and All Hell Let Loose (2011).[6][3] Hastings was knighted in 2002 for services to journalism, reflecting his influence across print media and historical scholarship.[7][8] Early Life and Education Family Background and Childhood Max Hastings was born on 28 December 1945 in London, England, to parents deeply embedded in journalism and writing.[2] His father, Macdonald Hastings, was a war correspondent for Picture Post during World War II, later becoming a broadcaster, author, and adventurer known for serialized exploits such as serving as the "Eagle Special Investigator" in the 1950s comic Eagle, where he undertook daring stunts including acting as a target for a circus knife-thrower.[9] His mother, Anne Scott-James, was a pioneering figure in British journalism, appointed as the first woman's editor at Picture Post in 1941 and later editor of Harper's Bazaar from 1945, before transitioning to columns in the Daily Mail and gardening writing.[9] Both parents exemplified a journalistic ethos shaped by mid-20th-century media, with Macdonald's career emphasizing frontline reporting and Anne's focusing on fashion and lifestyle editorial roles, though their marriage was characterized by frequent discord.[9][10]Raised amid this volatile household, Hastings was primarily cared for by his nanny, Jessie Strafford, who joined the family at his birth and remained for 18 years, providing stability amid parental absences and conflicts.[9] By age three or four, he recalled scarcely recognizing his mother due to her professional commitments, highlighting early emotional distance in family dynamics.[9] At six, he was captivated by his father's Eagle persona, which brought vivid tales of peril into their home, fostering an early fascination with adventure and reporting.[9] The household included Macdonald's extensive personal arsenal, reflecting his wartime experiences, which Hastings accessed as a child; notable incidents included emptying a Luger pistol into a garden seat and, at age 11, firing it at the television during a Perry Mason episode, causing it to explode in smoke and shards, an event that precipitated a major family confrontation.[10][9]These formative years exposed Hastings to a blend of journalistic glamour and domestic turmoil, with his parents' 1962 divorce further straining relations, particularly with his mother, whom he later described as persistently critical.[9][10] Despite the challenges, the environment instilled an affinity for storytelling and risk, evident in his idolization of his father's exploits over his mother's more reserved demeanor.[9] Formal Education and Early Influences Hastings attended Charterhouse School as a scholar, where he received a classical education typical of leading British public schools of the era, emphasizing discipline and intellectual rigor.[1] This boarding school experience, common among his social class, instilled early habits of independence and exposure to historical narratives through extracurricular reading and debates.[2]In 1964, he enrolled at University College, Oxford, as an exhibitioner, pursuing studies in history amid the vibrant intellectual environment of the university.[1][2] However, after completing only one year, Hastings departed without a degree, driven by an impatience with academic routines and a desire to engage directly with real-world events through journalism.[1] This decision reflected his skepticism toward prolonged theoretical study, favoring empirical observation over ivory-tower abstraction—a pattern evident in his later career prioritizing primary sources and eyewitness accounts.[11]His early influences were profoundly shaped by family, as the son of journalist Macdonald Hastings, a war correspondent for the Daily Express, and grandson of writers, immersing him in a household where storytelling and factual reporting were daily norms.[1] His father provided pivotal guidance around age 15 or 16, advising persistence in writing despite setbacks and warning against complacency in professional ambition.[12] These familial precedents, coupled with exposure to his parents' demanding careers—his mother Anne Scott-James also a prominent journalist—fostered Hastings' precocious commitment to narrative-driven truth-seeking over formal credentials.[10] Raised partly by a nanny amid his parents' "difficult" dynamics during postwar austerity, he developed resilience that later underpinned his field reporting.[10]This blend of institutional grounding and domestic imperatives propelled Hastings toward immediate professional immersion, bypassing extended academia for apprenticeships in newsrooms, where he honed skills in distilling complex realities into accessible prose.[11] Journalistic Career Entry into Reporting and Early Assignments Hastings began his journalistic career as a researcher for BBC television historical documentaries in 1963–1964, while still a student.[2] After dropping out of University College, Oxford, in 1965 following one year of study, he joined the London Evening Standard as a reporter, initially contributing to its gossip column during university vacations before taking a full-time role.[13][14] In 1967–1968, he paused newspaper work to participate in a World Press Institute fellowship in the United States, which informed his first book, America 1968: The Fire This Time, published when he was 23.[1]By 1968, Hastings had advanced to foreign correspondent for the Evening Standard, a position he held until 1970, while also freelancing for BBC Television's Twenty-Four Hours current affairs program.[2][1] His early assignments focused on international hotspots, including reporting from the Biafran War (1967–1970) and multiple trips to Vietnam during the war's later stages, where he covered combat operations and political developments.[15] These dispatches marked his transition to war correspondence, emphasizing on-the-ground observation amid the conflicts' chaos.[1]Throughout his twenties, Hastings reported from more than 60 countries, accumulating experience in 11 wars, including Northern Ireland's Troubles, which honed his skills in rapid, firsthand analysis under pressure.[15][5] This period established his reputation for empirical, unvarnished accounts, drawing on direct access to events rather than secondary sources.[1] War Correspondence and Field Reporting Hastings commenced his career as a war correspondent in 1967, initially covering the Nigerian Civil War in Biafra, where he reported on the humanitarian crisis and combat operations for British newspapers.[16] By 1968, he had embedded with U.S. forces in Vietnam, witnessing the Tet Offensive and producing on-the-ground dispatches that highlighted tactical engagements and the war's escalating intensity; he later recalled being part of a group of foreign journalists hosted by President Lyndon Johnson in January 1968 amid the conflict's turning point.[17] His Vietnam reporting, broadcast via BBC television and print outlets, emphasized frontline soldier experiences over strategic analysis, drawing from direct exposure to ambushes and air operations.[18]Throughout the 1970s, Hastings expanded his field assignments to include the Troubles in Northern Ireland, where he documented urban skirmishes and sectarian violence; the Cambodian incursion amid the Vietnam spillover; the Yom Kippur War in the Middle East, reporting from Israeli frontlines; the Turkish invasion of Cyprus in 1974; and the Rhodesian Bush War, capturing guerrilla tactics and counterinsurgency efforts.[15] These dispatches, often for the Daily Telegraph and BBC, totaled coverage of at least eight conflicts before age 30, prioritizing eyewitness accounts of infantry maneuvers and logistical challenges over official briefings.[16]In 1982, during the Falklands War, Hastings served as a pooled reporter with British task force units, marching ("yomping") with Royal Marines and Paras across the islands' terrain; he became the first journalist to enter Port Stanley on June 14 following Argentine surrender, filing immediate reports on the battle's culmination and British casualties, which informed his co-authored book The Battle for the Falklands.[19] His on-site observations critiqued amphibious landings at San Carlos and the fog of command, based on interviews with exhausted troops amid harsh weather and limited embeds.[20] Across these 11 wars spanning four decades, Hastings' reporting—totaling over 60 countries—relied on mobility with forward units, though he later reflected on the personal toll and ethical tensions of proximity to combat, as detailed in his memoir Going to the Wars.[15][16] Newspaper Editorships and Leadership Roles Hastings was appointed editor of The Daily Telegraph in February 1986 by proprietor Conrad Black, who sought to revitalize the conservative broadsheet amid competitive pressures from tabloids and other dailies.[8] He served in this role until 1989, then became editor-in-chief from 1990 to 1995, overseeing editorial direction and expansion efforts that included bolstering foreign coverage and opinion pieces aligned with the paper's traditional Tory leanings.[1][2] During his tenure, circulation stabilized around 1 million daily, and he received the British Press Awards' Editor of the Year in 1988 for enhancing journalistic standards.[1]In 1996, Hastings returned to the Evening Standard as editor, a position he held until his retirement in February 2002, succeeding Louis Kirwan and focusing on local London issues alongside national politics in the afternoon paper.[1] Under his leadership, the publication emphasized investigative reporting and cultural commentary, though it faced circulation challenges typical of evening newspapers in the digital era's onset, hovering near 400,000 copies daily. He later reflected on these experiences in his 2002 memoirEditor: A Memoir, critiquing ownership influences and the demands of maintaining editorial independence.[1]Beyond direct editorships, Hastings held broader leadership positions in British journalism, including membership on the Press Complaints Commission from 1991 onward, where he contributed to self-regulatory standards amid debates over press ethics.[21] His roles underscored a commitment to rigorous reporting over sensationalism, though critics noted his conservative biases shaped coverage of events like the Gulf War and domestic policy.[2] Military History Writings World War II-Focused Works Hastings's first major work on World War II, Bomber Command (1979), examines the RAF's strategic bombing campaign against Germany from 1940 to 1945, drawing on interviews with over 200 survivors and archival sources to highlight the human and strategic dimensions.[22] The book details the high attrition rates—over 56,000 British and Commonwealth aircrew killed—and critiques the Air Staff's and Arthur Harris's emphasis on area bombing as inefficient and morally costly, while absolving individual crews of blame for executing orders.[23] It portrays the campaign's evolution from early ineffective raids to precision efforts like the Dambusters, underscoring technological advancements alongside the devastating impact on German civilians, estimated at 600,000 deaths.[24] Reception praised its balanced narrative, interweaving personal accounts with policy analysis, though some veterans contested its severity toward leadership.[22]In Overlord: D-Day and the Battle for Normandy (1984), Hastings provides a tactical and operational history of Operation Overlord from planning through the Normandy breakout in August 1944, incorporating perspectives from Allied and German participants based on diaries, letters, and post-war accounts.[25] The narrative challenges romanticized views by emphasizing logistical failures, high casualties—over 425,000 Allied and 240,000 German in the campaign—and the tenacity of German defenses under Rommel and Kluge, portraying the battles as a protracted slog rather than swift triumph.[26] Hastings highlights command errors, such as Montgomery's cautious advances, and the role of air superiority in enabling the eventual Falaise Pocket encirclement.[27] Critics acclaimed it for its vivid, multi-sided depiction, with Kirkus Reviews calling it an "exceptional" stand-alone account.[25]Armageddon: The Battle for Germany, 1944-1945 (2004) chronicles the war's endgame from the Ardennes Offensive through the Rhine crossings and Soviet advance to Berlin, integrating Western Allied operations with the Eastern Front's brutal dynamics, where the Red Army inflicted most German casualties.[28] Drawing on Soviet archives and veteran testimonies, Hastings estimates total German military deaths at around 5 million in this period, emphasizing the Wehrmacht's collapse amid resource shortages and Hitler's delusional orders, contrasted with Allied inter-service rivalries and the Red Army's vengeful conduct.[29] The book underscores causal factors like fuel deficiencies crippling German armor and the psychological toll of total war, avoiding moral equivalency by attributing Axis aggression as the root.[30] Reviews lauded its comprehensive scope and grim realism, with The New York Times noting its portrayal of the "slog of war."[30]Hastings's capstone WWII volume, All Hell Let Loose: The World at War, 1939-1945 (2011; published as Inferno in the US), synthesizes his prior research into a global narrative prioritizing civilian and soldier experiences over grand strategy, supported by eyewitness accounts from all theaters.[31] Spanning 700 pages, it quantifies the war's toll—50-60 million dead, including 27 million Soviet citizens—and dissects causation through empirical lenses, such as Japan's resource-driven imperialism and Hitler's racial ideology fueling conquest.[32] Hastings critiques Allied strategic bombing and Soviet atrocities without relativizing Axis crimes, arguing the war's necessity stemmed from totalitarian expansionism.[33] The work received widespread acclaim for its human focus and accessibility, earning praise as a "magisterial" overview from reviewers.[32] Analyses of Other 20th-Century Conflicts Hastings examined the Korean War (1950–1953) in his 1987 book The Korean War, presenting it as a grueling conflict initiated by North Korea's invasion of South Korea on June 25, 1950, which drew in United Nations forces led by the United States against Chinese and Soviet-backed communists. Drawing on interviews with over 200 veterans from both sides, archival documents, and official records, he detailed key battles such as the Pusan Perimeter defense in August–September 1950 and the Chinese intervention at Chosin Reservoir in November–December 1950, emphasizing the harsh terrain, logistical challenges, and high casualties—over 36,000 American deaths and millions of Korean civilian losses—that defined the war's stalemate nature.[34][35] Hastings argued that the conflict exposed the limits of U.S. airpower and firepower against ideologically motivated infantry, portraying it as the first post-World War II war where Western alliances failed to achieve outright victory, resulting in an armistice on July 27, 1953, that preserved the pre-war divide at the 38th parallel.[36][37] This analysis critiqued Truman administration decisions, such as General MacArthur's Inchon landing on September 15, 1950, for overextending advances north of the 38th parallel, which provoked massive Chinese entry and prolonged the fighting.[38]In Vietnam: An Epic Tragedy, 1945–1975 (2018), Hastings chronicled the protracted Indo-China conflict from the Japanese surrender on August 15, 1945, through the French defeat at Dien Bien Phu on May 7, 1954, to the fall of Saigon on April 30, 1975, framing it as a divisive struggle that claimed around two million Vietnamese lives alongside 58,000 American fatalities. Incorporating Vietnamese-language sources, diaries, and interviews alongside U.S. and French archives, he dissected the North Vietnamese Army's and Viet Cong's guerrilla tactics, such as the Tet Offensive launched on January 30, 1968, which inflicted heavy losses—over 45,000 communist dead—but failed militarily while eroding U.S. public support.[39][40] Hastings faulted American strategy under Presidents Johnson and Nixon for underestimating enemy resilience and over-relying on firepower, as in Operation Rolling Thunder's bombing campaign from March 1965 to October 1968, which dropped 864,000 tons of bombs yet did not break Hanoi's will.[41] He applied equal scrutiny to South Vietnamese leadership's corruption and the North's ruthless purges, rejecting one-sided narratives to highlight mutual atrocities, including the My Lai massacre on March 16, 1968, and communist land reforms that executed tens of thousands in the 1950s.[42][43] The work concludes that U.S. withdrawal via the Paris Accords on January 27, 1973, stemmed from domestic political exhaustion rather than military defeat, underscoring the war's role in reshaping global perceptions of limited interventions.[44]Hastings addressed the origins of World War I in Catastrophe 1914: Europe Goes to War (2013), attributing the conflict's outbreak on July 28, 1914, primarily to Germany's aggressive Schlieffen Plan and blank-check support for Austria-Hungary's ultimatum to Serbia following the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand on June 28, 1914. Relying on diplomatic cables, military dispatches, and soldier testimonies, he described the rapid escalations—including Russia's mobilization on July 30, 1914, and Germany's invasion of Belgium on August 4, 1914—that mobilized over 70 million men in weeks, leading to the Marne battle's halt of German advances from September 5–12, 1914.[45] While acknowledging alliance rigidities and miscalculations by France and Britain, Hastings contended that Berlin's bid for continental dominance forced reactive responses, rejecting revisionist views that diffused blame across powers and emphasizing empirical evidence of premeditated German war planning from 1912 onward.[46] His narrative integrated operational details, such as the British Expeditionary Force's retreat from Mons on August 23, 1914, to illustrate how early tactical errors entrenched the Western Front stalemate, with initial casualties exceeding one million by year's end.[47] This pre-war focus complemented his broader assessments of 20th-century warfare's human and strategic costs, prioritizing primary accounts over ideological interpretations.[48] Methodological Approach and Empirical Rigor Max Hastings employs a methodology centered on integrating archival research with extensive oral histories to construct multifaceted narratives of military conflicts. His process begins with immersion in primary documents, including unit war diaries and official records, to establish strategic and operational frameworks, as demonstrated in works like Overlord (1984), where he draws on declassified archives to assess Allied command decisions. Complementing this, Hastings conducts numerous interviews with participants, prioritizing eyewitness testimonies to capture the human dimensions of warfare; for instance, in Bomber Command (1979), he interviewed over 70 RAF personnel, including Air Chief Marshal Sir Arthur Harris, to evaluate the ethical and tactical realities of strategic bombing.[1][15]This dual approach extends to fieldwork across relevant geographies, enabling Hastings to verify accounts against physical sites and local perspectives. In The Korean War (1987), he traveled to China, South Korea, the United States, the United Kingdom, and Russia, incorporating survivor interviews to challenge prevailing Western narratives and highlight logistical and morale factors often overlooked in secondary analyses. His firsthand experience as a war correspondent in 11 conflicts, including Vietnam and the Falklands, informs this rigor, allowing him to authenticate depictions of soldiers' physical and psychological strains—such as exhaustion and hunger—through empirical parallels rather than abstraction.[1][15][49]Hastings maintains empirical rigor by cross-referencing personal testimonies with quantitative data, such as casualty figures and timelines, to test narrative plausibility and dispel patriotic myths. In Sword (2025), he scrutinizes D-Day assumptions, like the feasibility of capturing Caen, by aligning unit diaries and veteran reports with precise loss statistics from the Suffolk Regiment (seven dead in initial actions), ensuring claims withstand evidentiary scrutiny. Vietnam war reporter and first man into Port Stanley, Max Hastings admits to Louise Flind that he likes having his wife to look after him Anything you can’t leave home without? Nowadays, my Kindle – it used to be a Trollope, which has always been my great ‘comfort food’ in foreign places. Something you really miss? Sounds unbelievably soppy – my wife. As Penny [Levinson] says, she’s not treated as a wife, but as a nanny. Although I’ve spent so much of my working life abroad in faraway places, I’ve always been a terrible baby about it. Earliest childhood holiday memories? Because my parents [journalists Macdonald Hastings and Anne Scott-James] were barely on speaking terms, we all went to different destinations. My father would go somewhere like Africa to shoot things, my mother would go to the South of France, and my sister and I would be sent to Butlin’s with Nanny. Were you always a traveller? The only way to advance one’s career spectacularly was by going to far-flung places. People ask me, ‘Was Vietnam horrendous?’ and, of course, it was at one level. One remembers two things about it – one was the man-made horrors, the other the sublime natural beauty. You were 24 when you went to war-torn Vietnam – were you terrified? Personal raw ambition was what drove one on – sometimes one was absolutely terrified, but you also felt incredibly lucky to do these assignments which got you all over the front pages. How come you were the first journalist to enter Port Stanley in the Falklands war? I was the first PERSON to enter Port Stanley. I was with the Parachute Regiment and they were ordered to halt because they thought the Argentines were almost ready to stop fighting and negotiate. I saw Stanley Cathedral and the Argentine lines about half a mile ahead. I took off my camouflage gear, put my hands in the air and thought, if I can walk up this road and live, then I can bore everybody to death for the next 30 years – which indeed I have. Can you tell me about interviewing the Vietnam veterans? I sit entranced listening to Appalachian coalminers’ sons and Arkansas hillbillies and their tales. It’s absolutely riveting. Did your height and specs make travel in the jungle difficult? One was always a conspicuous and often ridiculous figure. I did the parachute course when I was young, with constant mockery from the instructors who’d say, ‘Come on, Hastings, anybody as big as you just has to step out and you’re on the ground.’ You have been to pretty faraway places Researching another book on the Second World War, I was up on the Manchuria border with Russia, and you do feel at the far end of the world. How did you file your reports in the past? In the 1973 Middle East war, when I was up with the Israeli army on the Suez Canal, I would have to drive for three or four hours across Sinai to the nearest telephone. In the Falklands, you had to get to a ship with a satellite link. Where did you go on your honeymoons? I went to Hong Kong with my former wife in 1972, and did it very unromantically. Having no money, I offered her a choice: either we can go together to somewhere like Paris or Rome or, if we go to Hong Kong, we can only afford it if you fly commercial and I’ll hitch a ride with the RAF. She settled for Hong Kong. Do you go on holiday? In the winter – we don’t like going in the summer because we love our garden. We go somewhere like Malaysia or Kenya; I work while Penny walks on the beach. Do you lie on the beach? No. I’ve always loathed sunbathing. Hotel or apartment? We used to rent a ranch in Kenya, and loved riding out among the elephants and giraffes but most of them have now been slaughtered by the locals. Have you made friends when you’ve been away? We don’t make friends but we often take friends. I’m often working on a book and I only emerge at meals, and Penny and I are perfectly happy with each other’s company – at lunchtime, we read our books and at dinner we talk. Do you have a go at the local language? Absolutely not. Only speak French. Biggest headache? American immigration can be hellish. The strangest place you’ve slept in? That’s easy – on top of Mount Kent in the Falklands War. It was so cold that you couldn’t possibly sleep. Top travelling tip?
