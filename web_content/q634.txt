The district’s economy primarily revolves around agriculture, with rice cultivation being the most prominent crop. The district is renowned for producing high-quality Basmati rice that is exported worldwide. The region has a long history of rice cultivation, with farmers relying on traditional methods of farming. The district’s terrain is perfect for rice cultivation, with vast paddy fields and the availability of water throughout the year. The rice fields in Kulgam are irrigated through a complex network of canals and streams, making it possible to cultivate rice twice a year. The district’s rice production has increased significantly over the years, with the government providing support through subsidies, loans, and training programs for farmers. Additionally, the district administration has established rice procurement centers to ensure that farmers get a fair price for their crops. The district administration has been taking steps to promote the cultivation of other crops to diversify the agriculture sector. In conclusion, Kulgam’s rice bowl status is well-deserved, given the district’s significant contribution to the state’s rice production. The district’s rice is renowned for its quality, and its farmers work hard to ensure that they maintain the high standards that have made it famous worldwide. Kulgam district Geography Location and Boundaries Kulgam District occupies the southern portion of the Kashmir Valley in Jammu and Kashmir, India, nestled along the banks of the Veshaw River at the foothills of the Pir Panjal range.[7] The district headquarters, Kulgam town, lies approximately 68 kilometers south of Srinagar and 17 kilometers southwest of Anantnag.[1] Geographically, the district extends between 33°15' north latitude and 74°35' east longitude, covering an area of 1,067 square kilometers.[2][7] The district is bordered by Anantnag District to the east and north, Shopian District to the west, Pulwama District to the northwest, and Ramban District to the south.[1][8] These boundaries facilitate road connectivity to adjacent areas, supporting administrative and economic linkages within the region.[1] The terrain transitions from valley plains in the north to higher elevations toward the Pir Panjal in the southwest, influencing local accessibility and settlement patterns.[8]Climate and Topography Kulgam district exhibits a diverse topography dominated by the southwestern Pir Panjal mountain range, which forms a natural barrier and influences local drainage patterns. The district spans approximately 1,067 square kilometers, with elevations ranging from valley floors to high mountain peaks exceeding 3,000 meters in the Pir Panjal. The headquarters at Kulgam town lies at 1,739 meters (5,705 feet) above sea level, surrounded by undulating hills, fertile plains, orchards, and paddy fields. Major rivers such as the Veshaw (also spelled Vishow) and its tributaries, including Nallah Veshav, drain the area, originating from high-altitude sources like Kausar Nag Lake in the Pir Panjal range. These watercourses carve scenic valleys and support agricultural landscapes, with features like the Kerwaha plain contributing to the region's picturesque terrain.[9][1] The climate of Kulgam is temperate, characteristic of the Kashmir Valley, with four distinct seasons influenced by its mid-latitude position and orographic effects from surrounding mountains. Winters from December to February are severe, with average low temperatures around -2°C (28°F) and frequent snowfall accumulating several feet in higher elevations, leading to sub-zero conditions and frozen water bodies. Summers, peaking in June to August, remain mild with daytime highs averaging 27°C (81°F) and lows around 15°C (59°F), moderated by altitude and occasional cool breezes. Spring (March-May) and autumn (September-November) serve as transitional periods with moderate temperatures and blooming flora in lower valleys. Precipitation totals approximately 700-1,000 mm annually, concentrated in the summer monsoon (June-September), which brings heavy rains—July seeing up to 9.9 days of measurable precipitation—while winter receives moisture from western disturbances as snow. This seasonal variability supports horticulture but also poses risks of flooding in valleys and avalanches in mountains.[10]Natural Resources Kulgam district possesses fertile alluvial soils and favorable agro-climatic conditions that support extensive agriculture, with paddy as the primary crop covering significant cultivated areas, alongside maize, pulses, vegetables, and horticultural produce such as apples.[3][2] The district's economy relies heavily on these land-based resources, often described locally as contributing to its role as a "granary of Kashmir" due to productive rice fields and orchards. Abundant freshwater resources include the Vishav River, numerous streams such as Veshaw and Sunnam, high-altitude springs, and lakes like Kausar Nag, which feed irrigation systems covering approximately 20,447 hectares through canals (14,150 ha), springs and tanks (869 ha), wells and tube wells (2,273 ha), and other sources (3,155 ha).[3][11][2] These water bodies, totaling 387 identified in conservation plans with ongoing restoration efforts (40 restored by March 2021), sustain paddy cultivation and provide potential for hydropower, though overexploitation via illegal sand mining from nallahs poses environmental risks.[12][13] Forests span 440.78 km² across diverse elevations between 33°28′N to 33°49′15″N and 74°39′30″E to 75°07′45″E, featuring coniferous species like deodar, kail, and fir, alongside broadleaf varieties and extensive pastures on lower slopes that support timber, non-timber products, and wildlife such as black bears and leopards.[14] Recent assessments indicate growing tree cover, with Kulgam contributing significantly to regional increases (e.g., 6,240 hectares added in one study period), though illegal felling of species like walnut remains a concern.[15] Mineral resources are limited to minor deposits, primarily sand and gravel extracted from riverbeds and nallahs like Vishu, with no major metallic or industrial minerals identified in district-specific surveys; extraction is regulated but subject to frequent illegal activities, as evidenced by seizures of machinery and vehicles in operations as recent as 2024-2025.[16][17][18]History Pre-Modern Period The region encompassing modern Kulgam district exhibits evidence of ancient settlement during the Kushana period, with archaeological excavations at Kutbal yielding stamped tiles and other artifacts indicative of advanced civic organization, artistic production, and cultural sophistication under Kushana rulers around the 1st century AD.[9][19] Further, the Devsar area served as an ancient center for learning and bronze craftsmanship, influenced by Gandhara artistic traditions, featuring a Kushana-era bronze Buddha sculpture and a 10th-century bronze frame associated with King Shankarvarman, measuring approximately 6 feet 2 inches by 4 feet 4 inches and weighing about 3 maunds 4 seers.[20][21] In the medieval era, the area originally known as Shampora transitioned under Islamic influence when Mir Syed Mohammad Hussain Simnani, a Sufi saint from Simnan in Iran born in 701 AH (1302 AD), arrived in Kashmir around 760 AH (1359 AD) and established it as a base for missionary activities predating the fuller arrival of Mir Sayyid Ali Hamadani.[9][22] Simnani renamed the settlement Kulgam, deriving from the Sanskrit terms kul (clan) and gram (village), and settled on a cliff overlooking the Veshaw River, where he died in 792 AH (1390 AD) and was buried; his shrine, constructed with a masonry plinth and deodar wood superstructure, continues to draw interfaith devotees.[9][19] His efforts facilitated local conversions, including that of Salar Sanz, father of Sheikh Nur-ud-din Noorani, who adopted the name Salar-ud-din upon embracing Islam in the region.[9] Kulgam gained further spiritual prominence in 779 AH (1377 AD) with the birth of Sheikh Nur-ud-din Noorani, known as Nund Rishi, in the village of Kaimuh; revered across Hindu and Muslim communities as the founder of the Rishi ascetic order, his legacy underscores the area's role in syncretic Kashmiri mysticism.[9][19] During the Mughal period, the region saw administrative consolidation as a tehsil under the oversight of Sheikh Hamza Makhdoom, incorporating areas like Doru, Banihal, and Shopian, reflecting its integration into broader imperial governance structures by the 16th-17th centuries.[9]Formation and Administrative Evolution Kulgam was originally part of the broader Anantnag region during the Mughal era, where administrative structures drew from Sher Shah Suri's earlier reforms emphasizing revenue collection and local governance.[9] A tehsil encompassing Kulgam was established during Mughal rule, facilitated by the influence of Sheikh Hamza Makhdoom (c. 1494–1576), a prominent Sufi figure whose spiritual authority aided in organizing local administration; this tehsil initially covered areas including Doru, Banihal, Gulabgarh, and Shopian.[9] Under Dogra rule following the Treaty of Amritsar in 1846, which established princely Jammu and Kashmir, Kulgam remained integrated within Anantnag district without independent status, serving as a sub-division focused on agrarian oversight and frontier security.[9] Post-1947 accession to India, it continued as a tehsil in Anantnag, with administrative functions handled through Srinagar's oversight, including land revenue and judicial matters, amid evolving state reorganization efforts in the 1960s and 1970s that did not alter its sub-district position.[9] The district of Kulgam was formally created on April 2, 2007, by carving out territory from Anantnag district as part of a Jammu and Kashmir government initiative to form seven new districts for improved administrative efficiency and proximity to rural populations.[1] This evolution reduced Anantnag's area and jurisdiction, establishing Kulgam with its own sub-divisions, tehsils (initially including Kulgam, Devsar, and others), and blocks to handle local development, law enforcement, and resource allocation independently.[1] The move addressed longstanding demands for decentralized governance in south Kashmir, enhancing responsiveness to regional needs like infrastructure and conflict resolution.[1]Insurgency Era and Counter-Operations The Kashmir insurgency, which erupted in 1989 with the infiltration of trained militants from Pakistan and the formation of groups like Hizbul-ul-Mujahideen (HM), extended to south Kashmir regions including the area later designated as Kulgam district in 2007. Kulgam's forested hills and remote villages became sites for militant overground worker networks, arms caches, and ambushes on security convoys during the 1990s peak, when local recruitment surged amid grievances over rigged elections and Islamist propaganda. Security forces, primarily the Indian Army's Rashtriya Rifles under Counter Insurgency Force (Victor Force), responded with area domination operations, establishing forward posts to disrupt supply lines from across the Line of Control. By the early 2000s, counter-militancy efforts shifted toward intelligence-driven precision strikes, reducing HM's dominance in Kulgam as foreign mercenaries from Lashkar-e-Taiba infiltrated. Notable incidents included the May 9, 2017, attack on a J&K Bank van near Laroo, where HM militants killed five policemen—including an Assistant Sub-Inspector—and two guards using grenades and automatic weapons, highlighting persistent threats to soft targets.[23] Joint operations by the Army, Central Reserve Police Force, and local police neutralized over 50 militants in Kulgam between 2010 and 2019, per compiled incident data, though civilian protests often complicated cordons following high-profile killings like that of HM commander Burhan Wani in nearby Pulwama in 2016. Following the August 2019 abrogation of Article 370, which integrated Jammu and Kashmir fully into India and curtailed separatist funding channels, militancy in Kulgam transitioned to low-intensity hybrid warfare involving small foreign terrorist modules. Recruitment of locals plummeted, with security forces reporting fewer than five active indigenous militants by 2023, attributed to enhanced human intelligence and economic incentives.[24] Persistent threats prompted extended forest operations, such as Operation Akhal in July-August 2025, where forces trapped 4-5 militants in Gadar forests over nine days, killing at least one and recovering arms.[25] A September 2025 encounter in the same area eliminated two terrorists but cost two soldiers' lives, underscoring the risks of engaging battle-hardened infiltrators amid terrain advantages.[26] These operations reflect a data-driven strategy prioritizing elimination over kinetic sweeps, yielding a 70% drop in violent incidents district-wide since 2019 per official Mohammad Yousuf Tarigami, the party's veteran leader and Kashmir's sole CPI(M) legislator, has secured victory in five consecutive elections, including the 2024 Jammu and Kashmir Legislative Assembly polls held on September 25, where he garnered 33,634 votes (45% share) to defeat independent candidate Sayar Ahmad Reshi by 7,838 votes.[53][54] Tarigami's consistent hold reflects local support for his advocacy on labor rights, anti-militancy stances, and development initiatives, despite regional shifts following the 2019 abrogation of Article 370.[55] The 2024 elections marked the first assembly polls in the region in a decade, with Kulgam recording a voter turnout of approximately 64%, signaling renewed electoral engagement amid delimited constituencies and the shift to a Union Territory framework.[56] Contenders included candidates from the Jammu and Kashmir Peoples Democratic Party (PDP), such as Mohammad Amin Dar, and independents, but mainstream parties like the National Conference (NC) supported Tarigami through an alliance, underscoring CPI(M)'s pivotal role in the People's Alliance for Gupkar Declaration (PAGD) coalition.[57] The Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), while appointing Farooq Ahmad Rather as district president in early 2025 to expand in the Kashmir Valley, fielded no viable contender in Kulgam and remains marginal in local Muslim-majority politics.[58] District-level politics also involve the District Development Council (DDC), elected in 2020-2021, where NC and allies dominated seats, focusing on grassroots governance post-reorganization. Voting patterns in Kulgam historically favor parties emphasizing autonomy and welfare over separatism, though anti-incumbency challenges Tarigami on issues like unemployment and infrastructure delays.[59] For parliamentary representation, the district falls under the Anantnag-Rajouri Lok Sabha seat, won by NC's Mian Altaf Ahmad in 2024, aligning with broader Valley trends toward NC-led coalitions.[60]Militancy and Rebel Activities Kulgam district, located in south Kashmir, has served as a significant base for Islamist insurgent groups since the escalation of the Kashmir insurgency in the late 1980s, with Hizbul Mujahideen (HM) maintaining operational dominance through recruitment of local youth and ambushes on security forces.[61] HM, founded in 1989, has historically focused on guerrilla tactics in the region, including IED placements and hit-and-run attacks, leveraging the district's forested terrain for hideouts and training.[61] Local militants, often radicalized via overground worker networks, have facilitated arms smuggling and logistics, contributing to sustained rebel presence despite counterinsurgency efforts.[27] Rebel activities intensified in the 2010s, with HM commanders orchestrating targeted killings and attempts to disrupt civilian movement, such as efforts to attack the Amarnath Yatra pilgrimage routes passing near Kulgam. In July 2024, security operations in Chinigam area neutralized four local HM militants planning such disruptions, highlighting ongoing threats to regional stability.[62] Encounters frequently stem from intelligence on rebel movements, where militants initiate firefights to evade capture, resulting in exchanges that underscore their reliance on small arms and improvised explosives.[63] Recent years post-Article 370 abrogation in 2019 have seen persistent HM operations, including a December 2024 gunbattle in Kulgam where five militants, led by veteran commander Farooq Ahmad Bhat (active for over a decade), were eliminated after initiating combat.[64] In September 2025, another clash killed two terrorists and two soldiers, reflecting rebels' adaptive tactics amid heightened surveillance.[26] An August 2025 operation in Kulgam forests, lasting eight days, targeted entrenched insurgent modules, indicating prolonged rebel entrenchment and occasional foreign fighter involvement.[65] These incidents, drawn from security force reports, reveal a pattern of low-intensity but ideologically driven rebellion aimed at secession, with HM claiming responsibility for select attacks to assert influence.[66]Counter-Terrorism Measures and Outcomes Security forces in Kulgam district, comprising the Indian Army, Central Reserve Police Force (CRPF), and Jammu and Kashmir Police, primarily employ intelligence-led cordon-and-search operations in forested and remote areas to neutralize militants affiliated with groups such as Lashkar-e-Taiba (LeT) and The Resistance Front (TRF).[24] These operations involve joint teams establishing cordons around suspected hideouts, followed by targeted engagements based on specific intelligence inputs, as seen in the prolonged anti-terror efforts in south Kashmir's rugged terrain.[67] Additional measures include enhanced patrolling, surprise checkpoints (naka points), and geo-fencing technology for high-security events like the Amarnath Yatra to monitor pilgrim routes and detect threats.[68][69] Notable operations include Operation Akhal in the Akhal Devsar area starting August 1, 2025, where security forces eliminated at least three militants over multiple days of confrontation in dense forests.[70] In Operation Guddar, launched in the Guddar forest area on September 8, 2025, two militants were killed, alongside the loss of two Army personnel and injuries to others during intense gun battles.[71][26] These actions have resulted in the recovery of arms and ammunition, disrupting militant logistics, though encounters often extend over days due to militants using natural cover for evasion.[72] Outcomes reflect a broader post-2019 trend in Jammu and Kashmir of reduced terrorist-initiated incidents—down from 228 in 2018 to 43 by late 2023—attributable to intensified counter-insurgency grids and operations targeting overground workers and infiltration routes, with Kulgam operations contributing to local eliminations amid persistent south Kashmir activity.[73][74] However, challenges persist, as evidenced by security force casualties in prolonged engagements and occasional civilian impacts from targeted killings by militants in the district prior to abrogation.[24] High-level reviews by Army Northern Command and state police leadership underscore ongoing adaptations to maintain dominance in militancy-prone zones like Kulgam.[67]Economy Agriculture and Horticulture Agriculture forms the backbone of Kulgam district's economy, employing approximately 92% of the population and serving as the primary livelihood source.[2] The main field crops include paddy and maize, supplemented by vegetable cultivation, though rice production has declined significantly from its historical prominence as the "Rice Bowl of Kashmir."[75][76] Horticulture, particularly apple cultivation, dominates the sector due to the district's favorable temperate climate and soil conditions. This shift enabled accelerated funding for agriculture and horticulture, sectors comprising over 80% of the district's economic activity. Horticulture, dominated by apple cultivation, expanded with 23,277 hectares under fruits in 2023-24, producing 219,460 metric tons, primarily apples, alongside exports of 17,020 metric tons generating Rs. 61 crore in revenue.[3][87] Infrastructure enhancements post-2019 have bolstered economic connectivity and productivity. Public works department roads totaled 1,310 km by 2023-24, supporting rural access crucial for horticultural transport. In October 2025, Chief Minister Omar Abdullah inaugurated urban projects including a Solid Waste Management and Material Recovery Facility at Lirrow, aimed at improving sanitation and enabling sustainable economic operations in growing settlements. Rural road projects under Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana Phase IV, with 13 sanctioned in Kulgam including seven in Damhal Hanjipora block, addressed long-standing connectivity gaps, with completions reported as recently as October 2025.[87][88][89] Small-scale industrialization showed modest progress, with micro, small, and medium enterprises numbering 13,066 units by end-2023-24, reflecting policy incentives for local entrepreneurship amid J&K-wide investment proposals exceeding Rs. 80,000 crore since 2019, though actual ground-level industrial setup in Kulgam remains limited due to its agrarian focus. Skill development initiatives, including a farm-based Industrial Training Institute at Khuri Batpora, were established to modernize horticultural practices like high-density plantations, aligning with union territory efforts that increased overall fruit production by 31% since 2018-19.[87][90][87] Despite these advances, economic vulnerabilities persist, including weather-induced early harvests in higher reaches during October 2025 and reported 40% crop losses in Kulgam from highway disruptions in September 2025, underscoring ongoing logistical challenges for perishable exports. District-level MSME growth and infrastructure outlays contribute to J&K's nominal GSDP compound annual growth rate of 7.53% from 2019-20 onward, though real growth has trailed pre-abrogation averages in some analyses.[91][92][93]Infrastructure Transportation and Connectivity Kulgam district relies predominantly on road networks for transportation, with National Highway 444 (NH-444) providing an alternate route from Srinagar through Budgam, Pulwama, Shopian, and Kulgam to Qazigund, enhancing connectivity to southern Kashmir and facilitating the transport of horticultural produce like apples. [94] The district headquarters is situated 68 kilometers south of Srinagar and 17 kilometers from Anantnag, linked by roads to adjacent districts including Shopian, Pulwama, Anantnag, and Ramban. [95][1] Infrastructure developments include the 8-kilometer Kulgam bypass on NH-444, stretching from Pahloo to Lirow and incorporating a 378-meter Brazloo bridge, with construction progress reviewed in June 2025 and completion targeted for February 2026 to alleviate traffic congestion and improve goods movement. [96] Rural connectivity has advanced under the Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana, granting road access to isolated villages such as those in south Kashmir after over 70 years without prior links, alongside macadamization of local roads. [89] However, some segments, like the Chattbal-Kulgam road, remain in poor condition as of January 2025, prompting local appeals for repairs. [97] Public transport features regular bus services from Srinagar and Jammu to Kulgam, supplemented by intra-district options, with district authorities designating pick-up and drop points in 2025 to optimize traffic flow in the town center. [95][98] Air connectivity is accessed via Srinagar International Airport, 68 kilometers north of Kulgam. [95] Rail access requires travel to the nearest station at Anantnag, 15 kilometers away, as the district has no operational railway stations.
