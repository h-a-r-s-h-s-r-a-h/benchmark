Glipa andamana is a species of tumbling flower beetle belonging to the family Mordellidae in the order Coleoptera.[1] Described by the French entomologist Maurice Pic in 1941, it is classified under the genus Glipa and is native to the Andaman Islands in the Bay of Bengal. Little additional information is available on its morphology, ecology, or conservation status due to its obscurity in entomological literature.[2] Taxonomy Classification Glipa andamana is a species of tumbling flower beetle belonging to the family Mordellidae. Its full taxonomic classification is as follows: Kingdom: Animalia; Phylum: Arthropoda; Class: Insecta; Order: Coleoptera; Suborder: Polyphaga; Infraorder: Cucujiformia; Superfamily: Tenebrionoidea; Family: Mordellidae; Subfamily: Mordellinae; Genus: Glipa; Species: G. andamana.[1]The binomial name is Glipa andamana Píc, 1941, originally described by the French entomologist Maurice Pic.[1] No synonyms are currently recognized for this species. The genus Glipa, established by John Lawrence LeConte in 1859, comprises approximately 139 species distributed primarily in tropical and subtropical regions.[3] Species in this genus are characterized by a distinctive wedge-shaped body and short elytra that do not fully cover the abdomen, typical of tumbling flower beetles.[4] Etymology and naming Glipa andamana was first described by the French entomologist Maurice Pic in 1941, in a short note published in L'Échange, Revue Linnéenne. The description was based on specimens collected from the Andaman Islands, which served as the type locality for the species.[1]The specific epithet "andamana" directly refers to the Andaman Islands, honoring the geographic origin of the type material, a common practice in taxonomic nomenclature for locality-based names. The genus name Glipa was established by John Lawrence LeConte in 1859.[5]Pic, a prolific coleopterist known for his extensive work on the fauna of the Indomalayan region, contributed significantly to the taxonomy of Mordellidae through numerous descriptions in his journal L'Échange.[6] Description Adult morphology The adult Glipa andamana exhibits the characteristic wedge-shaped body form typical of the genus Glipa within the family Mordellidae, with a compact, elongate structure that tapers posteriorly, measuring approximately 5-7 mm in length based on genus averages for related species.[7] The elytra are short and truncate, failing to cover the exposed abdominal segments, which contributes to the beetle's distinctive "tumbling flower beetle" silhouette often associated with rapid, erratic movements.[6]Coloration in adult G. andamana is predominantly dark brown to black, consistent with many Glipa species, though subtle variations such as a metallic sheen or faint elytral markings may occur, as inferred from generic patterns in congeners like G. oculata and G. octopunctata.[7] The head is small and retracted, featuring large, finely granulated eyes that do not extend to the occiput, paired with 11-segmented antennae that are serrate to slightly clavate, with the terminal segments forming a loose club.[7]The legs of adult G. andamana show adaptations for jumping, particularly the hind legs, which possess enlarged femora and elongated tibiae lacking prominent ridges except for a short subapical one, enabling powerful leaps characteristic of Mordellidae.[7] Sexual dimorphism is subtle but present, with males potentially displaying more pronounced sculpturing or pubescence on the elytra compared to females, as observed in several Glipa species.[7] Immature stages The immature stages of Glipa andamana remain poorly documented, with no direct observations reported for this species; descriptions are thus inferred from studies of congeneric species in the genus Glipa and more broadly from the family Mordellidae.[6]Larvae of Glipa species are typically cylindrical and slightly C-shaped, measuring approximately 9–11 mm in length at maturity, with a hardened, sclerotized exoskeleton featuring distinct body segments for protection during burrowing.[8][9] They possess chewing mouthparts adapted for feeding on decaying organic matter and are wood-boring or soil-dwelling, often tunneling into dead wood, leaf litter, or plant stems.[6][10]The pupal stage is exarate, with appendages free from the body, and occurs within a pupal chamber in the soil or inside wood, where the non-feeding pupa undergoes metamorphosis without further feeding.[9][8] Development follows holometabolous metamorphosis typical of Coleoptera, with the larval stage estimated to last 1–2 years based on family-wide patterns observed in wood-associated species.[6][11]Significant knowledge gaps persist, as immature stages have been described for fewer than 50 Mordellidae species globally, none of which include G. andamana or close congeners, necessitating further field studies for species-specific details.[12][6] Distribution and habitat Geographic range Glipa andamana is a beetle species endemic to the Andaman Islands, India. The species is known exclusively from this archipelago based on type specimens collected there.It was first described in 1941 by French coleopterist Maurice Pic, and there are no confirmed records of the species from other locations. Due to its obscurity, no additional records or surveys have been documented since description.The isolated nature of the Andaman populations makes G. andamana particularly susceptible to threats posed by human activities, such as habitat destruction and invasive species introduction, which exacerbate the risks associated with island biogeography for endemic taxa.[13] Preferred habitats Little is known about the specific habitats of Glipa andamana due to the lack of post-description observations. As a member of the Mordellidae family, adults are likely terrestrial and associated with floral microhabitats, while larvae typically develop in decaying wood or soil, consistent with detritivorous habits of the family in tropical settings.[14]The Andaman Islands feature humid tropical weather with annual rainfall often exceeding 3,000 mm in areas like Port Blair, which sustains lush vegetation potentially suitable for these beetles.[15]As part of the Indomalayan biodiversity hotspot, the Andaman Islands host rich associated flora and fauna, though the precise ecological niche of G. andamana remains undocumented.[16] Biology and ecology Life cycle Glipa andamana, like other members of the family Mordellidae, is believed to undergo complete metamorphosis with four life stages: egg, larva, pupa, and adult, though specific details for this species are unknown.[17]Eggs are typically laid on plants, in flowers, or within decaying wood near suitable larval food sources, based on family patterns. Hatching likely occurs within a few weeks, depending on environmental conditions.[14]The larval stage, as inferred from the genus Glipa, involves elongate larvae developing within plant stems or decaying organic matter, feeding on plant tissues, fungi, or detritus. Pupation probably occurs in soil or wood chambers. Specific durations and triggers for G. andamana in the Andaman Islands are not documented.[14][17][3]Adult lifespan and generation timing for G. andamana remain unstudied, but family members are generally short-lived, focusing on reproduction.[11] Behavior and diet Like other species in the genus Glipa, G. andamana adults are likely phytophagous, consuming pollen and nectar from various flowers, potentially including Asteraceae and Apiaceae, acting as generalist pollinators. Specific host plants and pest status for G. andamana are unknown.[3]A characteristic of Mordellidae, including Glipa, is the "tumbling" escape behavior: when disturbed, adults drop and tumble erratically using their arched abdomen and hind legs to evade predators. This diurnal species is presumably active on flowers during daylight.[11][9]Reproductive behaviors likely follow Mordellidae patterns, with males aggregating on flowers for mating and females ovipositing on plants or soil.
