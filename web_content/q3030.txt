--- [Order of the Liberator General San Martín - Wikipedia] ---
The Order of the Liberator General San Martín (Spanish: Orden del Libertador General San Martín) is the highest decoration in Argentina. It is awarded to foreign politicians or military, deemed worthy of the highest recognition from Argentina. It is granted by the sitting President of Argentina.

Order of the Liberator General San Martín Historical Background Legacy of José de San Martín José de San Martín was born on February 25, 1778, in Yapeyú, Corrientes Province, then part of the Spanish Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata.[7] After early education in Spain, he entered Spanish military service at age 11, serving in campaigns in North Africa and later distinguishing himself in the Peninsular War against Napoleonic forces from 1808 to 1812.[8] In March 1812, disillusioned with Spanish absolutism, San Martín arrived in Buenos Aires and defected to the revolutionary independence movement, where he reorganized patriot cavalry units and secured key victories, such as the Battle of San Lorenzo on February 3, 1813, against royalist invaders.[9] Appointed governor-intendant of the Cuyo region in 1814, he methodically assembled the Army of the Andes, emphasizing rigorous training, logistical preparation, and merit-based promotions to build a disciplined force of approximately 5,000 men by late 1816.[10] In a feat of strategic audacity, San Martín orchestrated the crossing of the Andes beginning January 17, 1817, dividing his army into multiple columns to traverse treacherous high-altitude passes over 24 days, sustaining about 1,700 casualties from cold, altitude, and privation but preserving combat effectiveness.[11] This maneuver enabled a surprise invasion of Chile, culminating in the Battle of Chacabuco on February 12, 1817, where patriot forces routed 1,500 Spanish troops, capturing Santiago and installing Bernardo O'Higgins as supreme director.[12] A subsequent royalist counteroffensive was decisively crushed at the Battle of Maipú on April 5, 1818, with San Martín's 5,000 patriots inflicting over 2,000 casualties on 5,500 Spaniards for minimal losses, effectively securing Chilean independence and providing naval resources for further operations.[12] Shifting focus northward, San Martín launched an amphibious expedition from Chile in August 1820, landing at Pisco and maneuvering to enter Lima unopposed on July 12, 1821; he proclaimed Peruvian independence on July 28, 1821, assuming the role of Protector to stabilize the viceregal capital against royalist holdouts in the highlands.[13] San Martín's leadership prioritized professional discipline over charismatic appeal, implementing strict codes of conduct and promotions earned through competence, which minimized desertions and maximized operational cohesion in his armies.[14] Demonstrating strategic restraint, he ceded command after the July 26, 1822, Guayaquil conference with Simón Bolívar, formally resigning on September 20, 1822, before Peru's constituent congress to avert personal dictatorship and facilitate unified liberation efforts, retiring to private life in Europe thereafter.[15] His campaigns empirically advanced independence across southern South America—Argentina's consolidation by 1816, Chile's by 1818 via treaty with O'Higgins, and Peru's coastal liberation by 1821—achieving these with fewer instances of post-victory factionalism than contemporaneous northern efforts, as evidenced by Chile's prompt constitutional governance post-Maipú and Peru's avoidance of immediate caudillo rule under San Martín, grounding the order's prestige in his model of effective, non-aggrandizing liberation.[16]Establishment of the Order The Order of the Liberator General San Martín was instituted on August 17, 1943—the 93rd anniversary of José de San Martín's death—through Decree No. 5000 issued by the Argentine executive under the provisional government established after the June 4 military revolution that ended Ramón S. Castillo's presidency.[17][18] This creation occurred amid Argentina's policy of neutrality in World War II and efforts at internal consolidation following political upheaval, positioning the order as a diplomatic instrument to recognize external contributions without duplicating honors reserved for nationals.[2] The decree specified the order's purpose as conferring Argentina's supreme distinction exclusively upon foreign civilians or military personnel whose services in military, diplomatic, political, or related fields warranted the nation's highest gratitude, thereby emphasizing international prestige over domestic awards during a period of global tension and national reorientation.[18] The original insignia was designed by Argentine sculptor and engineer Ángel Eusebio Ibarra García, whose work integrated motifs from San Martín's independence campaigns to symbolize liberation and republican values. Initial bestowals in the mid-1940s underscored the order's role in bolstering Argentina's postwar diplomatic outreach to foreign leaders and allies.[2]Design and Insignia Physical Description of Elements The collar of the Order consists of 18 links, including Andean condor motifs, laurel crowns, and a central circular medallion featuring the effigy of José de San Martín.[19] These links are crafted from 925/1000 silver, molded, chiseled, and gold-plated, with articulated connections via horizontal pivots for flexibility.[19] Higher-grade collars may incorporate gold, platinum elements, and diamonds, as seen in specimens with thousands of such components produced by specialized jewelers.[20] The badges across grades feature a central medallion with the profile effigy of San Martín, encircled by flaming and straight rays, set within a blue enamel ring edged in white enamel bearing the inscription "Libertador San Martín."[21][4] The reverse displays Argentina's national coat of arms in gold and enamel on a burnished gold background, surrounded by a triple crown of rays.[19] Badges for lower grades (Officer and Knight) are suspended from ribbons or neck chains, while higher grades (Grand Officer, Commander, Grand Cross) include additional breast stars with radiating arms, often embellished with enamel and, in senior classes, synthetic or genuine jewels. The overall design, established in 1943 and refined by decree in 1957, was created by Argentine artist Ángel Eusebio Ibarra García.[18] Sashes and ribbons utilize Argentina's national colors of light blue (celeste) and white, with the Grand Cross sash worn from the right shoulder to the left hip in a wide band, approximately 10 cm across, featuring celeste fabric with white borders or stripes.[22] Lower grades employ narrower ribbons: neck ribbons for Commander (about 4 cm wide, celeste with white edges), and breast ribbons for Officer and Knight (2-3 cm wide, pinned to the left chest). Rosettes of matching ribbon material denote civilian wear for Officer and Knight grades. Insignia are manufactured using gold for structural elements, vitreous enamel for colored detailing, and precious or semi-precious stones in select high-grade pieces, with production overseen by Argentina's Casa de Moneda or authorized jewelers to ensure uniformity.[19][23]Symbolic Significance The effigy of José de San Martín adorning the obverse of the order's insignia encapsulates his instrumental military strategies in forging Argentina's sovereignty, exemplified by the 1817 Army of the Andes expedition that decisively routed royalist forces at Chacabuco and Maipú, thereby liberating Chile and paving the path to Peruvian independence in 1821. This imagery prioritizes San Martín's emphasis on coordinated continental alliance over individualistic or populist appeals, reflecting causal mechanisms of disciplined logistics and inter-nation cooperation that severed Spanish colonial ties without devolving into prolonged civil strife.[24][4] Encircling the central motif, the laurel wreath from which the star pendant hangs denotes martial triumph, a motif rooted in classical antiquity and empirically recurrent in South American independence emblems following victories against Iberian dominion. On the reverse, Argentina's coat of arms incorporates the Phrygian cap atop a pike—held by clasped hands signifying union—directly evoking emancipation, as the cap's historical association with freed Roman slaves evolved into a 19th-century republican standard for rejecting monarchical subjugation, evidenced in early post-colonial coinage and flags.[4][25] The curved corvo sword of San Martín, positioned athwart the laurels, further symbolizes resolute defense of autonomy, tying the order's heraldry to his campaigns' tangible outcomes in territorial consolidation. Instituted on August 17, 1943, amid worldwide contests between totalitarian ideologies, the ensemble deliberately omits contemporaneous partisan iconography, instead anchoring recognition in enduring merit for liberty—a deliberate counterpoint to era-specific dogmas, aligning with San Martín's own eschewal of personal aggrandizement for institutional stability.[4][2] By design, the order distinguishes itself from Argentina's citizen-focused honors, such as the Order of May, through its orientation toward non-nationals, thereby emblemizing the projection of San Martín's unification ethos outward as a meritocratic extension of national influence, predicated on reciprocal advancements in peace and self-determination rather than ideological conformity.[2]Organizational Structure Classes and Grades The Order of the Liberator General San Martín consists of six hierarchical classes, designed to confer distinctions of escalating prestige upon foreign dignitaries based on their contributions to international relations or merit deserving of Argentine recognition. These classes, established under Decree-Law No. 16.628 of December 17, 1957, which restructured the order to award it exclusively to foreign civil or military officials, are as follows: Collar, Grand Cross, Grand Officer, Commander, Officer, and Knight.[26][21] The Collar represents the pinnacle of the order, reserved for sovereigns and heads of state, symbolizing the utmost rarity and diplomatic elevation, with insignia comprising a chain of 18 links featuring Andean condors and laurel crowns.[19] The Grand Cross follows as the senior class for high-ranking foreign leaders, entailing a sash and breast star, while the Grand Officer denotes notable officials through a badge with ribbon. Descending further, Commander, Officer, and Knight classes award progressively smaller badges suspended from ribbons, tailored to mid- and lower-level diplomats, military officers, or equivalents, emphasizing a structured meritocracy in bestowal.[21] This framework evolved from the original 1943 decree, with 1948 amendments under Law No. 13.202 refining privileges such as insignia specifications and award protocols prior to the 1957 exclusivity to non-Argentines, which eliminated domestic eligibility to prioritize foreign honors and streamline international precedence.[27] The classes maintain relative precedence mirroring recipient stature: Collar for supreme figures, with lower grades calibrated to officials or personnel of diminishing seniority, ensuring the order's role as a calibrated instrument of state diplomacy.[19]| Class | Spanish Name | Insignia Features | Precedence Focus | |---|---|---|---| | Collar | Collar | Chain with 18 links, condor motifs | Sovereigns, heads of state | | Grand Cross | Gran Cruz | Sash, large breast star | Senior foreign leaders | | Grand Officer | Gran Oficial | Badge with ribbon bar | High-ranking officials | | Commander | Comendador | Breast badge | Mid-level diplomats/military | | Officer | Oficial | Smaller suspended badge | Junior officials | | Knight | Caballero | Minimal badge | Entry-level merit recipients |

- Time Period: Post-WW2 - Institution: 30 September 1984 - Country: Argentina (Other Countries) The Order of the Liberator General San Martin (or Orden del Libertador General San Martín in Spanish) is the highest decoration awarded by the Governement of Argentina. It is given to foreign politicians or military, deemed as worthy of the highest recognition by the country and it’s granted by the sitting President of Argentina. The order receives its name from José de San Martín, Father of the Land and Liberator. The award was established in 1943 (Decree 5000 from August 17th, 1943) and modified on May 21st 1948 (Decree 16628 from 1957, which creates the Order). The rules used today date from December 18th, 1967. The Grand Master of the Order presides over a national council and is the President of the Nation, same who confers the award. The Order headquarters is the Ministry of Foreign Relations, International Commerce and Cult. The grades of the Order are: - Collar (Collar) - Grand Cross (Gran Cruz) - Grand Officer (Gran Oficial) - Commander (Comendador) - Officer (Oficial) - Member (Caballero) The Order of the Liberator General San Martin Medal Design The medal was designed by Ángel Eusebio Ibarra García. It’s struck in gold and shows a condor, the sable and General San Martin’s portrait in a frame made of sun rays. Only one Order with the Extraordinary grade was awarded: to Eva Perón in 1952. It was made of 3821 pieces of gold and platinum, and 753 precious stones. It was made by the jewelers joyería Ghiso S.A. and designed by Aída Louzao. After the Military Dictatorship of 1955, the collar was disassembled and the pieces went to auction. The reverse of the Argentinian $5 bill shows the collar of the Order.


--- [Monument to General San Martín] ---
This monument to the General José de San Martín, recognised as the liberator of Argentina, was created in 1862 by French sculptor Louis Joseph Daumas. It was the first equetrian statue in the city. In around 1910, the German sculptor Gustav Eberlein designed the red granite base and the four corner sculptures, which represent landmark moments in San Martín's campaign for the country's independence - "The Departure to War", "The Battle", "The Victory" and the "The Return of the Victor". The figure of Mars, god of war, can be seen at the front of the platform, and on the other three faces, there is a series of bas-reliefs depicting different battles. José de San Martín (1778-1850) was the greatest hero of the Argentine War of Independence and is also recognised as the liberator of Peru and Chile. An outstanding general and strategist, in 1812 he created the Regiment of Mounted Grenadiers, whose barracks were located in this square. Between 1813 and 1818, San Martín led crucial moments in the fight for independence, defeating Spanish troops at the Battle of San Lorenzo, reorganising the Army of the North, becoming Governor of Cuyo and creating the Army of the Andes, which he led across the mountains into Chile to liberate the neighbouring country from Spanish rule, before moving north to liberate Peru in 1820.
