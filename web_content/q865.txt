--- [Max Sisulu - Wikipedia] ---
Max Vuyisile Sisulu (born 23 August 1945) is a South African politician and businessman who was Speaker of the National Assembly from May 2009 to May 2014. A member of the African National Congress (ANC), he was a member of the party's National Executive Committee from December 1994 to December 2017. 
Born in Soweto, Sisulu is the son of anti-apartheid activists Albertina and Walter Sisulu. Between 1963 and 1990, at the height of apartheid, he lived outside of South Africa with the exiled ANC and its military wing, Umkhonto we Sizwe. An economist by training, he was the ANC's head of economic planning from 1986 to 1990, and he remained influential in ANC economic policymaking in subsequent decades. 
In April 1994, in South Africa's first democratic elections, Sisulu was elected to represent the ANC in the National Assembly, the lower house of the new South African Parliament. For much of the First Parliament, he was the chairperson of the Portfolio Committee on the Reconstruction and Development Programme (RDP). Then, from February 1997 to November 1998, he served as Chief Whip of the Majority Party. However, he resigned from his seat in November 1998 to work in business, first at Denel and later at Sasol. 
He did not return to Parliament until the April 2009 general election, pursuant to which he was elected as the Speaker. He was the first man to serve as Speaker since the end of apartheid, and the first black man ever to serve as Speaker in the South African Parliament. He served in the office throughout the Fourth Parliament but was replaced by Baleka Mbete after the May 2014 general election.

Max Vuyisile Sisulu (born 23 August 1945) is a South African politician and former Speaker of the National Assembly, serving from 6 May 2009 to 29 May 2014.[1] Born in Soweto to anti-apartheid leaders Walter and Albertina Sisulu, he went into exile in 1963 amid intensifying apartheid repression, spending nearly three decades abroad with the African National Congress (ANC) and its military wing, Umkhonto we Sizwe (MK), where he rose to command roles and acknowledged authorizing bombings during the armed struggle.[2][3] Upon returning post-apartheid, Sisulu contributed to ANC economic policy as head of its economic transformation desk and held executive positions, including Deputy Minister of Human Resources Development and CEO of the Land Bank, before his parliamentary leadership focused on legislative oversight and institutional reform.[2][1] His career reflects the intergenerational continuity of ANC involvement, from liberation warfare to democratic governance, though tempered by the party's broader challenges in economic delivery. Speakership and Fourth Parliament Election as Speaker (2009) Max Sisulu was elected as Speaker of the National Assembly on 6 May 2009, during the first sitting of the Fourth Parliament following the 22 April general elections.[37][38] The African National Congress (ANC), which had secured 264 of the 400 seats with 65.9% of the national vote, nominated Sisulu without opposition from other parties, Children and Personal Losses Max Sisulu and his wife, Elinor Batezat Sisulu, had at least four sons, including Mlungisi, Vuyisile, Sandile, and Duma. One son, Mlungisi (known as Lungi), born on August 20, 1966, in Moscow to Max Sisulu and Mercy Vuthela during his parents' exile, died on January 3, 2008, in London from cerebral malaria while pursuing studies there.[87][86][95] The Department of Foreign Affairs confirmed the death, attributing it to complications from the illness, and the ANC issued statements expressing sorrow over the "untimely" loss of the young family member.[96]Sisulu publicly demonstrated restraint in response to the tragedy, continuing his parliamentary commitments without evident interruption; he was elected Speaker of the National Assembly just over a year later, on May 14, 2009.[87] No verifiable records indicate that the loss derailed his professional path or led to prolonged withdrawal from public life, diverging from occasional ANC-framed accounts that invoke familial suffering to underscore inherited resilience amid political adversity. Such claims, while rhetorically potent in liberation narratives, lack causal evidence here, as Sisulu's tenure proceeded amid routine legislative demands.[97] Retirement and Later Activities Post-Parliamentary Engagements (2014–Present) Following his resignation from the National Assembly on 29 May 2014, Max Sisulu transitioned to non-partisan roles outside elected office, focusing on advisory and commemorative activities aligned with his family's anti-apartheid legacy.[1] He has served on the advisory board of the Canon Collins Trust, a UK-based charity supporting postgraduate scholarships for southern African students, where he contributes to governance alongside figures such as his wife, Elinor Sisulu, emphasizing educational empowerment in post-apartheid contexts.[98] This involvement reflects a shift toward philanthropy rather than political leadership, with no records of seeking re-election or formal ANC positions thereafter.[2]Sisulu has maintained a low-profile presence at historical commemorations, underscoring his elder statesman role. On 12 June 2024, he attended the 60th anniversary event of the Rivonia Trial at the Palace of Justice in Pretoria, accompanied by his wife, honoring the 1964 trial that convicted his father, Walter Sisulu, and others of sabotage.[99] Such appearances highlight continuity in preserving struggle-era memory without active policy engagement.In 2025, Sisulu marked his 80th birthday on 23 August, receiving public tributes from institutions like Parliament and the Ahmed Kathrada Foundation, which praised his lifelong commitment to democratic principles.[100] These acknowledgments affirm his retirement status, centered on reflective participation rather than operational roles, amid a broader pattern of diminished visibility since 2014.[101] Public Commentary and Legacy Reflections In April 2016, shortly after the Constitutional Court's ruling on President Jacob Zuma's non-compliance with the Public Protector's report regarding upgrades to his Nkandla homestead, Sisulu publicly reflected on the erosion of parliamentary integrity during his tenure. He described the events as "difficult and painful" to observe, acknowledging that Parliament's credibility had suffered a significant blow and emphasizing the need for it to "put its house in order" to restore public trust.[56] This admission highlighted lapses in oversight mechanisms under ANC dominance, where partisan considerations had undermined accountability.By March 2018, amid South Africa's enduring socioeconomic disparities—evidenced by a Gini coefficient of around 0.63, one of the highest globally, and racial wealth gaps persisting three decades post-apartheid—Sisulu urged citizens to sustain the pursuit of a non-racial, equal society.[102] His appeal, delivered as a veteran ANC figure, implicitly critiqued the slow pace of transformation under ANC governance, including failures in equitable resource distribution despite policy rhetoric on redress. Yet, such calls occurred against a backdrop of empirical shortfalls, such as youth unemployment exceeding 60% in some metrics and stalled initiatives like comprehensive land reform, where redistribution had achieved less than 10% of targeted agricultural land by the late 2010s.[102]In the 2020s, Sisulu's public engagements have remained sparse, with minimal direct interventions on pressing issues like the ANC's cadre deployment policy, which critics from independent and market-oriented analyses attribute to systemic governance harms, including corruption proliferation and inefficiency in state-owned enterprises.[103] Despite this reticence, his continued association with ANC events suggests enduring loyalty to the party's framework, even as data underscores policy inertia—such as land expropriation debates yielding no substantive progress by 2025, perpetuating rural inequities. Conservative commentators have framed this alignment as emblematic of legacy insulation from accountability, contrasting Sisulu's early anti-apartheid credentials with the ANC's post-liberation deviations toward patronage networks.[81] Assessment and Legacy Achievements in the Struggle and Parliament Max Sisulu went into exile in the 1960s following persecution by apartheid security forces due to his family's prominent anti-apartheid activism. He joined Umkhonto we Sizwe (MK), the military wing of the African National Congress (ANC), and contributed to its operations during the armed struggle against apartheid.[104] His endurance in exile included surviving a 1982 assassination attempt in Lusaka, Zambia, where ANC deputy chief representative Adolphus Mvemve was killed alongside two others, underscoring the risks faced by ANC personnel abroad in sustaining pressure on the regime.[104]Upon returning post-1990 unbanning of the ANC, Sisulu entered Parliament as a member from 1994 to 1998, serving as chairperson of the Portfolio Committee on the Auditor-General, which oversaw public financial accountability during the nascent democratic transition.[26] Elected Speaker of the National Assembly on 6 May 2009, he presided over proceedings until 8 May 2014, becoming the first black South African to hold the post-apartheid speakership and ensuring the assembly's business proceeded under established rules amid a multiparty environment.[40][26] During his tenure, he facilitated special debates on national issues, exercising the Speaker's authority to address urgent public concerns.[105] Critiques of Effectiveness and ANC Legacy Critics of Max Sisulu's tenure as Speaker of the National Assembly from 2009 to 2014 have pointed to the institution's limited success in curbing executive overreach and corruption during Jacob Zuma's presidency, a period marked by state capture that drained an estimated hundreds of billions of rand from public coffers through undue influence by private interests, including the Gupta family.[106] Although Sisulu initiated probes, such as appointing a committee in response to Public Protector Thuli Madonsela's Nkandla report on Zuma's homestead upgrades funded improperly by state entities, parliamentary oversight mechanisms under his leadership were widely seen as insufficient to prevent or expose systemic graft, with one analysis estimating direct costs exceeding $100 billion in Zuma's second term alone.[107][108] This reflects broader ANC governance shortcomings, where one-party dominance fostered institutional erosion rather than robust accountability, as evidenced by repeated failures to enforce accountability on executive actions despite constitutional mandates.Economic policies emblematic of ANC rule, such as Black Economic Empowerment (BEE), have drawn scrutiny for perpetuating concentrated wealth among a narrow elite rather than dismantling white monopoly capital or fostering broad-based prosperity, with unemployment hovering above 30% and inequality metrics showing minimal progress for the black majority three decades post-apartheid.[109][110] Critics argue BEE's scorecard-driven approach incentivized fronting and cronyism over merit and productivity, failing to create sustainable black-owned enterprises and instead entrenching racial quotas that deterred investment and skill development.[111] Sisulu's alignment with ANC policy frameworks during his parliamentary career implicitly endorsed this paradigm, which empirical data links to stalled growth and persistent poverty, undermining claims of transformative efficacy.While leftist observers credit Sisulu's familial anti-apartheid symbolism with bolstering ANC morale and symbolic unity, centrist and right-leaning analysts emphasize causal factors like entrenched cadre deployment and patronage networks—hallmarks of ANC dominance—as primary drivers of governance decay, evidenced by the party's national vote share dropping to 40.18% in the 2024 elections, its first non-majority since 1994.[112] This electoral contraction, amid public disillusionment with service delivery failures and corruption scandals, dilutes Sisulu's legacy, tying it to an ANC record where narrative triumphs over verifiable outcomes in metrics like GDP per capita stagnation and infrastructure collapse.[113] Proponents of multiparty competition argue that such dominance, unchallenged during Sisulu's speakership, prioritized loyalty over institutional integrity, contributing to South Africa's downgraded credit ratings and investor flight.
