It is home ported at the CG Academy, New London, Connecticut. It is the only active commissioned sailing vessel in the U.S. maritime services. She is one of five such training barques in world. Remarkably, her surviving sister ships include the Mircea of Romania, Sagres II of Portugal, Gorch Fock of Germany, and Tovarich of Russia. Today's Eagle, the seventh in a long line of proud cutters to bear the name, was built in 1936 by the Blohm & Voss Shipyard, Hamburg, Germany, as a training vessel for German Navy cadets. It was commissioned Horst Wessel and served as a training ship for the Kriegsmarine throughout World War II. Following World War II, the Horst Wessel, in the age-old custom of capture and seizure, was taken as a war prize by the United States. Initially, the Soviet Union selected Horst Wessel during the division of Nazi vessels by the victorious Allies. The four available sailing ships had been divided into three lots--two large merchant ships being grouped together. The Soviets drew number 1, Great Britain number 2, and the U.S. number 3. Before the results of the draw were officially announced, the U.S representative, through quiet diplomacy, convinced the Soviets to trade draws. And so, on May 15, 1946, the German barque was commissioned into U.S. Coast Guard service as the Eagle and sailed from Bremerhaven, Germany to New London, Connecticut. On her voyage to the United States she followed Columbus's route across the mid-Atlantic. She rode out a hurricane during her trip and arrived in New London safely. She weathered another hurricane in September 1954 while enroute to Bermuda. She hosted OpSail in New York as part of the World's Fair in 1964. She again hosted OpSail in 1976 during the United States' Bicentennial celebration. She hosted the centennial celebration for the Statue of Liberty in 1986 as well. One of the major controversies regarding the cutter was generated when the Coast Guard decided to add the "racing stripe" to her otherwise unadorned hull in mid-1976. She was the last cutter so painted and many in the sailing community decried the new paint job. Eagle serves as a seagoing classroom for approximately 175 cadets and instructors from the U.S. Coast Guard Academy. Sailing in Eagle, cadets handle more than 20,000 square feet of sail and 5 miles of rigging. Over 200 lines must be coordinated during a major ship maneuver. The sails can provide the equivalent of several thousand through-shaft horsepower. The ship readily takes to the task for which it was designed. Eagle's hull is built of steel, four-tenths of an inch thick. It has two full length steel decks with a platform deck below and a raised forecastle and quarterdeck. The weather decks are three-inch-thick teak over steel. --- [USCGC Eagle (WIX-327) - Wikipedia] ---
USCGC Eagle (WIX-327), formerly Horst Wessel and also known as Barque Eagle, is a 295-foot (90 m) barque used as a training cutter for future officers of the United States Coast Guard. She is one of only two active commissioned sailing vessels in the United States military today, along with USS Constitution. She is the seventh Coast Guard cutter to bear the name in a line dating back to 1792, including the Revenue Cutter Eagle.
Each summer, Eagle deploys with cadets from the United States Coast Guard Academy and candidates from the Officer Candidate School for periods ranging from a week to two months. These voyages fulfill multiple roles. The primary mission is training the cadets and officer candidates, but the ship also performs a public relations role for the Coast Guard and the United States. Often, Eagle makes calls at foreign ports as a goodwill ambassador.
The ship was built as the German sail training ship Horst Wessel in 1936; it served to train German sailors in sail techniques until decommissioned at the start of World War II. The vessel was given anti-aircraft armament and re-commissioned in 1942. At the end of the war, Horst Wessel was taken by the U.S. as war reparations.

USCGC Eagle (WIX-327) Historical Background Origins and Construction as Horst Wessel ![Segelschulschiff Horst Wessel][float-right] The sail training barque Horst Wessel was ordered on 1 October 1935 as part of the German Navy's efforts to expand its training fleet following the 1932 sinking of the school ship Niobe, which highlighted the need for additional purpose-built vessels to instruct cadets in traditional seamanship.[12][13] Named after Horst Wessel, a Sturmabteilung member killed in 1930 whose eponymous hymn became a Nazi anthem, the ship symbolized the regime's ideological integration into naval traditions.[11] She formed the second vessel in a series of steel-hulled, three-masted barques designed for the Reichsmarine—soon to become the Kriegsmarine—alongside sisters like Gorch Fock (launched 1933) and later Albert Leo Schlageter.[3] Construction commenced at the Blohm & Voss shipyard in Hamburg, Germany, with the keel laid down on 15 February 1936.[12] The hull, built of steel to withstand rigorous ocean voyages, measured 295 feet in length and displaced 1,813 tons at full load, equipped with a hybrid sail-and-auxiliary engine configuration for cadet instruction under varied conditions.[12] Launched on 13 June 1936 in a ceremony attended by Adolf Hitler and Rudolf Hess, the vessel underwent fitting out before commissioning into naval service on 17 September 1936 as the flagship of the Kriegsmarine's sail training squadron.[12][14] This design emphasized endurance and handling, with extensive rigging to teach square-rig sail management essential for naval officer development in an era of transitioning from sail to mechanized fleets.[1]Kriegsmarine Service During World War II With the outbreak of World War II on September 1, 1939, the Horst Wessel ceased long-distance training voyages in the North Atlantic due to heightened risks from Allied naval blockades and submarine warfare.[13] She was decommissioned from active sailing operations and relocated to Stralsund in the Baltic Sea, functioning as a stationary training platform for the Marine Hitler Youth, the naval branch of the Hitler Youth organization, where cadets received instruction in seamanship fundamentals.[12][13] In 1941, the vessel entered reserve status as a stationary training ship moored alongside a tender for support, but by mid-May, she resumed limited training duties from the Kriegsmarine's homeport at Kiel, Germany, emphasizing sail-handling and naval discipline under constrained wartime conditions.[11] These activities avoided exposure to open-ocean threats, prioritizing the development of petty officers and junior personnel through shore-based and short-range exercises rather than extended cruises.[14] The Horst Wessel fulfilled this instructional role for the Kriegsmarine until the war's end in May 1945, contributing to personnel training amid resource shortages and strategic priorities that de-emphasized non-combatant sail vessels.[4] No records indicate involvement in combat operations or significant voyages during this period, reflecting the ship's adaptation to defensive, land-proximate functions in the face of maritime interdiction.[12] Capture as War Reparations and Transfer to U.S. Control Following the unconditional surrender of Nazi Germany on 8 May 1945, the sail training ship Horst Wessel was among the Kriegsmarine vessels seized by Allied forces as war reparations for damages inflicted during World War II.[1] Stationed in Bremerhaven at war's end, the vessel was designated a prize and allocated to the United States under the division of German naval assets agreed upon by the Allied powers.[4] This allocation reflected broader reparations policies, where operational ships like the Horst Wessel—one of three steel-hulled barques built for the German navy—were repurposed rather than scrapped.[15] In preparation for transfer, the ship underwent evaluation and minor repairs in Bremerhaven to ensure seaworthiness for an Atlantic crossing.[3] A U.S. Coast Guard prize crew, consisting of officers and enlisted personnel, assumed control, with assistance from retained members of the original German crew to leverage their familiarity with the vessel's rigging and systems.[4] This hybrid crewing arrangement minimized risks during the initial shakedown and voyage.[13] On 15 May 1946, the Horst Wessel was formally commissioned into U.S. Coast Guard service as USCGC Eagle (WIX-327) during a ceremony in Bremerhaven, marking the official transfer to American control.[4] The barque then departed Germany under sail and auxiliary power, arriving at its new homeport in New London, Connecticut, on 16 August 1946, after a 3,000-nautical-mile journey that included stops for provisioning and training drills.[3] This acquisition addressed the Coast Guard's postwar need for a dedicated sail-training platform to replace aging vessels, enabling hands-on instruction in traditional maritime skills for academy cadets.[1]Initial U.S. Coast Guard Commissioning and Renaming Following its capture by Allied forces in April 1945 and designation as a war prize, the former German training barque Horst Wessel underwent evaluation and minor repairs in Bremerhaven, Germany, under U.S. Navy oversight before transfer to the U.S. Coast Guard as partial reparations for wartime damages inflicted on American merchant shipping.[3][16] The vessel retained its original steel hull, three-masted barque rig, and auxiliary diesel engine, with no major structural alterations at this stage, preserving its capability for sail training while adapting it symbolically to U.S. service.[4] On May 15, 1946, the ship was formally commissioned into the U.S. Coast Guard as USCGC Eagle (WIX-327) during a shipboard ceremony held in Bremerhaven harbor.[17][13] The renaming from Horst Wessel—a name honoring a Nazi Party figure—to Eagle symbolized the vessel's repurposing as a symbol of American maritime resilience and training excellence, with the new designation drawing from the bald eagle as the national emblem.[3][15]Lieutenant Commander Arthur H. McGowan assumed command, supported by a plankowner crew of six Coast Guard officers and 55 enlisted personnel, who integrated with retained German sailors for operational continuity during the transition.[13] Immediately after commissioning, Eagle embarked on its maiden U.S. voyage under Coast Guard colors, departing Bremerhaven on May 29, 1946, bound for its homeport at the U.S. Coast Guard Academy in New London, Connecticut, covering approximately 3,400 nautical miles across the Atlantic.[3][17] The transit, completed in 40 days despite variable weather and reliance on both sails and auxiliary power, validated the ship's seaworthiness and the mixed crew's proficiency, arriving in New London on July 8, 1946, to begin its role in cadet training.[4] This initial commissioning marked the only active sailing vessel in the U.S. military inventory at the time, emphasizing hands-on seamanship instruction over mechanized operations.[18]Design and Engineering Features Original German Specifications and Construction Details The Horst Wessel was ordered on 1 October 1935 as part of the Kriegsmarine's expansion of sail training vessels, following the prototype Gorch Fock. Her keel was laid down on 15 February 1936 at the Blohm & Voss shipyard in Hamburg, Germany.[12][19] The ship was launched on 13 June 1936 and completed on 16 September, entering commission on 17 September 1936.[12][20][19] As a three-masted barque with a steel hull, the Horst Wessel represented an improved design over the Gorch Fock, featuring larger dimensions and steel spars instead of wooden yards for greater durability.[21] She measured 295 feet in length, with a beam of 39 feet and a draft of 17 feet.[12][21] Displacement at full load was 1,813 tons, with a gross register tonnage of 1,390.[12][21] The vessel carried 22 sails totaling 1,983 square meters (approximately 21,350 square feet) in area, enabling a maximum speed under sail of 18 knots.[21] Her mainmast rose 149 feet above the deck.[21]Propulsion was augmented by a 750 horsepower auxiliary diesel engine, providing a speed of 10 knots under power.[21] Intended primarily for training Kriegsmarine cadets in seamanship, the ship featured no significant armament in her original configuration.[12]Post-War Modifications and Adaptations Upon transfer to U.S. control in 1945 as war reparations, the former Horst Wessel underwent initial repairs and modifications in Bremerhaven, Germany, to meet American safety and operational standards prior to its transatlantic voyage and commissioning.[22] These included structural assessments and basic refits to ensure seaworthiness, after which the vessel sailed under Coast Guard command to New London, Connecticut, arriving on January 7, 1946.[4] It was formally commissioned as USCGC Eagle (WIX-327) on May 15, 1946, following additional adaptations such as repainting in Coast Guard colors and removal of Kriegsmarine insignia.[4][18] In 1965, the propulsion system was upgraded with a Maschinenfabrik-Augsburg-Nürnberg (MAN) diesel engine rated at 750 horsepower, replacing the original wartime configuration to enhance auxiliary power for training operations.[4] Concurrently, navigation and detection capabilities were modernized with the installation of AN/SPS-23 surface search radar, AN/SPA-4 radar display, and AN/UQN-1D sonar, adapting the barque for post-war maritime training environments that incorporated electronic aids alongside traditional sail handling.[4] A major overhaul occurred between 1979 and 1983 at the U.S. Coast Guard Yard in Curtis Bay, Maryland, focusing on safety, performance, and watertight integrity through phased alterations to internal subdivision, hull strengthening, and rigging systems.[23] This included replacement of the 1965 MAN engine with a Caterpillar diesel unit in 1980 for improved reliability and fuel efficiency.[4] Mid-1976 saw the addition of the iconic Coast Guard "racing stripe" to the hull, a visual identifier applied despite initial resistance from traditional sailing enthusiasts.[4][24] Subsequent adaptations addressed evolving training needs, such as installing washing machines and modifying berthing areas in the late 20th century to accommodate female cadets, expanding capacity from an all-male configuration to support co-ed operations without compromising the vessel's sailing rig.[25] The original eagle figurehead was also revised to incorporate the U.S. Coast Guard crest in its talons, symbolizing American service while retaining the ship's traditional iconography.[26] These changes preserved Eagle's core design as a steel-hulled barque while integrating mechanical and habitability improvements for sustained cadet instruction.[27]Symbolic Elements Including Figurehead The figurehead of USCGC Eagle depicts a bald eagle in flight, embodying American symbolism of vigilance, strength, and maritime tradition while marking the vessel's post-World War II repurposing from its origins as the German sail training ship Horst Wessel. Constructed in 1936 at the Blohm & Voss shipyard in Hamburg, the original figurehead portrayed a Reichsadler—a heraldic eagle clutching a swastika-emblazoned wreath—consistent with Third Reich naval iconography intended to evoke imperial authority and ideological loyalty. After the ship's seizure as war reparations in 1946, all Nazi insignia, including the swastika, were removed to facilitate its integration into U.S. service, with the eagle motif retained but adapted to signify national rebirth rather than prior regime affiliations.[28] Over decades, the figurehead underwent multiple replacements to address wear, scale mismatches, and material degradation, each iteration refining its alignment with U.S. Coast Guard identity. In 1953, a 5-foot eagle salvaged from the historic Revenue Cutter Salmon P. Chase was installed temporarily, appearing undersized on the 295-foot barque; this yielded in 1968 to a restored version of the modified original German carving. A fiberglass replica followed in 1971 for durability, replaced in 1976 by a 13-foot mahogany eagle. The extant figurehead, fitted circa 2022 during a $29 million overhaul, was handcrafted over three years by woodcarver Shane Kinman using a fiberglass core with internal supports, expanding foam fill, and a quarter-inch gold-painted skin; it spans 15 feet in length and weighs 2,000 pounds, featuring an anatomically precise bald eagle design and a teak-inlaid Coast Guard shield supplanting any residual Nazi-era motifs. This evolution underscores causal adaptations for seaworthiness and symbolism, prioritizing empirical functionality over historical fidelity.[28] On July 1, 2025, heavy seas near San Francisco dislodged the figurehead's rear section, necessitating repairs that affirm the ongoing empirical challenges of preserving wooden or composite maritime symbols in operational service. Beyond the figurehead, the vessel's symbolic repertoire includes the U.S. Coast Guard ensign and the ship's dedicated emblem, which integrate heraldic eagles and shields to denote official training mission and lineage continuity.[29]Training and Operational Mission Cadet Seamanship and Leadership Training Program The Cadet Seamanship and Leadership Training Program utilizes USCGC Eagle to deliver practical maritime instruction to United States Coast Guard Academy cadets, focusing on the operation of a traditional square-rigged barque in open-ocean conditions. This training bridges academic theory with hands-on application, requiring cadets to manage the vessel's 22,000 square feet of sails and six miles of rigging while contending with variable winds, waves, and currents.[30][31] Every cadet completes a minimum of six weeks aboard Eagle across their academy years, with underclassmen typically performing enlisted duties such as helm watch and lookout while upperclassmen assume junior officer roles leading teams in deck operations. Core skills include basic seamanship—encompassing knotting, splicing, and sail handling—celestial and piloting navigation, ship-handling maneuvers, and engineering maintenance tasks like engine monitoring and aloft repairs.[31][30][2] Leadership development occurs through a structured watch system and hierarchical progression, where cadets advance from basic responsibilities to commanding divisions during evolutions like tacking or reefing sails, thereby cultivating decision-making under stress, resilience, and adherence to Coast Guard core values. Damage control drills and emergency simulations further reinforce teamwork and rapid response capabilities, essential for afloat operations.[30][2][32] The program's demanding physical and mental requirements—such as climbing rigging in rough seas or maintaining vigilance during extended watches—build character, conquer individual fears, and emphasize collective effort for vessel safety, preparing cadets for commissioning as officers capable of leading in high-stakes maritime environments.[30][31]Structure of At-Sea Training Voyages The at-sea training voyages of USCGC Eagle occur annually during the summer, forming a key element of seamanship instruction for U.S. Coast Guard Academy cadets and officer candidates. These cruises generally last 120 to 160 days for the vessel, departing from New London, Connecticut, and incorporating transatlantic or trans-Pacific legs with port visits to Europe, the Caribbean, or the U.S. West Coast.[33][34] Cadet involvement is divided into rotating phases to accommodate class sizes, with each segment typically spanning six weeks to ensure every Academy cadet completes at least one voyage. Incoming freshmen ("swabs") are split into three groups for phased embarkations, third-class cadets (juniors) into two phases, and upperclassmen integrate into leadership-focused rotations. Up to 150 cadets per phase augment the permanent crew of about 50 active-duty members, who provide instruction while operating the ship under sail or auxiliary power.[31][33][8] Phase changes coincide with Friday port arrivals, followed by Saturday turnover, minimizing downtime and allowing brief in-port training for new arrivals. At sea, the routine centers on four-hour watch rotations for helm, deck, and engineering duties, interspersed with evolutions like setting or furling the barque's 22,000 square feet of sail across six miles of rigging. Cadets conduct celestial navigation, damage control simulations, and maintenance under varying weather, progressing from basic tasks for underclassmen to divisional command for seniors.[30][35][30] This phased structure emphasizes practical application of physics, meteorology, and human factors in a low-tech environment, building resilience and decision-making without modern aids, while port calls facilitate cultural exposure and public relations.[31][30]Integration with U.S. Coast Guard Academy Curriculum The USCGC Eagle serves as a seagoing classroom integral to the U.S. Coast Guard Academy's professional development and leadership curriculum, where every cadet completes a minimum of six weeks of sail training aboard the vessel to apply theoretical knowledge in practical maritime operations.[31] This hands-on experience emphasizes traditional seamanship skills, including navigation, ship-handling, and engineering, while fostering teamwork, decision-making under variable conditions such as wind, waves, and currents—challenges not replicable on modern powered vessels.[31][30] The program's structure integrates with the Academy's broader summer training evolutions, transforming the barque's labor-intensive environment—managing over 22,000 square feet of sail and six miles of rigging—into a rigorous test of character and resilience.[30] Training occurs primarily during cadet summers, with participation phased by academic year to build progressive proficiency and responsibility. Incoming freshmen during Swab Summer, the initial military indoctrination period, are divided into three groups for one-week rotations aboard Eagle, introducing basic nautical traditions and seamanship fundamentals.[8] Third-class cadets (rising juniors) undertake extended phases in their sophomore summer, typically comprising two segments totaling several weeks, where they stand watches, perform deck evolutions, and qualify in core competencies like celestial navigation and damage control.[36][8] First-class cadets (seniors) join select summer voyages in leadership capacities, the vessel's transfer to the U.S. Coast Guard as a war prize. Commander Gordon P. McGowan assumed command in Bremerhaven, Germany, leading a small crew to prepare the ship for its transatlantic crossing, departing on January 31, 1946, and arriving in New York on May 15, 1946.[13] McGowan's leadership established the foundation for Eagle's role as a training barque, with subsequent officers drawn primarily from Coast Guard Academy graduates experienced in maritime operations and cadet instruction.[2] Changes in command occur through formal ceremonies symbolizing the transfer of authority and responsibility, typically every two to three years to align with operational cycles and career progressions.
