Facts About Teotihuacan Ocelot The Teotihuacan Ocelot is a captivating alabaster sculpture unearthed at the ancient site of Teotihuacan in central Mexico. This artwork, also referred to as the Teotihuacán Ocelot, portrays a feline, specifically an ocelot, meticulously carved from a single piece of premium alabaster. Depicted in a reclining position, the ocelot is believed to have functioned as an offering vessel. The sculpture features geometric patterns and designs that echo the funerary masks and architectural motifs characteristic of Teotihuacan. Originally, the eyes of the ocelot would have been inlaid with shells or precious stones, and a hollow depression on its back was likely intended to hold temple offerings. It is speculated that these offerings could have included human hearts used in ritual sacrifices. Discovered in 1889 by a laborer at the base of the Pyramid of the Sun in Teotihuacan, the sculpture was initially offered to the Museo Nacional but was not acquired. Subsequently, it was sold to an English traveler and ultimately made its way to the British Museum in 1926, courtesy of the Christy Fund. Interestingly, a similar sculpture made of calcite, depicting a jaguar, was also found at the site and is now part of Mexico's national collection.
