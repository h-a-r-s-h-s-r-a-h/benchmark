--- [Abasto de Buenos Aires] ---
The Abasto Shopping is one of the largest shopping malls in Buenos Aires, Argentina, and a landmark example of Art Deco architecture in Latin America.
The building served as the city's central wholesale fruit and vegetable market (Mercado de Abasto) from 1893 to 1984. Designed by architects José Luis Delpini, Viktor Sulčič, and Raúl Bes—the same team responsible for La Bombonera stadium—the current Art Deco structure was completed in 1934 and represents a pioneering use of reinforced concrete in Argentine architecture. 
After the market closed in 1984, the building remained abandoned for over a decade before being purchased in 1996 by IRSA, the real estate company led by Eduardo Elsztain.IRSA restored the building's historic façade while converting it into a modern shopping center, which opened in November 1998.
The area surrounding the Abasto has deep cultural significance in tango history, particularly as the neighborhood where legendary singer Carlos Gardel—known as El Morocho del Abasto ("the dark-haired guy from Abasto")—lived from 1927 to 1933. Today, the surrounding area, though officially part of the Balvanera neighbourhood, is commonly referred to as Abasto. The shopping center is served by the adjoining Line B metro station Carlos Gardel.


--- [Viktor Sulcic] ---
Viktor Sulcic was a Slovenian born Art Deco architect in Argentina. After completing his studies of architecture in Florence and Bologna, Sulcic emigrated to Argentina in 1924. He joined two other architects there: Jose Luis Delpini and Raul Bes. The two most famous buildings, projected by the team, are located in Buenos Aires: fruit and vegetable market (in 1999 converted into a shopping mall) Abasto (1934), and Boca Juniors stadium La Bombonera (1940). --- [Mercado de abasto] ---
The Mercado de Abasto is a landmark building in the Abasto neighborhood of Buenos Aires, Argentina, originally established as the city's central wholesale market for fruits, vegetables, and flowers, operating from 1893 until 1984 before being repurposed into a large shopping complex in 1998.[1] Historical Development Constructed initially in 1893 to meet the growing demand for fresh produce distribution amid Buenos Aires' rapid urbanization, the Mercado de Abasto quickly became a vital economic hub, handling the bulk of the city's supply chain for perishable goods.[2] By the early 20th century, it symbolized the porteño golden age, incorporating innovative features like escalators, extensive telephone lines, and direct subway connectivity upon the line's opening in 1930.[1] The original structure was demolished in the 1930s due to overcrowding and obsolescence, leading to the construction of the current Art Deco edifice between 1934 and 1937, designed by architects José Luis Delpini, Raúl Bes, and Viktor Sulčič— the same team behind the iconic La Bombonera stadium.[1] This new building, Argentina's first to use cement for both its façade and interiors, featured a striking Corrientes Avenue entrance with five massive leaded-glass archways and earned an architectural prize in 1937 for its design.[3] A devastating fire in 1952 damaged parts of the facility, but its robust construction allowed for swift repairs overseen by Delpini, ensuring continued operations.[1] Economic and Cultural Significance At its peak, the Mercado de Abasto was more than a commercial center; it was a cultural touchstone for working-class porteños, inspiring the 1955 Argentine film Mercado de Abasto, which portrayed its role in everyday life and tango lore— the neighborhood was also home to legendary singer Carlos Gardel.[1] The market's closure in 1984 stemmed from logistical challenges, including traffic congestion from produce trucks in the urban core, prompting relocation to the modern Mercado Central in La Matanza, Greater Buenos Aires.[1] Vacant for nearly 15 years, the site fell into disrepair, exacerbating local issues like crime and urban decay in the surrounding Abasto area during the 1980s and 1990s.[1] Modern Transformation In 1997, investor George Soros acquired the property and funded a $2 million restoration, reopening it as Abasto Shopping on November 10, 1998, which catalyzed neighborhood revitalization alongside projects like Puerto Madero.[1] Today, the complex spans approximately 116,000 square meters and is the largest shopping center within the City of Buenos Aires. It houses more than 230 stores with international brands (e.g., Adidas, Lacoste), a 12-screen cinema, food court offering Argentine parilla and kosher options—including the world's only kosher McDonald's outside Israel—plus family attractions like a children's museum, indoor theme park with a three-story Ferris wheel, and arcade.[4] Accessible via Line B subway at Carlos Gardel station and open daily from 10 a.m. to 10 p.m., it remains a bustling, family-oriented destination that preserves the building's Art Deco heritage while adapting to contemporary retail needs.[2] Plot Synopsis Mercado de Abasto is a 1955 Argentine musical comedy film directed by Lucas Demare, starring Tita Merello as Paulina.Set in the bustling Mercado de Abasto in 1950s Buenos Aires, the story revolves around Paulina, a dedicated market worker who navigates love and hardship amid the vibrant community of vendors and laborers. --- [Abasto Shopping: An Old-Time Market Turned Mall] ---
Abasto, the huge art deco style complex in the neighborhood of the same name, is Buenos Aires largest shopping mall. The mall’s distinguished façade along Corrientes Avenue features five huge leaded glass archways that are emblematic of the neighborhood, especially when lit in the evening. Inside, a huge atrium floods the building with natural light. The centralized mall features over 250 stores, a children’s museum, a small theme park, a food court, and a cinema with 12 screens. Buenos Aires’ Original Fruit & Vegetable Market The unique building was originally the site of Buenos Aires’ central vegetable market, inaugurated in 1893. It slowly grew outward as the population boomed, until the original structure was demolished in the 1930’s to make room for a larger building. The current Art Deco structure dates to 1934 and took three years to build. The building was designed by José Luis Delpini, Raul Bes and Slovenian-born architect, Viktor Sulčič — the same team that constructed Buenos Aires’ most famous stadium, La Bombonera in the La Boca neighborhood. The building highlights Buenos Aires’ golden age — the new market included all the latest amenities of the era including escalators, phone lines throughout the building and a connection with the underground subway. A 1952 fire in the building burned for various days but thanks to the sturdy old-world construction, Abasto survived the blaze and Delpini was able to reform the damaged parts. A 1955 movie called Mercado de Abasto (Abasto Market) set in the mall highlights what a central place the market played in everyday life. By 1984 Buenos Aires’ population had grown so much that housing the vegetable market in the center of the city became impractical due to the congestion the trucks caused in the neighborhood bringing in produce in from the province. Abasto was shut down and the vegetable market was relocated to the Mercado Central in the Buenos Aires province, near the international airport Ezieza, where it remains today. When Abasto Mall was an Empty, Blight in the Neighborhood To the dismay of residents, after the vegetable market shut down, the one-time jewel of the neighborhood remained closed until the end of the 1990’s. During that time the neighborhood around Abasto became notorious for squats, prostitution, and drug use and was generally avoided by people who didn’t live there. During Abasto’s nearly 15 years of hibernation, various proposals were thrown around to determine the building’s destiny while the neighborhood languished. One proposal included converting the market into an arts center similar to Paris’ Georges Pompidou Art Center. Abasto Mall’s Rebirth Alas, in 1997, Hungarian-American billionaire, George Soros (who was also behind the restoration of Alto Palermo and Galerías Pacífico) purchased the building and invested $2 million in its restoration. The resurrection of Abasto as a shopping mall in 1998 instantly ushered in a new era to the blighted barrio. The reopening of the building took place during the same time as the waterfront restoration of another dodgy neighborhood, Puerto Madero. In both cases, the restoration and reintroduction of commerce greatly improved the neighborhood. Shopping & Movies This mall has some decent shopping with international name brands such as Lacoste, Samsonite and Adidas and smaller national outlets. The movie theater is one of the city’s most comfortable and offers a range of films from Hollywood blockbusters to sentimental national productions. The prices at many of the smaller stores tend to be more reasonable than those at other malls such as Galerías Pacífico, but a costlier than at the maze of street-side stores in the surrounding neighborhood, which carry similar merchandise. Food Court & Kosher McDonalds Abasto Mall is a mall designed with kids in mind. In addition to the celebrated Children’s Museum, there’s an arcade and a small indoor theme park with a Ferris wheel that spans all three floors. The food court offers the usual parilla, pasta, pan-Asian offerings. Abasto’s food court also claims fame to the only Kosher McDonald’s outside Israel. The McD’s is a hangout for the Orthodox fast food lovers among the neighborhood’s surrounding Jewish community. There is also a Kosher Empanada outlet here. One of the nice things about Abasto Mall is that thanks to its vastness and open construction, it never seems to get really crowded. Even at Christmas time, when parents bring their kids here to see Santa Claus, things are quite relaxed.
