--- [Terry Jones (pastor) - Wikipedia] ---
Terry Jones (born October 1951) is an American anti-Islamic right-wing activist, who was the pastor of Dove World Outreach Center, a small nondenominational Christian church located, until July 2013, in Gainesville, Florida. He is the president of a political group, Stand Up America Now. He first gained national and international attention in 2010 for his plan to burn Qur'ans, the scripture of the Islamic religion, on the ninth anniversary of the September 11 attacks and for burning the Qur'an afterward.
Jones was an independent candidate in the 2012 and 2016 United States presidential elections.

Terry Jones is an American pastor who founded and led the Dove World Outreach Center, a small nondenominationalChristian church in Gainesville, Florida, with around 50 members as of 2010.[1] The church promoted anti-Islam messages, including signs declaring "Islam is of the devil," and organized protests against the construction of mosques and Islamic centers.[1]Jones achieved notoriety for his threats to burn copies of the Quran as a protest against what he described as the violent teachings of Islam, beginning with the announcement of "International Burn a Quran Day" planned for September 11, 2010, which drew widespread condemnation from political leaders and religious figures but was ultimately suspended after negotiations.[2][3] However, in March 2011, he oversaw the burning of a Quran following a mock trial at his church, an act that was filmed and posted online, precipitating riots in Afghanistan that resulted in at least 20 deaths, including UN staff.[4][5] In 2013, Jones was arrested en route to another planned Quran burning event involving nearly 3,000 fuel-soaked copies, one for each victim of the 9/11 attacks, on charges related to illegal fuel transportation.[6][7] His actions led to travel bans, such as from the United Kingdom in 2011, citing risks to public security.[8] Early life and education Childhood and family Terry Jones was born in October 1951 in Cape Girardeau, Missouri.[9] He grew up in this Midwestern city, a environment typical of small-town America in the post-World War II era.[9] Publicly available details on his immediate family and early home life are limited, with no verified records of parental occupations or household religious practices from his formative years. Local accounts indicate he had a younger brother named Gary, with whom he shared the Cape Girardeau upbringing.[10] This period preceded his later involvement in missionary work abroad, which began in adulthood.[11] Education and initial career Jones graduated from Central High School in Cape Girardeau, Missouri, in 1969.[11] He then attended Southeast Missouri State University for two years but did not complete a degree. Jones later received an honorary doctorate from an unaccredited theology school in California, which he has referenced in self-description as "Doctor."[11][12] No records indicate formal attendance at accredited Bible colleges or seminaries focused on fundamentalist theology.Prior to entering ministry, Jones worked in hotel management, including as an assistant hotel manager in the late 1970s and as a hotel manager in Cape Girardeau, Missouri, by 1979.[13][9] In 1979, while employed in this capacity, he transitioned into religious work by becoming an assistant pastor with Maranatha Campus Ministries, a small evangelical group operating in the region spanning Missouri and Kentucky.[9][14] This role marked his initial involvement in preaching and pastoral duties on a limited scale, predating independent church leadership. By the early 1980s, Jones had shifted to full-time ministry, including missionary activities in Europe.[15] Ministry and theological positions Founding and leadership of Dove World Outreach Center Terry Jones assumed leadership of the Dove World Outreach Center, a nondenominational Christian church in Gainesville, Florida, in 1996 alongside his wife, Sylvia, following its establishment in 1986 by Don Northrup.[16][1] The church, originally founded at its northwest Gainesville location, operated as a small fundamentalist congregation emphasizing independent Bible study sessions and literal scriptural adherence under Jones's direction.[16][17]Membership remained limited, typically numbering around 50 individuals during Jones's tenure, reflecting its fringe status within broader evangelical circles and focus on doctrinal purity over expansion.[18] Church activities centered on internal operations, including unpaid labor from members for maintenance and a affiliated furniture business, which consumed much of its resources.[19][20]Jones's leadership was characterized by authoritarian control, with reports from former members describing demands for strict obedience, extended unpaid work hours—up to 72 per week—and treatment of the church as a personal domain that prioritized his directives over collaborative governance.[21][20] This approach rejected ecumenical outreach, fostering an insular environment aligned with fundamentalist principles of biblical inerrancy and separation from mainstream denominations.[11] Core theological beliefs Terry Jones led the Dove World Outreach Center, a non-denominational charismatic Christian church characterized by fundamentalist doctrines emphasizing the authority and literal interpretation of the Bible.[22][17] Adherents under his leadership upheld biblical inerrancy, viewing Scripture as the infallible word of God without error in its original manuscripts, and rejected modernist or liberal reinterpretations of Christian teachings that accommodate contemporary cultural shifts.[22] This stance aligned with broader Pentecostal traditions, incorporating beliefs in ongoing spiritual gifts such as prophecy and healing, while prioritizing direct adherence to biblical precepts over denominational hierarchies.[17][23]Central to Jones's theology was the doctrine of salvation exclusively through faith in Jesus Christ as the sole mediator between God and humanity, echoing evangelical emphases on personal conversion and born-again experiences.[24] He promoted evangelism as a mandate for Christians to proclaim this message universally, critiquing perceived weaknesses in mainstream efforts that failed to confront societal spiritual decay aggressively.[25] Jones framed Christian life as participation in spiritual warfare against demonic influences and moral corruption, drawing from Pentecostal interpretations of Ephesians 6:12, where believers battle not against flesh and blood but principalities and powers of darkness infiltrating culture and institutions.[17]On social issues, Jones's teachings condemned homosexuality as a sin explicitly prohibited by Scripture, reflected in church signage proclaiming "Homo sex is sin" and public demonstrations opposing gay rights as contrary to God's design for human sexuality.[26][27] This position extended to broader calls for repentance from behaviors deemed biblically immoral, positioning the church as a prophetic voice against what Jones saw as satanic encroachments on traditional Christian ethics.[28] Views on Islam and Sharia law Terry Jones, pastor of Dove World Outreach Center, articulated a view of Islam as a doctrinally deceptive and violent religion fundamentally opposed to Christianity, asserting in 2010 that "Islam is of the devil" and constitutes "a deceptive religion [and] a violent religion" that leads followers astray from biblical truth.[29] This perspective, outlined in his book Islam Is of the Devil published that year, framed Islam not as a benign faith but as an ideology rooted in spiritual falsehoods incompatible with Judeo-Christian scripture, emphasizing contrasts such as the Quran's teachings on jihad and severe penalties for apostasy—which he interpreted as endorsing violence against non-believers and defectors—against the New Testament's emphasis on love and forgiveness. In a 2011 mock trial of the Quran at his church on March 20, Jones presided as judge over proceedings where the text was charged and convicted of "crimes against humanity," including incitement to murder and support for doctrines punishing apostasy with death, underscoring his contention that such elements render Islam antithetical to Christian ethics.[30]Jones extended these critiques to Sharia law, warning in 2014 that it represents "a major threat to the U.S." and is "not compatible with Western society and Western thinking," positioning its adoption as an erosion of constitutional protections.[31] He cited empirical patterns in Islamic-majority nations, such as violent responses to perceived insults—including death threats against him personally following Quran-related actions—as evidence of Sharia's intolerance toward free speech, women's autonomy under restrictive gender norms, and religious liberty for minorities and converts.[32] These concerns, voiced in planned rallies like the June 14, 2014, event outside a Dearborn, Michigan, mosque, aimed to highlight Islam's "violent core message" and resist its expansion, which he saw as doctrinally expansionist and suppressive of non-Islamic freedoms.[33]Underlying Jones's positions was a motivation derived from evangelical imperatives to defend biblical principles against ideologies he deemed predatory, distinguishing between individual Muslims—acknowledging potential for "good Muslims"—and the systemic flaws of Islam itself, without animus toward persons but vigilance against doctrinal threats to Western civilizational norms.[11] He maintained that empirical reactions, such as global unrest tied to his demonstrations, validated his scriptural-based warnings rather than personal provocation.[34] Public activism against Islam "Islam Is of the Devil" campaign In 2009, Terry Jones, pastor of Dove World Outreach Center in Gainesville, Florida, initiated the "Islam Is of the Devil" campaign by erecting large signs on church property declaring the phrase, which he derived from his interpretation of biblical passages depicting spiritual opposition to Christianity, such as warnings against doctrines inspired by demonic forces.[35][36] The campaign's core message posited that Islamic theology originates from satanic deception, aiming to alert Christians to what Jones viewed as Islam's incompatibility with biblical truth and its potential to lead adherents astray spiritually.[36]As part of the effort, Jones authored and self-published the bookIslam Is of the Devil: Know the Spiritual Truths That Will Bring the Christian Church Back to Its God-given Position in 2010, expanding on the theme by arguing that Islam promotes violence and falsehoods antithetical to Christian doctrine, drawing on scriptural exhortations to discern false religions.[37] The book and signs sought to provoke public discourse on these theological claims, with Jones stating the initiative was intended to educate believers about perceived existential threats from Islamic teachings to Christian faith.[36]The campaign quickly drew local controversy in Gainesville, where the signs prompted protests and picketing by Muslim residents and community members opposed to the characterization of their faith.[38] Church-affiliated families escalated tensions by sending children to local schools wearing T-shirts emblazoned with "Islam is of the Devil," leading school officials to prohibit the apparel in August 2009 and send at least two students home for non-compliance.[39][38] These incidents garnered initial media coverage in Florida outlets, marking the first widespread publicity for Jones's church and its anti-Islam stance, though national attention remained limited until subsequent events.[35] International Burn a Quran Day and burnings In September 2010, Terry Jones, through his Dove World Outreach Center, announced plans for "International Burn a Quran Day" on September 11, the ninth anniversary of the September 11 attacks, framing it as a symbolic stand against what he described as Islamic extremism and the perceived threats posed by Sharia law.[40] The proposed event involved burning copies of the Quran to protest radical Islam's role in terrorism, drawing immediate global condemnation from figures including U.S. President Barack Obama, who labeled it a "stunt" that could endanger American lives abroad, and Defense Secretary Robert Gates, who personally phoned Jones to urge cancellation due to risks to troops in Afghanistan and Iraq.[41] On September 9, Jones suspended the plans, citing assurances from intermediaries that the proposed Park51 Islamic center near Ground Zero in New York would relocate—a claim later proven unfounded, as the project proceeded without moving.[42][43]The 2010 threat, though unrealized, amplified debates over free speech limits versus incitement, with Jones maintaining that canceling under pressure evidenced Islam's broader intolerance for criticism, a view he reiterated amid warnings from military officials of retaliatory violence against U.S. personnel.[12]On March 20, 2011, Jones executed a Quran burning at his Gainesville, Florida, church following a self-organized "International Judge the Quran Day" mock trial, where participants convicted the text of offenses including promoting murder, terrorism, and death—acts Jones linked to passages he interpreted as endorsing violence.[44] The event received minimal initial media coverage, but a video circulated online in late March, igniting protests in Afghanistan.[45]These demonstrations escalated violently on April 1, 2011, in Mazar-i-Sharif, where a mob of approximately 1,000 attacked a United Nations compound, beheading and killing seven UN staffers and four Nepalese guards in reprisal explicitly tied to the burning; Afghan officials noted the attackers exploited the protests as cover for premeditated assault.[46][47] Over the ensuing days, riots spread to other Afghan cities including Kandahar and Jalalabad, resulting in at least 20 confirmed deaths and dozens injured, with protesters chanting against the U.S. and burning effigies.[48][49]Mainstream reporting, including from outlets like The New York Times and The Guardian, emphasized Jones' responsibility for the unrest while downplaying the doctrinal triggers for blasphemy-related outrage in Sharia-influenced regions, where empirical patterns of disproportionate retaliation—such as fatwas and mob violence over perceived insults—align with Jones' critique of Islam's causal links to extremism rather than mere reaction to external provocation.[4][44] Jones expressed no remorse, arguing the deaths exposed the faith's inherent volatility, a position supported by the rapid escalation from a small-scale U.S. act to widespread fatalities abroad.[50] Protests and demonstrations In April 2011, Jones organized an anti-Sharia law demonstration planned for April 22 outside the Islamic Center of America in Dearborn, Michigan, a city with a significant Muslim population, intending to highlight concerns over Islamic legal practices in the United States.[34] Local prosecutors sought to prevent the event by arguing it would breach the peace, leading to a Wayne County jury verdict on April 22 finding that Jones and his associate intended to provoke disorder.[51] Jones was briefly detained in county jail after refusing to post a $1 "peace bond" as ordered by a judge, who also barred him from the mosque vicinity, but he posted bond later that day and proceeded with a limited protest involving about a dozen supporters, countered by several hundred Muslim demonstrators.[52] In November 2011, a Michigan appeals court reversed the breach-of-peace finding, ruling the restriction violated Jones's First Amendment rights.[31]Jones's protests extended to symbolic acts critiquing U.S. political figures for perceived tolerance of Islamic expansion. On June 12, 2012, his Dove World Outreach Center displayed a hanged effigy of President Barack Obama alongside a baby doll labeled "Future Muslim America," framing it as a warning against policies enabling Sharia influence.[53] Similarly, on January 20, 2013—during Obama's second inauguration—Jones ignited effigies of Obama and Secretary of StateHillary Clinton at the church grounds, citing their administration's approach to Islam as justification, resulting in a citation for illegal open burning.[54]These actions formed part of broader rallies opposing the proposed Park51 Islamic center near the former World Trade Center site, which Jones decried as insensitive to 9/11 victims and emblematic of creeping Sharia.[42] On 9/11 anniversaries post-2011, Jones sought to stage commemorative demonstrations emphasizing anti-Islam messages, but such efforts often intersected with legal hurdles; for instance, in September 2013, he faced felony charges in Florida after authorities intercepted his vehicle en route to a planned event in Orlando, where he intended to burn Qurans symbolically tied to the attacks' victim count.[55] Involvement with Innocence of Muslims film In September 2012, Terry Jones promoted the short film Innocence of Muslims as an effort to highlight historical criticisms of Muhammad, framing it as a challenge to Islamic doctrines that prohibit depictions or scrutiny of the prophet.[56][57] The film's producer, Nakoula Basseley Nakoula (under the pseudonym Sam Bacile), contacted Jones to assist in publicizing the trailer, which Jones screened and endorsed online and through his network, aligning it with his broader activism against perceived Sharia-imposed blasphemy restrictions.[58][59]The 14-minute video portrays Muhammad as a deceitful figure engaging in violence, pedophilia, and homosexuality, based on interpretations of historical accounts cited by its creators as evidence of Muhammad's character flaws rather than reverential hagiography.[60][57] Jones integrated promotion of the film into "International Judge Muhammad Day" on September 11, 2012, a symbolic event where he conducted a mock trial evaluating Muhammad's actions against Sharia standards, aiming to demonstrate inconsistencies in Islamic legal reverence for the prophet and protest global blasphemy enforcement.[61]This activity coincided with the September 11, 2012, attack on the U.S. consulate in Benghazi, Libya, which killed Ambassador J. Christopher Stevens and three other Americans; initial reports from U.S. intelligence and media attributed the violence to spontaneous outrage over the film, amplified by Jones's promotion.[62][59] However, forensic evidence, including pre-attack planning by Ansar al-Sharia militants and surveillance of the consulate, indicated the assault was a deliberate al-Qaeda-inspired operation timed for the 9/11 anniversary, with the film's controversy serving as opportunistic cover rather than the primary driver, underscoring how jihadist networks exploit criticism of Islam to mobilize while broader suppression of such discourse in Muslim-majority contexts heightens volatility when it emerges.[63][64] Legal challenges Domestic arrests and restrictions In August 2010, the Gainesville Fire Department denied Terry Jones a permit to burn Qurans on September 11 as part of his "International Burn a Quran Day" event, citing fire safety regulations that prohibited open burning without proper authorization.[65] Jones maintained that the denial infringed on his First Amendment rights and consulted lawyers who advised that he could proceed without the permit, though the event was ultimately canceled amid widespread condemnation.[66] Authorities justified the restriction on public safety grounds, including the potential for uncontrolled fires and disruptions, despite Jones' church, Dove World Outreach Center, being located in Gainesville.[67]In April 2011, during a planned protest outside the Islamic Center of America in Dearborn, Michigan, a judge ordered Jones to post a $1 peace bond and imposed a restriction barring him from approaching within 100 feet of the mosque, citing risks of breach of the peace due to anticipated counter-protests.[51] Jones refused the bond, leading to brief incarceration, and a three-year injunction was initially upheld to keep him away from the site, which his legal team challenged as viewpoint discrimination favoring Muslim sensibilities over protected speech.[31] In 2013, a federal judge ruled that Dearborn's requirement for Jones to sign a broad indemnification agreement as a condition for protest permits violated the First Amendment by imposing undue burdens not applied neutrally, effectively limiting his ability to demonstrate near the mosque without assuming liability for others' potential violence.[68]On September 11, 2013, Polk County Sheriff's deputies in Florida arrested Jones and associate pastor Marvin Sapp Jr. en route to a public park for a planned Quran burning commemorating the 9/11 attacks, charging them with felony unlawful conveyance of fuel for transporting nearly 3,000 kerosene-soaked Qurans in their vehicle.[6] Jones faced an additional misdemeanor charge for openly carrying a firearm, which he held legally permitted; deputies cited transportation hazards and fire risks as pretexts, though Jones alleged the stop was a pretextual entrapment to halt the event amid fears of public unrest.[69] In April 2014, Jones accepted a plea deal resolving the charges with probation and community service, avoiding trial but preserving his claim that the arrest prioritized hypothetical threats from opponents over constitutional protections for expressive conduct.[70] These incidents reflected a pattern where local authorities invoked safety and logistical concerns—often tied to anticipated reactions from Muslim communities or international actors—to curtail Jones' activities, rulings emphasizing "time, place, and manner" limits on speech that courts deemed permissible despite First Amendment tensions.[71] International travel bans and condemnations In January 2011, the United Kingdom's Home Office barred Terry Jones from entering the country, determining that his presence would not be conducive to the public good due to his planned participation in anti-Islam demonstrations organized by far-right groups.[8] The decision followed lobbying by anti-fascist organizations and reflected concerns over potential public disorder from his advocacy of Quran burnings.[72]In October 2012, Canadian border authorities denied Jones entry at the Ambassador Bridge crossing into Ontario, where he intended to speak at a Toronto event criticizing Islam. Officials cited an outstanding arrest warrant from a 2011 Michigan incident involving failure to pay a peace bond related to a Dearborn protest, rendering him inadmissible under immigration rules.[73][74]Denmark imposed a two-year entry ban on Jones in May 2017 as part of its first blacklist of "hate preachers," aimed at curbing hate speech and safeguarding public order; he was included alongside five Islamic clerics accused of promoting extremism, such as calls for violence against non-believers.[75] This measure demonstrated an application of restrictions to inflammatory rhetoric from both Christian and Muslim sources, though Western bans on Jones contrasted with rarer enforcement against imported Islamist preachers until that policy shift.[76]An Egyptian court sentenced Jones to death in absentia on November 28, 2012, convicting him alongside seven Coptic Christians for charges tied to the "Innocence of Muslims" film, including insulting Islam, harming national unity, and inciting sectarian strife.[77] The ruling, issued under post-Arab Spring Islamist influence, exemplified blasphemy penalties in Sharia-influenced systems, where Jones's promotional role in the film's distribution—without direct production involvement—sufficed for liability, unlike protections for religious critique in secular jurisdictions.[78]These actions underscored disparities in global responses to anti-Islam expression: Western entry bans prioritized averting unrest over absolute free speech, often yielding to fears of Islamist backlash, while Egypt's verdict reflected causal intolerance where defamation of Muhammad triggers capital punishment absent equivalent reciprocity for Christian critiques in Muslim contexts. Empirical patterns, such as Denmark's balanced list versus unilateral targeting in the UK and Canada, highlight selective enforcement amid broader media amplification of Jones's provocations compared to routine unpunished incitements to jihad elsewhere.[79] Threats and personal security Sources and nature of death threats Following the March 20, 2011, burning of a Quran by members of Jones's Dove World Outreach Center, death threats against him escalated, originating predominantly from jihadist groups and Islamist extremists who framed the act as blasphemy warranting lethal retaliation under vehicles and buildings while demanding retribution.[83] Overall, the Afghan unrest linked to the burning claimed over 20 lives in its first two days, illustrating a causal chain from the provocative act to organized mob violence incited by religious authorities and militants who issued calls for Jones's elimination.[84]In contrast, historical instances of Quran burnings or anti-Christian tolerance, rather than universal backlash to offense.[85] Impact on personal life and safety measures Following the Quran burnings in March 2011, Terry Jones and his congregation at Dove World Outreach Center faced heightened personal security concerns due to over 300 death threats received via email, telephone, and other means, some specifying dates, times, and locations.[48][86] Church without evident retreat, viewing persistence as a moral imperative against perceived Islamic extremism.[91] Later years and death Post-activism activities In the years following his most prominent In February 2017, he worked as an Uber driver in Florida but was suspended by the company pending investigation into complaints from riders who recognized him and cited his social media posts denouncing Islam.[92] He was subsequently dismissed after a formal complaint highlighted his history of anti-Islamic activism and continued online expressions of those views.[93]The Dove World Outreach Center persisted as a modest congregation without notable growth or new public initiatives, focusing on routine services under Jones's leadership. Jones reiterated warnings about Islam's doctrinal threats in intermittent social media updates and church-affiliated posts, though these garnered limited attention compared to earlier efforts.[94] Health decline and death In the years following his high-profile activism in the early 2010s, Terry Jones maintained a low public profile, with reports indicating involvement in private ventures such as operating a French fry stand in Florida by 2015 and driving for Uber in 2017.[95][92] No verified public records document significant health decline, specific diagnoses, or his death as of 2025. The Dove World Outreach Center, under his leadership, appears to have ceased operations, with the property sold or repurposed by the mid-2010s, though details on formal succession remain unavailable.[96] Reception and legacy Support from free speech advocates The American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) defended Terry Jones's 2010 plan to burn copies of the Quran as protected speech under the First Amendment, stating that while the proposed act was "ugly," it remained a form of expression that could not be curtailed due to anticipated offense or backlash.[97] Legal experts similarly affirmed the constitutionality of such symbolic protests, analogizing Quran burning to flag desecration cases like Texas v. Johnson (1989), where the Supreme Court ruled that offensive political expression merits robust protection regardless of public revulsion.[98]Conservative commentator Ann Coulter criticized military and political leaders, including General David Petraeus, for publicly condemning Jones's intentions, arguing that symbolic book burnings—whether of the Quran, Bible, or flag—constitute core political speech and that preemptive denunciations reward threats of violence, effectively eroding free expression.[99]Civil liberties advocates challenged restrictions on Jones's protests, such as those imposed during his 2011 Dearborn, Michigan, event near an Islamic center, contending that denying him a platform based on content or potential unrest violated principles of viewpoint neutrality.[100]Proponents of unrestricted speech viewed Jones's provocations as essential tests of Western tolerance limits, particularly against religious critique, with the ensuing global reactions—including riots and fatalities after his March 20, 2011, Quran burning—serving to expose patterns of disproportionate intolerance and pressure tactics that demand confrontation rather than accommodation to safeguard open discourse.[34] These defenses elevated Jones among some First Amendment absolutists, framing his persistence despite condemnations and threats as a bulwark against creeping censorship influenced by fear of Islamist reprisals.[101] Criticisms and accusations of provocation Critics, including political figures and media commentators, accused Terry Jones of fostering bigotry and deliberately provoking international unrest through his Quran burnings, labeling his actions as hate speech that endangered lives abroad.[102][103] Following the March 20, 2011, burning of a Quran under Jones's supervision at his Gainesville church, an Afghan mob stormed a United Nations compound in Mazar-i-Sharif on April 1, killing 12 people, including seven UN staffers; outlets such as ABC News and NPR directly linked the violence to Jones's "stunt," with some commentators asserting moral culpability for the deaths despite no evidence of direct causation or endorsement of violence by Jones.[5][104] This attribution exemplified a causal fallacy, wherein the responder's violent reaction—rooted in doctrinal sensitivities to perceived blasphemy—was retroactively imputed to the initial expressive act, absolving the perpetrators while presuming predictable aggression from certain audiences without interrogating underlying ideological triggers.[4]Prominent evangelical leaders publicly disavowed Jones, with figures like Franklin Graham and organizations such as the National Association of Evangelicals condemning the burnings as counterproductive and contrary to Christian witness, thereby distancing mainstream Protestantism from his fringe congregation of fewer than 50 members.[105]Mainstream media, often exhibiting left-leaning biases in coverage of religious critique, amplified portrayals of Jones as an "extremist" or "hate figure" to underscore his marginal status, a tactic that critics argue served to discredit broader, evidence-based examinations of Islamic doctrines like Sharia enforcement by associating them with isolated provocations rather than substantive theological disputes.[102][106] Jones's defenders, though outside this section's focus, countered that such dismissals prioritized emotional appeals to interfaith tolerance over verifiable scriptural analysis, such as Quran passages endorsing corporal punishments, yet detractors maintained his methods inherently undermined rational discourse.[103]Legally, U.S. authorities found no basis for incitement charges against Jones, as his symbolic burnings failed to meet the Brandenburg v. Ohio standard requiring intent, likelihood, and imminence of lawless action; First Amendment protections shielded the acts as protected speech, even if inflammatory, underscoring a disconnect between domestic free expression rights and foreign repercussions attributed to him by international condemners.[107][108] This framework highlighted tensions in global norms, where Western legal tolerances for doctrinal critique clashed with expectations of self-censorship to avert unrest, a dynamic often exploited by media narratives framing Jones's persistence as reckless provocation rather than principled opposition to perceived threats from Islamic supremacism.[44] Broader societal impact Jones's planned and executed Quran burnings, particularly the 2011 event following the September 11 attacks, precipitated widespread riots in Afghanistan that resulted in at least 30 deaths and over 100 injuries, underscoring the causal chain from symbolic criticism of Islam to lethal jihadist mobilization—a pattern not observed in equivalent provocations against Christianity or Judaism.[34][6] These outcomes empirically highlighted asymmetries in religious tolerance, as no comparable global violence ensued from acts like Bible burnings, which occur without mass fatalities or international fatwas.[109]The controversies amplified debates on free speech limits during 9/11 commemorations, prompting U.S. military leaders like General David Petraeus to warn of risks to troops, and influencing subsequent caution in public events to avoid Islamist backlash.[34] This dynamic revealed practical constraints on Western expressions of remembrance due to threats from Muslim-majority regions, where blasphemy incurs severe penalties absent in liberal democracies.[110]His activism contributed to mainstreaming concerns over Sharia's incompatibility with secular governance, as evidenced by parallels drawn between his calls to halt Muslim immigration and later Reply to pastor terry jones concept on violence in islam part 1 mpg Early life and education Jones, a native of Cape Girardeau, Missouri, graduated from Cape Girardeau's Central High School in 1969. He then attended Southeast Missouri State University for two years. Jones received no academic degree in theology but was given an honorary degree from the unaccredited California Graduate School of Theology in 1983, which sought to disassociate itself from him during the 2010 Koran burning controversy. Career Jones worked as an assistant hotel manager in the late 1970s, then became an assistant pastor with Maranatha Campus Ministries in Kentucky. He went to Cologne, Germany with his first wife to work as a missionary and founded and led the Christliche Gemeinde Köln (CGK) in 1981, with that church growing to as many as 1,000 members over the years, initially as a branch of the Maranatha Campus Ministries and a sister church to Dove World Outreach Center of Gainesville, Florida. On September 18, 1997, Jones spoke during the First Session of the 105th Congress at a hearing before the Commission on Security and Cooperation in Europe in Washington D.C., concerning the religious persecution of Christians in Europe. While living in Germany, Jones was fined $3800 by a Cologne court for using the "doctor" title awarded with an honorary degree from an unaccredited school. According to the German Evangelical Alliance, Jones was released from the leadership of the Christliche Gemeinde Köln in 2008 due to his indefensible theological statements and his craving for attention. The Gainesville Sun reported that he left the church in Germany after being accused of fraud. A leader of the Cologne church said that Jones "didn't project the biblical values and Christianity, but always made himself the center of everything." German press agency Deutsche Presse-Agentur reported that church members said Jones ran the Cologne church like a sect leader and used psychological pressure on members, “subordinating all activities to his will.” Der Spiegel reported that Jones had been ejected by Cologne church for creating "a climate of control and fear." Following Jones' departure, the CGK closed, then reopened under new, independent, leadership. Jones came to lead the Dove World Outreach Center in Gainesville, Florida church by way of his association with the Maranatha Campus Ministries. In October 2010, Jones founded the political organization, Stand Up America Now. On October 27, 2011, Jones announced that he was running for President of the United States, as an independent candidate, with no political party affiliation. He remarked that he was entering the race because none of the candidates were adequately communicating the dire state of the U.S. economy to the U.S. citizenry. His platform calls for the deportation of all illegal immigrants, withdrawal of American troops abroad, and a reduction in bureaucracy and corporate tax rates. In March 2013, Florida media sources reported that Jones, with Dove World Outreach Center, and the organization Stand Up America Now plan to leave Gainesville, Florida and move to Tampa, Florida. In August 2013, the Bradenton Herald reported that the Pastor had purchased property near Bradenton and planned to move his ministry there. Jones relocated to Bradenton, Florida, breaking with most of his 15-member flock at Dove Outreach, but retaining his associate pastor Wayne Sapp. In 2015, Jones opened a Fry Guys Gourmet Fries stand at a mall in Bradenton, selling Buffalo wings and speaking his mind until later in the week when a mall manager, concerned about potential trouble, requested him to stay out of the mall, remove photos of himself from the premises and take his name off the lease, though he remains an owner of the company. He also owns a furniture moving company, TSC, with his brother. TSC deliveries became backlogged and received consumer complaints shortly after the January 15 Charlie Hebdo attack raised concerns about al Qaida. Personal life His first wife, Lisa Jones, died in 1996, after which he married Sylvia Jones, with whom he runs TS and Company. His daughter from his first marriage, Emma, has distanced herself from his beliefs and practices. "Islam Is of the Devil" Terry Jones authored a book titled Islam Is of the Devil. In July 2009, Dove World Outreach Center posted a sign on its lawn which stated in large red letters "Islam is of the Devil," resulting in objections from the community and media attention. Students from the church attended area schools in August 2009 wearing T-shirts with "Islam is of the Devil" printed on the back, for which they were sent home. Koran burnings Jones believes Islam promotes violence and that Muslims want to impose sharia law in the United States. He did not become widely known until after announcing plans to burn copies of the Koran. The plan to burn Korans was first announced on Twitter on July 12, 2010, and was promoted on Facebook and on YouTube. National and International discussion, objections and protests contributed to extensive media coverage. U.S. Secretary of State Hillary Clinton said, "It's regrettable that a pastor in Gainesville, Florida with a church of no more than fifty people can make this outrageous and distressful, disgraceful plan and get, you know, the world's attention." The commander of the International Security Assistance Force in Afghanistan, General David Petraeus said, "It is precisely the kind of action the Taliban uses and could cause significant problems. Not just here, but everywhere in the world we are engaged with the Islamic community." The pastor responded to Petraeus' statement that, "We understand the General's concerns. We are sure that his concerns are legitimate. [Nonetheless] [w]e must send a clear message to the radical element of Islam. We will no longer be controlled and dominated by their fears and threats." President Barack Obama was asked on September 9, 2010, on ABC's Good Morning America about the Quran burning controversy. He said, "You could have serious violence in places like Pakistan or Afghanistan. This could increase the recruitment of individuals who would be willing to blow themselves up in American cities or European cities." He said, "I just want him to understand that this stunt that he is talking about pulling could greatly endanger our young men and women in uniform who are in Iraq, who are in Afghanistan. We're already seeing protests against Americans just by the mere threat that he's making." "I just hope he understands that what he is proposing to do is completely contrary to our values as Americans, that this country has been built on the notions of religious freedom and religious tolerance," Obama said. "He says he's someone who is motivated by his faith ... I hope he listens to those better angels and understands that this is a destructive act that he's engaging in.” Asked if the event could be stopped, Obama replied, "My understanding is that he can be cited for public burning … but that's the extent of the laws that we have available to us." US Secretary of Defense Robert Gates called Jones asking him not to go through with his Koran burning. Later on September 9, Jones announced the cancellation of the event and a plan to fly to New York to meet with the Imam of Park51, Feisal Abdul Rauf. Jones alleged that Imam Muhammad Musri, the president of the Islamic Society of Central Florida, had arranged the meeting and that a promise had been given him to move the Park 51 mosque in exchange for the event cancellation. This claim was denied by both Imams. On March 21, 2011, Jones and some supporters held a mock trial of the Koran and set a copy on fire as a "punishment" for “crimes against humanity.” Jones was assisted by Ahmed Abaza, an Egyptian ex-Muslim, and a Texas Imam, Mohamed El Hassan who argued for and against the accusations. Reaction to the event resulted in riots and deaths in Afghanistan. On April 28, 2012, Jones burned a copy of the Koran, protesting the imprisonment of an Iranian-American Pastor, Saeed Abedini in Iran. An arrest defeated Jones' effort to hold a Koran-burning protest on September 11, 2013, but Jones and Sapp held a protest on September 11, 2014, in which the ISIS flag and hundreds of Korans were burned. Dearborn, Michigan On April 29, Jones led a rally at the Dearborn City Hall, designated as a free speech zone. Riot police were called out to control counter protesters. Jones led a rally at City Hall and then planned to speak at the annual Arab Festival on June 18, 2011, but on his way there he was blocked by protesters, six of whom were arrested. Police said they did not have enough officers present to maintain safety. On April 7, 2012, Jones led a protest in front of the Islamic Center of America in Dearborn, speaking about Islam and Free Speech. The mosque was placed on lock down. Thirty police cars were there to block traffic and prevent a counter protest. Jones returned to Dearborn in October 2012 and led a small protest against alleged "Muslim bullying of non-Muslims" outside Edsel Ford High School. School officials denied there was a problem. Obama effigy In 2012, Jones hanged an effigy of Barack Obama in the front yard of the Dove World Outreach Center. Another effigy of Barack Obama was burned along with an effigy of Bill Clinton in January 2013. Innocence of Muslims film In September 2012, it was reported by The Atlantic that Terry Jones was involved in the promotion of a movie vilifying Islam, titled Innocence of Muslims. The movie led to protests in Egypt, Yemen, Tunisia and Libya. In Cairo, protesters breached the wall of the U.S. Embassy and burned the flag. The U.S. consulate in Benghazi, Libya, was largely burnt and looted; killing Ambassador J. Christopher Stevens and three other American citizens. Jones screened the film for his followers on September 11, 2012, a day he dubbed, "International Judge Mohammad Day". The Innocent Prophet film In December 2012, Belgium raised its terror alert before the release of The Innocent Prophet, another anti-Islam movie with connections to Jones. United Kingdom Jones was invited to an English Defence League rally in Luton in February 2011 to share his views on Islamic extremism. Anti-fascist group Hope not Hate successfully petitioned the Home Secretary to have Jones banned from entering the UK. Gainesville, Florida Following the April 28, 2012 Koran burning, Jones was fined $271 by Gainesville Fire Rescue for violating fire safety rules. Dearborn, Michigan Jones planned to protest in April 2011 outside the Islamic Center of America. On the day he was to attend the protest, local authorities questioned him in Court, required him either to post a $45,000 "peace bond" to cover Dearborn's cost if Jones was attacked by extremists or to go to trial. Jones contested that requirement, and the jury voted on April 22 to require the posting of a $1 "peace bond", but Jones and his co-pastor Wayne Sapp continued to refuse to pay. They were held briefly in jail, while claiming violation of First Amendment rights. That night Jones was released by the court. The ACLU had filed an amicus brief in support of Jones's protest plans. On November 11, 2011, Wayne County Circuit Court Judge Robert Ziolkowski vacated the “breach of peace” ruling against Jones and Wayne Sapp on the grounds that they were denied due process. Terry Jones and his organization Stand Up America Now won a victory in court on August 30, 2013 over the City of Dearborn and its Chief of Police, Ronald Haddad. Terry Jones was represented by the Thomas More Law Center. Judge Denise Page Hood wrote, "The Court finds that Plaintiffs are entitled to summary judgment as to their claim that Ord. No. 17-33, requiring Plaintiffs to sign an indemnification agreement, is a violation of their First Amendment rights of Freedom of Speech and Freedom of Expressive Association. The related ordinance, Ord. No. 17-28(d), requiring the chief of police to grant a special events permit only after an indemnification agreement is signed, also violates Plaintiffs’ First Amendment rights." Judge Hood concluded that both ordinances were unconstitutional. Canada On October 11, 2012, Jones was denied entry to Canada, where he was scheduled to attend a debate on free speech, because of a previous legal infraction in Dearborn, Michigan, and because the German government had fined him 20 years before for using the title of "Doctor". The Dearborn charge was challenged and overturned in November 2011, and Jones had held an honorary doctorate, but the Canadian government refused to allow him entry without documentation of the cases, effectively barring him from the event. Polk County, Florida In April 2013, Jones announced plans for a Koran burning event to be held on September 11, 2013. In Iran, Pakistan, and at an Interfaith Conference in Vienna there have been calls for the United States government to stop this event. Army General Lloyd Austin III, commander of US Central Command called Jones on September 9, 2013, to ask him cancel the event, however Jones declined. Police arrested Jones on September 11, 2013, before he could burn 2,998 Korans soaked with kerosene at a park in Polk County, Florida. He was charged with unlawfully conveying fuel and openly carrying a firearm. The charge of unlawfully conveying fuel, made against both Jones and Marvin Wayne Sapp, who was driving, was dismissed in October, 2014 by Circuit Court judge Roger Alcott, who wrote "For purposes of the legal analysis, the nature of the books in the grill does not matter... It would not matter if the materials in the grill were books, oak wood or BBQ briquettes. The material facts simply do not demonstrate a violation of Florida statutes." This dismissal was upheld by the Second District Court of Appeals in June, 2015. Denmark In May 2017 he was banned from entering Denmark according to an announcement by Inger Støjberg, then Danish Minister for Immigration, Integration and Housing. Death threats Since July 2009, Jones and his church have received hundreds of death threats by phone and mail, while some have been published on websites overseas. In March 2011, the Pakistani radical Islamist group Jama'at-ud-Da'wah issued a US$2.2 million reward and fatwa calling for Jones' death. After the 2012 Koran burning protest, an Iranian cleric called for Jones to be executed. In November 2012, an Egyptian court convicted Jones in absentia and sentenced him to death on charges linked to the Innocence of Muslims film along with 7 Egyptian Coptic Christians. Al Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula featured Jones on a 'Wanted Dead' poster in its tenth edition of Inspire Magazine in March 2013. As of 2015, three of eleven persons on this list have been the target of terror attacks: Stephane Charbonnier, Lars Vilks, and Kurt Westergaard.

Supported by Pastor Who Burned Koran Demands Retribution GAINESVILLE, Fla. — Before a Koran was burned at his modest church here on March 20, the pastor Terry Jones held a self-styled mock trial of the holy book in which he presided from the pulpit as judge. The prosecutor was a Christian who had converted from Islam. An imam from Dallas defended the Koran. Sitting in judgment was a jury of 12 members of Mr. Jones’s church, the Dove World Outreach Center. After listening to arguments from both sides, the jury pronounced the Koran guilty of five “crimes against humanity,” including the promotion of terrorist acts and “the death, rape and torture of people worldwide whose only crime is not being of the Islamic faith.” Punishment was determined by the results of an online poll. Besides burning, the options included shredding, drowning and facing a firing squad. Mr. Jones, a nondenominational evangelical pastor, said voters had chosen to set fire to the book, according to a video of the proceedings. Mr. Jones said in an interview with Agence France-Presse on Friday that he was “devastated” by the killings of 12 people in a violent protest in Afghanistan when a mob, enraged by the burning of a Koran by Mr. Jones’s church, attacked the United Nations compound in the northern city of Mazar-i-Sharif. “We don’t feel responsible for that,” he told the news service. Unlike the worldwide outcry that greeted the pastor’s plan to burn 200 copies of the Koran on Sept. 11 — which he ultimately abandoned — the event last week at the 50-member church was largely ignored by the news media. As of 2 p.m. on Friday, the video of the Koran’s burning on the church Web site had been viewed only 1,500 times. “The local strategy of everybody was to ignore this,” said the Rev. Lawrence D. Reimer, pastor of the United Church of Gainesville. “It’s just a horrible tragedy that this act triggered the deaths of more innocent people.” Some church members were surprised by the violent reaction in Afghanistan on Friday, said Fran Ingram, an assistant at the church. She explained that it was decided in the weeks leading up to the burning that a jury of churchgoers and volunteers would hear both sides before deciding what to do. In a statement, Mr. Jones demanded that the United States and United Nations take “immediate action” against Muslim nations in retaliation for the deaths. “The time has come to hold Islam accountable,” he said. He also called on the United Nations to act against “Muslim-dominated countries,” which he said “must alter the laws that govern their countries to allow for individual freedoms and rights, such as the right to worship, free speech and to move freely without fear of being attacked or killed.” Some members of the Dove World Outreach Center said they feared they would be attacked. “We have a huge stack of death threats,” Ms. Ingram said. “We take precautions. I have a handgun. A lot of us have concealed weapons permits. We’re a small church, and we don’t have money to hire security.” Before the March 20 service, Mr. Jones asked if the church’s Web site was streaming the event, according to the video. He was assured that it was. Mr. Jones then gave the “defense attorney” the opportunity to leave. “It is not that we burn the Koran with some type of vindictive motive,” Mr. Jones said. “We do not even burn it with great pleasure or any pleasure at all. We burn it because we feel a deep obligation to stay with the court system of America. The court system of America does not allow convicted criminals to go free. And that is why we feel obligated to do this.” On the video, a pastor named Wayne Sapp is seen igniting a kerosene-drenched copy of the Koran with a plastic lighter. Members of the church watch the book burn for several minutes while several photographers snap pictures. Finally, Mr. Jones says, “That actually burned quite well.” Lizette Alvarez reported from Gainesville, and Don Van Natta Jr. from Miami. Timothy Williams contributed reporting from New York. As the week began, there was Terry Jones, infamous burner of Korans and the No. 2 target on an al-Qaida hit list, in plain sight at a Florida mall. "We want our fries good and crispy."The 63-year-old preacher has faced hundreds of death threats. He's got a $2.2 million bounty on his head from the Islamist group Jamaat-ud-Dawa. When fears of global terrorism were once again stoked, Jones moved back into crusade mode. "I can understand if you don't agree with so-called burning someone's holy book. But I don't know how you can agree with sharia. You don't see Mennonites going around chopping people's heads off."A woman with white-blond hair appeared at the counter — Mary Reiter, 62, petite, high-heeled sandals, a pink pedicure, fresh off a yoga class. "We're with you," she told Jones. Usually, Reiter shops at a different mall, but she brought her husband and son and a friend to support Jones and have lunch at Fry Guys."I give you credit for standing up for your beliefs," she said, looking up at the white-haired preacher. "I'm a history buff. The same thing happened in Nazi Germany. People didn't stand up and fight. Reiter asked, curious to know how many others had stepped forward. "At least there's still some decent people then."Business at Fry Guys picked up after news reports that Jones was at the mall.
