--- [Gliese 146 - Wikipedia] ---
Gliese 146 is a star with an orbiting exoplanet in the constellation Horologium. Gliese 146 is also catalogued as HD 22496, HIP 16711, SAO-216392, and LHS 1563. With an apparent visual magnitude of 8.64, it is too faint to be visible to the naked eye. Gliese 146 is located at a distance of 44.4 light years based on parallax measurements, and is drifting further away with a heliocentric radial velocity of +21 km/s.
This is an ordinary K-type main-sequence star with a stellar classification of K6.5V. It has 68% of the mass of the Sun and 67% of the Sun's girth. Gliese 146 is radiating 12% of the luminosity of the Sun from its photosphere at an effective temperature of 4,385 K. Classified as a suspected variable star, Gliese 146 was found to be a flare star, with average flare frequency 0.23 flares per day.
Its velocity relative to the Sun is 38.1 km/second, and its galactic orbit ranges between 20,800 and 25,400 light years from the center of the Galaxy, placing it within a thin disk. It belongs to the Hyades supercluster of stars, and is one of 155 K-type stars within 50 light years.

Gliese 146, also known as HD 22496, is an orange dwarf star of spectral type K5V located approximately 44 light-years (13.6 parsecs) from the Sun in the southern constellation Horologium.[1] This main-sequence star has a mass of 0.684 solar masses, a radius of 0.674 solar radii, and an effective temperature of 4385 K, rendering it cooler and smaller than the Sun but still capable of hosting a planetary system.[2] With an apparent visual magnitude of 8.57, it is visible only through binoculars or a small telescope from the Southern Hemisphere.[1]The most notable feature of Gliese 146 is its confirmed exoplanet, HD 22496 b, a Neptune-like world discovered in 2021 through radial velocity measurements—the first such stand-alone detection using the ESPRESSO spectrograph on the Very Large Telescope.[2] The planet has a minimum mass of 5.57 Earth masses (m sin i) and orbits its star in a nearly circular path with a period of 5.091 days at a semi-major axis of 0.051 AU, receiving about 23 times the insolation flux of Earth.[1] No additional planets have been confirmed in the system, though detection limits from radial velocity data allow for potential undetected low-mass worlds (m sin i < 5–7 Earth masses) in the habitable zone (roughly 0.18–0.36 AU).[2]Gliese 146 exhibits characteristics typical of active K dwarfs, including a rotation period of about 35 days and low metallicity ([Fe/H] = −0.08), which influences its stellar activity and potential for planetary habitability.[2] As a nearby, bright target, it continues to be monitored for further planetary signals and atmospheric studies, contributing to our understanding of sub-Neptune formation around cool stars.[1] Stellar properties Astrometry and visibility Gliese 146 has an apparent visual magnitude of 8.57, rendering it too faint for naked-eye visibility from Earth, as magnitudes brighter than approximately 6 are typically required for unaided observation under dark skies.[3]In the J2000.0 epoch, its equatorial coordinates are right ascension 03ʰ 35ᵐ 00.93987ˢ and declination −48° 25′ 08.9085″.The star's parallax, measured by the Gaia mission, is 73.52 ± 0.04 mas, corresponding to a distance of 13.60 ± 0.02 parsecs, or 44.35 ± 0.07 light-years.[4]Its proper motion components are +404.45 ± 0.06 mas/yr in right ascension and +307.37 ± 0.08 mas/yr in declination.[4]The heliocentric radial classification.[3]Gliese 146 is identified in various catalogs as HD 22496, HIP 16711, SAO 216392, LTT 1698, and 2MASS J03350093-4825089.[3] Physical parameters Gliese 146, also known as HD 22496, is classified as a main-sequence K5V star, characteristic of a late-type K dwarf with subdued chromospheric activity compared to earlier spectral types. This classification aligns with its position on the main sequence, where hydrogen fusion dominates its energy production, placing it in an evolutionary stage typical for stars of its mass in the solar neighborhood.[2]The star possesses a mass of 0.684±0.013M⊙, approximately 68% that of the Sun, and a radius of 0.674±0.020R⊙, or about 67% solar.[2] Its luminosity is 0.121L⊙, reflecting reduced nuclear fusion rates due to its lower mass, with an effective temperature of 4385±21 K that imparts an orange hue to its photosphere.[2] The surface gravity is logg=4.69±0.05 (cgs units), consistent with expectations for a main-sequence dwarf of this size and mass, while its metallicity is slightly subsolar at [Fe/H]=−0.08±0.02 dex.[2]Positioned approximately 13.6 pc from the Sun, Gliese 146 ranks among the known K-type stars within 50 light years, highlighting its proximity and relevance for studies of local stellar populations.[2] Kinematics and activity Gliese 146 exhibits a proper motion of approximately 404 mas/yr in right ascension and 308 mas/yr in declination, combined with a radial velocity of +21.4 km/s, resulting in a space velocity relative to the Sun of 38.1 km/s. It is suspected of photometric variability, designated as NSV 1203 with a magnitude range of 8.57 to 8.7.[2] Planetary system Discovery The planetary system around Gliese 146, also known as HD 22496, was first targeted for debris disk searches as part of the SCUBA-2 Unbiased Nearby Stars (SUNS) survey, selected in 2009 among approximately 500 nearby stars to probe for circumstellar dust using submillimeter observations on the James Clerk Maxwell Telescope.[5] This effort was complemented by the Herschel Space Observatory's DEBRIS and DUNES key programs, which observed HD 22496 in far-infrared wavelengths to detect cold debris disks around sun-like stars; no such disk was identified in these surveys, with upper limits established by 2015.[6]The exoplanet HD 22496 b was discovered in 2021 through a blind radial velocity search using Doppler spectroscopy, employing the ESPRESSO spectrograph on the European Southern Observatory's Very Large Telescope.[2] Observations spanned from October 2018 to April 2021, yielding 41 high-precision measurements that revealed a periodic signal indicative of a planetary companion, with stellar activity mitigated via Gaussian process modeling.[2] This marked ESPRESSO's inaugural standalone planet detection, independent of prior instruments like HARPS, demonstrating the instrument's sub-meter-per-second precision for identifying low-mass planets around quiet, nearby K-type stars like Gliese 146.[2]The discovery was announced in a paper by Lillo-Box et al., published in Astronomy & Astrophysics in October 2021, confirming HD 22496 b as the sole known planet in the system with no evidence for additional companions from the radial velocity data.[2] As a nearby K dwarf system, Gliese 146's detection highlights the potential for habitability studies, where ESPRESSO's sensitivity enables characterization of Earth-mass worlds in the habitable zone of stable, long-lived stars, contributing to broader surveys of local stellar populations.[2] HD 22496 b HD 22496 b, also known as Gliese 146 b, is a confirmed exoplanet orbiting the K-type star HD 22496, classified as a sub-Neptune at the boundary between rocky super-Earths and gaseous mini-Neptunes based on its minimum mass and expected composition.[2] Detected via radial velocity measurements from the ESPRESSO spectrograph, it represents the instrument's first standalone planet discovery.[1] The planet's orbit is characterized by a period of 5.09071±0.00026 days and a semimajor axis of 0.0510−0.0026+0.0024 AU, placing it in a close-in configuration around its host star.[1] The orbit is nearly circular, with an eccentricity upper limit of e<0.15 at 95% confidence, consistent with zero eccentricity within observational uncertainties.[2]The planet's mass is constrained only by radial velocity data, yielding a minimum mass of msini>5.57−0.68+0.73M⊕ (approximately 5.6 Earth masses), as the orbital inclination remains unknown due to the lack of transit observations.[1] Assuming a random inclination distribution, the true mass is likely between 5.6 and 16 M⊕, keeping it below the Neptune mass threshold and firmly in the planetary regime with over 99% probability.[2] No transits were detected in TESS photometry, implying an upper limit on the planet's radius of about 0.34 R⊕ or a near-edge-on inclination exclusion, leaving the physical radius undetermined and preventing direct density measurements.[2]Given its mass range and non-transiting status, HD 22496 b is inferred to be a volatile-rich sub-Neptune, potentially hosting a substantial hydrogen-helium envelope over a rocky core, though a more Earth-like composition cannot be ruled out without radius constraints.[2] The close orbit results in an equilibrium temperature of approximately 573 K and insolation 23 times that of Earth, subjecting the planet to intense stellar radiation that likely induces tidal locking, with one side perpetually facing the star.[1] This configuration, combined with the host star's occasional flares despite its low overall activity as a late K-dwarf, poses significant challenges to atmospheric retention; models suggest high-energy inputs could drive substantial mass loss from any extended atmosphere over billions of years, potentially eroding it to a bare rocky core.[2] Consequently, habitability prospects are dim, as the planet lies well inside the system's habitable zone (spanning 40–200 days) and experiences conditions inhospitable to liquid water.[2]No other planets have been confirmed in the HD 22496 system, though injection-recovery analyses indicate sensitivity to additional companions with minimum masses above 5 M⊕ at longer periods near the habitable zone inner edge.[2]

Horologium Constellation: The Ultimate Guide Horologium (The pendulum clock) Horologium is one of the constellations located in the southern celestial hemisphere. Its name comes from ancient Greek and means "Pendulum Clock." The name of the constellation only takes the name of the Greek language. Still, it is not related to the culture since, in ancient Greece, there were no pendulum clocks. The constellation Horologium was first introduced in 1756 by the French astronomer Nicolas-Louis de Lacaille. The brightest star in the constellation Horologium is Alpha Horologii (α Hor/HD 26967/HR 1326), with an apparent magnitude of +3.86. The constellation Horologium is home to deep-sky objects, mainly star clusters and galaxies. There are no occurrences of meteor showers within the boundaries of the constellation. The Mythology And History Of The Horologium Constellation Horologium is one of the 88 modern constellations. It does not appear or have associated histories in ancient Western cultures because it was not visible in those regions. Horologium is one of the faintest constellations. Most of its stars cannot be seen with the naked eye, so it was necessary to wait for telescopes to be invented to see them. Early Modern Period Having observed and cataloged nearly 10,000 southern stars for two years in 1756, the French astronomer Nicolas Louis de Lacaille first described the constellation as <<l'Horloge à pendule & à secondes>> meaning Clock with pendulum and seconds. Lacaille devised fourteen new constellations in previously unexplored regions of the celestial southern hemisphere, which were not visible from Europa. All but one honored scientific instruments and thus symbolized the Age of Enlightenment. The constellation's name was Latinized to Horologium in an updated catalog and chart published posthumously in 1763. Nowadays In 1922 the International Astronomical Association added the constellation Horologium to the 88 official night sky constellations. Since then, it has not undergone significant modifications in its boundaries or name. Horologium currently covers an area of 49 square degrees, thus ranking 58th in size among the 88 constellations in the night sky. How To Find The Horologium Constellation? Visibility By Region Horologium resides in the first quadrant of the Southern Hemisphere (SQ1) between latitudes 30° N and 90° S, which means that we can see the constellation in the night sky from all countries of the southern hemisphere of the earth and some countries of the northern hemisphere. Horologium is visible in Europe, Africa, Australia, and Antarctica. Horologium is not visible in regions above 30°N such as the USA, Canada, and most of Europe, such as Italy, France or Germany, Russia, Groenlandia, Japan, and Alaska. Visibility By Season The best month to visualize the Horologium constellation is in December, which is visible during the winter in the northern hemisphere, and during the summer in the southern hemisphere. Finding Horologium Constellation The constellation Horologium is very faint and difficult to locate, so you can support yourself using the bright stars nearby, such as the star Achernar or Canopus. The easiest way to locate the constellation Horologium is to locate the second brightest star in the sky, Canopus, then draw a straight line towards the star Achernar Eridanus. The constellation Horologium is located right in the middle of the two. Related Constellations The constellations bordering Horologium are Eridanus, Hydrus, Reticulum, Dorado, and Caelum. In addition, Horologium belongs to the family of constellations discovered by Lacaille; these include Antlia, Caelum, Circinus, Fornax, Mensa, Microscopium, Norma, Octans, Pictor, Reticulum, Sculptor, and Telescopium. Stars in Horologium Constellation The Circinus constellation officially contains 30 stars, of which 7 form the central figure of the constellation; these are Alpha Horologii, R Horologii, Beta Horologii, Mu Horologii, Zeta Horologii, Eta Horologii, and Iota Horologii.
