--- [Otto Schlüter - Wikipedia] ---
Otto Schlüter (12 November 1872 in Witten – 12 October 1959 in Halle) was a German geographer. Schlüter was a professor of geography at the University of Halle from 1911 until his death. He is credited with creation of the term cultural landscape, which is one of the turning points of geographical history.


--- [Otto Schlüter - Universitäts- und Landesbibliothek Sachsen ...] ---
Startpage » Collect + Preserve » Collections » Estates and Autographs » Otto Schlüter estates and autographs Otto Schlüter The geographer Otto Schlüter was born on 12.11.1872 in Witten and died on 12.10.1959 in Halle (Saale). Otto Schlüter studied geography, geology, mineralogy and philosophy in Freiburg (im Breisgau) from 1891 to 1898 and later in Halle and Berlin. He completed his studies in 1896 with a doctorate in settlement geography. In 1906, Otto Schlüter also habilitated with a thesis on settlement geography. Otto Schlüter then worked as a private lecturer at the University of Berlin from 1906 to 1909 and at the University of Bonn from 1909 to 1911. In 1911 he was appointed full professor of geography at the University of Halle. In 1938 Schlüter retired and from 1948 to 1951 he was again entrusted with a professorship for Geography of the Cultural Landscape and for Methodology of Geography. In 1952 he received the title Dr. rer. nat. h.c. from the University of Leipzig. Schlüter was a member of several geographical societies and societies for geography; he was a full member of the Academy of Sciences (Leopoldina-Carolina) in Halle, became Vice President in 1943 and was President of the Leopoldina in 1952/53. Otto Schlüter was a founder of modern anthropogeography, especially settlement geography. The focus of his research was on the genesis of the cultural landscape, its physiognomy and the ancient landscape. In addition to a large number of publications, Otto Schlüter was the editor of the “Atlas des Saale- und mittleren Elbegebietes” (Atlas of the Saale and Middle Elbe Region). A note written in pencil was found with the estate (located in capsule 1), which shows more manuscripts than are actually available. The note reads: received in 1959. However, no entry was made in the 1959-1961 receipt journals. The missing manuscripts (13 fascicles) were handed over to the ULB on 17 July 2000 by Dr. Christian August, Klausenerstrasse 5, 48151 Münster. The documents were in quarto capsules; the majority of the manuscripts are surrounded by a whitish paper sleeve with the title inscription in Schlüter’s hand. However, the inscription did not always match the content. Since Schlüter had often made changes within the lecture texts and corrected page numbers or changed parts of the lecture by inserting more manuscript sheets, the order was very difficult; some of the manuscripts were left in the existing order. --- [Otto Schlüter Explained] ---
Otto Schlüter (12 November 1872 – 12 October 1959) was a German geographer. Schlüter was a professor of geography at the University of Halle from 1911 until his death. He is credited with creation of the term cultural landscape, which is one of the turning points of geographical history.[1] Otto Schlüter studied geography, geology, mineralogy, and philosophy between 1891 and 1898, first in Freiburg im Breisgau, and later in Halle and Berlin. After teaching at the University of Berlin and University of Bonn, in 1911 he became a professor of geography at the University of Halle.[2]

This collection contains the output of many Archive Team projects, both ongoing and completed. Thanks to the generous providing of disk space by the Internet Archive, multi-terabyte datasets can be made available, as well as in use by the Wayback Machine, providing a path back to lost websites and work. Our collection has grown to the point of having sub-collections for the type of data we acquire. If you are seeking to browse the contents of these collections, the Wayback Machine is the best first stop. Otherwise, you are free to dig into the stacks to see what you may find. The Archive Team Panic Downloads are full pulldowns of currently extant websites, meant to serve as emergency backups for needed sites that are in danger of closing, or which will be missed dearly if suddenly lost due to hard drive crashes or server failures.
