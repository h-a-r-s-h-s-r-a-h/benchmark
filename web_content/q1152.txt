The Muzaffarabad fort, constructed in the 17th century by the Chak dynasty of Kashmir, stands as a testament to their architectural prowess. Its construction was completed in 1646 during the reign of Sultan Muzaffar Khan, the founder of Muzaffarabad. Later, Maharaja Gulab Singh and Rambir Singh, the Dogra rulers, undertook the task of renovating and expanding the fort to support their political and military endeavors. However, as the year 1947 approached, the Dogra forces withdrew, leaving the fort abandoned. Locally known as the ‘Ratta Qila,’ the fort exhibits remarkable architectural features, showcasing the skillful craftsmanship of its builders. The construction was carried out using stone masonry using lime surki mortar. The architects incorporated terraces and sturdy retaining walls, particularly on the northern side, to withstand the forces exerted by the Neelum River, which surrounds the fort on three sides. The eastern side of the fort was well protected against floodwaters, while certain sections on the north side suffered damage following the 2005 earthquake. Perched at a high altitude, the fort offers a captivating panoramic view of the city.

The Red Fort, locally known as Rutta Qila or Muzaffarabad Fort, is a 16th-century defensive fortification situated in Muzaffarabad, the administrative capital of Azad Jammu and Kashmir, overlooking the strategic confluence of the Neelum and Jhelum rivers.[1][2] Constructed initially by the Chak dynasty rulers of Kashmir starting around 1559 to counter potential Mughal incursions, the fort was completed by 1646 and later renovated in 1846, featuring robust walls built from local river stones and mortar for enhanced durability.[3][4] Its architecture reflects advanced engineering for the era, with a design leveraging the natural U-shaped bend of the Neelum River to fortify three sides, minimizing vulnerability while providing panoramic surveillance of approaching threats.[3][1]Historically, the fort served as a military outpost under successive regional powers, including the Dogra rulers, before falling into partial abandonment following shifts in territorial control after the 1947 partition.[5] Severely damaged during the 2005 Kashmir earthquake, which measured 7.6 on the moment magnitude scale and epicentered nearby, the structure suffered extensive wall collapses and structural breaches, underscoring the seismic risks inherent to its riverside cliff location despite its engineered resilience.[6][7] Today, the fort stands as a key archaeological and tourist site, preserving artifacts of Kashmiri heritage and offering insights into pre-Mughal defensive strategies amid ongoing conservation efforts to mitigate further deterioration.[1][5] History Origins and Construction The Red Fort in Muzaffarabad was initially constructed starting in 1559 by the Chak dynasty rulers of Kashmir as a strategic defensive outpost to counter potential threats from Mughal expansion and to serve as a secondary bulwark behind the frontline Fateh Garh Fort near Dub Gali Pass.[5][8] Positioned on a rocky promontory encircled by the U-shaped bend of the Neelum River (historically known as Kishanganga), the site was selected for its natural fortifications, providing oversight of riverine approaches and surrounding valleys.[5]Construction efforts under the Chaks employed local masons using rubble stone foundations, rounded river stones, and early brickwork bound with lime and clay mortars, forming a rudimentary multi-tiered structure with an eastern main gate on the upper level.[5] The fort's incomplete state persisted after the Mughal annexation of Kashmir in 1586, which diminished its immediate military relevance under imperial oversight.[2]In 1646, Sultan Muzaffar Khan, chief of the Bomba tribe and a regional governor under Mughal suzerainty originating from the Hazara region, repaired and substantially completed the fort while founding the adjacent city of Muzaffarabad, naming it after himself.[5][9] This phase enhanced the fort's defensive capabilities, incorporating red brick masonry for walls that lent it the enduring "Red Fort" designation, and solidified its role as a regional administrative and garrison center amid the post-Chak power vacuum.[3] The work reflected pragmatic adaptations to the terrain, prioritizing durability over ornamentation to withstand sieges and environmental stresses.[5] Mughal Annexation and Early Decline The Chak Dynasty of Kashmir initiated construction of the Red Fort in 1559 as a strategic defensive outpost on the western bank of the Neelum River, anticipating expansionist threats from the Mughal Empire.[3][10] This positioning allowed it to function as a secondary bulwark protecting Muzaffarabad, the emerging regional center founded earlier by Sultan Muzaffar Khan in the early 16th century.[2] The fort's red-brick structure was designed for rapid assembly and fortification, reflecting the Chak rulers' urgent military priorities amid regional instability.[3]In 1586, Mughal Emperor Akbar completed the annexation of Kashmir through military campaigns led by his generals, incorporating the valley into the empire after defeating the last Chak sultan, Yusuf Shah II, who had fled to seek alliances with the Safavids.[3] This conquest rendered the Red Fort obsolete as a frontline defense against Mughal incursions, as the threat it was built to counter had been neutralized. Construction, which had progressed intermittently, continued until its reported completion in 1646, but Mughal administrators showed little interest in maintaining or expanding the site, prioritizing instead more central Kashmiri strongholds like those in Srinagar.[2][10]The fort's early decline under Mughal rule stemmed directly from this shift in strategic calculus; with Kashmir securely under imperial control, peripheral outposts like Muzaffarabad's Red Fort lost their military rationale and were largely neglected, leading to gradual structural decay from exposure to the elements without upkeep.[3] Mughal records from the period, such as those in the Ain-i-Akbari, make no significant mention of investments in the fort, underscoring its diminished role in the empire's defensive network.[2] This neglect marked the onset of a prolonged period of disuse, exacerbated by the rugged Himalayan terrain that isolated the site from major administrative centers.[10] Renovations under Later Dynasties In the mid-19th century, following the Treaty of Amritsar in 1846 whereby the British sold Kashmir to Gulab Singh after defeating the Sikh Empire, the newly established Dogra rulers of Jammu and Kashmir undertook significant renovations to the Red Fort in Muzaffarabad. MaharajaGulab Singh initiated repairs and extensions to the fort's structure, adapting it for enhanced political administration and military operations amid the region's strategic vulnerabilities.[5][3]These efforts addressed deterioration from prior neglect under Afghan and Sikh governance, which had seen the fort primarily as a defensive outpost rather than a maintained administrative center. The renovations included reinforcing walls and expanding enclosures to accommodate Dogra governance needs, reflecting the dynasty's emphasis on consolidating control over frontier territories.[11][12]Gulab Singh's successor, Maharaja Ranbir Singh, oversaw the completion of these projects in the ensuing years, ensuring the fort's operational viability until its gradual abandonment post-1947 partition. No major structural overhauls beyond these Dogra-era interventions are recorded under subsequent interim administrations, as the site's military utility waned with modern geopolitical shifts.[5][3] Military Use and Final Abandonment The Red Fort served as a key military installation throughout its history, initially designed as a defensive outpost by the Chak dynasty against potential Mughal incursions. Under Sultan Muzaffar Khan in 1646, the structure was repaired and fortified to function as a robust base for regional defense and control.[5] In 1846, MaharajaGulab Singh of the Dogra dynasty initiated major reconstructions and expansions, adapting the fort for integrated political governance and military campaigns, a role continued by his successors until the mid-20th century.[8]During the Dogra era, the fort housed garrisons that played a tactical role in regional security, including during the 1947-1948 Kashmir conflict, where it supported state forces amid tribal incursions and the broader Indo-Pakistani struggle for control of Jammu and Kashmir.[13] Pakistani-backed forces captured Muzaffarabad in late October 1947, prompting the evacuation of remaining Dogra troops from the fort by mid-1947, after which it saw limited or transitional use by incoming administrations.[14]The fort's active military role persisted briefly under Pakistani control but became obsolete with the construction of modern cantonments suited to contemporary warfare, reported as early as 1926 during the British-Dogra period or extending to 1958 post-partition.[12][11] Following this shift, the structure was fully abandoned as a defensive or operational site, transitioning to neglect amid prioritization of new infrastructure and vulnerability to environmental degradation.[3] Architecture Materials and Construction Techniques The Red Fort in Muzaffarabad was primarily constructed using rounded stones, rubble stone, and red brick masonry, which contributed to its distinctive reddish appearance and durability in the region's terrain.[5][3] These materials were sourced locally, reflecting adaptive vernacular practices suited to the Himalayan foothills' geology, where abundant riverine stones and clay-rich soils were available. The use of red bricks, in particular, aligns with the fort's nomenclature and provided a balance of strength and workability for load-bearing walls.Binding techniques employed lime and clay mortars, applied in layered masonry to ensure adhesion and flexibility against seismic activity common in Azad Jammu and Kashmir.[5][3] Construction occurred in phases spanning from the Chak dynasty's initiation in 1559 to completion under Sultan Muzaffar Khan in 1646, resulting in heterogeneous material integration, with earlier sections favoring rubble and stone for foundational stability and later additions incorporating more refined brickwork.[5] This phased approach, documented in historical accounts, minimized logistical challenges while enhancing defensive resilience through irregular, interlocking stone placements.No advanced mechanical aids were used; instead, labor-intensive methods prevailed, including manual quarrying, shaping via chiseling, and scaffolding with timber for elevated sections.[3] Mortar preparation involved slaking lime from local limestone kilns and mixing with clay for plasticity, a technique inherited from pre-Mughal Kashmiri building traditions that prioritized earthquake resistance over ornamental precision. Subsequent repairs under Dogra rule in the 19th century reused similar materials, perpetuating the original composite methodology despite incremental deterioration.[5] Layout and Defensive Features The Red Fort in Muzaffarabad is strategically positioned at the confluence of the Neelum and Jhelum rivers, with the U-shaped bend of the Neelum River providing natural protection on three sides, functioning as a formidable barrier against invaders.[1] This geographic advantage enhanced the fort's defensibility, limiting access primarily from the west.[4]The fort's layout encompasses an enclosed area reinforced by high walls constructed from red bricks, stones, and lime mortar, designed to withstand sieges. Sultan Muzaffar Khan, the fort's namesake and founder of Muzaffarabad, undertook initial repairs to address damage from prior construction phases and skirmishes, transforming it into a fortified military outpost overlooking the Neelum and Jhelum rivers.[5] These efforts involved reinforcing brick and stone walls but were limited in scope, reflecting the era's focus on utility over longevity.[3]Under subsequent rulers, including Sikh and early Dogra administrations, further renovations occurred in 1846, coinciding with the fort's transition to Dogra control after Maharaja Gulab Singh's acquisition of Kashmir from the British East India Company via the Treaty of Amritsar.[3] These works, continued and finalized by Gulab Singh's successor, Maharaja Ranbir Singh (r. 1857–1885), included structural reinforcements and adaptations for ongoing military use, such as bolstering ramparts against artillery threats.[16] However, no evidence exists of systematic conservation initiatives detached from strategic imperatives, with maintenance often reactive to sieges or erosion.[5]Such pre-modern interventions preserved the fort's core layout—encompassing barracks, audience halls, and watchtowers—but failed to prevent gradual deterioration, as resources prioritized active defense over archival restoration. The Red Fort, also known as "Rutta Qila," is a medieval fort situated in Muzaffarabad, Azad Kashmir, and spans over an area of 60,000 square feet. It retained strategic military importance because of Muzaffarabad's location as a gateway into the larger Kashmir region. Historically, Muzaffarabad was a vital military outpost, especially during the 16th century when the region was under the rule of the Chak Dynasty. The Chaks ruled Kashmir from 1554 to 1582, during a time of heightened tension with the expanding Mughal Empire. Faced with frequent military conflicts, the Chak rulers recognized the need for a fortress in the region to defend against Mughal incursions. In 1559, the Chak rulers established the Red Fort as a defensive measure in its initial form. The fort was designed to serve as a second line of defense and provide additional protection to Fateh Garh Fort, located to the west of Muzaffarabad in a region then known as Chakri Bahak, (Cross Meadow). The strategic positioning of the fort at Muzaffarabad played a crucial role in regional defense efforts. However, the Chaks were defeated, and the Mughal Empire, under the leadership of Emperor Akbar the Great, successfully annexed Kashmir in 1586. Following the annexation, the Red Fort came under Mughal control. In 1592, Akbar visited Kashmir and preferred traveling through the Muzaffarabad region on his way back to the capital, as it was the most convenient route. During this visit, Akbar stayed in Muzaffarabad for a week. Recognizing the importance of the region, a sarai (inn) was constructed in 1597 to accommodate the emperor on future visits. The sarai, named “Saraa-e Akbari,” was built by Ali Mardan Khan, the Governor of Kashmir. However, despite the initial attention given to the fort during Akbar’s reign, the fort gradually lost its significance under subsequent Mughal rulers and was largely neglected. The fort’s next significant phase of development came in 1646 under Sultan Muzaffar Khan, a ruler from the Bamba Dynasty. Sultan Muzaffar Khan, the founder of the city of Muzaffarabad, recognized the deteriorating state of the Red Fort and undertook major repairs and reconstruction. His efforts revitalized the fort, transforming it into a strong military base once again. The reconstruction of the fort under Muzaffar Khan’s leadership marked a turning point in its history, as the fort became a central element in the military strategy of the region. In 1846, the Dogra Dynasty, under the leadership of Maharaja Gulab Singh, took control of the fort. Recognizing its strategic importance, Maharaja Gulab Singh initiated extensive repairs and expansions of the Red Fort. The reconstruction efforts continued under his successor, Maharaja Ranbir Singh. Together, these rulers extended the fort and gave it much of the form it retains today. Under the Dogra rulers, the fort became not only a military stronghold but also a base for political operations in the region. The Red Fort remained in active military use until 1958, when a new cantonment was established, rendering the fort obsolete for modern military needs. Subsequently, the fort was abandoned as a military base but retained its historical significance. Today, the fort is under the control of the Department of Tourism and Archaeology of the Government of Azad Kashmir, ensuring its preservation as an important cultural and historical site. The strategic location of the Red Fort is enhanced by its proximity to the Neelum River, which enters Pakistan at Taobat and is known as the Kishanganga River in India. The river’s encirclement of the fort in a U shape enhances its defensive capabilities, making it a carefully selected site for military fortification. The northern part of the fort featured terraces with steps that lead directly to the riverbank, providing access to the Neelum river below. A pathway around the fort’s perimeter, constructed along the side of the Neelum River, allows visitors to explore the site. The city of Muzaffarabad is visible across the river from the fort in a beautiful panorama. While the fort no longer serves any military function, it remains an important cultural landmark. The Department of Tourism and Archaeology of Azad Kashmir manages the site, preserving its historical significance and ensuring that visitors can appreciate its strategic, architectural, and historical importance. The Red Fort is a notable example of defensive architecture, strategically protected on three sides by the U-shaped Neelum River. This natural barrier enhances the fort’s defensive capabilities. The fort is designed with three distinct levels. The main gate is located on the eastern side of the upper level, while the lower level is on the northern side. In its original form, the northern part of the fort had stairs that led down to the river, providing direct access. The lower level connects to the larger upper level through an intervening middle level, ensuring a structured and multi-layered defense system. The fort has undergone several phases of repair and reconstruction over different periods, which is reflected in the variety of materials used in its construction. While the fort's name, "Red Fort," is derived from the reddish color of the bricks and the plaster used in its structure, other materials such as rounded stones, rubble stone, and lime and clay mortar were also used. The fort’s elongated shape spans approximately 60,000 square feet, with a length of around 600 feet and a varying width ranging from 100 to 140 feet. It is fortified by strong bastions placed at intervals along its perimeter, providing points for lookouts and defense. The entrance is located on the river side, making it more easily defensible. The combination of natural and man-made defenses and the fort’s strategic placement make the Red Fort an impressive example of military architecture.
