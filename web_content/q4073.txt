--- [Chemical Industry Medal - Wikipedia] ---
The Chemical Industry Medal is an annual American award given to an industrial chemist by the Society of Chemical Industry America (SCI America). The medal has been awarded since 1933, when it replaced the Grasselli Medal. It was initially given to  "a person making a valuable application of chemical research to industry. Primary consideration shall be given to applications in the public interest." As of 1945, the criterion became "a person who ...  has rendered conspicuous service to applied chemistry." More recently it has been awarded "for contributions toward the growth of the chemical industry."

Today it is widely acknowledged as the highest honor in American industrial chemistry. Perkin was a founding Member of SCI and this Medal was first presented in New York to Perkin himself in 1906. Sir William Henry Perkin: Sir William Henry Perkin (1838-1907) at the age of 18 created the world’s first synthetic aniline dye, which revolutionized color chemistry and opened up new possibilities for a whole range of industries; most notably, textiles and clothing. Perkin was born in England and entered the Royal College of Science at 15. At 18, in private experiments attempting to make quinine, he inadvertently created a dye. Just six months later mauve was being used in a London dyehouse. He enjoyed international acclaim and went on to more discoveries and opened his own factories. He ‘retired’ from industry to focus on ‘pure science’ at the age of 36. 2025 Perkin Award Medalist Bruce DeBruin Bruce DeBruin was born in rural southeastern Ohio along with six brothers, into a family of meager means. At an early age he discovered an interest in math, chemistry, and understanding how processes operate. He fostered this love of science at The Ohio State University majoring in chemical engineering. Bruce paid his way through college by working in a welding fabrication shop. In the shop, he built dozens of the pieces of equipment that he would use throughout his engineering career. Bruce has spent his entire career at Eastman Kodak and Eastman Chemical Companies. His first projects were working on the kinetics, crystallization, and solid liquid separation of couplers and dies used in photographic film and paper. With the challenge of optimizing conditions for a new product each week, the skills of understanding the challenges of problem solving in a quick and efficient manner were honed. After a couple of years, controlling the reactions diverged to work on one of the first analytical instruments with a computer (a new tool for the industry). This was quickly adapted to function in a production environment allowing reactions to be controlled with this capability. Bruce then had his first opportunity to work in a polyester chemical recycling facility where he led the improvement processes to increase the capacity by 50%. With this demonstrated capability to solve complex problems, Bruce moved from plant to plant with challenges of reducing environmental emissions, reducing waste, increasing efficiency, deleting safety issues, and increasing capacity. Transferring into a management role, Bruce challenged the team to create long term visions for each department. One of those innovations required was an improved polyester recycling (termed Methanolysis) reactor that was safer and able to manage a wide range of impurities. Developing this technology from the lab, it was scaled-up to a full-scale replacement in the existing plant. This would be the first group of plants that Bruce would develop, design, construct, and startup in his early career. Following this success, Bruce went to further innovate in the field copolyesters developing recycling technologies for polyester lines, designing and starting up a copolyester polymer line that used lower energy content raw materials in Malaysia and modifying continuous polyester processes to produce zero waste in product transitions. The next adventure was creating a PET process for consumer bottles that had the lowest energy usage, lowest emissions, lowest waste at an advantaged capital cost and construction time. This became known as IntegRex and was the cornerstone of Eastman’s sale of the North American PET business to DAK Americas. In the following years, Bruce modified triacetate film to eliminate the bright defects in LCD screens, developed a continuous process to make polyvinyl butyral for windshields, deciphered the environmental cause of transparent insulating window film corrosion, redesigned the heritage insoluble sulfur tire additive reactor to resolve product quality issues, redesigned the new insoluble sulfur process to eliminate degradation issues, doubled the capacity of an adhesive resin process while improving safety and quality. Bruce’s current contributions have come full circle as he is working on the world scale Eastman Methanolysis recycling process resolving the conflicts between a widely contaminated PET feedstock into the highest purity polyester products with lower energy consumption. Moore Medal The Society of Chemical Industry America established the Gordon E. Moore Medal as the premier recognition for early career success in innovation, as reflected both in market impact and improvement to the quality of life. By highlighting extraordinary individuals and their work, this recognition aims to promote public understanding of research and development in the modern chemical industries, enhance the interest of students in applied chemistry by providing role models, and emphasize the role of creative research in the global economy. Based on the concept that the award name should draw attention to young innovators and connect with to the established Perkin Medal, the SCI America Executive Committee named the award for Gordon E. Moore, co-founder of Intel and a seminal figure in the establishment and development of the semiconductor industry. Mr. Moore has often described Intel as a chemical company. As a research chemist and then director of R&D, Gordon Moore carried out pioneering work on silicon transistors, the integrated circuit, semiconductor computer memory, and the microprocessor, while well under the age of 45. 2025 Gordon E. Moore Medalist Lin Feng Lin Feng is a distinguished scientist and group leader at Eastman, recognized for her impactful contributions to the development and commercialization of Tetrashield™ protective resin systems, a breakthrough family of polyester polyol resins for high-performance coatings. Her innovation successfully addressed health and food safety concerns with Bisphenol-A (BPA) in food contact applications and has improved sustainability and energy efficiency. Lin is the principal inventor and champion behind this new technology. Building on the unique molecular architecture of TMCD monomer, originally used in Eastman’s Tritan™ copolyester, she leveraged this chemistry into coatings that deliver superior hydrolytic stability, corrosion resistance, and high-temperature durability. Under her leadership, more than 10 TetrashieldTM resin products in the portfolio have been commercialized successfully and sold to customers around the world and in many end market applications. Lin earned her Ph.D. in Materials Science from the University of Cincinnati and began her career at Eastman in 2014. She has authored 24 publications and holds 13 patent families. Early in her career, Lin excelled in fundamental research, earning the 2019 Best Paper Award from the American Coatings Association for her impactful work. She progressed through various technical roles before advancing into people leadership positions, where she develops talent and inspires her R&D team by leading through example.
