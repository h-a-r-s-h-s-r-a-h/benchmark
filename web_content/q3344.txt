--- [Getty Center - Wikipedia] ---
The Getty Center, in Los Angeles, California, US, is a campus of the Getty Museum and other programs of the Getty Trust. The $1.3 billion center opened to the public on December 16, 1997, and is well known for its architecture, gardens, and views overlooking Los Angeles. The center sits atop a hill connected to a visitors' parking garage at the bottom of the hill by a three-car, cable-pulled hovertrain people mover.
Located in the Brentwood neighborhood of Los Angeles, the center is one of two locations of the J. Paul Getty Museum and draws 1.8 million visitors annually. (The other location is the Getty Villa in the Pacific Palisades neighborhood of Los Angeles.) The center branch of the museum features pre-20th-century European paintings, drawings, illuminated manuscripts, sculpture, and decorative arts; and photographs from the 1830s through present day from all over the world. In addition, the museum's collection at the center includes outdoor sculpture displayed on terraces and in gardens and the large Central Garden designed by Robert Irwin. Among the artworks on display is the Vincent van Gogh painting Irises.
Designed by architect Richard Meier, the campus also houses the Getty Research Institute (GRI), the Getty Conservation Institute, the Getty Foundation, and the J. Paul Getty Trust. The center's design included special provisions to address concerns regarding earthquakes and fires.

- 56 - 80shares The Getty Center is a fantastic destination for a day trip, offering something for everyone to enjoy. It is located in Brentwood, Los Angeles, California, and showcases a remarkable collection of European paintings, drawings, and sculptures and a selection of 19th and 20th-century American and European photographs. Situated on a 24-acre hilltop campus in the Santa Monica Mountains, the Getty Center sits 900 feet above the 405 freeways. Visitors can admire the Los Angeles skyline from numerous vantage points, and on a clear day, one can see the San Bernardino Mountains to the east and the Pacific Ocean to the west. Getty Center Tramway Upon your arrival, you’ll be greeted by the main gate. As you approach what seems like a subway stop at the rear of the garage, you’ll discover an automated three-car, cable-pulled tram. This tramway, your first taste of the Getty Center experience, takes you on a scenic ride to the hilltop campus. As it ascends the 900-foot mountain, you’ll have a breathtaking view of the freeway below. This ride, while not for the faint-hearted, is a thrilling start to your Getty Center adventure. Exhibitions Pavilions and Gardens As you first enter the campus, you will notice five pavilions: the North, East, South, West, and the Exhibitions Pavilion. The Exhibitions Pavilion hosts traveling art collections and various Foundation artworks, while the permanent collection is displayed chronologically throughout the other four pavilions. The oldest art is housed in the north pavilion, while the newest is in the west pavilion. Glass-enclosed bridges and open terraces on the second floor provide views of the surrounding hillsides and central plaza. Additionally, a tree-lined walkway leads down to the main garden. Getty Center Tours – Family Activities Several informative campus tours are available: the Garden Tour, a 45-minute trip through the Getty Gardens; the Architecture Tour, a daily 45-minute event; the Collection Highlights Tour, an overview of major works; and the Exhibits at the Center. Family-friendly activities include a fun introduction to art study and hands-on activities like making masks, sculpture, and art treasure hunts. The programs are held throughout the year and are free. The Sketching Gallery, Located on the upper level of the East Pavilion, allows you to sketch from original paintings and casts of sculptures from the Museum’s collection. Getty Center History The plans for the building were first announced in 1983, and Richard Meier was chosen as the architect the following year. After several delays, construction finally began in 1989, and the Center opened its doors in December 1997. The building estimates amount to an astonishing $1.3 billion. It’s a modernist blend of concrete and steel, with an homage to the styles of Neutra and Wright. The building features 1.2 million square feet of beige-colored travertine stone from Italy, creating the texture of fossilized leaves, feathers, and branches. Tips For Visiting the Getty Center The Getty Center in Los Angeles is a renowned art museum and cultural Center that offers a rich experience for visitors. Here are some key details to consider for your visit: - Location and Accessibility: The Getty Center is at 1200 Getty Center Drive, Los Angeles, CA 90049. You can access the Center by car, and parking is available for $20 per car or motorcycle or $15 after 3 pm. If you prefer public transportation, the 761 bus line stops at the entrance, providing a convenient and eco-friendly option. - Opening Hours: The Getty Center is open from 10:00 am to 5:30 pm on Tuesday through Friday and Sunday. On Saturdays, it has extended hours from 10:00 am to 8:00 pm. The Center is closed on Mondays. - Admission and Tickets: Admission to the Getty Center is free, but a timed-entry ticket must be reserved in advance to manage crowds and ensure a more enjoyable experience. Additional Tips: - Plan to spend at least a few hours fully experiencing the Center. - Consider visiting the Getty Villa, located 15 miles away, which houses ancient Greek and Roman art in a recreated Roman house. - The Getty Center also offers a variety of dining options, including a café with a panoramic view of Los Angeles, a casual restaurant, and several snack bars. These options cater to different tastes and dietary preferences, ensuring a satisfying dining experience during your visit. - Remember to check the official Getty Center website for the most current information on exhibits, events, and any special requirements for visiting. - Nearby Attractions: The Skirball Cultural Center, offering insights into Jewish history and culture, is nearby. If you’re interested in art, consider visiting the Los Angeles County Museum of Art (LACMA), which is about a 30-minute drive away.

The Getty Center, located on a 1,200-foot hill in the Santa Monica Mountains, is one of Los Angeles’ most breathtaking cultural landmarks. This $1.3 billion museum complex, which opened in 1997, combines world-class art collections, breathtaking modernist architecture, and expansive landscaped gardens—all with panoramic vistas of Los Angeles and the Pacific Ocean. The museum, founded by oil millionaire J. Paul Getty, is free to the public (with a parking fee) and welcomes approximately 1.8 million visitors each year. This thorough book delves into its history, must-see artworks, architectural marvels, gardens, and insider advice for an unforgettable visit. History & Legacy The Vision of J. Paul Getty In 1954, J. Paul Getty established a tiny museum in his Malibu ranch house, today known as the Getty Villa. In 1982, the Getty Center was established thanks to the Getty Trust’s $6 billion endowment following his death. In 1997, the Getty Center debuted after 14 years of construction and quickly became an architectural symbol. Architectural Genius Designed by Richard Meier, a Pritzker Prize-winning architect. Key Features: Travertine stone facade (1.2 million square feet, imported from Italy). Pavilions made of glass and steel provide ample natural light. Tram ride from parking garage to hilltop campus. Main Attractions at The Getty Center The Getty’s Art Collections The museum showcases pre-20th-century European paintings, sculptures, and decorative arts, including: European Paintings (Pre-1900) Vincent van Gogh’s “Irises” (1889) is one of the museum’s most iconic paintings. Rembrandt’s “The Abduction of Europa” (1632). Claude Monet’s “Sunrise” (1872). Illuminated Manuscripts Gold-leafed pictures can be seen in medieval and Renaissance publications. Highlights include “Hours of Jeanne d’Evreux” (14th century). Sculpture & Decorative Arts Antique French furniture from the 18th century, including Marie Antoinette’s desk. Bernini and Canova created baroque sculptures. Photography Collection More than 35,000 photographs, including works by Ansel Adams and Man Ray. The Central Garden Robert Irwin designed this 134,000-square-foot garden as a living piece of art. Features: A waterfall pouring down a stone ravine. There are more than 500 plant species, including uncommon blooms. Inspired by Vincent van Gogh’s brushstrokes, this hedge design resembles a maze. The Architecture Tour Free daily tours explore Richard Meier’s design philosophy. Highlights: The travertine stone walls include fossilized plants and feathers. The Harold M. Williams Auditorium boasts exceptional acoustics. The Getty Research Institute Rare books, artist archives, and exhibitions make this a treasure trove for scholars. Temporary shows generally showcase avant-garde art. The Views & Photo Spots The best view of the Los Angeles skyline is from the South Promontory. The West Pavilion overlooks the Pacific Ocean and Santa Monica Mountains. Sunset illuminates the travertine stone brilliantly. Visitor Tips Admission & Hours Entry is free. Parking is $20-$25, but is cheaper after 3 PM. Open Tuesday to Sunday (closed Mondays). Best Time to Visit Weekday mornings tend to be less crowded. Sunset (ideal for golden hour photographs). How to Get There Parking: On-site garage (fills up by noon on weekends). For rideshare, drop off at the main door. Public transportation: Metro Bus 734 stops nearby. Dining Options The restaurant offers upscale dining with views of the garden. Café offers casual meals and coffee. Picnics are permitted, but food cannot be consumed in the galleries. Special Experiences Open at night on summer Saturdays. Access free audio guides via the Getty app. Nearby Attractions The Getty Villa (Malibu) features ancient Greek and Roman paintings. UCLA Hammer Museum (15-minute drive). Santa Monica Pier (a 20-minute journey). The Getty Center offers a sensory experience that combines art, nature, and architecture, in addition to being a museum. Whether admiring Van Gogh, exploring the gardens, or viewing LA’s skyline, this cultural gem provides a peaceful getaway from the metropolis.
