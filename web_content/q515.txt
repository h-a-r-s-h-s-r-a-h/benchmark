--- [Chief Meteorologist Charlie Lopresti’s secret to growing giantpumpkins] ---
Chief Meteorologist Charlie Lopresti’s secret to growing giant pumpkins Most of you know our Chief Meteorologist Charlie Lopresti as a weather expert. You might not know he's also an expert at growing giant pumpkins and he has some blue ribbons to prove it. CBS 13 took a trip to Charlie’s pumpkin patch to find out his secret to success. "I don't think anybody has ever looked at a pumpkin and not smiled," Charlie said. Before Charlie had his mind set on meteorology, he had a passion for pumpkins. It's an outdoor outlet. "Our lives are so structured, going to work, we have families, that you need something that makes no sense whatsoever and that's exactly what growing giant pumpkins is,” Charlie said. Charlie’s grandmother grew up on an olive farm in Sicily and even brought her tomato seeds to the United States, which his family continues to grow today. “I think it's probably just in my blood,” Charlie said. "But after a while I got kind of bored growing tomatoes.” He took his green thumb to the next level, growing pumpkins with his dad in the 1980s. "And back then it was a big deal when you got maybe a 100 or 200-pound pumpkin,” Charlie said. Now he grows gourds that weigh over 1,000 pounds. "And every year there's a new bar that's set and it has to do with genetics and growing techniques and it's kind of fun to push the limit, push the envelope if you will,” Charlie said. Two years ago, Charlie won the weigh-off at the Damariscotta Pumpkinfest and Regatta with this 1,711-pound pumpkin. Last weekend, he got another blue ribbon for his 811.5-pound pumpkin at the Cumberland Fair, but he says it's not as much about the competition as it is about the community. “I've never met a giant pumpkin grower I don't like. We actually have a group, we actually have a club in the State of Maine and they're just as crazy as we are,” Charlie said. And they're always looking for new members. Charlie says success takes a good seed and a lot of work. “There's irrigation that needs to happen, there's pruning that needs to happen. There's something I’m doing in the patch every day, every day this summer, every day in the spring, every day of the fall,” Charlie said. And as this meteorologist knows firsthand, weather is a huge factor. "It's been the most extreme humidity on record we've had this summer, so things like that will lead to disease,” Charlie said. But he says the hard work does pay off and he'd love to share his passion with others. "If you want to grow giant pumpkins, send me an email. I’d love to share seeds. If I can spread this crazy hobby around the state kind of like Johnny Appleseed, I’d love to do that,” Charlie said. Charlie will be competing this Sunday in the weigh-off at the Damariscotta Pumpkinfest and Regatta. If your interested in growing giant pumpkins, email Charlie atclopresti@sbgtv.com.

ASSOCIATED PRESS • September 23, 2021 The Cumberland Fair is scheduled to start Sunday and run through Oct. 2. The fair was first held in 1868 and is one of the biggest annual agricultural events in southern Maine. The coronavirus pandemic canceled Maine’s 2020 agricultural fair season, but this year many of them have been able to go on. The Cumberland Fair is famous for its annual pumpkin and squash weigh-off event. Winning pumpkins sometimes weigh more than 1,000 pounds.
