According to John Flansburgh, each song had to be fact-checked for the album (a first in the band's career), due to the impressionable nature of the target audience—children. The original "Why Does the Sun Shine?" was recorded in the 1950s and based on encyclopedic facts from the time, which were later discovered to be incorrect; rather than being made of gas, the sun exists mostly in the fourth state of matter—plasma. By the time the album's fact-checker, Eric Siegel, pointed this out to the band, they had already recorded the song and the accompanying video was already in the works. Not wanting to drop the fan-favorite song from the album but also not wanting to perpetuate incorrect information, the band was faced with a conundrum. While discussing the problem in the studio one day, one of the recording engineers, Jon Altschuler, suggested the band write an "answer song" called "The Sun Is A Miasma Of Incandescent Plasma", which is exactly what they did. - Also of relevance is the (TMBG-original) song "The Sun Is On Fire", which is also directly refuted in the line "The sun is a quagmire / It's not made of fire". It should be noted, however, that there was probably no intentional reference to this abandoned demo from 2001. - Video directed by David Cowles and Liesje Kraai with excerpts from Hine Mizushima's video of "Why Does The Sun Shine?". According to the October 27, 2021 episode of This Might Be a Podcast, which featured Cowles, this was the fourth video that he completed for the album. Eric Siegel From This Might Be A Wiki Eric Siegel is the director and chief content officer of the New York Hall of Science, and has been involved with art and science museums for over 25 years. He worked as the factual consultant for Here Comes Science, the first They Might Be Giants album to have a "fact-checker." One of his most notable contributions to the album was pointing out the necessity for an addendum to "Why Does The Sun Shine?", because the Sun is not made of gas, as stated by the song, but plasma. This was corrected in "Why Does The Sun Really Shine?". Eric is also a musician, he plays the guitar and piano, and has composed music for network and cable television.

--- [Here Comes Science - Wikipedia] ---
Here Comes Science is the fourth children's album and fourteenth studio album by American alternative rock band They Might Be Giants,  packaged as a CD/DVD set. The album is (as the title suggests) science-themed, and is the third in their line of educational albums, following 2005's Here Come the ABCs and 2008's Here Come the 123s. It was nominated for the "Best Musical Album For Children" Grammy.
