About Motaz Azaiza https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motaz_Azaiza From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia Motaz Hilal Azaiza (Arabic: معتز هلال عزايزة; born 30 January 1999)[1] is a Palestinian photojournalist from the Gaza Strip.[2][3][4][5] He is known for covering the Israel–Hamas war, drawing a large social media following. In 2023 he was named Man of the Year by GQ Middle East and one of his photos, showing a girl trapped in rubble from an Israeli air strike, was named one of Time's top 10 photos of 2023. Early life and education Azaiza was raised in the Deir al-Balah Camp in the Israeli-occupied Gaza Strip.[5] He attended Al-Azhar University in Gaza, graduating in 2021 with a degree in English studies.[5] As of 2023, he was employed by UNRWA.[6] Photojournalism Prior to the 2023 Israel–Hamas war, Azaiza's online posts mostly focused on photographing daily life in his native Gaza Strip.[3][5] He told The Guardian he did not intend to become a war journalist and wished "people knew me for my art, I wanted to capture the beauty of my people".[7] His dream was to become a travel photographer according to Grazia UK, but he could not yet afford the visa expenses.[8] Although he covered the 2014 Gaza War and the 2021 Israel–Palestine crisis, his social media accounts did not gain much attention at the time.[5] There are few foreign journalists in the Gaza Strip due to Israel and Egypt denying them access to the territory, which has led to Azaiza becoming a key reporter on the ground in Gaza.[9] Prior to the Hamas-led attack on Israel on 7 October 2023, Azaiza's profile on Instagram had approximately 25,000 followers.[5][9] On 13 October, his Instagram account was restricted, but access was restored the following day.[10] His follower count had increased to one million by 17 October,[11] nine million by October 30,[3] 12.5 million by November 3,[9] and 13 million by November 7.[5] As of 27 December, Azaiza's Instagram profile had 17.5 million followers,[12] with the number reaching over 18 million by January 2024.[13] In November 2023, GQ Middle East named him as their Man of the Year, with editor Ahmad Ali Swaid stating that "he reminds us that no matter who we are or where we're from, it's us – ordinary people, men, and women – who have the power to enact that very change that we want to see."[14][15] Azaiza's photograph, "Seeing Her Through My Camera," part of his extensive coverage of Gaza during the 2023 Israel–Hamas war, was listed among Time's top 10 photos of 2023. In late October, following an Israeli airstrike, Azaiza used a low shutter speed on his camera to capture the moment, revealing a young girl trapped under rubble at the Al Nusairat refugee camp. This technique allowed him to witness her in the darkness where the naked eye couldn't confirm her condition before a Civil Defense rescue worker's light illuminated her face.[16] In January 2024, Azaiza appeared on Mehdi Hasan's final show with MSNBC to discuss the dangers of reporting from Gaza under Israel's bombardment.[17] Later that month, after 108 days of reporting, Azaiza and some of his family evacuated to Egypt and then Doha, Qatar via Al-Arish Airport, their first time on a plane.[18] Azaiza subsequently began meeting with ministers, diplomats, and media figures to share his accounts, frustrated that his attempts to broadcast what was happening in Gaza had not changed things. In an interview with The Guardian, he mentioned the traumatic flashbacks and feelings of guilt and hopelessness he experienced after leaving.[7] Personal life On 11 October 2023, at least 15 of Azaiza's relatives were killed in an Israeli airstrike on the Deir al-Balah Camp, shortly after the outbreak of the 2023 Israel–Hamas war.[11][19] See also Bisan Owda Wael Al-Dahdouh Plestia Alaqad Violence against journalists in the 2023 Israel–Hamas war References Al-Hlou, Yousur (19 November 2023). Motaz Azaiza Motaz Hilal Azaiza (born 1999) is a Palestinian photojournalist and filmmaker from Gaza, who documented the Israel-Hamas war through social media videos and photographs depicting destruction and civilian conditions in the Gaza Strip. [1][2] Azaiza, raised in Deir al-Balah, graduated from Al-Azhar University in Gaza and initially shared images of everyday life before focusing on conflict coverage after October 7, 2023, which propelled his Instagram following to millions. [3][4] He reported from Gaza for the first 108 days of intensified fighting, wearing a press-marked flak jacket, before leaving the territory in early 2024 amid deteriorating conditions. [5][6] His work received recognition, including a spot on the 2024 TIME100 list, but also drew awards from journalism festivals. [2][1] Azaiza has encountered backlash from some Palestinian audiences for comments interpreted as criticizing Hamas-led resistance, associating with Palestinian Authority President Mahmoud Abbas, and acquiring a foreign passport from a Caribbean nation, prompting accusations of disloyalty. [7][8][9] Early Life and Background Childhood and Family Origins Motaz Hilal Azaiza was born on January 30, 1999, in Deir al-Balah, a city in the central Gaza Strip. He grew up in the nearby Deir al-Balah refugee camp, originally established in 1949 for Palestinians displaced during the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, where living conditions have long included overcrowding, limited access to resources, and dependence on international aid.[4][10][3] As a child and adolescent, Azaiza's family resided in Gaza under Hamas governance, which assumed control of the territory in June 2007 following its electoral victory and violent ouster of Fatah forces, an event that prompted Israel and Egypt to impose a blockade restricting movement of goods and people to curb weapons imports and militant activities. This period encompassed economic challenges, with Gaza's unemployment rate exceeding 40% by the early 2010s, alongside exposure to escalatory violence in conflicts such as the 2008–2009 Gaza War (when Azaiza was nine years old) and the 2014 Gaza War (at age 15), which involved Hamas rocket fire into Israel and Israeli airstrikes and ground operations resulting in over 2,000 Palestinian and 70 Israeli deaths combined. Azaiza's immediate family included parents and siblings, all originating from Deir al-Balah, reflecting the intergenerational Palestinian residency in the Strip amid these constraints.[11][3][5]Education in Gaza Motaz Azaiza pursued higher education at Al-Azhar University in Gaza City, an institution affiliated with the Egyptian Al-Azhar University system and focused on Islamic and secular studies.[10] He enrolled in the English Language and Literature program, completing his bachelor's degree in 2021.[10][12][13] This period coincided with ongoing regional tensions, including the May 2021 Israel-Hamas escalation, which damaged infrastructure and disrupted academic operations across Gaza, though specific impacts on Al-Azhar during Azaiza's studies are not detailed in available accounts.[14] Gaza's educational framework, administered by Hamas since its 2007 takeover, operates under a curriculum that has drawn criticism for embedding anti-Israel narratives, glorification of martyrdom, and restricted exposure to pluralistic viewpoints.[15][16] Textbooks and instructional materials in Hamas-controlled schools and universities often portray historical conflicts in a manner that emphasizes Palestinian victimhood and resistance while omitting or demonizing Israeli perspectives, limiting students' access to balanced global discourse.[15][17]Al-Azhar University, while offering programs like English literature, functions within this constrained environment, where internet access, international exchanges, and uncensored media are curtailed by Hamas governance and periodic blockades, potentially reinforcing insular ideological frameworks.[18] Azaiza's university experience did not include formal training in journalism or photography, fields in which he later self-taught through personal practice.[10][13] His early photographic interests emerged around this time, initially capturing daily life in Gaza for social media, but these pursuits were extracurricular and not integrated into the curriculum.[4][13] The absence of diverse pedagogical influences in Gaza's higher education may have contributed to the perspectives evident in his later work, though Azaiza has not publicly attributed his worldview directly to academic experiences.Professional Career Pre-2023 Photography and Content Creation Motaz Azaiza initiated his photography endeavors in 2014, with his inaugural Instagram post appearing in May of that year featuring a photograph of vibrant flower petals. He obtained his first camera in 2015, courtesy of a cousin, and nurtured a personal passion for the medium beginning around age 16. As a self-taught practitioner, Azaiza worked autonomously in the Hamas-governed Gaza Strip, lacking formal ties to conventional media organizations prior to 2023.[6][5][19][20] Azaiza's pre-2023 output predominantly showcased vignettes of quotidian existence in Gaza, encompassing portraits of locals in mundane pursuits like fruit selling at markets or family picnics, all within the confines of the enclave's longstanding movement restrictions and blockade. This material, shared via his Instagram account, emphasized routine human experiences rather than overt political advocacy or conflict imagery.[3][21][10] By early October 2023, Azaiza's follower base stood at roughly 25,000, indicative of a niche domestic audience appreciative of his depictions of Gaza's constrained normalcy, yet garnering negligible global notice. His independent, hobbyist approach underscored a grassroots content creation model, distinct from credentialed journalistic networks.[6][21]Documentation During the 2023 Israel-Hamas War Following the October 7, 2023, Hamas attacks on Israel, which initiated the escalation of the Israel-Hamas war, Motaz Azaiza shifted to intensive real-time documentation of conditions in Gaza via Instagram, posting photographs and videos of destruction from Israeli airstrikes and ground operations.[21][22] His content emphasized rubble-strewn streets, collapsed buildings, and civilian hardships in areas like Gaza City and Al-Zahra, with early posts such as one on October 11, 2023, illustrating Israeli bombings turning vibrant neighborhoods into "ghost towns."[23][24] Azaiza's photographs captured specific incidents of civilian casualties and rescue operations, including a widely shared image from October 2023 depicting Nada Jwaifel pinned under debris in her family's bombed home at Al-Nuseirat refugee camp, where strikes had killed seven of her siblings.[25][26] This image, disseminated rapidly online, prompted international aid responses that enabled her extraction and medical treatment, averting potential amputation.[27][28] He continued such unembedded fieldwork across Hamas-administered zones for over 100 days, navigating active combat zones without formal press accreditation or external safety coordination.[6][29] The empirical reach of Azaiza's outputs surged, with his pre-war follower count of around 25,000 expanding to 13 million by mid-November 2023, driven by viral shares of unfiltered Gaza footage amid limited traditional media access.[21][22] This growth reflected his focus on immediate, on-the-ground visuals rather than contextual analysis, though his positioning in restricted-access areas underscored reliance on local mobility amid Hamas oversight of northern and central Gaza.[30][31]Post-2023 Activities and International Engagements Motaz Azaiza evacuated Gaza on January 23, 2024, after 108 days of on-the-ground documentation, departing via Egypt's El Arish International Airport aboard a Qatari military aircraft to Doha, Qatar.[32][33] From Qatar, Azaiza transitioned to international advocacy, reducing direct fieldwork in Gaza while leveraging his platform for global outreach.[6] In 2024 and 2025, Azaiza undertook multiple speaking tours, including events in Europe such as London in May 2024 and Belfast and Derry in August 2024.[34][35] He conducted U.S. tours under the "Gaza Through My Lens" banner, organized with UNRWA USA to raise funds for Palestinian refugees, targeting at least $1 million per tour for shelter, food, medical aid, and mental health support in Gaza.[31][36] These efforts occurred amid Israeli government accusations of UNRWA's ties to Hamas, including claims of staff participation in the October 7, 2023, attacks, which prompted temporary funding suspensions by several donor nations.[37] The 2025 U.S. tours spanned cities including Boston, Philadelphia, Chicago, Minneapolis, San Francisco in June, and a fall four-city itinerary that included Atlanta on October 17, where Azaiza discussed his Gaza experiences to foster awareness and support.[31][38] In September 2025, Azaiza established the Motaz Foundation Inc. to channel funds and visibility toward Gaza aid.[39] Azaiza maintained advocacy via Instagram, where he shared tour updates, speeches, and calls for aid, sustaining engagement with over 16 million followers while expressing ongoing concern for those remaining in Gaza.[40][41] This online
