--- [Planet Waves - Wikipedia] ---
Planet Waves is the fourteenth studio album by American singer-songwriter Bob Dylan, released on January 17, 1974,  by Asylum Records in the United States and Island Records in the United Kingdom. 
Dylan is supported on the album by longtime collaborators the Band, with whom he embarked on a major reunion tour (documented on the live album Before the Flood) following its release. With a successful tour and a host of publicity, Planet Waves was a hit, enjoying a brief stay at No. 1 on the US Billboard charts—a first for the artist—and No. 7 in the UK. Reviews were generally positive; critics were not as negative as they had been with some then-recent Bob Dylan albums (namely Self Portrait and Dylan) but still not enthusiastic for the album's brand of laid-back roots rock, but it was nevertheless considered an improvement over those albums.
The album was originally set to be titled Ceremonies of the Horsemen, a reference to the song "Love Minus Zero/No Limit", from the 1965 album Bringing It All Back Home; the release was delayed two weeks when Dylan decided to change the title at the last minute. Another, earlier, working title was Wedding Song.

Planet Waves Overview Album Details Planet Waves is the fourteenth studio album by Bob Dylan, released on January 17, 1974, by Asylum Records in the United States and Island Records in the United Kingdom.[7][8] The album was initially proposed to be titled Ceremonies of the Horsemen, a phrase drawn from Dylan's earlier song "Love Minus Zero/No Limit," but Dylan changed the title at the last minute.[9] Classified as roots rock, the album runs for a total length of 42:12 and features Dylan collaborating closely with The Band, marking a significant reunion following their informal sessions that later yielded The Basement Tapes.[10][11] It was produced by Rob Fraboni, who oversaw the recording process to capture the group's warm, organic sound.[7][12] The release became Dylan's first album to reach number one on the Billboard 200 chart, highlighting the commercial success of this partnership.[13]Context in Dylan's Career In the early 1970s, Bob Dylan was emerging from a period of relative seclusion following his 1966 motorcycle accident near Woodstock, New York, which had prompted a retreat from the intense public scrutiny of his mid-1960s electric phase and led to a focus on recovery and domestic stability.[14][15] During this time, Dylan prioritized family life with his wife Sara and their children, which influenced a turn toward more introspective and home-centered themes in his songwriting, reflecting a desire for simplicity amid personal healing.[16] This phase marked a hiatus from major touring, lasting nearly eight years, during which he explored roots-oriented sounds in albums like John Wesley Harding (1967) and Nashville Skyline (1969), emphasizing acoustic intimacy over the experimental rock of his earlier work.[17][18] Planet Waves (1974) arrived as Dylan's first full-length studio album since New Morning (1970), following the soundtrack Pat Garrett & Billy the Kid (1973)—which featured sparse contributions to Sam Peckinpah's Western film—and the outtakes collection Dylan (1973), both released on Columbia Records and signaling a transitional, low-key output amid his creative reorientation.[19] The album represented a pivotal reunion with The Band—formerly Dylan's backing group the Hawks during his controversial 1965–1966 electric tours—marking their first formal studio collaboration since the informal Basement Tapes sessions in 1967, which had captured spontaneous roots explorations at Dylan's Woodstock home.[20] This partnership built on their shared history of blending folk, rock, and country elements, allowing Dylan to delve deeper into unadorned, collaborative songcraft.[19] The release of Planet Waves also signified Dylan's brief departure from Columbia Records, his label since 1962, to sign a lucrative deal with Asylum Records under David Geffen, reflecting a strategic shift toward a more artist-friendly environment during a period of industry flux.[21][19] As a prelude to Dylan's long-awaited return to the road, the album set the stage for his 1974 reunion tour with The Band—their first joint performances since 1966—which was later documented on the live double album Before the Flood (1974), capturing the duo's revitalized chemistry before packed arenas.[20][22]Production Recording Sessions The recording sessions for Planet Waves occurred over 11 days from November 2 to 14, 1973, at The Village Recorder studio in Santa Monica, California.[12] These sessions spanned six dates—November 2, 5, 6, 8, 9, and 14—yielding the album's 11 tracks through a process that began with setup and initial takes on the first day.[23] Engineer and producer Rob Fraboni noted the rapid pace, with the group entering the studio after rehearsals in Malibu to capture material spontaneously.[12] The production emphasized a live band feel, with minimal overdubs to preserve the raw energy of performances recorded on 16-track tape.[24] Fraboni, selected for his familiarity with Dylan's style, focused on efficient engineering to match the group's improvisational approach, where The Band followed Dylan's lead in loose takes.[12] This method balanced acoustic and electric elements through the musicians' setup, prioritizing natural interplay over layered production.[25] Key events included multiple attempts at "Forever Young" during the final session on November 14, where the slow version was finalized after several takes.[26] Outtakes from these sessions, such as "Nobody 'Cept You" recorded on November 2, were later released on compilations like The Bootleg Series Volumes 1–3.[27] Challenges arose from the album's initial working title, Ceremonies of the Horsemen, which was changed to Planet Waves at the last minute, contributing to a sense of haste in completion.[9] The project has been viewed as rushed despite the efficient sessions, leading to its quick wrap-up ahead of the upcoming tour.[28]Personnel The album Planet Waves primarily features Bob Dylan as the lead artist, performing vocals, guitar, harmonica, and piano across the recordings.[8] Backed by his longtime collaborators The Band, the core ensemble delivered a tight, roots-oriented sound that marked a reunion following the 1967 Basement Tapes sessions (released as an album in 1975).[2] The Band's contributions were integral to the album's ensemble style, with each member providing both instrumental and vocal support. Robbie Robertson handled lead guitar duties, shaping the album's melodic structures with his distinctive phrasing.[8] Rick Danko played bass and contributed backing vocals, anchoring the rhythm section with a warm, fluid tone.[2] Levon Helm provided drums and additional vocals, driving the tracks with his straightforward, propulsive style.[8] Garth Hudson contributed keyboards, including organ and clavinet, adding textural depth to the arrangements.[8] Richard Manuel played piano and drums while delivering backing vocals, bringing emotional resonance through his multi-instrumental versatility.[2] On the production side, Rob Fraboni served as both producer and chief engineer, capturing the sessions' spontaneous energy at The Village Recorder in Los Angeles.[12] He was assisted by engineer Nat Jeffery, who handled additional technical support.[2]Robbie Robertson also received special assistance credit for his involvement in the process.[2] The album involved no major guest musicians beyond the core lineup, emphasizing a collaborative group dynamic in all tracks.[8]Artwork and Packaging Cover Art The cover art for Planet Waves was hand-drawn by Bob Dylan himself, showcasing his longstanding interest in visual art that dates back to the early 1960s.[29] The front cover features an abstract illustration in black ink on a white background, depicting three enigmatic figures interpreted as a band performing on stage, with one resembling Dylan centrally positioned.[30] Accompanying elements include symbolic details such as an anchor tattoo on one figure's forehead, a pierced heart emblem on a jacket, the word "Moonglow" on a sleeve, a grey crystal, a peace sign, and a badge bearing the inscription "cast iron songs & torch ballads," which encapsulates Dylan's vision of the album's blend of sturdy folk-rock compositions and emotive ballads.[30] These design motifs evoke themes of musical performance and personal introspection, with the figures and objects drawing from Dylan's sketchbook imagery to symbolize order imposed on emotional chaos.[29] The cosmic and wave elements subtly nod to the album's title, inspired by astrological influences.[30] In contrast, the back cover adopts a structured layout with black lettering on white, framed by a golden border, divided into three columns: left-side handwritten diary-like excerpts, a central personnel list overlaid on the title "Planet Waves" with undulating wave graphics, and a right-side track listing including recording credits.[30] Produced solo by Dylan without a photographer, the artwork embodies a raw, handmade aesthetic that underscores the collaborative intimacy of the project with The Band, setting it apart from more commercial designs in Dylan's discography.[29][12] This personal touch highlights Dylan's multifaceted artistry, bridging his songwriting with visual expression.[29]Title and Design Changes The album was originally titled Ceremonies of the Horsemen, a phrase borrowed from the lyric in Bob Dylan's 1965 song "Love Minus Zero/No Limit" from Bringing It All Back Home, evoking apocalyptic imagery associated with the biblical Four Horsemen. An alternative cover featuring a painting of a dancer on the front and a photograph of Dylan wearing a top hat on the back had been prepared for the original title.[30][31] This title was altered in a last-minute decision by Asylum Records to Planet Waves, shifting to a more concise and marketable astronomical reference that aligned with the record's motifs of cosmic cycles, time, and personal transformation. The change postponed the release by two weeks, from the January 3 start of Dylan's tour with The Band to January 17, 1974.[9] The initial U.S. pressing utilized a textured gatefold sleeve featuring Dylan's own ink drawings of the album's musicians on the front and back covers, while the inner spread printed the complete lyrics on one side and additional artwork on the other. A separate gold-colored folded insert listed production credits and the track sequence, enhancing the personal, handcrafted feel of the package.[32][33] Post-release, the core design elements persisted across vinyl reissues and digital formats without significant modifications, preserving the original aesthetic's intimacy.
