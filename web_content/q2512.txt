--- [PSR J0952−0607 - Wikipedia] ---
PSR J0952−0607 is a massive millisecond pulsar in a binary system, located between 3,200–5,700 light-years (970–1,740 pc) from Earth in the constellation Sextans. As of 2022, it holds the record for being the most massive neutron star known, with a mass 2.35±0.17 times that of the Sun—potentially close to the Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff mass upper limit for neutron stars. The pulsar rotates at a frequency of 707.31 Hz (a period of 1.4137 ms), making it the second-fastest-spinning pulsar known, and the fastest-spinning pulsar known within the Milky Way.
PSR J0952−0607 was discovered by the Low-Frequency Array (LOFAR) radio telescope during a search for pulsars in 2016. It is classified as a black widow pulsar, a type of pulsar harboring a closely-orbiting substellar-mass companion that is being ablated by the pulsar's intense high-energy solar winds and gamma-ray emissions. The pulsar's high-energy emissions have been detected in gamma-ray and X-ray wavelengths.

PSR J0952−0607 is a binary millisecond pulsar in the constellation Sextans, situated approximately 20,000 light-years (6.3 kpc) from Earth, recognized as the fastest-spinning neutron star in the Milky Way disk with a rotation period of 1.41 milliseconds (spin frequency of 707 Hz) and the most massive precisely measured neutron star at 2.35 ± 0.17 solar masses.[1][1][2] It orbits a low-mass substellar companion of 0.032 ± 0.002 solar masses every 6.42 hours in a nearly circular binary system (eccentricity < 0.004), where the pulsar's high-energy radiation irradiates and ablates the underfilling Roche lobe companion, classifying it as a "black widow" pulsar with an intrinsic magnetic field strength of about 6 × 10⁷ gauss and a system inclination of 59.8 ± 1.9 degrees.[1][1][2]Discovered in 2017 by an international team using the Low-Frequency Array (LOFAR) telescope at 135 MHz as part of a targeted search for radio counterparts to unassociated gamma-ray sources from the Fermi Large Area Telescope, PSR J0952−0607 was initially identified through its extreme spin rate, surpassing previous records for galactic disk pulsars.[2][2] Subsequent observations, including gamma-ray timing analysis in 2019 confirming pulsations up to 100 GeV and X-ray spectroscopy revealing a soft thermalspectrum consistent with a hot neutron star surface, have highlighted its high luminosity and evolutionary history as a recycled pulsar that accreted mass from its companion to achieve its rapid spin and high mass.[3] Its properties place it near the theoretical maximum mass limit for neutron stars, providing critical constraints on the equation of state for ultra-dense matter and the boundary between neutron stars and black holes.[1] Location and Identification Coordinates and Distance PSR J0952−0607 is located in the constellation Sextans.[4]The pulsar's equatorial coordinates in the J2000 epoch are right ascension 09ʰ 52ᵐ 08.³¹⁹ˢ and declination −06° 07′ 23.49″.[5]Distance estimates to PSR J0952−0607 have been derived using both radio and optical methods, revealing a notable discrepancy. The radio-based distance is obtained from the pulsar's dispersion measure (DM) of 22.41149 pc cm⁻³, which quantifies the integrated electron density along the line of sight; applying Galactic electron density models such as NE2001 and YMW16 yields a range of 970–1740 pc (approximately 3200–5700 light-years).[5] In contrast, optical distance determination relies on spectrophotometry and multicolor photometry of the faint companion star, modeling its light curves and radial velocities to fit the system's geometry and luminosity; this approach suggests a much larger distance of about 6260 pc.[6]The discrepancy between the radio DM-based estimate and the optical measurement may arise from uncertainties in the Galactic electron density models or assumptions in the companion's atmospheric and orbital modeling, though the latter provides tighter constraints when incorporating the system's inclination and eclipse properties.[6] While the optical distance supports higher luminosity derivations for the pulsar's emissions, the radio range of 970–1740 pc is often preferred in pulsar catalogs for consistency with dispersion measure analyses across similar objects.[5] Discovery and Initial Observations PSR J0952−0607 was initially identified as an unassociated gamma-ray source, cataloged as 3FGL J0955.4−0600, during the Fermi Large Area Telescope (LAT) sky survey spanning 2008 to 2015.[2] This source appeared in the third Fermi LAT catalog (3FGL) released in 2015, lacking an identified counterpart at other wavelengths, prompting targeted searches for pulsars among such unassociated gamma-ray objects.[2]The radio counterpart was discovered on December 25, 2016, through a blind survey at 135 MHz using the Low-Frequency Array (LOFAR) telescope, revealing pulsations at a spin frequency of 707 Hz, making it the fastest-spinning millisecond pulsar known in the Galactic field at the time.[2] Confirmation followed on January 4, 2017, with additional LOFAR observations using high-band antennas, which detected coherent radio pulsations and established the binary nature of the system.[2] The discovery was led by a team including C. G. Bassa, J. W. T. Hessels, and R. P. Breton from institutions such as ASTRON and the University of Amsterdam.[2]Follow-up radio timing observations in 2017 refined the pulsar's parameters, utilizing LOFAR for sessions on January 28–29 and March 1, as well as the Green Bank Telescope at 350 MHz on March 1, to measure dispersion and orbital properties.[2] Complementary optical imaging was conducted with the Isaac Newton Telescope on January 17–18, 2017, identifying a faint variable companion star consistent with a low-mass binary system.[2] The findings were announced in a paper published on September 5, 2017, in The Astrophysical Journal Letters by Bassa et al., accompanied by a NASA press release highlighting the pulsar's extreme rotation rate.[2][7] Binary System Orbital Parameters PSR J0952−0607 orbits a low-mass companion with a period of 6.42 hours, derived from phase-connected radio timing observations using the Low-Frequency Array (LOFAR) and Green Bank Telescope (GBT).[8] The orbit is nearly circular, with an eccentricity consistent with zero within measurement uncertainties (e < 0.004 at 95% confidence).[9]The projected semi-major axis of the pulsar's orbit is 0.06267 light-seconds, corresponding to a semi-major axis for the companion's orbit of approximately 1,600,000 km when accounting for the system's total mass of about 2.4 solar masses from Kepler's third law.[8][10] The orbital inclination is 59.8° ± 1.9°, determined through modeling of optical light curves and radial velocity measurements of the companion using Keck telescope spectrophotometry.[10]No radio eclipses are observed in this system, despite the presence of an intrabinary medium from the companion, likely due to the moderate inclination and limited extent of ionized material, enabling pulsations to be detectable throughout the orbit.[8][9] These parameters, combined with radio timing, yield a companion mass function of approximately 1.8 × 10^{-6} solar masses, providing constraints on the component masses.[10] Companion Star Properties The companion to PSR J0952−0607 is a low-mass substellar object, with a mass of 0.032 ± 0.002 M⊙, characteristic of a brown dwarf-like body in a tight 6.42-hour orbit.[1] This mass places it well below the hydrogen-burning limit for main-sequence stars, highlighting its evolution as a disrupted low-mass companion in the binary system.[1]Spectrophotometric observations reveal a night-side effective temperature of 3085 ± 85 K, while the irradiated day-side reaches approximately 6200 K due to intense heating from the pulsar's relativistic wind.[1] The companion's luminosity, dominated by this irradiation, is estimated at 9.96 ± 1.20 L⊙ (in solar units), reflecting the energy input from the pulsar rather than intrinsic nuclear processes.[1] Keck telescope spectroscopy from 2018–2022, covering wavelengths of 3300–10,500 Å, shows spectra dominated by metal lines, best fit by a G1 template but indicative of a substellar composition altered by ablation.[1]As a classic "black widow" companion, the star exhibits strong evidence of ablation and evaporation driven by the pulsar's wind, including a heated day-side and significant optical variability exceeding 5 magnitudes over the orbital phases.[1] This variability manifests as flares and modulated brightness, with the companion appearing extremely faint at minimum light, challenging detection even with 10-m class telescopes.[1] Mass-loss rate estimates for such systems suggest ongoing evaporation at rates of ~10−12 to 10−11 M⊙ yr−1, consistent with the observed spectral signatures of material stripping.[1]Imaging and radial-velocity measurements from 2022 Keck observations (e.g., MJD 59637–59641) confirm these properties, revealing multicolor light curves that trace the illuminated hemisphere and support models of direct pulsar wind heating without significant intrabinary shocks.[1] Physical Characteristics Rotation and Spin Properties PSR J0952−0607 exhibits one of the most rapid rotations among known neutron stars, with a precisely measured spin period of 1.41379835502312 ms, equivalent to a spin frequency of 707.31 Hz. This exceptional speed positions it as the fastest-spinning neutron star in the Galactic disk, while ranking as the second-fastest overall, behind only the 716 Hz pulsar PSR J1748−2446ad located in the globular cluster Terzan 5.[8][9] The pulsar's rotation was initially characterized through radio observations with the Low-Frequency Array (LOFAR), spanning multiple epochs to establish a timing solution that resolves its binary orbital modulation.[8]The stability of this rotation is remarkable, featuring a low observed spin-down rate of P˙≈4.6×10−21 s s−1, which corrects to an even lower intrinsic value of approximately 2.0×10−21 s s−1 after accounting for galactic acceleration effects via the Shklovskii term. This minimal ν˙≈−2.4×10−15 Hz s−1 is characteristic of recycled millisecond pulsars, where prior mass transfer from the companion star accelerated the neutron star's spin to near-breakup velocities.[9] Such stability enables long-term timing precision, essential for detecting subtle orbital variations and pulsar emissions.[9]Radio pulse profiles of PSR J0952−0607 display narrow, double-peaked components separated by about 0.15 in pulse phase, with a steep spectrum indicative of low-frequency emission dominance. These features, observed at central frequencies around 149 MHz, facilitate accurate phase alignment with gamma-ray pulsations detected over seven years of Fermi Large Area Telescope data.[8][9]The pulsar's rotational energy loss rate, E˙≈6.4×1034 erg s−1, quantifies the power available from its spin-down and primarily drives the observed non-thermal emissions in radio and gamma rays, underscoring its role as an efficient particle accelerator.[9] Mass, Radius, and Age PSR J0952−0607 has a mass of 2.35±0.17M⊙, determined through spectrophotometric and imaging observations of its companion star using the Keck I telescope. These 2022 measurements involved modeling the companion's light curves and radial velocity variations with the ICARUS code and Markov chain Monte Carlo fits, yielding the neutron star's mass after accounting for the binary system's orbital inclination and companion properties.[1] Prior estimates before 2022 were less precise and lower, with no well-constrained value available from initial radio timing data.[1] This mass measurement positions PSR J0952−0607 as the heaviest known neutron star in the Galaxy, challenging models of dense matter and implying a maximum neutron starmass exceeding 2.19M⊙ at 1σ confidence.[1]The radius of the pulsar is constrained by theoretical mass-radius relations derived from equations of state for neutron star interiors.The characteristic age of PSR J0952−0607 is 4.9 Gyr, calculated from its spin period of 1.41 ms and observed period derivative of 4.6×10−21 s s−1, using the standard formulaτc=P/(2P˙) after corrections for galactic acceleration.[11] This age indicates the pulsar has been spinning down for billions of years since its formation, consistent with its classification as an old millisecond pulsar recycled through accretion in the binary system.[11] Magnetic Field Strength The surface magnetic field strength of PSR J0952−0607 is estimated at B≈6.1×107 gauss, derived from its spin period P and intrinsic spin-down rate P˙int using the standard dipole braking formula B=3.2×1019PP˙ gauss.[6] This value incorporates corrections for the Shklovskii effect due to the pulsar's proper motion, yielding an intrinsic P˙int≈2.0×10−21 s s−1, significantly lower than the observed P˙obs=4.6×10−21 s s−1.[6][12]This magnetic field places PSR J0952−0607 among the weakest known for neutron stars, with only about ten other pulsars in the ATNF catalog exhibiting lower intrinsic fields.[6][13] In contrast, typical young pulsars possess fields on the order of 1012 gauss, while millisecondpulsars like PSR J0952−0607 generally range from 108 to 1010 gauss.[12] The exceptionally low field is consistent with the recycling scenario in binary evolution, where prolonged accretion from a low-mass companion buries or suppresses the original field, allowing the neutron star to achieve and maintain its extreme spin rate of 707 Hz over billions of years.[6]The reduced magnetic field contributes to the pulsar's long-term rotational stability by minimizing energy loss through magnetic dipoleradiation, enabling sustained high-speed rotation without rapid deceleration.[12] This characteristic underscores PSR J0952−0607's role as a black widow pulsar, where ongoing low-level accretion further influences field evolution and pulsar properties.[6] Emissions and Detection Radio Emissions PSR J0952−0607 was discovered through targeted radio pulsation searches using the Low-Frequency Array (LOFAR) telescope, operating in the 110–188 MHz High Band Antenna (HBA) range with a central frequency of 135 MHz. The pulsar exhibits a narrow pulse profile that is double-peaked, comprising four distinct components, which evolves across frequencies due to scatter-broadening effects prominent at lower observing bands. Follow-up observations with the Green Bank Telescope (GBT) at 350 MHz confirmed the detection and provided higher-frequency data, revealing a mean flux density of 1.5 mJy at this band.The dispersion measure (DM) of the pulsar, measured as 22.41149 ± 0.00010 pc cm⁻³, initially suggested a distance of approximately 1.74 kpc via the YMW16 galactic electron density model, though subsequent optical observations of the companion estimate a distance of ~6.3 kpc.[2][6]Flux densities vary with frequency, averaging 45 mJy at 119.6 MHz, decreasing to 9 mJy at 178.2 MHz, and extrapolated to 21 ± 2 mJy at 150 MHz, reflecting typical spectral behavior for millisecond pulsars. Interstellar scattering significantly impacts the signal at low frequencies, with scattering timescales of 113 μs at 129 MHz and 47 μs at 168 MHz, broadening the pulse and reducing detectability below 70 MHz.In its 6.42-hour binary orbit, the radio emission shows no full eclipses, unlike some black widow systems, though tentative evidence suggests modulation from ionized intrabinary material at orbital phases φ ≈ 0.26–0.28. High-precision timing analysis, primarily from GBT observations spanning MJD 57747–57852, achieves a weighted root-mean-square post-fit residual of 5.6 μs, enabling refined ephemeris parameters including spin frequency and orbital elements. Polarization properties have not been reported in initial studies. Gamma-ray Emissions PSR J0952−0607 was identified as the counterpart to the unassociated gamma-ray source 4FGL J0952.1−0607 in the Fermi Large Area Telescope (LAT) catalogs, with detections spanning the energy range of 0.1–100 GeV using data from 2008 August 4 to 2018 June 21.[11] The source exhibited a photon flux of (2.25 ± 0.15) × 10^{-9} photons cm^{-2} s^{-1} and an energy flux of (2.6 ± 0.2) × 10^{-12} erg cm^{-2} s^{-1} above 100 MeV.[11]Pulsed gamma-ray emission was confirmed through a sensitive pulsation search using the H-test, yielding an H statistic of 102.9 with a probability of false association (PFA) of approximately 5.3 × 10^{-6}, corresponding to a detection significance greater than 4σ.[11] The phase-folded light curve, aligned with the radio timing ephemeris derived from ground-based observations, reveals a double-peaked profile with peaks separated by about 0.2 in rotational phase, consistent with emission from the pulsar's magnetosphere.[11] The gamma-ray pulses lag the radio pulses by approximately 0.15 in phase, suggesting that both arise from regions near the light cylinder in the outer magnetosphere.[11]The gamma-ray spectrum is well described by a power-law model with an exponential cutoff, characterized by a photon index Γ = 0.95 ± 0.40 (statistical) ± 0.05 (systematic) and a cutoff energy E_c = 1.62 ± 0.55 (statistical) ± 0.01 (systematic) GeV, with a test statistic TS = 147.77 indicating a good fit.[11] The inferred isotropic gamma-ray luminosity is L_γ ≈ 3.1 × 10^{32} (d / 1 kpc)^2 erg s^{-1}, representing about 0.5% of the pulsar's spin-down luminosity Ė = 6.4 × 10^{34} erg s^{-1} at a distance of 1 kpc.[11] No significant orbital modulation is observed in the gamma-ray flux, which may indicate that the high-energy emission is not strongly affected by the companion star, unlike the radio pulses that experience eclipses.[11] X-ray Emissions X-ray emission from PSR J0952−0607 was detected using XMM-Newton observations in 2019, confirming the source as 4XMM J095210.6−060713 with a detection significance of 8.5σ over 25 ks exposure. The spectrum is soft and thermal, well-fit by a blackbody model with temperature kT ≈ 0.45 keV and emitting radius ~0.3 km (at 1 kpc), consistent with emission from a hot spot on the neutron star surface heated by magnetospheric processes. No pulsed X-rays were detected, and the unabsorbed flux in the 0.3–10 keV band is (1.1 ± 0.1) × 10^{-13} erg cm^{-2} s^{-1}, implying a luminosity of ~10^{30} (d/1 kpc)^2 erg s^{-1}. These properties align with expectations for a recycled millisecond pulsar.[3] Scientific Significance Implications for Neutron Star Physics The high mass of PSR J0952−0607, measured at 2.35±0.17M⊙, places it near the upper limit for stable neutron stars, approaching the Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff (TOV) limit estimated at approximately 2.2–2.5 M⊙ depending on the equation of state (EOS).[1][14] This proximity tests the maximum mass supported by nuclear matter under extreme densities, where gravitational collapse to a black hole becomes inevitable. Such a massive object challenges theoretical models by requiring an EOS that remains sufficiently stiff to prevent instability, thereby providing stringent bounds on the dense-matter composition within neutron star cores.[1]The pulsar's mass imposes constraints on the presence of exotic matter phases, such as quark matter or hyperonic matter, which typically soften the EOS and reduce the maximum achievable mass below observed values. Models incorporating early transitions to deconfined quark phases or hyperon admixture often fail to support a 2.35M⊙neutron star without additional stiffening mechanisms, thus favoring purely nucleonic interiors or delayed onset of exotica at densities exceeding twice nuclear saturation.[1][15] This observation helps delineate the density threshold for phase transitions in the neutron star interior, aligning with broader astrophysical limits from multimessenger events. As of 2025, PSR J0952−0607 remains the most massive precisely measured neutron star in the galactic disk.Inferences from the mass also suggest a compact radius of approximately 13 km for this pulsar, indicative of a stiff EOS that resists compression at high densities.[14] This compactness is consistent with radius constraints from X-ray observations of other massive neutron stars (e.g., via NICER) and ties directly to tidal deformability measurements from the gravitational wave event GW170817, which favor radii around 11–13 km for 1.4M⊙ objects and imply similar scaling for higher masses.[14] The stiff EOS required here reinforces the need for strong repulsive interactions in nuclear models to maintain stability against collapse.A 2023 study by Kumar et al. utilized the relativistic mean field framework to incorporate the PSR J0952−0607 mass, constraining parameters in models like HPU1–HPU3 that include nonlinear meson couplings.[14] These models, tuned to reproduce nuclear matter properties and finite nuclei data, yield maximum masses aligning with the observation while predicting radii and tidal deformabilities compatible with GW170817 and NICER results, thereby impacting simulations of neutron star mergers and nuclear saturation parameters.[14] Such refinements highlight the pulsar's role in validating or excluding specific EOS parameterizations central to nuclear physics.Future observations with pulsar timing arrays, such as the International Pulsar Timing Array, hold promise for refining the mass measurement through long-term phase-coherent timing of the binary orbit, potentially reducing uncertainties to enable even tighter EOS constraints.[1] Classification as Black Widow Pulsar PSR J0952−0607 is classified as a black widow pulsar, a subclass of millisecond pulsars featuring a low-mass companion star of approximately 0.01–0.1 M⊙ in a tight orbit, where the companion undergoes ablation due to the pulsar's energetic relativistic wind.[5] This designation draws from the behavior of black widow spiders, which consume their mates, analogous to the pulsar's destructive influence on its companion through high-energy particle bombardment and irradiation.[1]The pulsar fits this class due to its exceptionally fast spin frequency of 707 Hz, the highest known for a field millisecond pulsar, combined with a short orbital period of 6.42 hours and a substellar companion mass of 0.032 ± 0.002 M⊙.[5] Evidence of companion evaporation includes material stripping driven by the pulsarwind, which fills the companion's Roche lobe.[1] PSR J0952−0607 exhibits even more extreme rotational dynamics while sharing the faint, irradiated companion signature observed in other black widow systems.Evolutionarily, PSR J0952−0607 represents a recycled millisecond pulsar that gained its rapid spin and much of its mass—likely up to ~1 M⊙—through earlier accretion from a progenitor companion, possibly a red giant, before transitioning to the current ablation phase.[1] The surviving companion is the remnant core of this donor, now being gradually dismantled over the binary's lifetime.Within spider pulsar systems, PSR J0952−0607 aligns with the black widow subclass rather than redbacks, distinguished by its lower companion mass (≲0.1 M⊙) and shorter orbital period (<10 hours), as opposed to redbacks' more massive companions (0.1–0.5 M⊙) and longer orbits up to days.[16]Key observational signatures reinforcing this classification include strong orbital modulation in the companion's optical emission, with variability of ~1.6 magnitudes indicating direct heating by the pulsar beam, and X-ray detection consistent with intrabinary shock emission from wind-companion interactions.[5][3]

The heaviest neutron star ever detected is shredding its companion while spinning on its axis over 700 times per second. The neutron star, known as PSR J0952-0607, was discovered in 2017 about 3,000 light-years from Earth in the constellation Sextans. Recent measurements show the star weighs 2.35 times as much as the sun, which makes it the heaviest neutron star known. Neutron stars are stellar corpses, remnants of supernova explosions left behind when giant stars die after they run out of fuel in their cores. These stars, while only a few miles wide, boast the mass of the entire sun and more, making them the densest known objects in the universe apart from black holes. Neutron stars are born spinning and can be detected only through beams of radio waves, X-rays and gamma rays, which they emit like cosmic light houses. Because of their blinking or pulsing nature, they are frequently referred to as pulsars. Related: The first telescope of its kind will hunt for sources of gravitational waves Most pulsars spin rather slowly, about once per second. PSR J0952-0607, on the other hand, completes over 700 rotations every second, which makes it one of the fastest spinning neutron stars known (in addition to being the heaviest). Thanks to its unique nature, PSR J0952-0607 can help scientists answer some profound questions about the nature of these puzzling objects. Scientists, for example, think that when neutron stars get too heavy, they collapse onto themselves and turn into black holes. But they don't know at what mass this collapsing process takes place. They also don't understand the state of the matter inside of these stars, which are so dense that atoms likely cannot exist in their regular form inside them and instead get squashed into a soup of free-floating quarks (the constituents of protons and neutrons). The density of neutron stars is so high that one cubic inch (16 cubic centimeters) weighs over 10 billion tons. "We know roughly how matter behaves at nuclear densities, like in the nucleus of a uranium atom" Alex Filippenko, Distinguished Professor of Astronomy at the University of California, Berkeley and one of the authors of a study describing the star, said in a statement. "A neutron star is like one giant nucleus, but when you have one-and-a-half solar masses of this stuff, which is about 500,000 Earth masses of nuclei all clinging together, it's not at all clear how they will behave." PSR J0952-0607 is part of a binary system known as a black widow pulsar. Named after the notorious black widow spiders, which consume their partners after mating, these systems consist of a neutron star that devours matter from a companion star. This infalling matter is responsible for the mind-boggling rotation speed of these pulsars. The neutron stars at the heart of the black widow pulsars are quite difficult to study by themselves as they are extremely faint. The astronomers were able to estimate the mass of PSR J0952-0607 by focusing on the remnants of the companion star, which has by now been reduced to the size of a large planet, about 20 times the size of Jupiter. Using the 3.2-feet (10 meters) W. M. Keck Observatory on Maunakea in Hawai'i, they were able to obtain spectra of the visible light emitted by the disappearing companion. By comparing the spectra to that of similar stars, they were able to measure the orbital velocity of the companion star and calculate the mass of the neutron star. Filippenko and his colleague Roger W. Romani, a professor of astrophysics at Stanford University, have studied about a dozen black widow binary systems in recent years, but only six of them had a companion star bright enough to enable them to calculate the neutron star's mass. "By combining this measurement with those of several other black widows, we show that neutron stars must reach at least this mass, 2.35 plus or minus 0.17 solar masses [before collapsing into black holes]," Romani said in the statement. "In turn, this provides some of the strongest constraints on the property of matter at several times the density seen in atomic nuclei. Indeed, many otherwise popular models of dense-matter physics are excluded by this result." The study was accepted for publication in the journal Astrophysical Journal Letters and is currently available online through the repository Arxiv. --- [‘Extreme’ Telescopes Find the Second-fastest-spinning Pulsar] ---
By following up on mysterious high-energy sources mapped out by NASA’s Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope, the Netherlands-based Low Frequency Array (LOFAR) radio telescope has identified a pulsar spinning at more than 42,000 revolutions per minute, making it the second-fastest known. A pulsar is the core of a massive star that exploded as a supernova. In this stellar remnant, also called a neutron star, the equivalent mass of half a million Earths is crushed into a magnetized, spinning ball no larger than Washington, D.C. The rotating magnetic field powers beams of radio waves, visible light, X-rays and gamma rays. If a beam happens to sweep across Earth, astronomers observe regular pulses of emission and classify the object as a pulsar. “Roughly a third of the gamma-ray sources found by Fermi have not been detected at other wavelengths,” said Elizabeth Ferrara, a member of the discovery team at NASA’s Goddard Space Center in Greenbelt, Maryland. “Many of these unassociated sources may be pulsars, but we often need follow-up from radio observatories to detect the pulses and prove it. There’s a real synergy across the extreme ends of the electromagnetic spectrum in hunting for them.” The new object, named PSR J0952–0607 — or J0952 for short — is classified as a millisecond pulsar and is located between 3,200 and 5,700 light-years away in the constellation Sextans. The pulsar contains about 1.4 times the sun’s mass and is orbited every 6.4 hours by a companion star that has been whittled away to less than 20 times the mass of the planet Jupiter. The scientists report their findings in a paper published in the Sept. 10 issue of The Astrophysical Journal Letters and now available online. At some point in this system’s history, matter began streaming from the companion and onto the pulsar, gradually raising its spin to 707 rotations a second, or more than 42,000 rpm, and greatly increasing its emissions. Eventually, the pulsar began evaporating its companion, and this process continues today. Because of their similarity to spiders that consume their mates, systems like J0952 are called black widow or redback pulsars, depending on how much of the companion star remains. Most of the known systems of these types were found by following up Fermi unassociated sources. The LOFAR discovery also hints at the potential to find a new population of ultra-fast pulsars. “LOFAR picked up pulses from J0952 at radio frequencies around 135 MHz, which is about 45 percent lower than the lowest frequencies of conventional radio searches,” said lead author Cees Bassa at the Netherlands Institute for Radio Astronomy (ASTRON). “We found that J0952 has a steep radio spectrum, which means its radio pulses fade out very quickly at higher frequencies. It would have been a challenge to find it without LOFAR.” Theorists say pulsars could rotate as fast as 72,000 rpm before breaking apart, yet the fastest spin known — by PSR J1748–2446ad, reaching nearly 43,000 rpm — is just 60 percent of the theoretical maximum. Perhaps pulsars with faster periods simply can’t form. But the gap between theory and observation may also result from the difficulty in detecting the fastest rotators. “There is growing evidence that the fastest-spinning pulsars tend to have the steepest spectra,” said co-author Ziggy Pleunis, a doctoral student at McGill University in Montreal. The first millisecond pulsar discovered with LOFAR, which was found by Pleunis, is J1552+5437, which spins at 25,000 rpm and also exhibits a steep spectrum. “Since LOFAR searches are more sensitive to these steep-spectrum radio pulsars, we may find that even faster pulsars do, in fact, exist and have been missed by surveys at higher frequencies,” he explained. During its nine years in orbit, Fermi has played a role in the discovery of more than 100 pulsars, either through direct detection of gamma-ray pulses or radio follow-up of unassociated sources. LOFAR is a radio telescope composed of an international network of antenna stations designed to observe the universe at frequencies between 10 and 250 MHz. Operated by ASTRON, the network includes stations in the Netherlands, Germany, Sweden, the U.K., France, Poland and Ireland. NASA’s Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope is an astrophysics and particle physics partnership, developed in collaboration with the U.S. Department of Energy and with important contributions from academic institutions and partners in France, Germany, Italy, Japan, Sweden and the United States.
