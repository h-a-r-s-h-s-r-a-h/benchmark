--- [How the University of Alabama has grown under Stuart Bell's ...] ---
UA President Stuart R. Bell will leave legacy of growth at the Capstone University of Alabama President Stuart R. Bell recently announced his intention to retire in July, after 10 years in the top job at the Capstone. Though some of the following projects grew from roots beyond a decade ago, his tenure at UA вЂ” counting teaching, the 67-year-old Bell has been at UA 26 years вЂ” can be seen as one of physical, fiscal and numerical growth, additions to and beautification of the campus, and expansion of research and outreach. Bell served 16 years on UA faculty, chair of the mechanical engineering department, and founder and first director of the Center for Advanced Vehicle Technologies, before moving to leadership roles at Louisiana State University. He came back to UA for the presidency in July 2015. As UA's 29th president, Bell has been credited with providing a steady hand at times of change and turmoil, including the restrictions and shutdowns imposed by the COVID-19 pandemic, and massive changes in college sports. Bell has also been serving as president of the SEC (Southeastern Conference). In his decade, more than 60 UA buildings have been renovated, and 26 new facilities added. Physical campus UA bought the 168 acres of the former Bryce Hospital grounds in 2010, making its span about 2,000 acres, with about 300 buildings covering more than 10 million square feet of space. From facilities constructed in 1861 as the Alabama State Hospital for the Insane, later named for innovative doctor Peter Bryce, an $83,750,000 restoration is underway, centered around two major projects: The Catherine and Pettus Randall Welcome Center, on the ground floor at Bryce Main is a 15,000-square-foot facility opened in 2024, serving as gateway to UA. Bell called it a "jewel in the crown" for the campus. The Smith Family Center for the Performing Arts will be a 130,000-square-foot facility, home to the Department of Theatre and Dance. Faculty and staff began moving offices in 2024, though there's still some time to go before the Smith Center -- named for the UA alumni family that donated $20 million in February, largest charitable gift for an academic facility in UA history -- is ready for full operation, including performances. Smith Center construction is currently adding on to Bryce Main what will be home to a 350-seat drama theater, 450-seat dance theater, and 250-seat studio theater, with scene shop, control rooms, rehearsal halls, storage and more. Faculty offices and classrooms are in the connected Bryce Main building. A grand gala opening is currently planned for early 2026. Marillyn Hewson Hall is a 108,000-square-foot $60 million Culverhouse College of Business facility, opened fall 2021. It's named for Tuscaloosa native and UA graduate Hewson, who rose to chairwoman, president and CEO of Lockheed Martin, one of the world's largest defense contractors. The Hewsons donated $15 million. The Alabama Intercollegiate Athletics Golf Facility spans 176 acres south of Jack Warner Parkway, west of 25th Avenue NE and along each side of Kicker Road NE, with a nine-hole course, maintenance facilities and more. Construction was completed July 2024. Drummond Lyon Hall, the 25,000-square-foot new home for Human Environmental Sciences, on Colonial Drive, was completed in August 2023. The Stran-Hardin adapted athletics arena was completed in 2018. The ongoing task of reconfiguring some outlying architecture into line with the campus' mix of Beaux Arts, Greek Revival and Georgian neo-classical buildings took a big tumble forward when the 1960s era 14-floor Tutwiler towers were demolished, with a touch of pyrotechnic flare, July 2022. The new five-story Julia Tutwiler Hall, 383,015 square feet west of the older facility, built at a cost of $144,900,659, was ready to accept freshman women in the next month. For more on ongoing or planned construction, such as a 10,136 seat, 258,626 gross square feet Athletic Competition Arena, see www.buildingbama.ua.edu/search. In 2021, the campus acquired its newest iconic figure, Tuska the 27-foot, 7-ton bronze elephant statue, trunk raised and trumpeting toward the Strip. Late Gulf States Paper (now Westervelt Company) CEO and President Jack Warner commissioned the statue, installed at his NorthRiver golf complex in 2000. It was uprooted and replanted on the west side of campus, at Wallace Wade Avenue and University Boulevard, in April 2021. Fundraising Bell launched the universityвЂ™s Rising Tide Capital Campaign in October 2016. Originally intended to end with $1.5 billion in fall 2026, it surpassed that number in February 2024 and so raised it to a $1.8 billion goal. The Smith family's donation tipped it over the $1 billion mark. Rising Tide has produced more than 1,100 new endowed scholarships and created almost 70 new endowed faculty support funds. New names Some building facades got updates, including new names honoring Black innovators and leaders such as Archie Wade, the university's first Black faculty member, and Autherine Lucy Foster, the first Black woman to attend classes at UA, in 1956. Her days were cut short by violent outbursts and threats from racists. Foster was 92 when she saw the former Bibb Graves Hall, named for an Alabama governor and Ku Klux Klan leader, become Autherine Lucy Hall, Feb. 11, 2022. She died just weeks later, and a grand commemoration for the lifelong educator was held in Foster Auditorium, where Gov. George Wallace held his infamous Stand in the Schoolhouse Door, trying to block the enrollment of Vivian Malone and James Hood, in 1963. Just outside Foster is the Autherine Lucy Clock Tower, central to Malone-Hood Plaza. The former A.B. Moore Hall became Archie Wade Hall in November 2021, for the man who integrated the UA faculty, teaching for 30 years in that facility, from 1970 to 2000. Wade also helped break segregation of seating at Denny Stadium, assisted coach Paul W. "Bear" Bryant in recruiting the Tide's earliest Black athletes, played minor league baseball, coached and taught at his alma mater Stillman College. Wade died this month at the age of 80. Six UA trustees, including Tuscaloosa's Judge John England, have led a Building Names Working Group since June 2020, reviewing names and histories of buildings on all three UA campuses, including those in Birmingham and Huntsville. Consulting with historians, they've chosen to change or remove namesakes with questionable pasts. The home campus, in Tuscaloosa, has also changed Nott Hall to Honors Hall, Morgan Hall to English Building, Manly Hall to Presidents Hall, and the Ferguson Student Center to the Alabama Student Center. Student outreach The BRIDGE leadership team, comprising undergraduate men of color, assists first-year students as mentors. Early stats indicated a 10% higher retention rate over those not choosing the voluntary program. For women of color, serving a similar function is Lucy's Legacy, a living-learning community exploring the significance and experiences of women of color at UA, named in honor of Autherine Lucy Foster. The Alabama Advantage Program is a needs-based scholarship that covers the financial needs that Pell Grants don't cover, providing up to $5,000 additional. Student growth Student population continued to explode under Bell, topping 40,800 students last fall, highlighted by the largest number of in-state first-year students in campus history. Second-year retention rates reached an all-time high of almost 90%. In-state student growth outpaces the state of Alabama's population growth, up 7.9% among first-time undergraduates. First generation students grew almost 10% in 2024 alone. UA has consistently had more than 1,000 National Merit scholars enrolled in academic years, putting them near the top of all institutions in the U.S. UA has been recognized as a Top Producing Institution of Fulbright U.S. Scholars. With 17 Rhodes Scholars, 16 Truman Scholars, 43 Hollings Scholars and 20 Boren Scholars in its history. Over the last decade, more than 90,000 students have earned degrees from the Capstone. Research During Bell's tenure UA earned the Carnegie ClassificationвЂ™s R1 status for very high research activity. Sponsored research funding has grown exponentially, reaching about $270 million in 2024. UA's statewide economic impact tops $2.9 billion annually. UA was chosen by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration for a $360 million grant to form the Cooperative Institute for Research to Operations in Hydrology. UA is home to the National Water Center, improving NOAA's ability to provide usable water resource information to improve forecasts, watches, warnings and other related services. UA was awarded $16.5 million for the Smart Communities and Innovation Building, which will house the Alabama Mobility and Power (AMP) initiative, designed to address the booming electric vehicle market, and ongoing transportation concerns. --- [$88 million sale of Bryce Hospital to the University of ...] ---
MONTGOMERY - The state on Thursday plans to sell to the University of Alabama the historic Bryce Hospital and adjoining Harper Center for $87.75 million: $77.75 million to be paid in installments plus $10 million in grounds improvements, state officials said. The state mental health advisory board of trustees today unanimously endorsed the planned sale, which Riley outlined to the board, said mental health department spokesman John Ziegler. University of Alabama spokeswoman Cathy Andreen said the executive committee of the university's board of trustees approved the purchase today. Riley in December announced plans to sell the hospital, which is home to about 325 patients with mental illness. Riley today briefed advisory board members on details of the sale and contact signing that is scheduled for Thursday, Ziegler said. Andreen said the purchase will allow future expansion of the University of Alabama campus onto 168 acres of land adjoining the university. Ziegler said the mental health department will be able to lease the hospital from the university for as long as four years after the sale while the department builds a new hospital in Tuscaloosa, near the Partlow developmental center. The sale price for Bryce is $72 million to be paid in installments by the university to the department, plus $10 million in grounds improvements the university agreed to provide: $6.5 million in improvements to the central pavilion of the hospital plus $3.5 million to decontaminate land polluted by gasoline and kerosene. In addition, Ziegler said the university agreed to pay the department $5.75 million to buy the Harper Center, a geriatric psychiatric hospital that sits on eight acres adjoining the Bryce property. Ziegler said the university agreed to lease the hospital to the state for as long as 99 years.
