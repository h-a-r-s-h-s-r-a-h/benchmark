--- [Figure skating at the 2022 Winter Olympics – Ice dance - Wikipedia] ---
The ice dance competition at the 2022 Winter Olympics was held on 12 and 14 February at the Capital Indoor Stadium in Beijing, China. Gabriella Papadakis and Guillaume Cizeron of France won the gold medals, Victoria Sinitsina and Nikita Katsalapov, representing the Russian Olympic Committee, won the silver, and Madison Hubbell and Zachary Donohue of the United States won the bronze. In addition to their gold medal victory, Papadakis and Cizeron also set new world record scores in both the rhythm dance and overall total.

Figure skating at the 2022 Winter Olympics – Ice dance Overview Event details The ice dance event at the 2022 Winter Olympics was held at the Capital Indoor Stadium in Beijing, China, which served as the primary venue for all figure skating competitions during the Games.[1] This facility, located in the Haidian District,[9] accommodated the short program (rhythm dance) on February 12, 2022, and the free dance on February 14, 2022.[1] A total of 23 teams, comprising 46 skaters, represented 17 nations in the competition.[10]Ice dance formed one of the four core figure skating disciplines at Beijing 2022, alongside men's singles, women's singles, and pair skating, with the team event serving as an additional mixed format.[1] The event took place under stringent COVID-19 protocols implemented by the International Olympic Committee and Chinese organizers, including a closed-loop management system, mandatory vaccinations, and daily testing, which influenced international travel logistics and athlete training preparations.[11] These measures aimed to mitigate health risks while ensuring the competition's continuity amid the ongoing global pandemic.[12]Medalists The gold medal in the ice dance event was won by Gabriella Papadakis and Guillaume Cizeron of France, who achieved a total score of 226.98 points, including a world-record 90.83 in the rhythm dance.[3][5] The silver medal went to Victoria Sinitsina and Nikita Katsalapov representing the Russian Olympic Committee, with a total score of 220.51 points.[3][13] Madison Hubbell and Zachary Donohue of the United States 2022 Winter Olympics followed the International Skating Union (ISU) format consisting of two segments: the rhythm dance followed by the free dance.[15] This structure emphasized technical precision in the first segment and creative expression in the second, with no short program as in singles or pairs skating.[16] In the rhythm dance, couples performed required elements to designated rhythms—Street Dance rhythms such as hip hop, disco, or blues for the 2021-2022 season—with a duration of 2 minutes 50 seconds plus or minus 10 seconds.[17] Key elements included a pattern dance (Midnight Blues for seniors), a pattern dance type step sequence, sequential twizzles, a step sequence, and a short lift, all executed to reflect the chosen rhythm's timing and style.[17]Vocal music was permitted provided it featured an audible rhythmic beat and avoided aggressive or offensive lyrics, aligning with ISU ethical standards.[15] The segment highlighted compulsory pattern dance elements to ensure uniformity in technical execution.[15] The free dance allowed greater artistic freedom, with a duration of 4 minutes plus or minus 10 seconds for senior couples.[17] Required elements comprised not more than three different short lifts or one short lift and one combination lift, a dance spin, synchronized twizzles, step sequences (one in hold and one not touching), and choreographic elements, performed to music with at least one tempo or rhythm change.[17] As in the rhythm dance, music selections had to maintain a clear rhythmic beat suitable for the discipline.[15] Advancement from the rhythm dance to the free dance was limited to the top 20 couples out of the 23 entrants, based on their scores from the first segment.[16] Final placements were determined by the combined total score from both segments under the ISU Judging System, which sums the technical elements score (base values plus grade of execution) and the program components score (evaluated on skating skills, transitions, performance, composition, and interpretation of the music).[15] Costume regulations adhered to ISU guidelines, requiring modest, dignified, and athletic attire without props or accessories that could detach and pose a safety risk; men wore full-length trousers, while women could wear skirts, trousers, or tights.[15]Schedule The ice dance competition at the 2022 Winter Olympics consisted of two segments held over two days at the Capital Indoor Stadium in Beijing.[18][4] The rhythm dance took place on February 12, 2022, starting at 19:00 local time (China Standard Time, UTC+8), with all 23 competing teams performing in five warm-up groups ordered by world ranking.[18][19] The session lasted approximately three and a half hours, concluding around 22:40, and with the medal ceremony held shortly following the free dance as part of the broader figure skating program timeline from February 4 to 20.[1][21]Qualification and entries Qualification system The qualification system for the ice dance event at the 2022 Winter Olympics was established by the International Skating Union (ISU) in consultation with the International Olympic Committee (IOC), emphasizing results from key international competitions to determine national Olympic Committee (NOC) quota places.[22] Quota places were primarily allocated based on results at the 2021 ISU World Figure Skating Championships in Stockholm, Sweden, with a focus on the number of couples advancing to the free dance segment.[23] Up to 19 quota places were awarded at the 2021 Worlds, where placement points were assigned equal to final placements for those ranking 1st to 16th (1 point for 1st, increasing by 1 per position), 16 points for qualifiers placing 17th or worse, and 17 points for non-qualifiers; NOCs earned 3 places if three couples qualified for free dance with total points ≤13, 2 places if two qualified with total ≤28, and the remaining places went to the highest-placed single qualifiers from other NOCs until 19 total.[24][25] The remaining quota places were filled through the Olympic Qualifying Event, held as the 2021 CS Nebelhorn Trophy in Oberstdorf, Germany, from September 22–25, 2021, where up to 4 additional places were available exclusively to NOCs that had secured fewer than the maximum or none previously, with only one place per NOC per discipline permitted.[26] The host nation, China, was guaranteed one place via the Additional Athletes Quota if not otherwise qualified through competitive results.[22] Overall allocation rules limited entries to a maximum of 3 couples per NOC, resulting in a total of 23 quota places (46 athletes) finalized by December 2021.[22] Due to the COVID-19 pandemic, the qualification process included adjustments such as reduced participation at the 2021 Worlds (limited to 24 couples instead of the standard 30) and the expanded role of the Olympic Qualifying Event to address gaps from affected NOCs.[27]
