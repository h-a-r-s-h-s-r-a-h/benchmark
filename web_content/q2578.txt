Santokben Jadeja (c. 1949 – 31 March 2011), popularly known as the "Godmother," was an Indian criminal and politician based in Porbandar, Gujarat, who transformed from a housewife into a dominant underworld figure after the 1985 murder of her husband, Sarman Munja Jadeja.[1][2] She allegedly orchestrated the killings of at least 14 individuals she held responsible for his death, commanding a gang engaged in extortion, abduction, and violent enforcement in the Saurashtra region during the 1980s and 1990s.[2] Entering politics amid ongoing legal battles, she served one term as a Member of the Legislative Assembly, leveraging her influence despite multiple imprisonments for murder and other charges.[2] Jadeja died of cardiac arrest at her ancestral home in Porbandar at age 62, leaving a legacy marked by her improbable ascent to power and persistent family entanglements in crime.[3][2] Early life Family background and socio-economic context Santokben Jadeja was born around 1946 in Porbandar, a coastal town in Gujarat's Saurashtra region, into a family of modest economic standing reflective of widespread rural poverty in post-independence India.[4] Her early environment was shaped by the challenges faced by working-class households, as evidenced by her husband Sharman Munjha's employment as a mill worker in local industry, which offered low wages amid limited opportunities for upward mobility.[4]Saurashtra in the mid-20th century, particularly during the 1970s and 1980s, grappled with structural economic disparities, including dependence on agriculture and informal labor sectors plagued by droughts, poor infrastructure, and uneven development.[5] Gujarat's statewide alcohol prohibition, enforced since 1960 under the Bombay Prohibition Act and continued post-state formation, inadvertently spurred bootlegging networks due to high demand and weak enforcement, creating economic niches outside formal systems.[6] Ineffective policing and governance vacuums in rural areas like Porbandar further encouraged community-level self-reliance, as state support for transitioning pastoral and agrarian groups remained inadequate, perpetuating cycles of marginalization and informal economic adaptation.[7]Jadeja communities in the region, tracing historical pastoral roots, exemplified resilience through involvement in seasonal labor and small-scale enterprises, though empirical data highlight persistent income inequalities, with per capita income in Gujarat lagging behind national averages until the late 1980s.[8] This context underscored the limited institutional avenues available, prioritizing familial and local networks for survival over reliance on distant administrative mechanisms. Marriage and pre-crime circumstances Santokben Jadeja was married to Sarman Munja Jadeja, a laborer employed at the Maharana Mill in Porbandar, Gujarat. The couple resided in modest conditions within the city, a coastal hub with a mixed economy reliant on fishing, trade, and declining textile industries. They raised four sons, with Santokben fulfilling the role of homemaker and primary caregiver.[1][9]Prior to 1986, Santokben had no documented criminal record or involvement in illicit activities, maintaining a conventional family life dependent on her husband's mill wages and informal labor union engagements. The Jadejas belonged to the Mer community, a marginalized group in Saurashtra facing socio-economic constraints, including limited access to stable formal employment.[1][10]Gujarat's prohibition policy, enforced since 1960, suppressed legal alcohol production while fostering a vast underground liquor economy in regions like Porbandar, where bootlegging offered supplemental income to lower-income and backward community members amid stagnant industrial opportunities. Police records from the era noted escalating illicit distillation and smuggling operations in Saurashtra, drawing participants from communities like the Mer due to economic necessity rather than established criminal networks. This informal sector reliance underscored the pre-1986 pressures on families like the Jadejas, though Santokben herself remained uninvolved.[11][12] Entry into organized crime Husband's murder and initial revenge In 1986, Santokben Jadeja's husband, Sarman Munja Jadeja, a bootlegger affiliated with the Mer community in Porbandar, Gujarat, was killed by members of a rival gang amid escalating operations post-killing.[1] Munja's elder brother, Arjan Jadeja, died of a heart attack shortly afterward, exacerbating family devastation in a region marked by weak law enforcement and communal gang Till 1986 Santokben Jadeja was playing the role of a home maker and a mother. Santokben Jadeja was an MLA from 1990 to 1995 as a candidate of Janata Dal and was close to Chimanbhai Patel. Santokben Jadeja was alleged to be behind the murder of 14 people, who she believed were responsible for her husband's murder. Santokben Jadeja was in the news in 2008 when her daughter-in-law was shot dead by her son Karan Jadeja. Santokben Jadeja was named in 2005 in the murder of Keshu Odedara, a councilor with Porbandar municipality. Santokben Jadeja was elected on the Janata Dal ticket from the Kutiyana constituency in the Porbandar district. Santokben Jadeja served as an MLA from 1990 to 1995. Santokben Jadeja died of a heart attack in Porbandar, Gujarat on 31 March 2011. Santokben Jadeja was the subject of a Hindi film, Godmother, where her role was played by Shabana Azmi, who later won the National Film Award for Best Actress. Santokben Jadeja was not happy with how she was portrayed in the film.
