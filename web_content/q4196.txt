--- [Kiyosi Itô - Wikipedia] ---
Kiyosi Itô (伊藤 清, Itō Kiyoshi; Japanese pronunciation: [itoː kiꜜjoɕi], 7 September 1915 – 10 November 2008) was a Japanese mathematician who made fundamental contributions to probability theory, in particular, the theory of stochastic processes. He invented the concept of stochastic integral and stochastic differential equation, and is known as the founder of so-called Itô calculus. He also pioneered the connections between stochastic calculus and differential geometry, known as stochastic differential geometry. He was invited for the International Congress of Mathematicians in Stockholm in 1962.
So much were  Itô's results useful to financial mathematics that he was sometimes called "the most famous Japanese in Wall Street".
Itô was a member of the faculty at University of Kyoto for most of his career and eventually became the director of their Research Institute for Mathematical Sciences.  But he also spent multi-year stints at several foreign institutions, the longest of which took place at Cornell University.

Kiyosi Itô (1915–2008) was a Japanese mathematician who pioneered the field of stochastic calculus, developing foundational tools such as Itô's lemma and stochastic differential equations that revolutionized the study of random processes like Brownian motion.[1][2]Born on September 7, 1915, in Hokusei-chō (now part of Inabe), Mie Prefecture, Japan, Itô grew up in a family where his father taught Japanese and Chinese literature at a high school.[1][3] He graduated from the Imperial University of Tokyo in 1938 with a degree in mathematics and earned his Doctor of Science degree there in 1945.[2] Early in his career, Itô worked at Japan's Cabinet Statistics Bureau from 1939 to 1943 before joining Nagoya Imperial University as an assistant professor in 1943.[1] In 1952, he became a professor at Kyoto University, where he remained until his retirement in 1979, later serving as professor emeritus; he also held visiting positions at Aarhus University (1966–1969) and Cornell University (1969–1975).[1][2]Itô's most enduring contributions emerged during World War II, when he published seminal papers on stochastic processes, including his 1942 work introducing stochastic integration for processes with quadratic variation, now known as Itô integrals.[1] This framework, often called Itô calculus, provided a rigorous mathematical basis for analyzing continuous-time random phenomena, building on earlier ideas from Albert Einstein and Norbert Wiener.[4] His innovations, such as the Itô formula (a stochastic version of the chain rule), enabled precise modeling of diffusion processes and have since permeated diverse disciplines beyond pure mathematics.[2] In physics, they underpin quantum field theory and statistical mechanics; in biology, they model population dynamics and genetics; in engineering, they support control theory; and in economics and finance, they form the backbone of option pricing models like the Black-Scholes equation.[2][4]Throughout his career, Itô received numerous prestigious honors recognizing his impact on probability theory and its applications.[1] In 1978 alone, he was awarded the Asahi Prize, the Imperial Prize of the Japan Academy, and the Japan Academy Prize.[2] Subsequent accolades included the Fujiwara Prize in 1985, the Wolf Prize in Mathematics in 1987, the Kyoto Prize in Basic Sciences in 1998, and the Carl Friedrich Gauss Prize from the International Mathematical Union in 2006.[1][2] He was elected to the Japan Academy, the U.S. National Academy of Sciences, and the French Académie des Sciences, and received honorary doctorates from institutions like ETH Zürich.[2][4] Itô died on November 10, 2008, at a hospital in Kyoto, Japan, at the age of 93, survived by his three daughters.[4] His legacy endures through the widespread use of stochastic methods in modern science and technology.[2] Early Life and Education Childhood and Family Kiyosi Itô was born on September 7, 1915, in Hokusei-chō (now part of Inabe), a rural farming town in Mie Prefecture, Japan, west of Nagoya.[4][1]His father worked as a high school teacher specializing in Japanese and Chinese literature, and the family resided in this modest rural setting during the Taishō era (1912–1926) and the early years of the Shōwa era (1926–1989).[5]Itô grew up in an environment shaped by the agricultural rhythms of the region, where formal education was primarily available through local schools. During his high school years, he became fond of mathematical descriptions in mechanics, demonstrating early aptitude that led to his acceptance as an excellent student at the Imperial University of Tokyo in 1935.[5][4] University Studies and Early Influences Kiyosi Itô entered the Faculty of Science at the Imperial University of Tokyo (now the University of Tokyo) in 1935, where he pursued studies in the Mathematics Department.[5] Initially drawn to analysis and differential equations during his undergraduate years, Itô's interests shifted toward probability theory as he encountered foundational works by European mathematicians. In 1935, alongside classmates Kunihiko Kodaira and T. Kawata, he encountered Andrey Kolmogorov's Grundbegriffe der Wahrscheinlichkeitsrechnung at a Tokyo bookstore; his fascination with the rigorous mathematical treatment of random phenomena grew after 1937.[5]Lacking dedicated professors in probability at the university, Itô pursued the subject through self-directed reading of key texts, including those by Kolmogorov and Paul Lévy, which exposed him to the emerging European school of probability theory. His rural childhood, marked by a modest upbringing in Mie Prefecture, further cultivated the self-reliance essential for this solitary approach to learning.[1]Itô graduated in 1938 with a bachelor's degree in mathematics, amid growing national tensions leading into World War II.[2] From 1939 to 1943, he served as a statistical officer at the Statistics Bureau of the Cabinet, a position that offered some flexibility for continued research despite wartime disruptions.[6] The war profoundly affected his academic life, with Japan isolated from international scholarly exchange, severe resource shortages limiting access to materials, and overall hardships that tested the resilience of researchers.[1] In 1945, Itô was awarded a Doctor of Science degree from the Imperial University of Tokyo for his dissertation on stochastic processes, a milestone achieved under these adverse conditions.[2] Professional Career Initial Appointments in Japan After graduating from Imperial University of Tokyo in 1938, Kiyosi Itô joined the Cabinet Statistics Bureau in Tokyo in 1939, where he was employed until 1943 conducting statistical analyses.[1] This role, during the height of World War II, involved routine data processing that did not directly engage him in military activities, instead affording him considerable free time to pursue independent research in probability theory, drawing on works by Andrey Kolmogorov and Paul Lévy.[1]In 1943, amid the ongoing war and resource shortages, Itô was appointed as an assistant professor in the Faculty of Science at Nagoya Imperial University (now Nagoya University), marking his entry into academia.[1] This position allowed him to deepen his studies on stochastic processes, building on his earlier doctoral work, while navigating the institutional challenges of wartime Japan.[1]Itô's career advanced significantly in 1952 when he was promoted to full professor of mathematics at Kyoto University, a position he held until his retirement in 1979.[7] During this tenure, he contributed to the development of probability theory within Japan's academic framework, establishing Kyoto as a center for such research.[2] International Positions and Later Roles Itô's international engagements began earlier with a Fulbright Fellowship as a visiting member at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, New Jersey, from 1954 to 1956.[7] He later served as a professor at Stanford University from 1961 to 1964.[7] In the mid-1960s, Itô expanded his academic influence internationally by serving as a professor at Aarhus University in Denmark from 1966 to 1969, where he contributed to the development of probability theory within a European context.[7] Following this, he took up a professorship at Cornell University in the United States from 1969 to 1975, fostering collaborations and mentoring students in stochastic processes during a period of growing global interest in his work.[7] These positions allowed Itô to bridge Japanese and Western mathematical communities, enhancing the dissemination of his stochastic integration methods abroad.[1]Returning to Japan in 1975, Itô assumed leadership as director of the Research Institute for Mathematical Sciences (RIMS) at Kyoto University from 1976 to 1979, overseeing interdisciplinary research in pure and applied mathematics.[7] In this role, he guided the institute's expansion and promoted international exchanges, solidifying RIMS as a hub for advanced studies in probability and related fields.[2]After retiring from Kyoto University in 1979, where he held the title of professor emeritus until his death, Itô joined Gakushuin University in Tokyo as a professor from 1979 to 1985, continuing his teaching and scholarly pursuits in a tradition common for eminent Japanese academics.[7] In his later years, he remained active in mathematical research and provided mentorship to emerging scholars in Japan, maintaining his commitment to the field despite advancing age.[1] Itô passed away on November 10, 2008, in Kyoto at the age of 93.[8] Mathematical Contributions Development of Stochastic Integration Kiyosi Itô's pioneering work on stochastic integration arose from the challenges posed by Brownian motion, a random process with continuous paths that are nowhere differentiable, rendering classical integration techniques inadequate for handling integrals along such irregular trajectories.[1] This motivation stemmed from the desire to rigorously analyze stochastic processes in probability theory, extending the tools of deterministic calculus to account for the inherent randomness and infinite variation inherent in Brownian paths.[9]Building on Norbert Wiener's 1923 formalization of Brownian motion as a mathematical process with well-defined probability measures on path spaces, Itô sought to define meaningful integrals with respect to these paths. His 1942 doctoral thesis, titled "On Stochastic Processes (Infinitely Divisible Laws of Probability)," published in the Japanese Journal of Mathematics, provided foundational work on stochastic processes with independent increments.[10] Itô introduced the stochastic integral in his 1944 paper "Stochastic integral," defining it specifically for martingales, focusing on square-integrable ones like the Wiener process.[11]The core innovation was the definition of the Itô integral as the limit in probability of Riemann-type sums, where the integrand is evaluated at the left endpoint of each subinterval to ensure non-anticipation, making the integral itself a martingale.[12] This construction inherently incorporates the quadratic variation of the process—nonzero for Brownian motion, unlike in deterministic cases—which plays a crucial role in the integral's properties and distinguishes it from the Stratonovich integral, introduced later, that uses midpoint evaluations for closer alignment with ordinary calculus rules.[9]Due to its publication in Japanese amid World War II, Itô's work initially garnered limited international attention, with broader recognition emerging only in the 1950s through English summaries, translations, and his presentations abroad.[1] Itô's Lemma and Stochastic Differential Equations Itô's lemma, also known as Itô's formula, provides the stochastic differential for a twice continuously differentiable functionf(t,x) of time t and an Itô process Xt satisfying dXt=μ(t,Xt)dt+σ(t,Xt)dWt, where Wt is a standard Brownian motion. The lemma states thatdf(t,Xt)=(∂t∂f+μ∂x∂f+21σ2∂x2∂2f)dt+σ∂x∂fdWt.This formula was introduced by Kiyosi Itô in his 1951 paper, where it serves as a fundamental tool for handling the dynamics of functions under stochastic perturbations.Building on his earlier development of the stochastic integral in 1944, Itô's lemma enables the analysis and solution of stochastic differential equations (SDEs) of the form dXt=μ(t,Xt)dt+σ(t,Xt)dWt. In his seminal 1951 work, Itô formalized the theory of such SDEs, establishing existence and uniqueness theorems under Lipschitz conditions on the drift μ and diffusion σ coefficients, thereby providing a rigorous framework for Markov processes driven by Brownian motion.[13]The derivation of Itô's lemma relies on an adjusted Taylor expansion that accounts for the non-zero quadratic variation of Brownian motion. Specifically, while the ordinary Taylor expansion for df includes terms up to second order, the stochastic case incorporates the relation (dWt)2=dt (in the mean-square sense), as higher-order terms like dt⋅dWt and (dt)2 vanish, but the second derivative term arises from the quadratic variation. This adjustment distinguishes Itô calculus from classical deterministic calculus and is detailed in Itô's 1951 exposition.A classic application of Itô's lemma is to geometric Brownian motion, an SDE modeling asset prices in finance: dSt=μStdt+σStdWt. Applying the lemma to f(t,St)=logSt yields d(logSt)=(μ−21σ2)dt+σdWt, illustrating how the second-order term corrects for the stochastic volatility. This example, rooted in Itô's SDE framework, highlights the lemma's utility without requiring explicit pathwise solutions.[13] Broader Impacts on Probability Theory Itô's foundational work on stochastic integration in the 1940s provided the rigorous mathematical framework that enabled subsequent developments in martingale theory during the 1950s and 1960s. Although martingales had been introduced earlier by researchers like Doob and Lévy, Itô's pathwise construction of stochastic integrals for Brownian motion allowed for their extension to general semimartingales, transforming martingale theory into a cornerstone of modern stochastic analysis.[14] His innovations, such as the use of regularization techniques inspired by Doob, ensured the logical consistency of these extensions, facilitating applications in areas requiring predictable processes under uncertainty.[1]Parallel to these advances, Itô made seminal contributions to the theory of diffusion processes throughout the 1950s and 1960s, culminating in his collaborative book Diffusion Processes and Their Sample Paths with Henry P. McKean Jr., published in 1965. This work rigorously characterized the sample path properties of diffusions, including continuity and irregularity, using probabilistic methods to bridge Markov processes and partial differential equations.[10] Itô's approach emphasized the representation of diffusions via stochastic differential equations, providing tools for analyzing multidimensional processes that were previously intractable.[14]Itô synthesized these stochastic methods in key textbooks that shaped probability education and research. His 1953 book Probability Theory, written in Japanese, introduced measure-theoretic foundations for stochastic processes, influencing generations of probabilists in Japan and beyond.[1] Later, Introduction to Probability Theory (1984 English translation of selected chapters from his 1978 Japanese edition) offered a concise, rigorous treatment of core concepts, emphasizing intuitive yet formal derivations of probabilistic tools.[10] These texts integrated stochastic calculus with classical probability, promoting its adoption as a unified framework.The interdisciplinary reach of Itô's stochastic calculus extends to diverse fields, where Itô processes and his lemma serve as essential tools for modeling random phenomena. In population genetics, diffusion approximations based on Itô's theory model allele frequency changes under selection and drift, as seen in works by Motoo Kimura that rely on these processes for neutral theory predictions.[14] In quantum mechanics, extensions like quantum stochastic calculus, inspired by Itô's framework, describe open quantum systems and continuous measurements, enabling the analysis of noise in quantum optics.[15] Financial modeling has been profoundly impacted, with the Black-Scholes-Merton option pricing formula (1973) deriving its partial differential equation solution through Itô's lemma applied to geometric Brownian motion.[16]In his later career, Itô advanced the study of Markov processes through semigroups of operators, particularly in the 1960s and beyond. His work on Poisson point processes attached to Markov processes (1963) and contributions to semigroup theory in probability (1993) provided operator-theoretic methods for constructing and analyzing transition semigroups, linking infinitesimal generators to stochastic flows.[10] These developments enriched the probabilistic understanding of Markov dynamics, influencing modern treatments of Feller processes and boundary value problems.[17] Recognition and Legacy Major Awards and Honors Kiyosi Itô was honored with several major awards recognizing his pioneering contributions to probability theory and stochastic analysis. In 1977, he received the Asahi Prize for his significant advancements in probability theory.[7] In 1978, he was awarded the Imperial Prize and the Japan Academy Prize of the Japan Academy for his work in stochastic analysis.[7]In 1985, Itô received the Fujiwara Prize.[7] In 1987, he was bestowed the Wolf Prize in Mathematics by the Wolf Foundation for his fundamental contributions to stochastic differential equations.[18] He earned an honorary doctorate from ETH Zürich in 1987.[7] In 1992, the University of Warwick conferred upon him an honorary Doctor of Science degree.[7] He also received a Docteur Honoris Causa from Université Paris VI in 1981.[7]Itô was elected as a member of the Japan Academy in 1991, a foreign associate member of the Académie des Sciences in 1989, a foreign member of the National Academy of Sciences in 1998, and an honorary member of the Moscow Mathematical Society in 1995.[7]Itô's lifetime achievements were further acknowledged in 1998 with the Kyoto Prize in Basic Sciences from the Inamori Foundation, celebrating his enduring impact on mathematics.[2] In 2003, he received Japan's Cultural Merit Prize.[7] Finally, in 2006, he became the inaugural recipient of the Gauss Prize from the International Mathematical Union, awarded for the applications of his mathematical research.[19] In October 2008, shortly before his death, he was awarded Japan's Order of Culture. Influence on Modern Mathematics and Applications Kiyosi Itô is widely recognized as the founder of modern stochastic calculus, a mathematical framework that has become indispensable in modeling random processes across multiple disciplines.[20] His development of stochastic integration and differential equations during the 1940s provided the tools to rigorously analyze phenomena involving uncertainty, such as stock price fluctuations and particle movements.[21] In quantitative finance, Itô's stochastic calculus underpins the Black-Scholes model for option pricing, enabling the valuation of derivatives by treating asset prices as solutions to stochastic differential equations driven by Brownian motion.[12] This application revolutionized risk management and portfolio optimization, with Itô's lemma serving as the key theorem for deriving the model's partial differential equation.[22] Similarly, in physics, stochastic calculus is essential for describing particle diffusion and Brownian motion, where Itô processes model the irregular paths of particles under random forces, as seen in statistical mechanics and quantum field theory.[20] These tools facilitate simulations of diffusion in bounded regions and the evolution of probabilistic systems, bridging pure mathematics with empirical observations in physical systems.[23]The enduring impact of Itô's work is further evidenced by the establishment of the Itô Prize by the Bernoulli Society for Mathematical Statistics and Probability, which honors outstanding contributions to stochastic analysis through biennial awards for the best papers in the journal Stochastic Processes and their Applications.[24] Launched in 2003, the prize—consisting of a $5,000 award presented at the society's conferences—celebrates Itô's legacy by recognizing advancements in areas like stochastic integration and diffusion processes, ensuring his foundational ideas continue to inspire cutting-edge research.[25]Itô's influence extended through his mentorship of a new generation of probabilists, as he supervised nine doctoral students whose work and descendants—totaling 159 mathematicians—propelled developments in stochastic analysis worldwide.[26] Notable among them were Takeyuki Hida, who advanced infinite-dimensional stochastic processes, and Shinzo Watanabe, who contributed to Lévy processes and their applications.[26] During his tenure as director of the Research Institute for Mathematical Sciences (RIMS) at Kyoto University from 1976 to 1982, Itô fostered international collaboration by hosting symposia and inviting global researchers, transforming RIMS into a hub for interdisciplinary exchanges in probability and its applications.[7] This leadership helped integrate stochastic methods into fields like physics and economics, promoting cross-cultural mathematical dialogue.[27]Itô's cultural legacy transcends technical contributions, as he likened the elegance of stochastic structures to the beauty found in music, emphasizing the aesthetic harmony in probabilistic models that mathematicians intuitively appreciate.[28] Despite initial challenges—his seminal 1942 paper on stochastic integrals was published in Japanese during wartime isolation, delaying Western recognition until translations in the 1950s—Itô's ideas achieved global adoption, influencing diverse fields from finance to biology through their universal applicability.[21] This widespread embrace underscores the timeless value of his innovations, which continue to shape modern scientific inquiry. Selected Publications Seminal Papers Kiyosi Itô's groundbreaking work on stochastic processes began with his 1942 doctoral thesis, titled "On Stochastic Processes (Infinitely Divisible Laws of Probability)," published in the Japanese Journal of Mathematics (volume 18, pages 261–301). This paper introduced the stochastic integral with respect to Brownian motion, a novel construction that allowed for the integration of non-anticipating processes against paths of unbounded variation, fundamentally advancing the analysis of random phenomena.Building on this foundation, Itô's 1944 paper "Stochastic Integral," appearing in the Proceedings of the Imperial Academy (volume 20, number 8, pages 519–524), provided early refinements to the stochastic integral by clarifying its properties and establishing it as a martingale under suitable conditions, which resolved key technical challenges in handling quadratic variation.A pivotal contribution came in 1951 with "On Stochastic Differential Equations," published as Memoir Number 4 of the American Mathematical Society (pages 1–51). Here, Itô formalized stochastic differential equations as integral equations involving his newly defined stochastic integrals, enabling precise solutions and existence theorems for diffusions modeled by such equations.[13]In the 1950s, Itô extended these ideas through several papers in the Proceedings of the Imperial Academy (later the Proceedings of the Japan Academy), focusing on diffusions and martingales. Notable among these is his 1950 work "Brownian Motions in a Lie Group" (Proceedings of the Imperial Academy, volume 26, pages 4–10), which generalized Brownian motion and diffusion processes to Lie group settings, with applications to geometric probability. His contributions to martingales during this period, including explorations of their role in stochastic integration and representation theorems, appeared in related publications and solidified the connection between martingale theory and diffusion processes.[29][9]These seminal articles not only established core tools in stochastic analysis but also inspired subsequent monographs that synthesized and applied these innovations across probability theory. Key Books and Monographs Kiyosi Itô's contributions to probability theory extended beyond research papers into influential textbooks and monographs that synthesized complex concepts for broader academic audiences, particularly in Japan during the mid-20th century. His early work, Probability Theory (1953), published in Japanese by Iwanami Shoten in Tokyo, served as a foundational textbook that introduced modern probability concepts, including measure-theoretic foundations, to Japanese students and researchers at a time when such materials were scarce in the language. This book played a pivotal pedagogical role in establishing rigorous probability education in postwar Japan, drawing on Itô's own developments in stochastic processes while providing clear expositions suitable for advanced undergraduates and graduates.[29]Building on this, Itô authored Stochastic Processes I, II (1957), also in Japanese and published by Iwanami Shoten as part of their Modern Applied Mathematics series, offering a comprehensive treatment of Markov processes, martingales, and diffusion theory. This two-volume work synthesized the era's advancements in stochastic analysis, emphasizing constructive proofs and applications to physical sciences, and became a standard reference for Japanese probabilists; its English translation, Essentials of Stochastic Processes (2006), by the American Mathematical Society, further disseminated these ideas globally, highlighting Itô's synthetic approach to unifying additive processes and their generalizations.[29]A landmark collaborative monograph, Diffusion Processes and Their Sample Paths (1965), co-authored with Henry P. McKean Jr. and published by Springer-Verlag, provided an in-depth exploration of diffusion theory through the lens of sample path properties, semigroups, and Feynman-Kac formulas, serving as both a research synthesis and pedagogical tool for understanding irregular paths in stochastic settings. This work, reprinted in 1974 and again in 1996 as part of Springer's Classics in Mathematics series, underscored Itô's role in bridging pure probability with applied fields like quantum mechanics and population dynamics, influencing generations of mathematicians with its rigorous yet accessible derivations.[29][30]Later, Itô contributed to Introduction to Stochastic Differential Equations (1976), co-authored with Shinzo Watanabe and included in the proceedings of the International Symposium on Stochastic Differential Equations (Kyoto, published by Kinokuniya Bookstore), which offered an applications-focused overview of stochastic integration and differential equations, emphasizing numerical methods and real-world modeling in physics and finance. This monograph extended Itô's foundational papers into a cohesive educational framework, aiding the transition from theory to practical implementation for researchers and engineers.[10]

Kiyosi Ito Quick Info Hokusei-cho (now Inabe, Mie Prefecture), Japan Kyoto, Japan Biography Kiyosi Ito studied mathematics in the Faculty of Science of the Imperial University of Tokyo. It was during his student years that he became attracted to probability theory. In [3] he explains how this came about:-Ever since I was a student, I have been attracted to the fact that statistical laws reside in seemingly random phenomena. Although I knew that probability theory was a means of describing such phenomena, I was not satisfied with contemporary papers or works on probability theory, since they did not clearly define the random variable, the basic element of probability theory. At that time, few mathematicians regarded probability theory as an authentic mathematical field, in the same strict sense that they regarded differential and integral calculus. With clear definition of real numbers formulated at the end of the19th century, differential and integral calculus had developed into an authentic mathematical system. When I was a student, there were few researchers in probability; among the few were Kolmogorov of Russia, and Paul Levy of France.In 1938 Ito graduated from the University of Tokyo and in the following year he was appointed to the Cabinet Statistics Bureau. He worked there until 1943 and it was during this period that he made his most outstanding contributions:- During those five years I had much free time, thanks to the special consideration given me by the then Director Kawashima ... Accordingly, I was able to continue studying probability theory, by reading Kolmogorov's Basic Concept of Probability Theory and Levy's Theory of Sum of Independent Random Variables. At that time, it was commonly believed that Levy's works were extremely difficult, since Levy, a pioneer in the new mathematical field, explained probability theory based on his intuition. I attempted to describe Levy's ideas, using precise logic that Kolmogorov might use. Introducing the concept of regularisation, developed by Doob of the United States, I finally devised stochastic differential equations, after painstaking solitary endeavours. My first paper was thus developed; today, it is common practice for mathematicians to use my method to describe Levy's theory.In 1940 he published On the probability distribution on a compact group on which he collaborated with Yukiyosi Kawada. The background to Ito's famous 1942 paper On stochastic processes (Infinitely divisible laws of probability) which he published in the Japanese Journal of Mathematics is given in [2]:- Brown, a botanist, discovered the motion of pollen particles in water. At the beginning of the twentieth century, Brownian motion was studied by Einstein, Perrin and other physicists. In 1923, against this scientific background, Wiener defined probability measures in path spaces, and used the concept of Lebesgue integrals to lay the mathematical foundations of stochastic analysis. In 1942, Dr. Ito began to reconstruct from scratch the concept of stochastic integrals, and its associated theory of analysis. He created the theory of stochastic differential equations, which describe motion due to random events.Although today we see this paper as a fundamental one, it was not seen as such by mathematicians at the time it was published. Ito, who still did not have a doctorate at this time, would have to wait several years before the importance of his ideas would be fully appreciated and mathematicians would begin to contribute to developing the theory. In 1943 Ito was appointed as Assistant Professor in the Faculty of Science of Nagoya Imperial University. This was a period of high activity for Ito, and when one considers that this occurred during the years of extreme difficulty in Japan caused by World War II, one has to find this all the more remarkable. Volume 20 of the Proceedings of the Imperial Academy of Tokyo contains six papers by Ito: (1) On the ergodicity of a certain stationary process; (2) A kinematic theory of turbulence; (3) On the normal stationary process with no hysteresis; (4) A screw line in Hilbert space and its application to the probability theory; (5) Stochastic integral; and (6) On Student's test. In 1945 Ito was awarded his doctorate. He continued to develop his ideas on stochastic analysis with many important papers on the topic. Among them were On a stochastic integral equation (1946), On the stochastic integral (1948), Stochastic differential equations in a differentiable manifold (1950), Brownian motions in a Lie group (1950), and On stochastic differential equations (1951). In 1952 Ito was appointed to a Professorship at Kyoto University. In the following year he published his famous text Probability theory. In this book, Ito develops the theory on a probability space using terms and tools from measure theory. The years 1954-56 Ito spent at the Institute for Advanced Study at Princeton University. An important publication by Ito in 1957 was Stochastic processes. This book contained five chapters, the first providing an introduction, then the remaining ones studying processes with independent increments, stationary processes, Markov processes, and the theory of diffusion processes. In 1960 Ito visited the Tata Institute in Bombay, India, where he gave a series of lectures surveying his own work and that of other on Markov processes, Levy processes, Brownian motion and linear diffusion. Although Ito remained as a professor at Kyoto University until he retired in 1979, he also held positions as professor at Aarhus University from 1966 to 1969 and professor at Cornell University from 1969 to 1975. During his last three years at Kyoto before he retired, Ito was Director of the Research Institute for Mathematical Sciences there. After retiring from Kyoto University in 1979 he did not retire from mathematics but continued to write research papers. He was also appointed at Professor at Gakushuin University. Ito gives a wonderful description mathematical beauty in [3] which he then relates to the way in which he and other mathematicians have developed his fundamental ideas:- In precisely built mathematical structures, mathematicians find the same sort of beauty others find in enchanting pieces of music, or in magnificent architecture. There is, however, one great difference between the beauty of mathematical structures and that of great art. Music by Mozart, for instance, impresses greatly even those who do not know musical theory; the cathedral in Cologne overwhelms spectators even if they know nothing about Christianity. The beauty in mathematical structures, however, cannot be appreciated without understanding of a group of numerical formulae that express laws of logic. Only mathematicians can read "musical scores" containing many numerical formulae, and play that "music" in their hearts. Accordingly, I once believed that without numerical formulae, I could never communicate the sweet melody played in my heart. Stochastic differential equations, called "Ito Formula," are currently in wide use for describing phenomena of random fluctuations over time. When I first set forth stochastic differential equations, however, my paper did not attract attention. It was over ten years after my paper that other mathematicians began reading my "musical scores" and playing my "music" with their "instruments." By developing my "original musical scores" into more elaborate "music," these researchers have contributed greatly to developing "Ito Formula."Ito received many honours for his outstanding mathematical contributions. He was awarded the Asahi Prize in 1978, and in the same year he received the Imperial Prize and also the Japan Academy Prize. In 1985 he received the Fujiwara Prize and in 1998 the Kyoto Prize in Basic Sciences from the Inamori Foundation. These prizes were all from Japan, and a further Japanese honour was his election to the Japan Academy. However, he also received many honours from other countries. He was elected to the National Academy of Science of the United States and to the Académie des Sciences of France. He received the Wolf Prize from Israel and honorary doctorates from the universities of Warwick, England and ETH, Zürich, Switzerland. He won the IMU Gauss prize in 2006. In [2] this tribute is paid to Ito:- Nowadays, Dr. Ito's theory is used in various fields, in addition to mathematics, for analysing phenomena due to random events. Calculation using the "Ito calculus" is common not only to scientists in physics, population genetics, stochastic control theory, and other natural sciences, but also to mathematical finance in economics. In fact, experts in financial affairs refer to Ito calculus as "Ito's formula." Dr. Ito is the father of the modern stochastic analysis that has been systematically developing during the twentieth century. This ceaseless development has been led by many, including Dr. Ito, whose work in this regard is remarkable for its mathematical depth and strong interaction with a wide range of areas. His work deserves special mention as involving one of the basic theories prominent in mathematical sciences during this century.A recent monograph entitled Ito's Stochastic Calculus and Probability Theory (1996), dedicated to Ito on the occasion of his eightieth birthday, contains papers which deal with recent developments of Ito's ideas:- Professor Kiyosi Ito is well known as the creator of the modern theory of stochastic analysis. Although Ito first proposed his theory, now known as Ito's stochastic analysis or Ito's stochastic calculus, about fifty years ago, its value in both pure and applied mathematics is becoming greater and greater. For almost all modern theories at the forefront of probability and related fields, Ito's analysis is indispensable as an essential instrument, and it will remain so in the future. For example, a basic formula, called the Ito formula, is well known and widely used in fields as diverse as physics and economics. Itô Kiyoshi (07.09.1915 - 10.11.2008) Kiyosi Ito studied mathematics in the Faculty of Science of the Imperial University of Tokyo. It was during his student years that he became attracted to probability theory. In 1938 Ito graduated from the University of Tokyo and in the following year he was appointed to the Cabinet Statistics Bureau. He worked there until 1943 and it was during this period that he made his most outstanding contributions. In 1940 he published On the probability distribution on a compact group on which he collaborated with Yukiyosi Kawada. The background to Ito's famous 1942 paper On stochastic processes (Infinitely divisible laws of probability) which he published in the Japanese Journal of Mathematics is given in [2]:- Brown, a botanist, discovered the motion of pollen particles in water. At the beginning of the twentieth century, Brownian motion was studied by Einstein, Perrin and other physicists. In 1923, against this scientific background, Wiener defined probability measures in path spaces, and used the concept of Lebesgue integrals to lay the mathematical foundations of stochastic analysis. In 1942, Dr. Ito began to reconstruct from scratch the concept of stochastic integrals, and its associated theory of analysis. He created the theory of stochastic differential equations, which describe motion due to random events. Although today we see this paper as a fundamental one, it was not seen as such by mathematicians at the time it was published. Ito, who still did not have a doctorate at this time, would have to wait several years before the importance of his ideas would be fully appreciated and mathematicians would begin to contribute to developing the theory. In 1943 Ito was appointed as Assistant Professor in the Faculty of Science of Nagoya Imperial University. This was a period of high activity for Ito, and when one considers that this occurred during the years of extreme difficulty in Japan caused by World War II, one has to find this all the more remarkable. Volume 20 of the Proceedings of the Imperial Academy of Tokyo contains six papers by Ito: (1) On the ergodicity of a certain stationary process; (2) A kinematic theory of turbulence; (3) On the normal stationary process with no hysteresis; (4) A screw line in Hilbert space and its application to the probability theory; (5) Stochastic integral; and (6) On Student's test. In 1945 Ito was awarded his doctorate. He continued to develop his ideas on stochastic analysis with many important papers on the topic. Among them were On a stochastic integral equation (1946), On the stochastic integral (1948), Stochastic differential equations in a differentiable manifold (1950), Brownian motions in a Lie group (1950), and On stochastic differential equations (1951). In 1952 Ito was appointed to a Professorship at Kyoto University. In the following year he published his famous text Probability theory. In this book, Ito develops the theory on a probability space using terms and tools from measure theory. The years 1954-56 Ito spent at the Institute for Advanced Study at Princeton University. An important publication by Ito in 1957 was Stochastic processes. This book contained five chapters, the first providing an introduction, then the remaining ones studying processes with independent increments, stationary processes, Markov processes, and the theory of diffusion processes. In 1960 Ito visited the Tata Institute in Bombay, India, where he gave a series of lectures surveying his own work and that of other on Markov processes, Levy processes, Brownian motion and linear diffusion. Although Ito remained as a professor at Kyoto University until he retired in 1979, he also held positions as professor at Aarhus University from 1966 to 1969 and professor at Cornell University from 1969 to 1975. During his last three years at Kyoto before he retired, Ito was Director of the Research Institute for Mathematical Sciences there. After retiring from Kyoto University in 1979 he did not retire from mathematics but continued to write research papers. He was also appointed at Professor at Gakushuin University. Ito received many honours for his outstanding mathematical contributions. He was awarded the Asahi Prize in 1978, and in the same year he received the Imperial Prize and also the Japan Academy Prize. In 1985 he received the Fujiwara Prize and in 1998 the Kyoto Prize in Basic Sciences from the Inamori Foundation. These prizes were all from Japan, and a further Japanese honour was his election to the Japan Academy. However, he also received many honours from other countries. He was elected to the National Academy of Science of the United States and to the Académie des Sciences of France. He received the Wolf Prize from Israel and honorary doctorates from the universities of Warwick, England and ETH, Zurich, Switzerland. Nowadays, Dr. Ito's theory is used in various fields, in addition to mathematics, for analysing phenomena due to random events. Calculation using the "Ito calculus" is common not only to scientists in physics, population genetics, stochastic control theory, and other natural sciences, but also to mathematical finance in economics. In fact, experts in financial affairs refer to Ito calculus as "Ito's formula." Dr. Ito is the father of the modern stochastic analysis that has been systematically developing during the twentieth century. This ceaseless development has been led by many, including Dr. Ito, whose work in this regard is remarkable for its mathematical depth and strong interaction with a wide range of areas. His work deserves special mention as involving one of the basic theories prominent in mathematical sciences during this century. from Article by: J J O'Connor and E F Robertson http://www-history.mcs.st-and.ac.uk/Biographies/Ito.html Впервые присуждалась премия Гаусса на Международном матеиатическом конгрессе в Мадриде в 2006 году.
