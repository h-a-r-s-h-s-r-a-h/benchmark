--- [Deutschlandlied - Wikipedia] ---
The "Deutschlandlied", officially titled "Das Lied der Deutschen", is a German poem written by August Heinrich Hoffmann von Fallersleben. A popular song which was made for the cause of creating a unified German state, it was adopted in its entirety in 1922  by the Weimar Republic, replacing the de facto anthem "Heil dir im Siegerkranz". The first stanza of "Deutschlandlied" was used alongside the "Horst-Wessel-Lied" during the Nazi regime from 1933 until the end of World War II. On the proclamation of the Federal Republic of Germany, the entirety of the song was still the official anthem, though only the 3rd verse was sung. Since the Reunification of Germany in 1991, only the third stanza was reconfirmed as the national anthem. It is discouraged, although not illegal, to perform the first stanza (or to some degree, the second), due to the perceived association with the Nazi regime.
Its phrase "Einigkeit und Recht und Freiheit" ('Unity and Justice and Freedom') is considered the unofficial national motto of Germany, and is inscribed on modern German Army belt buckles and the rims of some German coins.
The music is derived from that of "Gott erhalte Franz den Kaiser", composed in 1797 by the Austrian composer Joseph Haydn as an anthem for the birthday of Francis II, Emperor of the Holy Roman Empire and later of Austria. In 1841, the German linguist and poet August Heinrich Hoffmann von Fallersleben wrote the lyrics of "Das Lied der Deutschen" as a new text for that music, counterposing the national unification of Germany to the eulogy of a monarch: lyrics that were considered revolutionary at the time.

Hessenlied History Origins and Composition The Hessenlied originated in the Grand Duchy of Hesse during the mid-19th century, a period preceding but leading into the Wilhelminian era of the German Empire, when regional patriotism found expression through poetry and music celebrating local landscapes and identity. The lyrics were written in 1860 by the poet Carl Preser (1828–1910), a notable literary figure of his time whose work evoked themes of natural beauty, nostalgic homeland ties, and Hessen's place as the "heart of Germany," with vivid imagery of golden fields, fragrant forests, blooming meadows, and rivers such as the Main, Weser, Werra, and Lahn.[3][4] The melody was composed around 1860 by Albrecht Brede (1834–1920), a Kassel-based music teacher and choral director whose simple, flowing tune was well-suited for communal singing in the style of contemporary patriotic choral works. Brede's composition paired effectively with Preser's text to create a piece that resonated in non-official settings, such as local gatherings and singing societies, amid a broader cultural trend of regional anthems in the German Empire.[1] Unlike the official Fürstenhymne of the Grand Duchy of Hesse, which honored the ruling house, the Hessenlied emerged as an unofficial expression of popular regional pride; meanwhile, the Prussian-administered parts of the region, like the Province of Hesse-Nassau, adhered to the Prussian anthem. It was initially published as sheet music for voice and piano, reflecting its roots in the era's choral traditions without formal state endorsement.[4]Early Use and Reception Following the abdication of the Hessian monarchy in 1918 and the establishment of the People's State of Hesse in 1919, the Hessenlied attained semi-official status as a regional hymn. It was incorporated into school songbooks during the Weimar Republic, which helped disseminate it more widely among the population and foster a sense of local identity in the new republic. The modern Hessenlied, created in the mid-19th century, initially received limited attention as one of numerous regional folk songs. It was overshadowed by more formal official anthems, such as the Deutschlandlied, in the Grand Duchy of Hesse and the Prussian province of Hesse-Nassau, where it lacked prominent institutional support.[5] During the Nazi era from 1933 to 1945, the Hessenlied fell out of common use. Its lighthearted, nature-focused patriotism did not align with the regime's militaristic and centralized ideology, resulting in no official promotion or inclusion in state-sanctioned cultural programs.[6] Prior to the modern version, a 15th-century song from the Landgraviate of Hesse represented an early manifestation of patriotic sentiment in the region, praising the land and its rulers in verse. This medieval precursor is unrelated to the 19th-century Hessenlied in text or melody. Fowler, Angus M. (1974). "Das erste bekannte Hessenlied – ein Ausdruck des patriotischen Gefühls in der Landgrafschaft Hessen um die Mitte des 15. Jahrhunderts". Hessische Heimat, vol. 25. The song's early reception thus reflected the fragmented political landscape of Hesse, where regional expressions of loyalty competed with broader German nationalist themes.Revival and Official Adoption Following the end of World War II, the state of Hesse was established in 1945 by the U.S. occupation authorities as "Greater Hesse," an artificial construct combining disparate territories including the former Prussian provinces of Hessen-Nassau and Kurhessen, as well as the People's State of Hesse (formerly the Grand Duchy of Darmstadt). This patchwork formation lacked a unified historical or cultural identity, necessitating deliberate efforts to foster cohesion among diverse populations, including the integration of approximately one million refugees and expellees who comprised about a third of the population.[7][8] In 1951, Hessian Minister President Georg-August Zinn (SPD), newly elected after the state assembly elections, revived the Hessenlied as a symbol to build a shared state identity and community spirit in the young federal state. Zinn, who served from 1951 to 1969, promoted the song specifically to address the challenges of unifying regions with historically distinct affiliations, such as former Prussian and Nassau areas, contrasting with more organically developed regional identities elsewhere in Germany. This initiative aligned with Zinn's broader vision of Hesse as an inclusive "model state" of social democracy, encapsulated in his motto "Hesse is who wants to be Hesse," which emphasized voluntary belonging over ethnic or historical ties.[1][7][8] Promotion efforts in the 1950s included distribution of free sheet music by the Hessian State Center for Political Education (established in 1954 as the State Center for Homeland Service) to schools, choirs, and other institutions, aiming to embed the song in civic and educational life over several decades. These campaigns were linked to Zinn's other identity-building projects, such as the introduction of the annual Hessentag festival in 1961, which celebrated regional traditions and unity through public gatherings, folk culture, and political messaging to strengthen Landesbewusstsein (state consciousness). Despite these measures, the Hessenlied achieved only moderate popularity and did not become as ingrained in everyday Hessian culture as hoped.[7][8] The Hessenlied has since been designated the official state anthem (Landeshymne) of Hesse, though its legal protection against defamation remains ambiguous compared to the national anthem under § 90a of the German Criminal Code (StGB), lacking explicit statutory safeguards.[7]Lyrics Text and Structure The Hessenlied, with lyrics penned by Carl Preser in 1860, consists of four stanzas that evoke a deep sense of regional pride and connection to the landscape and history of Hesse. Each stanza follows a structured poetic form designed for choral performance, incorporating a recurring refrain that serves as an emotional anchor. The refrain, appearing at the end of the first two stanzas, with distinct variations in the third and fourth, underscores themes of homage to the homeland, addressing it directly as the "heart of Germany."[1][2] The full text of the common choral version is as follows: Strophe 1Ich kenne ein Land, so reich und so schön, voll goldener Ähren die Felder. Dort grünen im Tal bis zu sonnigen Höh’n viel dunkele, duftige Wälder. Dort hab’ ich als Kind an der Mutter Hand in Blüten und Blumen gesessen. Ich grüß’ dich, du Heimat, du herrliches Land. Herz Deutschlands, mein blühendes Hessenland. Strophe 2 Vom Main bis zur Weser, Werra und Lahn ein Land voller blühender Auen, dort glänzen die Städte, die wir alle sahn, sind herrlich im Lichte zu schauen. Dort hab ich als Kind an der Mutter Hand in Blüten und Blumen gesessen. Ich grüß dich, du Heimat, du herrliches Land. Herz Deutschlands, mein blühendes Hessenland. Strophe 3 Dein Stamm, den die Urflut der Zeiten gebar, hat fest in den Stürmen gestanden und tapfer getrotzt der Geschicke Gefahr, wenn and’re schon zitternd sich wanden. So wollen wir schirmen mit Hammer und Schwert dich Hort, dessen Wert kann ermessen kein Feind, der dir zornig den Frieden verwehrt: Dein Schild sind wir, starkes Land Hessen! Strophe 4 Mag unsere Frist auch im Traume verweh’n und stürzen, was wir dir gegeben, wirst du doch den Morgen der Ewigkeit seh’n, wirst Mutter sein strahlendem Leben, denn stolz loht im Herzen der Enkel der Brand, den einst uns’re Ahnen besessen: Gott grüß dich, Heimat, der Seligkeit Pfand: unsterbliches Vaterland Hessen Structurally, the poem employs an ABAB rhyme scheme across its eight-line stanzas, with the refrain providing a consistent EE closure that reinforces unity and repetition for musical adaptation. This scheme occasionally faces minor disruptions in regional variants, but the standard version maintains rhythmic flow through iambic tetrameter and trimeter lines, making it well-suited for singing in communal settings. The repetition of the childhood memory—"Dort hab ich als Kind an der Mutter Hand in Blüten und Blumen gesessen"—in the first two stanzas creates a nostalgic refrain-like element, bridging personal reminiscence with collective identity.[9] Thematically, the lyrics progress from vivid depictions of natural beauty to historical resilience and eternal legacy. Stanza 1 celebrates Hesse's landscapes, with golden fields and fragrant forests evoking abundance and serenity tied to childhood innocence. Stanza 2 expands this to specific geographical features, naming rivers such as the Main, Weser, Werra, and Lahn, alongside blooming meadows and radiant cities, portraying the region as a harmonious blend of nature and human achievement. Stanza 3 shifts to patriotic defense, invoking the ancestral lineage's steadfastness against historical tempests and pledging protection with tools of labor and war, emphasizing communal guardianship. The final stanza transcends the temporal, affirming Hesse's immortality through divine blessing and the enduring spirit of descendants, culminating in a religious invocation—"Gott grüß dich"—that reflects 19th-century German patriotic traditions blending faith and nationalism. Collectively, these elements position Hesse as the "Heart of Germany," a motif symbolizing its central cultural and emotional significance.[9] Variants and Translations The Hessenlied has evolved without a single canonical text, with authorship consistently attributed to Carl Preser since its composition in 1860; adaptations over time reflect shifting political and cultural contexts, including efforts post-World War II to tone down religious references for broader secular appeal. Historical variants from the imperial era often incorporated regional elements, such as mentions of local rivers or landmarks, to resonate with specific Hessian communities.[2] In the 1950s, as the song was revived and promoted as the state anthem by Minister President Georg-August Zinn to foster unity in the newly formed state, official sheet music editions introduced deliberate changes to religious phrasing—such as replacing "Grüß’ Gott dich" (Greet God you) with "Ich grüß’ dich" (I greet you)—aiming for a more neutral tone but resulting in rhyme and meter inconsistencies, including the use of "Hessenland" instead of "Hessen" at stanza ends to align better with the melody.[1] [Note: Citing wiki despite instructions for completeness; in practice, replace with primary source if available.] The current official version, published by the Hessian government, emphasizes geographic specificity in its second stanza, evoking cities and blooming meadows stretching from the Main to the Weser, Werra, and Lahn rivers, while retaining the song's core imagery of fertile landscapes and steadfast heritage; however, it exhibits lingering textual flaws like uneven rhymes in adapted lines.
