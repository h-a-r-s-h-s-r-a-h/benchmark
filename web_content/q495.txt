--- [Which is the largest saltwater lake in India: Exploring Chilika Lake ...] ---
India is blessed with many natural wonders, but few are as mesmerising as Chilika Lake in Odisha. Spanning nearly 1,100 square kilometres, Chilika is the largest saltwater lake in India and Asia’s biggest brackish-water lagoon, as recognised by the Ramsar Sites Information Service. This vast lagoon connects to the Bay of Bengal, forming a unique ecosystem where salt and freshwater merge to support extraordinary biodiversity. According to the Chilika Development Authority (CDA), the lake sustains thousands of fishing families and shelters over a million migratory birds each winter. It is also home to the rare Irrawaddy dolphins, protected under the Wildlife Protection Act. Declared India’s first Ramsar site, Chilika stands today as a living example of harmony between nature, wildlife, and local livelihoods. Formation and geographic location of Chilika Lake: How nature created India’s largest lagoon Chilika Lake lies on the eastern coast of Odisha, stretching across the Puri, Khurda, and Ganjam districts. The lake’s origin dates back approximately 3,500 to 4,000 years, when natural processes gradually separated a part of the Bay of Bengal due to the accumulation of silt and shifting ocean currents. Over time, a sandbar formed, trapping seawater behind it and giving rise to the massive lagoon that exists today. Chilika is not a typical inland salt lake; it is a brackish-water lagoon, meaning its salinity fluctuates depending on the inflow of seawater and freshwater. According to the Ramsar Sites Information Service (RSIS), Chilika Lake is recognised as a Ramsar Wetland of International Importance. It covers an area of approximately 116,500 hectares and is described as a brackish water lagoon supporting rich biodiversity and numerous migratory birds. The Daya and Bhargavi rivers feed the lake with freshwater, while a narrow channel near Satapada connects it to the Bay of Bengal, allowing tidal waters to mix in. According to the Chilika Development Authority (CDA), the lake’s water spread area varies seasonally, about 1,165 square kilometres during the monsoon and 906 square kilometres in the dry season. It serves as an essential ecosystem supporting fisheries, birds, and local livelihoods. Ecological significance and wildlife diversity: Why Chilika is a biodiversity hotspot Chilika Lake is not only India’s largest saltwater lake but also one of the most ecologically productive wetlands in the country. It is home to more than 200 species of fish, 150 species of aquatic plants, and countless invertebrates. The lake also supports over 400 species of birds, making it a paradise for ornithologists and wildlife enthusiasts. The Nalaban Island, located in the heart of the lake, is a declared bird sanctuary that attracts flamingos, pelicans, herons, egrets, and sandpipers each winter. These migratory birds travel thousands of kilometres from regions such as the Caspian Sea, Lake Baikal, and parts of Russia, transforming Chilika into a breathtaking spectacle of colour and sound. The island’s dense vegetation and tranquil waters provide ideal nesting and feeding grounds, making it a critical habitat for bird conservation and an important stopover in their long migratory journeys. One of Chilika’s most celebrated inhabitants is the Irrawaddy dolphin. Unlike the oceanic dolphin, this species is adapted to brackish and freshwater environments. Chilika is the only place in India where they are found naturally, and spotting them in the wild is a highlight for visitors. The lake’s shallow waters and rich food sources make it a perfect home for these intelligent marine mammals. Tourism, livelihood, and conservation efforts: Balancing people and nature Chilika Lake is one of Odisha’s most visited ecotourism destinations, drawing both domestic and international travellers. Popular entry points like Satapada, Rambha, and Barkul offer boat rides for dolphin watching, bird spotting, and island exploration. The lake’s breathtaking sunrises and sunsets make it a photographer’s dream. For the local population, particularly fishing communities, Chilika is more than just a tourist attraction; it’s their source of sustenance. Thousands depend on its waters for fishing and crab collection, making conservation critical to their survival. However, the lake faces challenges such as siltation, pollution, and overfishing. To combat this, the Chilika Development Authority (CDA) has launched several initiatives, including dredging the connecting channel to restore water flow, promoting sustainable fishing, and involving communities in conservation. These efforts have led to a resurgence of fish populations and an increase in Irrawaddy dolphin sightings, showing that preservation and livelihood can coexist. A natural treasure of India: Why Chilika Lake deserves global recognition Chilika Lake represents the perfect harmony between nature, wildlife, and human life. Its shimmering waters, vast bird colonies, and ancient legends make it one of the most captivating ecosystems in India. Recognised as both a Ramsar site and Asia’s largest lagoon, it continues to play a vital role in maintaining ecological balance and supporting cultural traditions. For travellers, researchers, and conservationists, Chilika is not just a lake; it’s a living, breathing world of biodiversity and spirituality. As India’s largest saltwater lake, it stands as a reminder of how nature’s wonders can nurture life, inspire awe, and bring communities together across generations. The Allure of Sambhar Lake in Jaipur Geography and Historical Significance Located in the districts of Jaipur, Ajmer, and Nagaur, Sambhar Lake in Jaipur spans over 90 square kilometers and plays a major role in India’s salt production. Its saline wetlands produce nearly 9% of the country’s total salt output. The lake derives its name from Maa Shakambhari Devi, the local goddess who is believed to have blessed the region with abundant salt to sustain local communities.
