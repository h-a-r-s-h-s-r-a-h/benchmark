Moosetape is the second and final studio album by Indian singer, rapper and songwriter Sidhu Moose Wala, released independently on 15 May 2021. Moose Wala served as the executive producer. Writing was handled by Sidhu dominantly among other artists, while the tracks were produced by The Kidd, Steel Banglez, Snappy, Wazir Patar, and JB. At 32 tracks, it is Moose Wala's longest album. It features guest vocals from Bohemia, Tion Wayne, Stefflon Don, Morrisson, Divine, Raja Kumari, Blockboi Twitch and Sikander Kahlon. The music video for the track "GOAT", featuring Jumana Abdu Rahman, surpassed 320 million views on YouTube as of October 2024.

Moosetape is a 32-track studio album by Indian Punjabi rapper, singer, and songwriter Sidhu Moose Wala, independently released on 15 May 2021.[1][2][3] The project features collaborations with artists such as Sikander Kahlon and Raja Kumari, alongside production contributions from Steel Banglez, blending trap-influenced beats with traditional Punjabi folkhop elements and themes of bravado, street life, and personal triumph.[1][4][5] Tracks were rolled out progressively rather than as a traditional simultaneous drop, generating anticipation among fans.[6][7] Moosetape marked Moose Wala's second major studio effort following PBX 1 and became his final album before his assassination on 29 May 2022.[8][9] Commercially, it shattered records as the first Indian album to surpass 1 billion streams on Spotify by November 2023, later reaching 1.5 billion, underscoring Moose Wala's enduring global influence in Punjabi hip-hop despite limited institutional promotion.[10][11][12] Background and Development Conception and Influences Sidhu Moose Wala developed the concept for Moosetape as an expansive, unpolished collection emphasizing Punjabi gangsta rap rooted in authentic personal narratives, including rivalries and tensions from his rural upbringing in Moosa village, Punjab, and the challenges of migration to Canada. He consistently composed lyrics drawn from real-life incidents, such as interpersonal conflicts and cultural dislocations, to convey raw depictions of Jat identity and street-level struggles. The album's formation followed a period of hiatus amid industry disputes, positioning it as a vehicle for direct expression unbound by conventional constraints.[13][14][7]A core motivation was personal tribute, with Moose Wala dedicating the project to his mother, whom he identified as his primary inspiration, infusing it with familial and emotional depth amid broader thematic explorations of heritage and displacement. By releasing it independently in May 2021 under his own imprint, he circumvented potential oversight from established labels, which had previously clashed with his provocative content on feuds and social issues, enabling full autonomy over the 32-track scope. This self-directed approach underscored his commitment to unaltered storytelling, free from external edits that might dilute references to village dynamics or migratory hardships.[15][6][16]External influences shaped the album's sonic direction, particularly American hip-hop artists like Drake, whom Moose Wala followed exclusively on Instagram, signaling a deliberate emulation of introspective flows and melodic trap elements adapted to Punjabi contexts. UK drill's aggressive percussion and sparse production also informed tracks, lending a confrontational edge to portrayals of conflict, as seen in fan-observed parallels to Western styles in songs like "Bitch I'm Back," despite no formal collaborations. These inspirations fused with local folk sensibilities to pioneer a hybrid gangsta rap aesthetic, prioritizing visceral realism over polished commercialism.[17][16][18] Recording Process The recording sessions for Moosetape spanned from late 2020 to early 2021, following Sidhu Moose Wala's previous album Snitches Get Stitches released earlier that year.[1] As executive producer, Moose Wala oversaw the process, which took place primarily in studios located in Punjab, India, to capture the album's regional sonic elements. Key collaborators included producers The Kidd, who handled tracks like "295," and Steel Banglez, who contributed to several beats during dedicated sessions with Moose Wala.[19][20]The assembly involved an iterative approach, with Moose Wala refining dozens of recordings to select 32 tracks for the final 1-hour-37-minute runtime, incorporating skits, intros, and full songs. Features such as Sikander Kahlon on "Racks and Rounds" were integrated through collaborative verses recorded in tandem, emphasizing layered vocals over custom beats. Creative execution highlighted the fusion of live instrumentation, including traditional Punjabi tumbi strings, with hip-hop production techniques to achieve a hybrid sound reflective of Moose Wala's style.[2][5]The independent setup necessitated hands-on involvement in engineering, leading to a characteristically unpolished audio aesthetic derived from in-house mixing rather than major-label facilities.[6] Musical Composition Style and Genres Moosetape exemplifies gangsta rap as its core genre, blending hardcore hip-hop structures with trap and drill influences evident in rapid-fire flows, sliding hi-hats, and ominous 808 basslines.[4][21] This fusion draws from Chicago and UK drill aesthetics while rooting in Punjabi musical traditions, incorporating bhangra's rhythmic propulsion and dhadi folk vocal inflections for a culturally grounded edge.[21][22]Production techniques emphasize gritty realism through layered synthesizers mimicking West Coast G-funk synths, pervasive ad-libs for texture, and sampled folk elements like tumbi strings or dhol percussion, setting it apart from polished mainstream Punjabi pop's melodic sheen.[21][4] These choices amplify raw, street-level authenticity, prioritizing dense sonic layering over commercial accessibility.[1]Relative to Sidhu Moose Wala's prior releases like PBX 1 (2018), Moosetape evolves toward broader resonance by weaving English phrases into hooks alongside Punjabi verses, enhancing accessibility for international Punjabi diaspora listeners familiar with global hip-hop.[1] This shift broadens its stylistic palette beyond regional folkhop confines, fostering a hybrid sound with transnational appeal.[21] Themes and Lyrics The lyrics of Moosetape recurrently emphasize loyalty as a foundational virtue in interpersonal and communal bonds, juxtaposed against betrayal's disruptive consequences, often depicted through narratives of retaliation in social hierarchies. In "Calaboose," Moose Wala illustrates betrayal's chain reactions, where disloyal associates face isolation and reprisal, reflecting unvarnished causal sequences in Punjabi village and gang dynamics without prescriptive moralizing.[23] Similarly, "Regret" explores remorse in fractured alliances, underscoring loyalty's role in sustaining self-made empires amid adversarial pressures. These motifs ground the work in Moose Wala's observed realities of rural Punjab, where personal oaths supersede institutional trust.[24]A pronounced rural-urban divide permeates the album, glorifying agrarian roots and self-reliant success against urban elitism and opportunism. Tracks like "Selfmade" chronicle Moose Wala's ascent from a middle-class village family in Mansa district to industry dominance, attributing achievements to grit, familial support, and divine favor rather than elitenetworks.[1] This autobiographical realism draws from his Hoora upbringing, portraying rural life as a bastion of authenticity and resilience, contrasted with city-driven betrayals and superficial gains.
