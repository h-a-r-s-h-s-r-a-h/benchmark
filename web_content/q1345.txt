Arthur Laverne Peterson (born June 27, 1926) is an American educator and politician. Peterson was born in Glyndon, Minnesota. He served in the United States Marine Corps during the Korean War. In 1947, he graduated from Yale University. He then received his master's degree from the University of Southern California in 1949. In 1962, he received his doctorate degree from University of Minnesota. He lived in Prescott, Wisconsin and worked for Eaton Plumbing and Heating. From 1951 to 1955, Peterson served in the Wisconsin Assembly and was a Republican. He taught political science at University of Wisconsin–Eau Claire and Ohio Wesleyan University. In 1966, Peterson accepted the position of President at Thunderbird School of Global Management, then known as the American Institute for Foreign Trade. During his time at Thunderbird, Peterson would land his own personal plane at the campus which once a WWII-era airfield. By the time of his departure to Pepperdine University in 1969, Peterson had helped Thunderbird achieve accreditation through the North Central Association of Colleges and Schools. In 1989, Peterson taught political science at Rocky Mountain College in Billings, Montana. He served in the Montana House of Representatives from 2000 to 2002 as a Republican.

--- [Arthur Laverne Peterson (1926-2023) - Find a Grave Memorial] ---
Peterson enlisted in the U.S. Navy in 1944 and was placed in officer training; he ultimately received a commission in the U.S. Marine Corps in 1947. After completing his undergraduate studies at Yale University in 1947, Peterson went on to earn his Master's Degree in Public Administration from the University of Southern California in 1948. His biography in the 1954 Wisconsin Blue Book indicates he "completed course work" for his PhD at the University of Chicago in 1950; his obituary states that he received his doctorate in philosophy from the University of Minnesota in 1962. He lived in Prescott, Wisconsin and worked for Eaton Plumbing and Heating during some part of his time at the University of Minnesota. From 1951 to 1955, Peterson served in the Wisconsin Assembly as a Republican. First elected in November 1950 and sworn in in January 1951, he sought leave from the legislature in August 1951 to return to the Marines and he served as a combat officer in the Marines during the Korean War. He was released from duty with the Marines in October 1952, holding the rank of Captain. He was reelected to the Legislature in November 1952, serving until the start of the new legislative session in January 1955. It is indicated in the 1954 Wisconsin Blue Book that while a legislator he continued to work at Eaton Plumbing and Heating in Prescott WI and was also a "freelance writer." He later taught political science at University of Wisconsin–Eau Claire and at Ohio Wesleyan University. In 1966, Peterson accepted the position of President at Thunderbird School of Global Management, then known as the American Institute for Foreign Trade. During his time at Thunderbird, Peterson would land his own personal plane at the campus which was once a WWII-era airfield. By the time of his departure to Pepperdine University in 1969, Peterson had helped Thunderbird achieve accreditation through the North Central Association of Colleges and Schools. In 1989, Peterson began teaching political science at Rocky Mountain College in Billings, Montana. He served in the Montana House of Representatives from 2000 to 2002 as a Republican. Peterson later lived in Billings, Montana. Peterson was a candidate for California's 39th district (US House of Representatives) in 2022, but he lost the Republican primary. Peterson was involved in public policy, and in politics, in many other roles and in many jurisdictions over the lengthy span of his career. Peterson's obituary states that among Wisconsin legislators, he was the leading Republican critic of the tactics of Senator Joe McCarthy. He was also Chairman of the Ohio Civil Rights Commission during the early years of the civil rights movement. He was chief-of-staff to the Republican National Committee and co-authored the RNC's strategy for its rebuilding efforts after the devastating defeats of 1964. In the late 1990s, Peterson served as president of the Center for the Study of the American Presidency in New York. There were other roles as well. Peterson's obituary also states that he became the prototype for one of the major characters in James Michener's novel, "Recessional" (the character of Ambassador St. Pres). Commenting on the (literary) demise of the character, Peterson is said to have remarked, "I think the deadly strike of the rattlesnake may come sooner than I would like, but it will be masked in the form of some worn out arteries of the heart." Peterson was always an active churchman and church musician in the Congregational Church (UCC). Along with his lifelong commitment to the church, Freemasonry was an important element in building the foundation that guided his life. Peterson died in Sun City, California on March 22, 2023, at the age of 96.
