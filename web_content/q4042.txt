--- [Ecstasy of Saint Teresa] ---
The Ecstasy of Saint Teresa (also known as Saint Teresa in Ecstasy; Italian: L'Estasi di Santa Teresa or Santa Teresa in estasi) is a sculptural altarpiece group in white marble set in an elevated aedicule in the Cornaro Chapel of the church of Santa Maria della Vittoria in Rome. It was designed and carved by Gian Lorenzo Bernini, the leading sculptor of his day, who also designed the setting of the chapel in marble, stucco and paint. The commission was completed in 1652.
The ensemble includes at the sides two sets of donor portraits of members of the Cornaro family, who watch the main central group as though in boxes in a theatre. The group is generally considered to be one of the sculptural masterpieces of the High Roman Baroque. The sculpture over the altar shows Saint Teresa of Ávila, a Spanish Carmelite nun (1515–1582), swooning in a state of religious ecstasy, while an angel holding a spear stands over her, following her own account of a vision she had.

Ecstasy of Saint Teresa Background and Commission Religious and Historical Context Saint Teresa of Ávila (1515–1582), a Spanish Carmelite nun and mystic, described in her autobiography The Life of Teresa of Jesus (written circa 1565) a profound vision of mystical union with God, wherein an angel appeared in bodily form and pierced her heart repeatedly with a golden spear tipped with fire, inflicting intense pain mingled with spiritual ecstasy and divine love.[6] This transverberation, as she termed it, symbolized the soul's transformation through God's penetrating grace, evoking both agony and rapture as pathways to union with the divine.[1] Canonized on March 12, 1622, by Pope Gregory XV alongside Saint Ignatius of Loyola, Teresa's swift elevation to sainthood—mere decades after her death—reflected the Catholic Church's recognition of her reforms within the Carmelite order and her writings' influence on contemplative prayer amid the spiritual fervor of the post-Tridentine era.[7] The Counter-Reformation, formalized by the Council of Trent (1545–1563), sought to reaffirm Catholic doctrines against Protestant iconoclasm by promoting art that vividly conveyed saints' experiences to foster emotional devotion and sensory engagement with faith.[8]Baroque style emerged as a visual strategy to depict mystical realities with dramatic intensity, countering Protestant emphasis on scriptural austerity by illustrating the tangible effects of grace, such as Teresa's ecstasy, to inspire believers toward emulation of holy figures and doctrinal adherence.[9] This approach privileged representations of divine intervention in human form, aligning with Trent's decree that sacred images should "excite the faithful to adoration" rather than mere instruction.[8] In 17th-century Rome, the epicenter of papal authority, Counter-Reformation popes leveraged extensive patronage to transform the city into a showcase of Catholic renewal, commissioning artworks that reinforced mystical traditions like Teresa's amid ongoing theological debates.[10]Gian Lorenzo Bernini, favored by Pope Urban VIII (r. 1623–1644) for major Vatican projects including the baldacchino over Saint Peter's altar, benefited from this environment of artistic investment, which continued under Pope Innocent X (r. 1644–1655) despite initial tensions, enabling sculptors to explore sensory depictions of sanctity under ecclesiastical oversight.[11][12] Such patronage underscored Rome's role in propagating reformed Catholicism through tangible evocations of saints' encounters with the divine.[9]Patronage Details and Creation Process ![Cornaro Chapel in Santa Maria della Vittoria][float-right] The Ecstasy of Saint Teresa was commissioned in 1647 by Venetian Cardinal Federico Cornaro (1579–1653) for the Cornaro family chapel in the church of Santa Maria della Vittoria in Rome, a site associated with the Discalced Carmelites whom Saint Teresa had reformed.[1][13] Cornaro, seeking a family burial space, leveraged his wealth and ecclesiastical ties to engage Gian Lorenzo Bernini, whose papal commissions had waned under Pope Innocent X, making his services available for private patronage.[14] The project, lacking strict financial limits, enabled extensive elaboration over five years, culminating in completion by 1652.[15] Bernini directed the execution through his studio workshop, collaborating with assistants on carving, molding, and assembly to integrate sculpture with architectural elements.[1] The central figures of Saint Teresa and the angel were sculpted from white Carrara marble to achieve lifelike textures in skin, drapery, and motion, while surrounding clouds employed stucco for volumetric illusion and gilded bronze rays emanated divine light symbolism.[16] A concealed window positioned above the composition channeled natural light through tinted glass, creating dramatic, ethereal illumination that enhanced the scene's transcendent effect without visible sources, a technique Bernini refined from prior works.[1] Contemporary documentation, including studio records, attests to iterative refinements in these multi-material assemblages, prioritizing sensory immersion over conventional altarpiece formats.[15]Physical Composition and Setting The Marble Sculpture and Supporting Elements The central marble sculpture features Saint Teresa of Ávila reclining on a bed of clouds, her body arched in a moment of transverberation, with an angel positioned above her holding a spear aimed at her heart.[1][2] Teresa's face is rendered with eyes half-closed, mouth slightly open, and head tilted back, while the angel exhibits a serene, youthful expression with one hand lifting her garment.[17] The figures are supported by sculpted clouds and enveloped by descending rays of light, enhancing the vertical composition.[18] The primary material for the two main figures is white Carrara marble, chosen for its translucency and capacity to capture fine details, with supporting elements including stucco for the clouds and gilt bronze for accents in the rays.[14][2] The overall height of the sculptural group measures approximately 3.5 meters (11 feet 6 inches), with a width of 2.79 meters and depth of 2.06 meters, allowing for the dynamic interplay of forms within a compact yet imposing scale.[19] Bernini employed advanced carving techniques to achieve lifelike textures, particularly in the deeply incised folds of Teresa's drapery, which cascade and bunch to suggest movement and ethereal lightness, contrasting with the smoother treatment of exposed skin surfaces.[17] Facial details were meticulously chiseled, with undercut shadows around the eyes and subtle variations in marble polish to convey depth and emotional intensity without additional pigmentation.[20] The angel's wings and the spear's shaft demonstrate precision in rendering feathers and metallic sheen through shallow relief and polished finishes.[18]Architectural Integration in the Cornaro Chapel The Cornaro Chapel in the church of Santa Maria della Vittoria, Rome, serves as the architectural frame for Bernini's Ecstasy of Saint Teresa, with the sculptor designing the entire space between 1647 and 1652 to create a cohesive environment that amplifies the central marble group.[13] The chapel's walls feature balcony-like structures resembling theater boxes, where relief sculptures depict members of the Cornaro family, including patron Cardinal Federico Cornaro, as engaged spectators observing the mystical event at the altar.[13] This arrangement positions chapel visitors as additional audience members, fostering a sense of voyeuristic participation and drawing them into the scene's dramatic intensity.[13] Illusionistic architectural elements, such as a coffered barrel vault and faux columns behind the sculpture, extend the spatial depth and enclose the viewer within the composition, enhancing the immersive quality characteristic of Baroque design.[13]Natural light enters through a hidden window positioned above and behind the broken pediment framing the altarpiece, directing focused beams downward onto the figures to simulate heavenly rays and heighten the ethereal atmosphere.[1] These gilded bronze elements extending from the angel's hand catch and reflect the light, reinforcing the illusion of divine intervention amid the chapel's otherwise subdued interior.[21] Bernini's integration of sculpture, architecture, stucco, and painted elements across the chapel anticipates concepts of a total artwork by manipulating visual and spatial cues to envelop the senses, transforming the space into a unified theatrical stage that intensifies the sculpture's emotional impact.[13] The viewer's approach draws them progressively closer, with the elevated niche and converging perspectives creating a dynamic spatial flow that aligns sightlines toward the climax of Teresa's transverberation. This environmental orchestration ensures the work's effect transcends isolated sculpture, embedding it within a multisensory ecclesiastical setting.Theological Foundations and Interpretations Fidelity to Saint Teresa's Mystical Account In Chapter 29 of her autobiography The Life of St. Teresa of Jesus (written circa 1562–1565), Saint Teresa of Ávila describes a visionary experience of transverberation, in which an angel pierces her heart repeatedly with a long golden spear tipped with fire, inflicting spiritual pain intertwined with profound sweetness.[22] She recounts: "I saw in his hands a long spear of gold, and at the end of the iron tip I seemed to see a point of fire. This he plunged into my heart once and again, and so deeply that it reached my entrails. When he drew it out, I thought he was drawing them out with it and he left me all wounded with a great fire of love."[22] Teresa emphasizes the non-carnal nature of the sensation, noting "the pain is not bodily, but spiritual, though it leaves marks on the body which last a long time and are very painful," while the accompanying sweetness renders it desirable despite its intensity.[22] Gian Lorenzo Bernini's sculpture (1647–1652) directly corresponds to this account through its depiction of the angel positioned above Teresa, grasping the spear in mid-thrust toward her chest, evoking the repeated piercing.[23] Teresa's figure exhibits a limp, reclining posture with head thrown back and eyes half-closed in upward ecstasy, mirroring the visionary rapture and physical aftermath of the "great fire of love" that overwhelms her faculties.[24] The hybrid expression of agony and bliss on her face aligns with her description of pain so acute it prompts complaint yet so "surpassing" in delight that she refuses relief.[22] This representation avoids invention or exaggeration by adhering to the core elements of Teresa's narrative—the angel, the fiery spear, the heart's penetration, and the resulting pain-joy dialectic—without introducing extraneous carnal or dramatic flourishes beyond the spiritual intensity she articulates.[23] Posthumous examination of Teresa's heart in 1873 revealed scars consistent with her self-reported wounds, lending empirical corroboration to the vision's physical impact as conveyed in both text and sculpture.[25]Traditional Religious and Counter-Reformation Perspectives In the mystical theology of the Catholic tradition, the Ecstasy of Saint Teresa aligns with descriptions of divine union employing bridal imagery and sensory metaphors, as articulated by Saint Teresa of Ávila in her autobiography The Life of Teresa of Jesus (written circa 1565). Teresa recounts a vision wherein an angel pierces her heart with a golden arrow, evoking intense spiritual rapture characterized by both pain and overwhelming sweetness, a phenomenon she frames as the soul's transformative encounter with God's love.[1] This account, endorsed by her collaborator Saint John of the Cross, who served as her spiritual director and analyzed such ecstasies as authentic elevations of the spirit toward union with the divine, reflects a long-standing patristic and medieval acceptance of bodily manifestations in mystical experience.[4] John of the Cross, in works like The Dark Night of the Soul (composed during his imprisonment in 1577–1578), affirms the legitimacy of these sensory-infused raptures as pathways to contemplative prayer, countering any presumption of mere delusion by grounding them in scriptural precedents of divine-human intimacy.[26] The Counter-Reformation era, formalized by the Council of Trent (1545–1563), promoted vivid, emotive representations in religious art to foster personal devotion and combat Protestant critiques of Catholic sensuality in worship. Bernini's sculpture, completed between 1647 and 1652, embodies this directive by rendering Teresa's ecstasy in dynamic marble forms that engage the viewer's senses, mirroring the immersive techniques of Saint Ignatius of Loyola's Spiritual Exercises (composed 1522–1524). Ignatius advocated visualizing sacred scenes with physical and emotional intensity to stir the will toward faith, an approach that profoundly shaped Baroque aesthetics and the Jesuit order's patronage of art intended to evoke immediate spiritual response.[1][27] Such depictions were not aberrations but strategic affirmations of the incarnational theology emphasizing the body's role in salvation, as the Church sought to rekindle fervor amid religious schism. Contemporary ecclesiastical reception affirmed the work's orthodoxy, with no documented condemnations from 17th-century clergy despite its sensory intensity; instead, it received approbation through its commission by Cardinal Federico Cornaro and installation in the Cornaro Chapel of Santa Maria della Vittoria, under the patronage of Pope Innocent X.[1] This acceptance underscores a doctrinal prioritization of spiritual realism over prudish restraint, viewing the sculpture as a faithful visualization of canonized mysticism—Teresa having been declared a saint in 1622 by Pope Gregory XV—that served evangelistic ends without transgressing liturgical decorum.[28] The absence of inquisitorial scrutiny or removal from its sacred setting attests to its alignment with prevailing theological norms, privileging the edification of the faithful through tangible encounters with divine mystery.[14]Secular, Erotic, and Psychological Readings Twentieth-century secular interpretations frequently recast Bernini's Ecstasy of Saint Teresa through an erotic prism, likening the saint's transverberation to orgasmic climax, with the angel's arrow symbolizing phallic penetration.[14] Psychoanalytic frameworks, rooted in Sigmund Freud's characterization of religious mysticism as sublimated sexual drives and hysteria, positioned Teresa as an exemplar of neurotic ecstasy, where spiritual visions masked libidinal impulses.[29]Jacques Lacan extended this by interpreting the sculpture's jouissance—an excessive, beyond-phallic pleasure—as emblematic of feminine enjoyment transcending ordinary desire, yet still framing it in psychosexual terms.[30] Art historians like Rudolf Wittkower highlighted Bernini's deliberate infusion of sensuality into the marble forms, viewing the undulating drapery and Teresa's contorted pose as Baroque innovations to evoke visceral emotional response, blending spiritual fervor with corporeal dynamism to engage viewers in the mystical event.[31] Such readings emphasize the sculpture's theatricality, where exaggerated bodily tension and the angel's poised thrust amplify perceptual immediacy, potentially inviting erotic projections independent of theological context.[32] Critiques of these erotic overlays contend they constitute anachronistic impositions, projecting post-Enlightenment secularism onto a Counter-Reformation artifact designed to affirm divine encounter, as Teresa's own account explicitly attributes the piercing to an angel's divine agency, yielding "pain so great" yet "so surpassing" in sweetness as to preclude withdrawal, framed unequivocally as union with God rather than carnal release.[1] Empirical analysis of historical reception reveals no contemporary evidence of widespread erotic scandal, suggesting modern over-sexualization stems from psychoanalytic paradigms now scrutinized for reductionist bias, prioritizing libido over contextual spiritual causality. Psychological and neuroscientific lenses provide causal parallels without erotic or pathological reduction, aligning mystical ecstasies like Teresa's with trance states induced by meditation or ritual, where physiological markers—elevated heart rate, hyperventilation, and endogenous opioid release—facilitate altered consciousness.[33] Functional neuroimaging demonstrates that such experiences correlate with suppressed default mode network activity, enabling ego-dissolution and unio mystica, akin to non-drug-induced religious raptures across traditions, underscoring brain states as enablers of interpretive content shaped by belief rather than inherent hysteria.[34] This framework preserves the event's veridical intensity while attributing variations to neuroplastic responses in sustained contemplation, diverging from Freudian etiology by privileging empirical correlates over speculative symbolism.[35]Reception and Evaluation Initial 17th-Century Responses Upon its completion in 1652 within the Cornaro Chapel of Santa Maria della Vittoria, Bernini's Ecstasy of Saint Teresa received acclaim from Roman contemporaries for its spiritual potency, with an avviso—a periodic news gazette—highlighting its devotional impact and Giovanni Passeri, a painter and chronicler, praising its lifelike intensity in drawing viewers into the saint's mystical union.[36] Poems composed soon after, such as those evoking the sculpture's capacity to kindle divine love in observers, further evidenced this approbation among artistic circles.[36] The Cornaro family, Venetian patrons who commissioned the chapel as a dynastic mausoleum, expressed pride through their integration of the work, depicting family members in marble reliefs as contemplative witnesses to the transverberation scene, underscoring its role in elevating their lineage's piety.[36] No contemporary records indicate familial reservations, and a dedicatory letter from the Discalced Carmelites affirms its seamless incorporation into chapel rites without liturgical disruption.[36] Clerical responses emphasized the sculpture's efficacy as a meditative aid, aligning with Counter-Reformation emphases on sensory engagement for faith; accounts from the 1670s, including Luigi Scaramuccia's observation that it induced ecstatic states in pilgrims, attest to its success in evoking emotional and spiritual stirring among the faithful.[37] Absent any documented scandals or prohibitions in diaries, letters, or ecclesiastical correspondence from the 1650s onward, the work functioned uncontroversially as a focal point for prayer within the church's daily observances.[36]Historical Controversies and Criticisms In the centuries following its completion, the Ecstasy of Saint Teresa faced sporadic criticisms primarily centered on its perceived sensuality and potential breach of religious decorum, though outright calls for its destruction or removal were rare and typically emanated from non-Catholic or secular viewpoints rather than institutional Catholic opposition.[28][38] During the 18th century, British traveler Lady Anna Miller expressed visceral discomfort with the sculpture's intense physicality upon viewing Baroque art in Rome, reflecting a growing Protestant-influenced prudery that viewed Catholic imagery as overly emotive and idolatrous.[39] Such reactions aligned with broader Reformation-era Protestant critiques of Counter-Reformation art, which condemned sensual depictions as distractions from scriptural purity, yet the work endured without bans in Catholic contexts.[38] By the 19th century, amid Victorian sensibilities emphasizing restraint, the sculpture's portrayal of Teresa's transverberation—rendered with arched body, parted lips, and implied rapture—was occasionally deemed indecorous in travel accounts, though documented demands for its demolition remain unsubstantiated in primary sources.[28] These oppositions often stemmed from cultural shifts prioritizing moral austerity over mystical embodiment, with Protestant travelers interpreting the scene through lenses of excess rather than theological fidelity to Teresa's own writings.[38]Empirical evidence indicates no ecclesiastical interventions or prohibitions against the piece, underscoring that criticisms were marginal and not reflective of Catholic consensus, which historically affirmed such art as consonant with Tridentine emphases on emotional engagement.[28][39] In the 20th and 21st centuries, scholarly debates intensified, with some art historians like Anthony Blunt arguing that the surrounding male Cornaro family portraits reinforced patriarchal structures, portraying Teresa's passivity as emblematic of gendered power imbalances in Counter-Reformation iconography.[38] Feminist analyses have similarly scrutinized the depiction of female ecstasy as potentially objectifying, imposing modern psychological or erotic frameworks that overlook Teresa's agency as a canonized mystic whose visions challenged clerical hierarchies.[38] Recent scholarship, including 2023 examinations, highlights ongoing contentions over the sculpture's "overly physical" qualities breaching decorum, often attributing such views to secular reinterpretations detached from the original mystical intent described in Teresa's Life (1565).[14][28] These critiques, while amplified in academic circles potentially influenced by ideological biases against traditional religious narratives, have not led to institutional actions, affirming the work's resilience against historical prudery or revisionist lenses.[38]Defenses of Artistic and Spiritual Merit Bernini's Ecstasy of Saint Teresa exemplifies technical virtuosity in Baroque sculpture through his innovative manipulation of marble to simulate textures, motion, and ethereal light effects. The drapery folds on Teresa's robe and the angel's garments convey dynamic movement and softness, achieved via precise chiseling that mimics fabric's pliability despite the stone's rigidity, marking a departure from classical restraint toward heightened realism and emotional immediacy.[16] This technique, combined with the strategic use of a hidden window directing natural light onto the figures, creates an illusion of divine illumination and suspended animation, immersing viewers in the scene's transcendent drama.[1] Theologically, the sculpture defends its merit by faithfully rendering Saint Teresa's autobiographical account of her transverberation—a piercing union of pain and divine love—as described in her Life (1565), where she details the angel's arrow evoking both agony and rapture.[13] This bodily-spiritual synthesis aligns with Carmelite mystical tradition, portraying ecstasy not as abstract but as viscerally transformative, thereby aiding contemplation and devotion among the faithful. Countering decorum critiques, the work's sensory appeal fulfills Counter-Reformation imperatives to evoke affective piety, as articulated in the Council of Trent's (1545–1563) emphasis on art's role in instructing and moving souls toward God.[1] Empirical evidence of spiritual efficacy includes the Cornaro Chapel's ongoing role in Roman Catholic liturgy and its draw for pilgrims seeking to experience Teresa's mysticism vicariously; annual visitor estimates to Santa Maria della Vittoria exceed 100,000, many citing the sculpture as a catalyst for deepened prayer and reflection.[14] Recent scholarship, such as analyses from 2022 onward, rebuts reductive erotic interpretations by reaffirming the piece's fidelity to Teresa's theology of infused contemplation, where physical metaphors underscore causal links between somatic response and union with the divine, enhancing art's proven capacity to sustain orthodox devotion.[40]

The Baroque period can be characterised by an ‘intense religiosity’ or emotion in all facets of its forms whether it be theatre, painting, or sculpture. It was brought in by the Counter Revolution that caused a will back to religion especially Catholicism, previously seen by the people as unattainable to the average man (sermons and holy texts were taught it Latin, a language spoken by few etc.). The flamboyance of Catholic representation was almost offensive to those who had little to live by, so commissioned artworks sought to provide some justification or relation to the people. Thus emotional expressionism manifested into the art of the time. An example could be the naturalism of Caravaggio’s The boy with the Basket of Fruit (c.1593) compared to previous Renaissance pieces. Who better was there to create a pride of this era than Bernini, specifically in his conception of the Ecstasy of Saint Teresa. Gian Lorenzo Bernini, The Ecstasy of Saint Teresa (1647-52). Cornaro Chapel, Santa Maria della Vittoria, Rome. This piece was conceptualised and made in 1652 by Gian Lorenzo Bernini, commissioned under the Papacy of Pope Innocent X. It currently resides in The Cornaro Chapel in Rome and was patroned by Francesco Burronini, who’s influence can be seen in the architecture of the greater work. Bernini both as a religious man and as someone willing to use his own pain to express passionate emotion was a clear fit for the era. His self-portrait, The Damned Soul (1619), expertly demonstrates his capability and use of the mirror to perfectly capture and translate his pose. As a prodigy, from a young age Bernini was accused as possessing some narcissism. He had tendency to use himself as a muse for religious figures which caused some controversy. He however was well rated among Popes and was therefore given reign to dominate the period. Teresa, the subject of this piece, was canonized in 1622 and she accounts her visit from the ‘highest rank of angel’. They met her with an arrow with an iron tip that was ‘plunged into her heart several times until it penetrated to [her] entrails’ causing her to ‘utter several moans’. It is called a, ‘spiritual pain’, though the body had some share in it and this sculpture is Bernini’s attempt to physically represent the spiritual pleasure in serving Christ. Gian Lorenzo Bernini, The Ecstasy of Saint Teresa (1647-52), detail. He does this expertly through the underlying sexual symbolism as Teresa appears to be having a bodily orgasm from the expression on her face. The painterly effect of the marble seems to transcend the physical in the angelic versus the mortal. The angel appears to be defying gravity with the softness of its wings and dress. In juxtaposition Teresa has a heavy mortality that drags her to the earth. Surrounding this, Bernini with his skills in architecture sets the stage. The visual elements of the spiritual interact with the proverbial ‘stage’. The theatre boxes at the sides are representations of the actual patron and his family, spectating and forming an interaction of real life. A hidden light source hides behind the rays of gold from a window. As the viewer walks up to the raised sculpture they are embraced by the light and warm of the elaborate designing. Above the sculpture, if you were to visit, you would see a white dove on the ceiling, representing the Holy Ghost as an additional spectator. Bernini makes every attempt to draw us into the piece and engulf us in it. The work clearly does what all Baroque work aimed to do: inspire faith and involve the viewer. And it did so with a spectacular level of drama. The physical embodiment of a spiritual Godly love creates a link between the elaborate and the average human, justifying the overindulgence linked to Catholicism. The work thus invites us all to feel of spirituality alongside Teresa, invoking in the viewers a sense of passion as required in art at this time . As Teresa is, in her own words, ‘utterly consumed by the Great love of God’, so are we the onlookers. Reaia Parkes (BA Art History and Film, 1st Year)


--- [Did Bernini’s Ecstasy of Saint Teresa Cross the Line?] ---
The Church of Santa Maria della Vittoria in Rome holds one of the most unusual Baroque sculptures, the Ecstasy of Saint Teresa. From the moment Gian Lorenzo Bernini finished it, the sculpture was labeled as problematic for its focus on the physical rather than the spiritual. This label stands even today. Read on to learn more about Benini’s statue and discover why it was criticized for being overtly physical. The Ecstasy of Saint Teresa in the Eyes of Contemporaries The majority of Bernini’s contemporaries received his Ecstasy of Saint Teresa in a positive light. This can be seen in the writings of his biographers, Domenico Bernini, the sculptor’s son, and Filippo Baldinucci. Domenico claims that Saint Teresa was his father’s most magnificent work of art. He describes it as the sweetest ecstasy with an angel who stands above the saint and pierces her heart with his golden arrow of divine love. From Domenico’s biography, we learn about the moment Bernini decided to represent. Bernini decided to visualize the height of her mystical experience. In Baldinucci’s biography, the Cornaro Chapel takes the place of Bernini’s greatest masterpiece in Rome. Both biographies tell us of a well-received artwork, but the Ecstasy of Saint Teresa had its critics, as well as Bernini. Indeed, some contemporary critics have described the Ecstasy of Saint Teresa as overly physical. However, only one written source of this opinion remains, and its author is not known. He claims that the problem with the sculpture is that the peak of the ecstasy is presented as physical pleasure. Thus, the sculpture equals indecency, and to the author of this document, this also reflects Bernini’s own religiosity and morality. Who Made the Ecstasy of Saint Teresa? Gian Lorenzo Bernini was born in Naples in 1598, the son of Florentine sculptor Pietro Bernini. Together with his family, Lorenzo moved to Rome around 1605, a city that he rarely left. It would take an official summons by the French King Louis XIV for him to leave Rome. During his time in this city, he was considered an equal to Michelangelo, with whom he shared a passion for sculpture and marble. Bernini began his career as his father’s apprentice in the chapel of Paul V in the Church of Santa Maria Maggiore. While working there, he caught the eye of Cardinal Scipione Borghese, one of the wealthiest art patrons in Rome. Bernini stayed in the cardinal’s service until 1624. The Catholic Church was his greatest patron. Bernini developed his art working on commissions for Popes Urban VIII and Innocent X, during whose pontificate the Ecstasy of Saint Teresa was created. However, it was only under Pope Alexander VII that Bernini’s authority over the artistic life in Rome was assured, and all the important commissions were passed to him and his associates. The most significant commission he had in Rome was the architectural, sculptural, and decorative work on the Basilica of Saint Peter in the Vatican, where he worked until his death in 1680. The Life of Saint Teresa of Ávila The most significant source for our knowledge of the life and work of Saint Teresa of Ávila is her autobiography. Teresa was born in 1515 in the Spanish region of Ávila. She is considered one of the most significant Spanish mystics, Carmelite nuns, and theologians in the Catholic Church. Her life coincided with significant changes within the Catholic Church. In Spain, the Inquisition was started in 1478 by Pope Sixtus IV, Martin Luther initiated the Protestant Reformation in 1517, and the Counter-Reformation and the Council of Trent soon followed. During her life, she continually worked on her writings, which are still translated and published to this day. Saint Teresa wrote five books in total, including her autobiography and texts of mystical experiences. Her greatest accomplishment was the reformation of the Carmelite monastic order and the founding of the Discalced Carmelite monastic order. By building 17 convents, she attempted to restore the order to its foundations of simplicity and humility. Meanwhile, she supported the nuns and believers in forming and maintaining a personal relationship with God. Even though Teresa’s writings and reform caused great controversies in the Catholic Church, she was canonized by Pope Gregory XV in 1622, 40 years after her death. Where Is the Ecstasy of Saint Teresa? The chapel where Ecstasy of Saint Teresa is located is a funerary chapel of the Venetian family Cornaro. It is situated in the northern part of the Church Santa Maria della Vittoria. Cardinal Federico Cornaro commissioned Bernini in 1645 for the construction and decoration of the chapel, and by the end of 1652, the work was finished. As the focus of the chapel, Bernini sculpted out of marble a group consisting of the saint and a seraph with a golden spear, ready to pierce Saint Teresa lying on a cloud. Above the sculpture, a window was built in, allowing light to enter the chapel and directly illuminate the work. The sculptural group is not just an altarpiece but also part of a bigger performance. The architecture of the tabernacle was designed to extend into the space of the believer, giving an effect of depth and leaving enough room for the window above the sculpture. Light comes in through the window and merges with the dim light of the candles, thus creating the feeling that the seraph and Saint Teresa are ascending towards the sky. The entire group appears detached from the viewer, hovering above the marble cloud. Where Did the Ecstasy of Saint Teresa Come From? Since Saint Teresa of Ávila was canonized in 1622, 23years before Bernini began his work, he did not have a pre-established iconography to follow. Unlike the early Christian saints who have been depicted by countless artists for millennia, Saint Teresa was yet to be typified in art. Due to the lack of visual sources, Bernini had to look for inspiration elsewhere. It is certain that Bernini used the saint’s autobiography, written in 1565, as a literary source for the production of his Ecstasy of Saint Teresa. Thus, Bernini’s representation differs from her earlier, more realistic depictions in which she is presented as a humble nun. Some of the earlier paintings of Saint Teresa did have a focus on the piercing of her heart by divine love, but they continuously ignored her femininity in order to emphasize her humility. Unlike these works, Bernini’s Saint Teresa in the Cornaro Chapel is a young and beautiful woman. Concerning her age, Bernini disregards her writings. Saint Teresa claims to have had her first mystical experience at the age of 45. The choice of depicting her as a young woman, together with her half-closed eyes and body thrown back, has led contemporaries to associate Bernini’s sculpture with eroticism. Saint Teresa’s Ecstasy in Her Own Words Today, it is widely accepted by scholars that Bernini used the saint’s autobiography, The Life of Teresa of Jesus, as a source of inspiration. Domenico’s biography of Bernini confirms that Bernini used the saint’s autobiography as a source of inspiration and leads to the passage where the saint describes her mystical ecstasy: “Beside me, on the left hand, appeared an angel in bodily form… He was not tall but short, and very beautiful… he appeared to be one of the highest ranks of angels who seem to be on fire… In his hands, I saw a great golden spear, and the iron tip there appeared to be a point of fire.” Teresa goes on to explain in vivid detail what happens during a mystical experience and how one’s soul and body are affected: “The entire body contracts; neither foot nor arm can be moved. If one is standing at the time, one falls into a sitting position as though transported, and cannot even take a breath. One only utters a few slight moans, not aloud, for that is impossible, but inwardly, out of pain.” The saint also recounts the feelings experienced during her mystical trance: “…no sense of anything but enjoyment, without any knowledge of what is being enjoyed… All the senses are taken up with this joy so that none of them is free to act in any way… now the soul enjoys incomparably more, and yet has still less power to show it. For there is no power left in the body – and the soul possesses none – by which this joy can be communicated.” The Ectasy of Saint Teresa & Baroque Art When talking about Bernini’s art, a key feature of Baroque culture must be considered: its tendency for theatricality. Baroque theater in the time of Pope Urban VIII attracted creative people, including Bernini, who occasionally worked on scenography. Looking at the chapel as a whole, from both sides of the altarpiece, are busts of the Cornaro family. These statues are peering into the chapel from side windows or rooms, resembling theater balconies. The figure on the right side of the chapel, closest to the altar, admires the scene, while the figure parallel to him is reading a book. Cardinals appear to be engaged in a heated debate about the nature of the experience. So, Did Bernini Really Cross the Line? The marble spectators emphasize Bernini’s ability to create a dramatic theatrical performance, where he united the sacred and profane aspects of theater by using lighting effects, architectural elements, and complex actor-audience relationships. Fusing these diverse aspects of theater, Bernini managed to create art that inspires intense religious experiences and emotions. The Baroque artist’s sense of theater is evident in many of his notable works, including the gilt-bronze baldachin inside St. Peter’s Basilica, Apollo and Daphne, and the Fountain of the Four Rivers. The grandeur, sensuous richness, vitality, movement, and emotional exuberance turned Bernini into the leading exponent of the Baroque. In the Ecstasy of Saint Teresa, Bernini’s theatricality calls upon the viewers, drawing them into the scene while expecting their emotional reaction and participation. Ultimately, it becomes clear that Bernini did not necessarily intend to evoke sensuality when creating his renowned sculpture. However, the Ecstasy of Saint Teresa does depict an ecstasy experienced by both soul and body, as explained by the saint herself. Combining elements of a mystical experience and the drama of theater into one sculpture, Bernini has managed to create a new way of representing religious experience while simultaneously creating a more visually compelling image, meant to motivate the “correct” type of devotion from the spectator.
