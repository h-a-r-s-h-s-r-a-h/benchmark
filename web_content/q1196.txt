--- [Dollywood - Wikipedia] ---
Dollywood is a theme park that is jointly owned by Herschend and country singer-songwriter Dolly Parton through her entertainment company, Dolly Parton Productions. It is located in the Knoxville metropolitan area in Pigeon Forge, Tennessee, near the gateway to The Great Smoky Mountains. Hosting nearly 3 million guests in a typical season from mid-March to the Christmas holidays, Dollywood is the biggest ticketed tourist attraction in Tennessee. It has won several international awards.
In addition to standard amusement park thrill rides, Dollywood features traditional crafts, food, and music of the Smoky Mountain area. The park hosts a number of concerts and musical events each year, including appearances by Dolly Parton and her family as well as other national and local musical acts. 
The theme park is the anchor of Parton's 150-acre (61 ha) Dollywood amusement destination, which also includes the 35-acre (14 ha) sister water park Dollywood's Splash Country, the 20-acre (8.1 ha) Dollywood's DreamMore Resort and Spa, and the 5-acre (2.0 ha) Dolly Parton's Stampede Dinner Attraction.

Dollywood is a theme park co-owned by Herschend and Dolly Parton, through Dolly Parton Productions. Situated in Pigeon Forge, Tennessee, near the Great Smoky Mountains, it attracts nearly 3 million visitors annually, making it Tennessee's largest ticketed tourist destination. The park operates from mid-March through the Christmas holidays and has garnered multiple international awards for its attractions and guest experience. On June 10, 1961, the park initially opened as "Rebel Railroad", a tourist attraction owned by the Robbins brothers. It featured a steam train, general store, blacksmith shop, and saloon, themed around the Civil War. The train ride included staged attacks by Union soldiers, train robbers, and Native Americans, defended by Confederates. On May 24, 1964, the Knoxville News Sentinel announced that the park would be renamed "Goldrush Junction" for the 1964 season. Along with the name change, the park adopted a Wild West theme similar to Tweetsie Railroad. In 1970, Art Modell, also owner of the Cleveland Browns football team, purchased Goldrush Junction. The park maintained the railroad and introduced an outdoor theater along with the Robert F. Thomas Chapel. In 1975, the park's name was changed to "Goldrush". In April of 1976, Jack and Pete Herschend, owners of Silver Dollar City, acquired Goldrush. The park continued operations as Goldrush throughout the 1976 season. In 1977, the Herschends rebranded Goldrush as "Silver Dollar City Tennessee", aligning it as a sister park to their original Silver Dollar City. They invested approximately $1 million in park upgrades. Also in 1977, the train ride was enhanced with two new steam locomotives, the #70 and #71, as well as engine #72 for spare parts, sourced from the White Pass & Yukon Route Railroad. On May 3, 1986, Silver Dollar City Tennessee reopened as Dollywood. The new Rivertown Junction area featured Smoky Mountain River Rampage, Back Porch Theater, Aunt Granny's Dixie Fixins' Restaurant, and Dolly's Tennessee Mountain Home. "Rags to Riches: The Dolly Parton Story" museum was also new. Attendance doubled. By 1986, Dollywood employed approximately 4,000 individuals, establishing itself as the largest employer in the community. In 1986, Dolly Parton acquired an interest in Silver Dollar City, and the park was renamed "Dollywood" for the 1986 season. In 1987, the Daydream Ridge area opened featuring the Mountain Slidewinder water toboggan ride, Mountain Dan's Burger House, Sweet Dreams Candy Shop, The Rainbow Factory blown glass shop, and Critter Creek Playground. Engine #70 was also restored to operation. In 1988, the 1,739-seat Celebrity Theater, featuring the "Showcase of Stars" celebrity concert series, was constructed. Five new children's rides were added to the Fun Country area, including a Zamperla Balloon Race. Also, the Dollywood Foundation was established. In 1989, Thunder Express, a steel mine train coaster, was built. 1989 marked the final season for the National Mountain Music Festival. In 1990, a 1924 antique Dentzel Carousel was relocated to the park. The 600-seat Gaslight Theater also opened. The Smoky Mountain Christmas Festival premiered in November. In 1991, Eagle Mountain Sanctuary and the Wings of America Theater were added, along with the 300-seat Valley Theater. In 1992, the Showstreet area was added, featuring the Showstreet Palace Theater, The Butterfly Emporium, The Backstage Restaurant, The Spotlight Bakery, Friendship Gardens, and WDLY-FM. The park's main entrance moved to Showstreet. Dollywood's annual attendance topped 2 million for the first time. In 1993, the Fun Country area was renovated to become The Country Fair with three new rides: The Wonder Wheel, Twist and Shout, and Tennessee Twister. Also new was "Sunset Musicfest". In 1994, the Gaslight Theater was renamed Heartsong Theater, featuring a multi-media musical about Dolly Parton's life. In 1995, the Jukebox Junction area was added, featuring Rockin' Roadway, The Pines Theater, Red's Diner, and Cas Walker's Music Store. In 1996, the Dollywood Boulevard area was added, featuring Thunder Road, Silver Screen Café, and Centerstage gift shop. In 1997, the "U Pick Nick" children's show, focused on themes from the Nickelodeon television network, was played in Celebrity Theater. The Flooded Mine dark-ride was closed and demolished in October, and Silver Screen Cafe became DJ Platters in the Dollywood Boulevard area. In 1998, Daredevil Falls opened in the area formerly occupied by the Flooded Mine. Thunder Express was closed in September and sold to Magic Springs Theme Park. The antique carousel was removed at the end of the season and replaced with a new carousel. In 1999, the Tennessee Tornado, a steel looping coaster, opened. The Southern Gospel Museum and Hall of Fame also opened, and the Balloon Race ride was removed. In 2000, the Daydream Ridge area was renovated and became Dreamland Forest, a children's mountain-themed interactive play area. Dollywood Overview Location and Ownership Dollywood is located in Pigeon Forge, Tennessee, nestled in the foothills of the Great Smoky Mountains and serving as a gateway to the adjacent Great Smoky Mountains National Park. The theme park occupies 165 acres of picturesque terrain, blending natural beauty with family-oriented entertainment spaces.[1][6] The park's ownership is divided equally, with a 50% stake held by Dolly Parton through her production company and the remaining 50% by Herschend Family Enterprises, a partnership established in 1986. Herschend Family Enterprises oversees daily operations, including staffing, maintenance, and guest services. Prior to 2020, Dollywood drew nearly 3 million visitors each year, establishing it as Tennessee's premier ticketed attraction.[7][1][8] This joint ownership also encompasses related properties, such as the 35-acre Dollywood's Splash Country water park and Dollywood resorts including DreamMore Resort and Spa.[9]Theme and General Features Dollywood's core theme intertwines the rich Southern Appalachian heritage with the spirit of country music and the personal narrative of its co-founder, Dolly Parton, a native of Sevier County, Tennessee.[1] The park celebrates regional traditions through daily demonstrations by master craftsmen in areas like blacksmithing and wood carving, evoking the area's historical artistry and self-sufficient lifestyle.[1] This foundation supports a family-friendly environment featuring live performances of country, bluegrass, Southern gospel, and Appalachian music, with numerous concerts annually that highlight Dolly Parton's musical legacy and down-home charm.[1] Key general features enhance the park's immersive appeal, including the iconic Dollywood Express, an authentic 110-ton coal-fired steam train that offers scenic rides through the property, serving as a central emblem of Appalachian transportation history.[10]Accessibility is prioritized with dedicated services such as ride boarding passes, sensory-sensitive hours, and accommodations for physical, visual, hearing, cognitive, and dietary needs.[11] The park operates seasonally from mid-March through early January, with the 2025 season commencing on March 15, aligning with various festivals that extend into the winter holiday period.[12] Ticket pricing follows a structured model, with one-day adult passes starting at $95 (plus tax; prices vary by date and promotions may apply), designed to provide flexible access to the 165-acre site nestled in the Great Smoky Mountains foothills.[13] The park's layout comprises 11 distinct themed areas interconnected by winding pathways that encourage exploration and storytelling immersion.[14] Decorative elements, foliage, and architectural details in each area reinforce the overarching narrative of Appalachian life and Dolly Parton's roots, complemented by theatrical shows and interactive exhibits that transport visitors into regional folklore and music history.[1] Dining and shopping options emphasize Southern hospitality, with numerous eateries offering regional cuisine such as fried chicken, fresh-baked breads, and homestyle sides, reflecting the park's commitment to authentic Appalachian flavors.[15] These venues, integrated throughout the layout, provide casual and buffet-style meals alongside retail spots selling crafts, memorabilia, and Dolly-inspired merchandise to extend the thematic experience beyond rides and shows.[15]History Early Iterations (1961–1985) The site that would eventually become Dollywood first opened on June 10, 1961, as Rebel Railroad, a modest 50-acre tourist attraction developed by brothers Grover and Harry Robbins from Blowing Rock, North Carolina. The centerpiece was a narrow-gauge, coal-fired steam locomotive named Klondike Katie, offering a 3-mile round-trip ride through the forested foothills of the Great Smoky Mountains, complete with staged "ambushes" by outlaws and Native Americans to enhance the Wild West adventure. Complementing the train were frontier-themed structures, including a saloon, general store, and blacksmith shop, which together created an immersive Civil War-era railroad experience amid the Appalachian landscape.[1] In 1964, under new ownership, the park was renamed Goldrush Junction to emphasize a gold mining theme inspired by the California Gold Rush, with expansions including additional track for the train and the relocation of a log flume ride from the 1964 New York World's Fair. The attraction grew to include mining simulations like gold panning, a wood shop, sawmill, and outdoor theater for live performances, alongside log cabins and a campground to appeal to families. By 1970, Cleveland Browns owner Art Modell acquired the property, investing in further developments such as children's rides and an expanded array of shops, which positioned Goldrush Junction as "Tennessee's Million Dollar Fun Attraction" while retaining its core railroad focus.[16][17] The park underwent a brief rebranding to simply Goldrush for the 1976 season, amplifying its Western motifs with minor enhancements to rides and shows, though it remained a seasonal operation drawing regional visitors. In 1977, brothers Jack and Pete Herschend—operators of the successful Silver Dollar City in Branson, Missouri—purchased the site and transformed it into Silver Dollar City, Tennessee, shifting the emphasis to authentic Appalachian culture and craftsmanship. This iteration introduced Craftsman's Valley, a dedicated area for live artisan demonstrations in pottery, glassblowing, and woodworking, alongside family-friendly additions like the Silver Dollar Grist Mill (completed in 1983) and the indoor dark ride roller coaster Blazing Fury, which debuted in 1978 and became a signature thrill.[1][16] By the mid-1980s, Silver Dollar City had established itself as a wholesome, educationally oriented park celebrating regional heritage, but faced challenges in broadening its appeal. In May 1985, country music icon Dolly Parton invested several million dollars to acquire a 50% ownership stake, partnering with the Herschend family who retained the remaining 50%, setting the foundation for a major rebranding that would infuse the site with her personal vision while preserving its mountain roots.[1][18][7]Establishment and Early Expansions (1986–1999) Dollywood officially opened to the public on May 3, 1986, following a rebranding of the existing Silver Dollar City theme park through a partnership between Dolly Parton and the Herschend Family Entertainment corporation.[1][18] Parton, a native of nearby Sevierville, Tennessee, invested significantly in the transformation to infuse the park with her personal vision of Appalachian culture, music, and family entertainment, marking a pivotal shift from its prior iterations as a generic frontier-themed attraction.[19] The opening season introduced enhanced shows, rides, and dining options, drawing 1.3 million visitors—a 75 percent increase from the previous year under Silver Dollar City operations.[1] The partnership with Herschend, established in 1986, provided operational expertise while allowing Parton creative control, a collaboration that has endured and driven steady growth throughout the 1990s.[1] Key early expansions focused on thematic areas that highlighted Smoky Mountain heritage. Rivertown Junction debuted as a new riverside section in the park's inaugural season, featuring the Smoky Mountain River Rampage white-water rafting ride and family-friendly eateries like Aunt Granny's All-Purpose Festival Hall, which quickly became a visitor favorite.[1] Craftsman's Valley, emphasizing traditional Appalachian artisanship, showcased demonstrations of blacksmithing, glassblowing, and woodworking, preserving cultural crafts central to Parton's ethos.[1] Showstreet served as the vibrant entryway, lined with theaters hosting live performances that blended country music and storytelling to welcome guests into the park's immersive world.[20] Early attractions underscored the park's blend of nature, history, and thrills. The Dollywood Express, a narrow-gauge steam train originally from the park's pre-1986 era, saw route enhancements to better integrate with the rebranded layout, offering scenic tours through the surrounding woodlands.[21] In 1991, the Eagle Mountain Sanctuary opened as an outdoor aviary dedicated to non-releasable bald eagles in partnership with the American Eagle Foundation, providing educational exhibits on wildlife conservation amid the park's natural setting.[22] By the late 1990s, Dollywood introduced more adrenaline-focused elements, culminating in the 1999 debut of Tennessee Tornado, an $8 million steel roller coaster with three inversions, a 128-foot drop, and speeds up to 70 mph—the park's first major looping ride designed to appeal to thrill-seekers while fitting the mountainous terrain.[1] Attendance surged during this period, surpassing 2 million visitors annually by 1992 and continuing to grow through targeted expansions and seasonal events that celebrated Appalachian traditions.[1] By 1999, the park had solidified its status as Tennessee's premier family destination, with over 2 million guests reflecting a 54 percent rise from the 1986 opening year and demonstrating the success of Parton's vision in elevating the former regional attraction to national prominence.[23]Major Developments (2000–2019) In the early 2000s, Dollywood focused on broadening its appeal through new themed areas and thrill attractions. The park introduced Thunderhead in 2004, a wooden roller coaster manufactured by Great Coasters International, featuring a 3,000-foot track with speeds up to 54 mph and airtime hills, which anchored the emerging Timber Canyon section. This addition enhanced the park's reputation for high-quality wooden coasters. In 2006, the full Timber Canyon area opened, encompassing a logging-themed environment with family rides such as Lumberjack Lifts—a drop tower—and the interactive Beaver Creek Boat Float, along with dining options like a 150-seat restaurant. These developments expanded the park's footprint and diversified its ride portfolio beyond traditional Appalachian themes. The mid-2000s continued with innovative thrill rides, including the 2007 debut of Mystery Mine in Timber Canyon, a $17.5 million steel coaster by Rocky Mountain Construction that incorporated a vertical lift, beyond-vertical drop, and underground theming inspired by a haunted mine. This ride introduced unique elements like magnetic brakes and a reverse incline, setting it apart from earlier attractions. By 2008, the park added River Battle, a $5 million interactive water battle ride in the Wilderness Pass area, allowing guests to engage in shoot-style water combat from boats. These investments reflected Dollywood's strategy to balance adrenaline-pumping experiences with interactive family elements, drawing increased attendance during the decade. Entering the 2010s, Dollywood emphasized major area expansions and record-breaking coasters. In 2012, the Wilderness Pass area launched with Wild Eagle, a $20 million wing coaster by Bolliger & Möller, the first of its type in the United States, offering unobstructed views as riders' feet dangled over the terrain at heights up to 135 feet. This addition integrated seamlessly with the natural landscape, enhancing the park's immersive outdoor appeal. In 2014, Jukebox Junction received FireChaser Express, a dual-launch family roller coaster by Rocky Mountain Construction, the nation's first such design, reaching 37 mph over a 2,173-foot track with inversions suitable for younger thrill-seekers. Further infrastructure growth included the 2015 opening of Dollywood's DreamMore Resort and Spa, a 300-room property with family suites, an indoor pool, and direct access to the park, representing a $110 million investment in on-site accommodations. In 2016, Lightning Rod debuted in Jukebox Junction as the world's fastest wooden roller coaster at launch, a $22 million launch coaster by Rocky Mountain Construction that propelled riders to 73 mph, blending wood construction with electromagnetic launches for unprecedented speed. The decade culminated in 2019 with Wildwood Grove, the park's largest expansion at $37 million across five acres, introducing 11 family-oriented experiences including the suspended coaster Dragonflier by Zamperla, interactive play areas like Fairy Garden, and the Till & Harvest Grove restaurant, all themed around woodland folklore to appeal to multi-generational visitors. These projects underscored Dollywood's commitment to evolving as a comprehensive resort destination while preserving its cultural roots.Recent Expansions (2020–present) In response to the COVID-19
