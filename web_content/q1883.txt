--- [86 Semele - Wikipedia] ---
86 Semele is a large and very dark main-belt asteroid with an orbital period of 5.5 years. It is rotating with a period of 16.6 hours, and varies in magnitude by 0.13 during each cycle. This object is classified as a C-type asteroid and is probably composed of carbonates.
Semele was discovered by German astronomer Friedrich Tietjen on January 4, 1866. It was his first and only asteroid discovery. It is named after Semele, the mother of Dionysus in Greek mythology.
The orbit of 86 Semele places it in a 13:6 mean motion resonance with the planet Jupiter. The computed Lyapunov time for this asteroid is only 6,000 years, indicating that it occupies a chaotic orbit that will change randomly over time because of gravitational perturbations of the planets. This Lyapunov time is the second lowest among the first 100 named minor planets.

--- [Extrasolar Planets – It's All Going On Up There] ---
Extrasolar planet HD 189733 b was discovered by astronomers at the Haute-Provence Observatory in southeast France on October 5th, 2005. The part of the name referring to the planet is just the “b”; the rest is the name of the star around which it orbits. This star is a binary system, thought to comprise an orange primary and red dwarf secondary, in the constellation of Vulpecula, “the fox”. Vulpecula’s only previous claim to fame was as the location of the first pulsar to be discovered, in 1967, by Jocelyn Bell (now Dame Jocelyn Bell Burnell) of Cambridge University. HD 189733 b was discovered using the ‘transit’ method of detection (watching for dips in light from the host star as the planet passes in front of it). It is now known to be a “hot Jupiter”, a type of exoplanet with physical similarities to Jupiter, but orbiting much closer to their parent star. In this case, the planet is so close that it is able to orbit HD 189733 in just over 2 days. The reason this particular planet deserves mention is thanks to the Spitzer infrared space telescope, which in May 2007 mapped the atmospheric temperature of HD 189733 b, giving us the first ever “map” of an exoplanet (see the NASA image, below). The image shows variations in the temperature at the top of HD 189733 b’s atmosphere, with brighter areas representing hotter regions. The planet is thought to be tidally locked (meaning the same side always faces the star) which would explain the great difference between the extremely hot white area in the middle of the image, and the (relatively) cool red zones round the edges.
