--- [Kara Walker - Wikipedia] ---
Kara Elizabeth Walker (born November 26, 1969) is an American contemporary painter, silhouettist, printmaker, installation artist, filmmaker, and professor who explores race, gender, sexuality, violence, and identity in her work. She is best known for her room-size tableaux of black cut-paper silhouettes. Walker was awarded a MacArthur fellowship in 1997, at the age of 28, becoming one of the youngest ever recipients of the award. She has been the Tepper Chair in Visual Arts at the Mason Gross School of the Arts, Rutgers University since 2015.
Walker is regarded as among the most prominent and acclaimed Black American artists working today.


--- [Kara E. Walker - MacArthur Foundation] ---
About Kara's Work Kara Walker is an artist whose works often deal with issues of race in the antebellum South. Walker's installations, drawings, and paintings are provocative and grimly humorous. In her installations, she creates narratives of the old South by cutting stylized, life-sized figures from black paper that she pastes, as in a mural, around the walls of the gallery she is showing in. Her use of the silhouette technique and related sources derive from the German fairy tale, history painting, and colonial portraiture. Walker’s installations, filled with slave-era, stereotypical figures, are graphic and violent tableaus that explore the vestiges of sexual, physical, and racial exploitation in a challenging manner. Biography Walker is an associate professor in the Visual Arts division at Columbia University. Since 1990, she has participated in group exhibits at galleries throughout the United States and abroad, including the Whitney Biennial (1997) and the Sao Paulo Biennial, Brazil (2002). She has had solo exhibits at Wooster Gardens in New York (1995, 1996, 1998), the Renaissance Society at the University of Chicago (1997), the San Francisco Museum of Modern Art (1997), and the Walker Art Center in Minneapolis (1997). Walker received a B.F.A. (1991) from the Atlanta College of Art and an M.F.A. (1994) from the Rhode Island School of Design. Last updated January 1, 2005 Published on July 1, 1997

Kara Elizabeth Walker: A Trailblazer in Art and Academia Born on November 26, 1969, Kara Elizabeth Walker is an acclaimed American artist and academic who delves into themes of race, gender, sexuality, violence, and personal identity through her diverse artistic practices. Renowned primarily for her expansive installations of black cut-paper silhouettes, Walker's work commands attention and discussion. She achieved early recognition in her career, securing a prestigious MacArthur fellowship in 1997 at the young age of 28, marking her as one of the award's youngest recipients. Since 2015, she has held the esteemed position of Tepper Chair in Visual Arts at Rutgers University's Mason Gross School of the Arts. Walker is celebrated as one of the leading contemporary African American artists, receiving widespread acclaim for her impactful and provocative artworks. Kara Walker was born in Stockton, California, into a family deeply engaged in the arts. Her father, Larry Walker, was both a painter and a professor, while her mother, Gwendolyn, worked as an administrative assistant. Despite a relatively peaceful upbringing in an integrated California suburb, a significant turning point in Walker's life came at age 13 when her family relocated to Stone Mountain, Georgia, a city that was a stark contrast to her previous environment and known for its ongoing Ku Klux Klan rallies. The racial tensions she experienced there deeply influenced her perspective and artistic expression. Walker pursued her formal art education with a BFA from the Atlanta College of Art in 1991, followed by an MFA in painting from the Rhode Island School of Design in 1994. Initially hesitant to incorporate race themes into her work, fearing stereotypical interpretations, she eventually embraced these elements during her studies at RISD, inspired in part by her father’s influence and her own experiences. Reflecting on her early inspiration, Walker shared a poignant childhood memory: sitting on her father’s lap in his studio, watching him draw, and realizing her own aspiration to be an artist—a decision firmly planted by the tender age of three. Kara Walker continues to inspire and challenge societal norms through her bold and innovative works, maintaining a significant voice in contemporary art and academia. Kara Walker's Artistic Journey: Confronting Historical Shadows through Silhouette Art Kara Walker is renowned for her evocative use of cut-paper silhouettes, primarily depicting black figures against white backgrounds, to explore deep-seated issues of American slavery, racism, and identity through stark, unsettling imagery. Beyond her iconic silhouettes, Walker's oeuvre extends to mediums such as gouache, watercolor, video animation, shadow puppets, and magic lantern projections. Her sculptural installations often achieve monumental scale, notably in works like her 2014 public exhibition "A Subtlety, or the Marvelous Sugar Baby," which paid tribute to the labor of those who historically toiled in the sugar industry. This piece, like many others, uses historical stereotypes to comment on ongoing social issues. Walker’s art consistently integrates elements from historical textbooks and American folklore, particularly from the Antebellum South, to address themes of identity and gender, especially concerning African-American women. Her work "Gone, An Historical Romance of a Civil War as It Occurred Between the Dusky Thighs of One Young Negress and Her Heart," debuted in 1994, draws from sources like Margaret Mitchell's Gone with the Wind and Walt Disney's fairy tales to critique the romanticized views of the South's past. Walker's style uses the silhouette, historically a genteel medium in American art, to create eerie scenes that expose the harsh realities of racial inequality and violence. Walker's technique involves placing viewers within an immersive, circular space surrounded by menacing elements of the Southern landscape, enhancing the narrative's emotional impact. This method, reminiscent of historical cycloramas, intensifies the viewer's experience, confronting them with grotesque, often brutal scenes that illustrate stark racial injustices. Her work has evoked strong reactions, with viewers sometimes feeling shocked or distressed, reflecting the powerful effect of her narrative art. Walker’s contribution to art has been widely recognized, with numerous accolades including the prestigious MacArthur "genius" grant at the age of 28. Her exhibitions, such as the comprehensive survey at the Walker Art Center titled "Kara Walker: My Complement, My Oppressor, My Enemy, My Love," have further cemented her status in the art world. Influenced by artists like Andy Warhol, Adrian Piper, and Robert Colescott, Walker has developed a unique visual language that challenges traditional interpretations of American history and compels viewers to confront the complexities of racism and its underlying desires and repulsions. In her interviews, including discussions with institutions like New York's Museum of Modern Art, Walker has expressed a nuanced understanding of her artistic motivations and the narrative power of genre and historical paintings. Her work not only revisits historical narratives but also redefines them, offering new insights into the ongoing dialogue about race and history in America. Kara Walker: Blurring Boundaries with Silhouette Installations Kara Walker is renowned for her distinctive, immersive site-specific installations that seamlessly integrate visual and performance art elements. In her works, Walker transcends traditional artistic boundaries, using life-size cut figurines and theatrical staging to evoke a strong performative element. She prioritizes the conceptual over the purely aesthetic, aiming to provoke thought rather than just please the eye. According to Shelly Jarenski's analysis, Walker's installations draw heavily on the nineteenth-century panorama tradition, which was originally a popular form of entertainment depicting vast landscapes or historical scenes. While panoramas are rooted in the visual and cultural contexts of their time, Walker repurposes this format to address persistent social issues and racial narratives stemming from the history of slavery in America. Her modern panoramas offer a visual representation of historical events and the ongoing cultural narratives of race and space, providing a contemporary counterpart to these largely textual historical records. Walker's innovative use of panorama techniques allows her to blend historical devices with the grim realities she portrays, deconstructing traditional aesthetic forms to challenge viewers' perceptions. Jarenski highlights how Walker's work differs from traditional panoramas by providing a visual impact that historical written sources alone cannot convey. Through her art, Walker not only depicts but also critically examines historical narratives, making her installations a potent medium for exploring the interplay between past and present. Walker describes her artistic process as a blend of "two parts research and one part paranoid hysteria," reflecting her deep engagement with historical analysis and a profound emotional response to these themes. This approach underscores the balance she maintains between rigorous research and the emotional intensity that characterizes her work, infusing her art with both intellectual depth and a palpable sense of unease. Overall, Kara Walker's work is a compelling exploration of the intersections of history, culture, and artistic expression, continually pushing the boundaries of how art can influence public discourse and personal reflection on critical social issues. Kara Walker: Evolving Narratives in Art Kara Walker is celebrated for her dynamic and poignant art installations that often blur the lines between visual and performance art. Her notable work from 2000, "Insurrection! (Our Tools Were Rudimentary, Yet We Pressed On)," features silhouetted characters illuminated by colored light projections, enhancing their transparent quality and evoking 1930s animated films. This installation also interactively casts viewers' shadows onto the wall, directly involving them in the artwork and prompting reflection on its intense themes. In 2005, Walker's exhibit "8 Possible Beginnings" introduced moving images and sound to her silhouette presentations, deepening the immersive experience for audiences. Here, she used her and her daughter's voices to explore the lingering impacts of slavery on her personal and artistic identity. Following Hurricane Katrina, Walker created "After the Deluge," drawing parallels between the televised images of the disaster's aftermath and historical depictions of African enslavement. This work highlighted the continued relevance of racial stereotypes in contemporary media. Walker's innovative 2018 installation, "Katastwóf Karavan," featured at the Prospect.4 art festival in New Orleans, utilized a steam-powered calliope housed in a wagon adorned with her signature silhouettes, playing songs of black protest and celebration. This piece underscored the historical and cultural themes prevalent in her work. Walker's commissioned works include the powerful 2014 installation "A Subtlety, or the Marvelous Sugar Baby," at the now-demolished Domino Sugar Refinery. This work, featuring a massive sphinx-like figure, confronted viewers with the historical exploitation inherent in the sugar industry and the broader implications of race and labor. In 2016, she presented "Slaughter of the Innocents (They Might be Guilty of Something)," depicting harrowing scenes that echoed the tragic choices faced by enslaved individuals like Margaret Garner. Turbine Hall, a monumental fountain that critiques historical narratives related to the Atlantic slave trade, interweaving references from various art historical and cultural sources. This work, inspired in part by the Victoria Memorial, serves as a counter-monument, challenging traditional public commemorations and encouraging viewers to reconsider historical legacies. Walker's varied projects also include designing a curtain for the Vienna State Opera and participating in educational initiatives like creating lesson plans for the J. Paul Getty Museum. Her art extends into performances and literary contributions, as seen in her involvement with MoMA PS1 and fine art editions of the libretto for "Porgy & Bess." Kara Walker's body of work continues to challenge, educate, and inspire by melding historical inquiry with cutting-edge artistic expression, making her one of the most significant artists of her generation. Controversies and Discussions Surrounding Kara Walker's Artwork Kara Walker's artwork often stirs controversy due to its provocative themes and imagery. Her 1995 piece "The Means to an End: A Shadow Drama in Five Acts" was removed from a 1999 exhibition at the Detroit Institute of Art following protests from African-American artists and collectors. The installation featured antebellum plantation scenes that prompted significant backlash regarding its representation of racial history. Another contentious piece by Walker, entitled "The moral arc of history ideally bends towards justice but just as soon as not curves back around toward barbarism, sadism, and unrestrained chaos," was initially covered due to its controversial content after being displayed in the Newark Public Library. This artwork included provocative depictions involving the Ku Klux Klan and other racially charged imagery. It sparked intense debates among library staff and the public about its appropriateness, leading to discussions on racism, identity, and historical representation in art. Prominent African-American artist Betye Saar criticized Walker's approach in the 1999 PBS documentary "I'll Make Me a World," accusing her art of catering to the preferences of the white art establishment with its "revolting and negative" portrayal of black stereotypes. This led to broader discussions and a symposium at Harvard University that delved into the implications of racist imagery in contemporary art. Noteworthy Exhibitions and Accolades Kara Walker's career is marked by several significant exhibitions. Her first museum survey in 2007, organized by Philippe Vergne for the Walker Art Center, demonstrated her broad appeal and provocative style. Other notable solo shows include "My Complement, My Oppressor, My Love" and "The Ecstasy of St. Kara," which have continued to engage and challenge audiences worldwide. Walker's commissioned works, such as the large-scale public art piece "A Subtlety, or the Marvelous Sugar Baby," unveiled in 2014, have garnered critical acclaim and public interest. This particular installation at the Domino Sugar Refinery played a critical role in discussing historical labor issues tied to racial and economic exploitation. Walker's works are included in numerous public collections, including the Tate Collection in London and the Minneapolis Institute of Art. Her profound impact on the art world has been recognized with numerous awards, such as the MacArthur fellowship and inclusion in Time magazine's 100 Most Influential People list. Personal and Professional Life Kara Walker has lived a life rich with artistic exploration and professional development. Originally from Providence, Rhode Island, she has taught visual arts at Columbia University and maintained studios in prominent New York locations. Walker's personal experiences and background continue to inform her artistic expression, contributing to her distinctive voice in contemporary art. Beyond her own artistic practice, Walker has been actively involved in the arts community, including her role on the board of directors of the Foundation for Contemporary Arts. Kara Walker 1969– Since rising to prominence in the mid-1990s, Kara Walker has used silhouette cutouts as a signature element in her large-scale tableaux and projections. Yet her appropriation of this popular nineteenth-century portrait technique diverges starkly from its genteel roots. Drawing on sources that include American slave narratives, the literature of the antebellum South, and minstrel shows, her works examine racist and sexist stereotypes as well as issues of identity, narrative representation, violence, and desire. Walker has likened placing the viewer in the center of these historical retellings to “creating a monster that swallows you.” In Mistress Demanded Swift and Dramatic Empathetic Reaction Which We Obliged Her, paper cutouts and colored gels are cast from an overhead projector onto the wall of a dark room. The images call to mind historical attractions such as shadow puppetry and silent-era animation techniques pioneered by German artist Lotte Reiniger. Instead of fairy tales, however, Walker’s projection reveals a gruesome scene in which a small African American girl plunges a machete into the abdomen of a bound “mistress.” An onlooker’s head is trapped in a grotesque metal contraption, and his hands and feet are in shackles. Although the scene seemingly represents a slave rebellion or escape, nothing in it suggests redemption. Rather, Walker’s hard-hitting depictions aim to confront us with imagery that, as she has put it, undermines “all our fine-tuned, well-adjusted cultural beliefs.” Each of the fifteen prints in her portfolio Harper’s Pictorial History of the Civil War (Annotated), features a lithographic enlargement of an illustration from a popular history book published by Harper’s Weekly in 1866 that Walker has “annotated” with screenprinted silhouettes. The placement and contours of the silhouettes often highlight particular elements of the original imagery. Exodus of Confederates from Atlanta features a silhouette outline within a silhouette that calls attention to an African American boy piling up belongings for white civilian evacuees. In Alabama Loyalists Greeting the Federal Gun-Boats, an oversized silhouette of a woman becomes part of the action; she appears to have been knocked down by Union supporters rushing to greet the northern warships. Although Walker’s interventions obscure much of the detail in the source imagery, they foreground an African American presence that had been minimized or ignored in the originals, underscoring an ongoing bias in popular media representations of newsworthy events. As she has explained, “as long as there are people saying ‘Hey, you don’t belong here’ to others, it only seems realistic to continue investigating the terrain of racism.” Dana Miller and Adam D.
