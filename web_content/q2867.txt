--- [Mauro Forghieri - Wikipedia] ---
Mauro Forghieri (13 January 1935 – 2 November 2022) was an Italian mechanical engineer, best known for his work as a Formula One racing car designer with Scuderia Ferrari during the 1960s and 1970s. He is credited with introducing the first designed rear wings to Formula One at the 1968 Belgian Grand Prix. He oversaw numerous technical developments during his tenure at Ferrari, including the creation of the 250 GTO and P-series sports racing cars, the Ferrari flat-12 series of engines, Ferrari's first turbocharged engine in the 126 C F1 car, and a prototype semi-automatic transmission in 1979. During Forghieri's tenure with Ferrari, the company won the F1 World Driver's Championship four times and the F1 World Constructors' Championship seven times. After leaving Ferrari in 1987, he worked at Lamborghini and Bugatti then founded the Oral Engineering Group in 1995.

- Mauro Forghieri - Mauro Forghieri Born 13 January 1935 Modena, ItalyNationality Italian Occupation Formula One car designer Mauro Forghieri (born January 13, 1935) is an Italian Formula One car designer. Contents Early life and Ferrari Forghieri was born in Modena, the only child of Reclus and Afra Forghieri. His father, a turner, did war work during World War II for the Ansaldo mechanical workshops of Naples. After the conflict, he took up work in the Ferrari workshop in Maranello. Meanwhile, Forghieri completed the Liceo Scientifico and obtained a degree in Mechanical Engineering from the University of Bologna. Despite his initial interest in aviation design, he accepted an offer from Ferrari, where he had been introduced by his father. He became part of the racing team in 1962, with the position of Chief of the Technical Department for racing cars. He was later promoted to Technical Director of the Racing Department. In 1970, Forghieri designed the Ferrari 312 series (consisting of the 312 and 312B formula one cars and 312P and 312PB sportscars). Under his guidance Ferrari won the driver's F1 world championship title four times, with John Surtees (1964), Niki Lauda (1975 and 1977), and Jody Scheckter (1979). Ferrari also won the constructors F1 world championship title eight times. Lamborghini and Bugatti After leaving Ferrari in 1987, Forghieri joined Lamborghini Engineering, a department created by Lee Iacocca, the then CEO of Chrysler, who had bought the Emilian car firm Lamborghini. In that organisation, which had the ex-Ferrari Daniele Audetto as sports director, Forghieri designed a V12 aspirated engine, which competed in the 1989 F1 championship on a Larrousse car (driven by Philippe Alliot). Following the encouraging performance of the engine, the project of designing a whole car was conceived, thanks to financing by the Mexican businessman Fernando Gonzalez Luna. The car, whose bodywork was designed by Mario Tolentino, was slated for a 1991 debut, but the day before the official presentation to the press, Gonzalez Luna disappeared with a conspicuous amount of money that had been paid by sponsors. Nevertheless, the car debuted thanks to financing by Carlo Patrucco, of the newly created Modena Team (also known as Lambo). The latter was an unsuccessful enterprise, however, and Forghieri left Lamborghini soon afterwards. In 1992, he became the technical director of the re-emerging Bugatti, where he stayed until 1994. In the same year, he was also called as an expert in the trial relating to the death of driver Ayrton Senna on the Imola track. Oral Engineering Group In 1994, Forghieri co-founded with Franco Antoniazzi and Sergio Lugli the Oral Engineering Group, a mechanical design company he still works for. The company was founded in 1909 in the then-German city of Molsheim, Alsace, by the Italian-born industrial designer Ettore Bugatti. The cars were known for their design beauty and numerous race victories. Famous Bugatti automobiles include the Type 35 Grand Prix cars, the Type 41 "Royale", the Type 57 "Atlantic" and the Type 55 sports car. The death of Ettore Bugatti in 1947 proved to be a severe blow to the marque, and the death of his son Jean in 1939 meant that there was no successor to lead the factory. With no more than about 8,000 cars made, the company struggled financially, and it released one last model in the 1950s before eventually being purchased for its airplane parts business in 1963. In 1987, an Italian entrepreneur bought the brand name and revived it as Bugatti Automobili S.p.A.[2] A film about the founding of Bugatti is being produced by Andrea Iervolino.[3] Under Ettore Bugatti The founder Ettore Bugatti was born in Milan, Italy, and the automobile company that bears his name was founded in 1909 in Molsheim located in the Alsace region which was part of the German Empire from 1871 to 1919. The company was known both for the level of detail of its engineering in its automobiles, and for the artistic manner in which the designs were executed, given the artistic nature of Ettore's family (his father, Carlo Bugatti (1856–1940), was an important Art Nouveau furniture and jewelry designer). World War I and its aftermath During the war Ettore Bugatti was sent away, initially to Milan and later to Paris, but as soon as hostilities had been concluded he returned to his factory at Molsheim.[4] Less than four months after the Versailles Treaty formalised the transfer of Alsace from Germany to France, Bugatti was able to obtain, at the last minute, a stand at the 15th Paris motor show in October 1919.[4] He exhibited three light cars, all of them closely based on their pre-war equivalents, and each fitted with the same overhead camshaft 4-cylinder 1,368cc engine with four valves per cylinder.[4] Smallest of the three was a "Type 13" with a racing body (constructed by the Bugatti themselves) and using a chassis with a 2,000 mm (78.7 in) wheelbase.[4] The others were a "Type 22" and a "Type 23" with wheelbases of 2,250 and 2,400 mm (88.6 and 94.5 in) respectively.[4] Racing successes The company also enjoyed great success in early Grand Prix motor racing: in 1929, a privately entered Bugatti won the first ever Monaco Grand Prix. Bugatti's racing success culminated with driver Jean-Pierre Wimille winning the 24 hours of Le Mans twice (in 1937 with Robert Benoist and in 1939 with Pierre Veyron). Bugatti cars were extremely successful in racing. The little Bugatti Type 10 swept the top four positions at its first race. The 1924 Bugatti Type 35 is one of the most successful racing cars - developed by Bugatti with master engineer and racing driver Jean Chassagne who also drove it in the car's first ever Grand Prix in 1924 Lyon.[5] Bugattis swept to victory in the Targa Florio for five years straight from 1925 through 1929. Louis Chiron held the most podiums in Bugatti cars, and the modern marque revival Bugatti Automobiles S.A.S. named the 1999 Bugatti 18/3 Chiron concept car in his honour. But it was the final racing success at Le Mans that is most remembered—Jean-Pierre Wimille and Pierre Veyron won the 1939 race with just one car and meagre resources. Aeroplane racing In the 1930s, Ettore Bugatti got involved in the creation of a racer airplane, hoping to beat the Germans in the Deutsch de la Meurthe prize. This would be the Bugatti 100P,[6][7] which never flew. It was designed by Belgian engineer Louis de Monge who had already applied Bugatti Brescia engines in his "Type 7.5" lifting body. Railcar Ettore Bugatti also designed a successful motorised railcar, the Autorail Bugatti .[8] Family tragedy The death of Ettore Bugatti's son, Jean Bugatti, on 11 August 1939 marked a turning point in the company's fortunes as he died while testing a Type 57 tank-bodied race car near the Molsheim factory.[9] After World War II World War II left the Molsheim factory in ruins and the company lost control of the property. During the war, Bugatti planned a new factory at Levallois, a northwestern suburb of Paris. After the war, Bugatti designed and planned to build a series of new cars, including the Type 73 road car and Type 73C single seat racing car, but in all Bugatti built only five Type 73 cars. Development of a 375 cc supercharged car was stopped when Ettore Bugatti died on 21 August 1947. Following his death, the business declined further and made its last appearance as a business in its own right at a Paris Motor Show in October 1952.[10] After a long decline, the original incarnation of Bugatti ceased operations in 1952. Design Bugatti models are known to focus on design.[11]Engine blocks were hand scraped to ensure that the surfaces were flat so that gaskets were not required for sealing, and many of the exposed surfaces of the engine compartment featured guilloché finishes on them. Safety wires were threaded through most fasteners in intricately laced patterns. Rather than bolt the springs to the axles as most manufacturers did, Bugatti's axles were forged such that the spring passed through an opening in the axle, a much more elegant solution requiring fewer parts. Bugatti himself described his competitor Bentley's cars as "the world's fastest lorries" for focusing on durability. Attempts at revival The company attempted a comeback under Roland Bugatti in the mid-1950s with the mid-engined Type 251 race car. Designed with help from Gioacchino Colombo, the car failed to perform to expectations and the company's attempts at automobile production were halted. In the 1960s, Virgil Exner designed a Bugatti as part of his "Revival Cars" project. A show version of this car was actually built by Ghia using the last Bugatti Type 101 chassis, and was shown at the 1965 Turin Motor Show. Finance was not forthcoming, and Exner then turned his attention to a revival of Stutz. Bugatti continued manufacturing airplane parts and was sold to Hispano-Suiza, also a former auto maker turned aircraft supplier, in 1963.[1] Snecma took over Hispano-Suiza in 1968. After acquiring Messier, Snecma merged Messier and Bugatti into Messier-Bugatti in 1977. Modern revivals Bugatti Automobili S.p.A. (1987–1995) Italian entrepreneur Romano Artioli acquired the Bugatti brand in 1987, and established Bugatti Automobili S.p.A.. Artioli commissioned architect Giampaolo Benedini to design the factory which was built in Campogalliano, Modena, Italy. Construction of the plant began in 1988, alongside the development of the first model, and it was inaugurated two years later—in 1990.[15] By 1989, the plans for the new Bugatti revival were presented by Paolo Stanzani and Marcello Gandini, designers of the Lamborghini Miura and Lamborghini Countach. The first production vehicle was the Bugatti EB110 GT which featured a 3.5-litre, 5-valve per cylinder, quad-turbocharged 60° V12 engine, a six-speed gearbox, and four-wheel drive. Stanzani proposed an aluminium honeycomb chassis, which was used for all early prototypes. He and president Artioli clashed over engineering decisions so Stanzani left the project and Artioli sought Nicola Materazzi to replace him in June 1990. Materazzi, who had been the chief designer for the Ferrari 288 GTO and Ferrari F40 replaced the aluminium chassis with a carbon fibre one manufactured by Aerospatiale and also altered the torque distribution of the car from 40:60 to 27:73. He remained Director until late 1992.[16][17] Racing car designer Mauro Forghieri served as Bugatti's technical director from 1993 through 1994.[18] On 27 August 1993, through his holding company, ACBN Holdings S.A. of Luxembourg, Romano Artioli purchased Lotus Cars from General Motors. Plans were made to list Bugatti shares on international stock exchanges. Bugatti presented a prototype large saloon called the EB112 in 1993. Perhaps the most famous Bugatti EB110 owner was seven-time Formula One World Champion racing driver Michael Schumacher who purchased an EB110 in 1994. Schumacher sold his EB110, which had been repaired after a severe 1994 crash, to Modena Motorsport, a Ferrari service and race preparation garage in Germany. By the time the EB110 came to market, the North American and European economies were in recession. Poor economic conditions caused the company to fail and operations ceased in September 1995. A model specific to the US market called the "Bugatti America" was in the preparatory stages when the company ceased operations. Bugatti's liquidators sold Lotus Cars to Proton of Malaysia. German firm Dauer Racing purchased the EB110 licence and remaining parts stock in 1997 in order to produce five more EB110 SS vehicles. These five SS versions of the EB110 were greatly refined by Dauer. The Campogalliano factory was sold to a furniture-making company, which became defunct prior to moving in, leaving the building unoccupied.[19] After Dauer stopped producing cars in 2011, Toscana-Motors GmbH of Germany purchased the remaining parts stock from Dauer. Mauro Forghieri (13 January 1935 – 2 November 2022) was an Italian mechanical engineer best known for his pivotal role in Scuderia Ferrari's success in Formula One and sports car racing, where he designed innovative chassis, engines, and aerodynamic features that led to multiple world championships and race victories.[1] Born in Magreta near Modena to Reclus Forghieri, a longtime Ferrari mechanic, he studied mechanical engineering at the University of Bologna, graduating in 1959.[1][2]Forghieri joined Ferrari in 1957 as an intern while still a student and returned full-time in 1959, quickly rising to head the motorsport department and testing activities in 1961 at the age of 26 under Enzo Ferrari's direct appointment.[3][2] Over nearly three decades until 1987, he served as technical director, overseeing the design of both chassis and engines, and played a central role in transforming Ferrari into a dominant force in motorsport.[4] His tenure saw Ferrari secure four drivers' championships—with John Surtees in 1964, Niki Lauda in 1975 and 1977, and Jody Scheckter in 1979—and seven constructors' titles (1964, 1975–1977, 1979, and 1982–1983), along with 54 Grand Prix wins.[1][5][4]Among his most notable designs were the Ferrari 156 "Sharknose" (1961), which helped Phil Hill win the 1961 drivers' title, and the 250 GTO (1962), where he refined the suspension and rear layout to make it a dominant GT racer with multiple victories at Le Mans, Targa Florio, and other endurance events.[3][1] In Formula One, Forghieri pioneered aerodynamic innovations, such as the large rear wings introduced on the 312 F1 car at the 1968 Belgian Grand Prix, and developed the revolutionary flat-12 engine for the 312B (1970), which powered successes for drivers like Jacky Ickx and Clay Regazzoni.[3][4] The 312T series (1975–1979), featuring a transverse gearbox for better weight distribution, delivered three drivers' titles and four constructors' crowns, while his work on the turbocharged 126C engines in the early 1980s clinched back-to-back constructors' championships with Gilles Villeneuve and Didier Pironi.[5][2][4]Beyond Ferrari, Forghieri briefly led engineering at Lamborghini starting in 1987, contributing to the Lamborghini V12 engine, before joining Bugatti in 1992 to work on advanced projects.[1] He later founded his own design consultancy, continuing to influence automotive engineering until his death from illness in Modena.[3] Regarded as one of the last "complete" racing car designers capable of overseeing an entire vehicle from concept to victory, Forghieri's legacy endures through his bold innovations, close collaborations with legendary drivers, and enduring impact on Ferrari's golden eras in the 1960s and 1970s.[1][2] Early life Family background Mauro Forghieri was born on 13 January 1935 in Modena, Italy, as the only child of Reclus Forghieri and Afra Forghieri.[6][7]Reclus Forghieri, a skilled pattern-maker and machinist, established the family's deep ties to the automotive world through his professional tenure. He collaborated with Enzo Ferrari on early Alfa Romeo racing projects in the 1930s, contributing to efforts like the Alfa Romeo 158 during Ferrari's time at the Milan-based team. Following World War II, Reclus joined the Scuderia Ferrari workshop in Maranello in 1947, where he worked as a machinist amid the company's postwar expansion.[4][8][2]Forghieri's childhood unfolded in Modena, a hub of Emilia-Romagna's burgeoning automotive industry, during and after the disruptions of World War II. The family endured separations and relocations due to the conflict, with Reclus working at the Ansaldo mechanical workshops in Naples before reuniting with Afra and young Mauro in Modena. This environment immersed Forghieri in the mechanical trades from an early age, as he observed his father's craftsmanship in local workshops, sparking a foundational interest in engineering. The postwar era's resurgence of Italian motor racing, centered in Modena's "Motor Valley," further nurtured his passion for mechanics, surrounded by the innovations of figures like Ferrari and Maserati.[9][10][1]Known throughout his life for his intense and driven personality, Forghieri acquired the nickname "Furia," meaning "Fury," which reflected his fiery temperament often displayed in high-stakes engineering environments.[11][12] Education Mauro Forghieri attended the Liceo Scientifico in Modena, a prestigious scientific high school that emphasized rigorous studies in mathematics and physics, laying a strong foundation for his future in engineering.[7]Following his secondary education, Forghieri enrolled in the Mechanical Engineering program at the University of Bologna, one of Italy's leading institutions during the post-war era. He graduated in 1959 with a laurea, the Italian equivalent of a master's degree, having completed coursework that equipped him with essential knowledge in mechanical systems and design principles relevant to automotive applications.[2][1][8]The University of Bologna's engineering faculty, thriving amid Italy's industrial reconstruction in the 1950s, fostered an environment of innovation and technical excellence, influencing Forghieri's peers and exposing him to the era's advancements in machinery and energy systems.[13] His family's longstanding ties to Ferrari facilitated key opportunities immediately following his graduation.[14] Ferrari career Entry and leadership roles Mauro Forghieri joined Ferrari in January 1960 as a junior designer shortly after graduating from the University of Bologna, having been recommended by his father Reclus, a longtime employee in the company's workshop.[15][16] At age 25, he initially worked under chief engineer Andrea Fraschetti in the engine department, performing calculations on 1.5-liter Formula 2 engines and serving as a liaison between design and testing teams.[15][16]His rapid ascent came in late 1961 amid the "Great Walkout," a crisis triggered by internal disputes involving Enzo Ferrari's wife Laura and leading to the departure of key figures, including chief designer Carlo Chiti and team manager Romolo Tavoni.[2][15] On October 30, 1961, Enzo Ferrari appointed the 26-year-old Forghieri as chief designer and technical director, a role he initially questioned due to his limited experience but accepted under Ferrari's directive to focus on the work while he handled external matters.[16][2] This promotion at age 27 in 1962 solidified his leadership over Ferrari's motorsport engineering efforts.[15]From 1961 to 1977, Forghieri served as technical director, overseeing the design and development of chassis, engines, and transmissions for Formula One and sports prototypes, while managing race operations and collaborating across departments.[15][16] In 1977, he transitioned to director of the sports, prototypes, and Formula One department, a position he held until 1984, leading teams of up to 200 engineers in a collaborative environment that emphasized total commitment despite modest resources.[15][16]Forghieri's leadership was tested by Enzo Ferrari's autocratic style, marked by intense scrutiny and occasional shouting matches, which demanded unwavering loyalty and quick adaptation to shifting priorities.[16][17] He also navigated the turbulent labor climate of the 1960s, including widespread unrest among workers and engineers that peaked in the 1961 walkout, forcing him to rebuild the technical staff amid threats of further exodus to rival teams like ATS.[15][2]By 1984, after 24 years of relentless involvement, Forghieri stepped down from his racing department role due to company restructuring under Fiat's growing influence and personal burnout from the high-stakes environment.[15][17] He briefly shifted to advanced research before fully departing Ferrari in 1987.[16] Key designs and innovations Mauro Forghieri's contributions to Ferrari's road cars in the early 1960s emphasized aerodynamic efficiency and handling improvements. For the 1962 Ferrari 250 GTO, he oversaw the redesign of the rear section and enhancements to power output, incorporating aerodynamic bodywork that reduced drag while maintaining structural integrity for racing applications.[3] Similarly, the 1964 Ferrari 275 GTB introduced independent rear suspension under Forghieri's direction, featuring a transaxle layout that improved weight distribution and ride quality over previous solid-axle designs.[18]In Formula 1, Forghieri pioneered the shift to more advanced chassis configurations. The 1961 Ferrari 156, known as the "Sharknose" for its distinctive front profile, adopted a mid-engine layout that enhanced balance and traction, powered by a 1.5-liter V6 engine designed to serve as a stressed chassis member.[2] Building on this, the 312 series debuted in 1966 with a [V12 engine](/page/V12 engine); Forghieri later introduced the flat-12 engine in the 312B (1970), a compact 3-liter unit co-developed with Franco Rocchi that provided superior power delivery and allowed for better packaging within the chassis for improved weight centralization.[19] Forghieri's evolution of the 3-liter V12 engine across subsequent models focused on refining airflow and combustion efficiency, addressing vibration issues inherent to the layout while boosting output to over 500 horsepower by the mid-1970s.[20]A hallmark innovation from Forghieri was the introduction of aerodynamic aids in F1. At the 1968 Belgian Grand Prix, he fitted the first rear-mounted wing to the Ferrari 312, generating downforce to improve rear traction on high-speed corners, a concept tested in wind tunnels and later adopted league-wide.[18] These designs, including the 312T's 1975 transverse gearbox, contributed to Ferrari's championship successes in the 1960s and 1970s.[21]Forghieri also advanced turbocharged technology with the 1981 Type 126 C, Ferrari's first turbo F1 engine—a 1.5-liter V6 producing approximately 560 horsepower—overcoming lag and reliability issues through refined intercooling and exhaust management.[2] In prototype racing, the 1979 512 BB LM featured a flat-12 engine tuned for endurance, with lightweight materials and aerodynamic refinements to meet Group 6 regulations.[18] He innovated cooling systems, such as hydraulic fans that adjusted airflow dynamically to engine demands, reducing drag compared to fixed mechanical fans.[2]Throughout the 1970s, Forghieri navigated significant engineering challenges, including material limitations like the scarcity of high-strength alloys and frequent FIA regulation changes that mandated safety features such as deformable structures and reduced fuel capacities, requiring iterative redesigns to maintain competitiveness.[18] Post-Ferrari career Lamborghini projects After leaving Ferrari in 1987, Mauro Forghieri was recruited by Chrysler Corporation—then the new owner of Automobili Lamborghini—as technical director of the newly formed Lamborghini Engineering division, a separate entity focused on motorsport development.[8] This role marked his transition from road and racing car design at Ferrari to spearheading Lamborghini's entry into Formula 1, leveraging his expertise in high-performance engines to enhance the brand's prestige under American ownership.[17]Forghieri's primary contribution was the design of the Lamborghini 3512, a 3.5-liter naturally aspirated V12 engine compliant with the 1989 FIA Formula 1 regulations, which emphasized normally aspirated powerplants over turbocharged units.[22] Unveiled in April 1988 and first tested in a Larrousse-Lola chassis at Dijon-Prenois later that year, the engine produced around 560-600 horsepower in its initial form, featuring advanced fuel injection and a compact layout derived from Forghieri's Ferrari turbo experience but adapted for reliability and outright power.[23] It debuted competitively in the 1989 season with the Larrousse team, where drivers like Philippe Alliot and Aguri Suzuki achieved occasional top-10 finishes, though the combination suffered from reliability issues common to new F1 power units.[2]The 3512 engine saw wider adoption across multiple teams during its short lifespan from 1989 to 1993, powering Lotus in 1990, Minardi and Ligier in 1991, and the short-lived Modena Team in 1991.[22] For the Modena Team, funded by Mexican businessman Fernando Gonzalez Luna, Forghieri extended his involvement beyond the engine to oversee the design of the complete Lambo 291 chassis, a sophisticated carbon-fiber monocoque that incorporated aerodynamic refinements and suspension geometry informed by his decades of Grand Prix experience.[17] Despite qualifying for several races—such as a 22nd-place start at the 1991 San Marino Grand Prix—the project collapsed amid funding disputes and the team's withdrawal after just four events.[23]Under Chrysler's stewardship, Forghieri's work at Lamborghini Engineering emphasized technological innovation to support the company's supercar lineup indirectly, with the F1 program serving as a marketing and R&D platform despite no race victories.[24] His tenure ended in late 1991 when Chrysler restructured the division, but the 3512's development highlighted Lamborghini's brief foray into top-tier racing.[13] Forghieri's leadership brought a level of engineering pedigree to the project, though financial constraints and the era's competitive landscape limited its success.[13] Bugatti contributions In 1992, Mauro Forghieri joined Bugatti Automobili as technical director and chief engineer, hired by company founder Romano Artioli to oversee engineering efforts during the brand's revival.[25][26]Forghieri played a key role in the development of the EB110 supercar, produced from 1991 to 1995, where he contributed to the design of its central 3.5-liter quad-turbocharged V12 engine, which delivered 560 horsepower and was integrated with a permanent all-wheel-drive system for enhanced traction and performance.[27][28] He also led enhancements for the EB110 Super Sport variant, boosting output to 603 horsepower through refined turbocharging, electronic fuel management, and larger injectors while maintaining the lightweight carbon-fiber monocoque chassis clad in aluminum body panels.[25][29] These innovations emphasized advanced electronic multipoint fuel injection and lightweight aluminum construction to achieve high performance in a road-legal package, marking a shift toward modern supercar engineering.[30]Forghieri additionally contributed to the EB112 sedan prototype, unveiled in 1993 as a luxury four-door grand tourerconcept with a front-mounted 6.0-liter V12 engine producing 455 horsepower, all-wheel drive, and a focus on refined grand touring dynamics.[31][32][33] The project highlighted his expertise in scaling high-performance powertrains for more practical vehicles, incorporating similar electronic fuel injection and aluminum elements for weight efficiency.Development at Bugatti faced technical challenges in blending Italian stylistic flair with rigorous production standards, compounded by financial strains that led to the company's bankruptcy in 1995, halting further EB110 production at 139 units and preventing the EB112 from reaching series manufacturing; Forghieri departed in mid-1994 amid these issues.[32][34] Oral Engineering and later ventures In 1995, Mauro Forghieri co-founded the Oral Engineering Group in Modena, Italy, alongside former Ferrari colleagues Franco Antoniazzi and Sergio Lugli, establishing it as a consultancy firm focused on mechanical design, research, and development for the automotive sector.[2][23] The company specialized in creating prototypes and providing technical support to manufacturers, drawing on Forghieri's extensive experience in engine and chassisengineering.[12]The firm undertook various consulting projects for clients including BMW and Ferrari, encompassing engine redesigns and vehicle component optimizations.[35] In the early 2000s, Oral Engineering collaborated on hybrid prototypes, applying advanced materials and efficiency-focused designs to automotive applications. Forghieri's leadership emphasized practical engineering solutions, often leveraging his prior work on high-performance engines to enhance client projects.[36]A notable endeavor under Forghieri's direction was his role as chief engineer for Project 1221 from the mid-2000s to 2008, where he oversaw the development of the MF1 supercar prototype.[2] This ambitious two-seat berlinetta intended to feature a gas-turbine engine targeting over 1,000 horsepower, built on a modular aluminum chassis for flexibility in configuration, with production planned for 199 limited units but ultimately unrealized due to funding challenges.[37][38] The project drew on Forghieri's Bugatti engine expertise to aim at rivals like the Veyron.[39]Beyond core automotive consulting, Forghieri took on advisory roles for Italian racing teams and contributed to the Modena historic car restoration community in the 2010s, including efforts to revive and prepare vehicles like the 1980 Ferrari Pinin for international concours events.[4][40] By the late 2000s, Oral Engineering had evolved into a boutique service provider, employing a small team of ex-Ferrari specialists to deliver tailored engineering solutions, allowing Forghieri to transition toward semi-retirement while maintaining selective involvement. Achievements Formula One championships Under Mauro Forghieri's technical direction at Ferrari from 1962 to 1984, the team secured four Formula One Drivers' Championships and seven Constructors' Championships, along with 54 Grand Prix victories.[17] His designs played a pivotal role in these successes, blending innovative engineering with strategic adaptations to evolving regulations.The first major triumph came in 1964, when John Surtees clinched the Drivers' Championship driving the Ferrari 158, powered by a mid-engine V8 that provided superior handling and reliability on demanding circuits like the Nürburgring and Monza.[41] This victory also marked Ferrari's Constructors' Championship win that year, with consistent podiums from Surtees and teammates Lorenzo Bandini and Ludovico Scarfiotti contributing to a total of 45 points.[42]A decade later, Forghieri's Ferrari 312T, featuring a dominant flat-12 engine, propelled Niki Lauda to the 1975 Drivers' Championship and secured the Constructors' title, ending an 11-year drought for Ferrari in the latter.[43] The flat-12's smooth power delivery and torque characteristics gave the car an edge in traction and outright performance, enabling Lauda to win five races amid fierce competition from Tyrrell and McLaren.[21] In 1977, an evolved version of the 312T series repeated the feat, with Lauda's three victories and strong team results overcoming reliability challenges to claim both titles.[4]The 312T lineage culminated in 1979, when Jody Scheckter drove the 312T4 to the Drivers' Championship, supported by Gilles Villeneuve's consistent scoring for a Constructors' double.[44] Scheckter's three wins highlighted the car's balanced chassis and refined flat-12, which maintained Ferrari's edge despite the rise of ground-effect designs.Forghieri's influence extended into the turbocharged era, where he led the development of the 126C from 1981 onward, navigating regulatory shifts from 1977 to 1986 that limited fuel and boost while favoring forced induction.[5] This strategic pivot paid off in 1982 and 1983, as the 126C's V6 turbo engine powered Gilles Villeneuve and Didier Pironi to Constructors' Championships, with key wins at Monaco and Silverstone underscoring Ferrari's adaptability in the high-boost, high-risk environment. Technical legacy Mauro Forghieri advanced Ferrari's mid-engine F1 designs with the Ferrari 158 in 1964, which incorporated a semi-monocoque chassis for improved structural integrity and weight distribution, fundamentally influencing subsequent F1 car architectures.[16] This shift from front-engine designs enabled better handling and balance, setting a template for modern single-seaters that prioritized aerodynamic efficiency and driver positioning. Complementing this, Forghieri introduced the first purpose-built rear wings in F1 on the Ferrari 312 at the 1968 Belgian Grand Prix, generating downforce to enhance cornering speeds and prompting the FIA to standardize and regulate such aerodynamic aids across the grid shortly thereafter.[5] These innovations not only boosted Ferrari's competitiveness but also accelerated the evolution of vehicle dynamics in motorsport, with rear wing principles becoming integral to high-speed stability in contemporary designs.[4]Forghieri's engine developments further cemented his influence, particularly with the flat-12 configuration debuted in the 1970 Ferrari 312B, which delivered over 450 horsepower from a compact, low-profile package that optimized airflow and chassis integration.[2] This engine's reliability and power-to-weight advantages established performance benchmarks for the 1970s, powering Ferrari to constructors' titles in 1975–1977 and 1979 while inspiring wider adoption of horizontally opposed layouts for their aerodynamic benefits. Transitioning to the turbo era, Forghieri led the creation of Ferrari's first turbocharged V6 in the 1981 126C, achieving qualifying outputs exceeding 600 horsepower through innovative "hot vee" architecture that minimized turbo lag and maximized boost efficiency.[16] These advancements set enduring standards for forced-induction technology in the 1980s, influencing engine strategies amid evolving regulations and contributing to Ferrari's 1982 and 1983 constructors' championships.[2]In sports car racing, Forghieri's designs such as the 250 GTO and 330 P series contributed to six consecutive victories at the 24 Hours of Le Mans from 1962 to 1967, along with multiple International Championship for Sports Cars titles, expanding his legacy beyond Formula One.[3]Beyond hardware, Forghieri's mentorship shaped generations of engineers, notably through his collaboration with Harvey Postlethwaite on the 126C, where he imparted expertise in integrated chassis-engine design that reinforced Ferrari's technical ethos and elevated Italian engineering prowess in global motorsport.[16] His guidance fostered a culture of holistic innovation at Maranello, embedding principles of precision and adaptability that permeated Ferrari's DNA and influenced subsequent talents in the field. Forghieri's broader impact extended to road cars, exemplified by his refinements to the 250 GTO for FIA homologation, which integrated racing-derived aerodynamics and powertrains into a production supercar framework, paving the way for the genre's emphasis on limited-run, track-capable vehicles.[3] In his post-Ferrari projects, he advanced prototype engineering at Lamborghini and Bugatti, incorporating composite materials in designs like the EB110 to achieve lighter, stiffer structures that enhanced performance and safety in high-end road and concept cars.[17]Recognized as one of the last complete designers in an era of increasing specialization, Forghieri oversaw every facet of vehicle development—from engines and gearboxes to chassis and suspension—producing elegant, championship-winning machines that exemplified integrated engineering excellence.[13] His holistic approach not only drove Ferrari's Formula One triumphs but also left a lasting imprint on motorsport's technical evolution, prioritizing conceptual innovation over siloed expertise. Later life and death Final professional activities In his later years, Mauro Forghieri transitioned further into consulting roles, particularly supporting Ferrari's heritage department in preserving and promoting the marque's racing legacy through historic events and restorations.[45] He provided technical expertise for initiatives like the rebuild of classic Ferrari models, drawing on his deep knowledge of the company's engineering history.[13]In the 2010s, Forghieri remained a sought-after voice in motorsport, contributing to numerous interviews and publications that offered insights into the Enzo Ferrari era and Scuderia Ferrari's golden years. He co-authored Forghieri on Ferrari: 1947 to the Present in 2013, a comprehensive account of the team's evolution based on his firsthand experiences. Additionally, he featured prominently in the 2017 documentary Ferrari 312B: Where the Revolution Begins, sharing details on the development of the iconic flat-12 engine and its role in Formula One successes.[46]Forghieri received several honors recognizing his lifetime contributions during this period, including induction into the FIVA Heritage Hall of Fame in 2021 alongside fellow Italian automotive icons like Marcello Gandini and Giorgetto Giugiaro.[47] In 2022, he was awarded honorary citizenship of Modena, his hometown and the heart of Italy's Motor Valley.[45]Through his firm Oral Engineering, based near Modena, Forghieri maintained advisory roles within the region's automotive cluster, mentoring emerging engineers and collaborating on R&D projects for clients including Ferrari and BMW.[35] By the late 2010s, he entered semi-retirement, residing in Modena and devoting time to his personal collection of Ferrari memorabilia, which reflected his enduring passion for the brand's heritage.[45] Death and tributes Mauro Forghieri passed away on 2 November 2022 in his hometown of Modena, Italy, at the age of 87, following a brief illness that led to natural causes.[3][48][1]Scuderia Ferrari issued an official statement mourning Forghieri as one of the most significant figures in the company's history, emphasizing his nearly four-decade association with the team and his role in transforming its racing department through innovative leadership.[3] To honor him during the 2022 Brazilian Grand Prix in Interlagos, Ferrari affixed "Ciao Furia" decals—referencing his nickname "Furia" (Fury)—to the cars of drivers Charles Leclerc and Carlos Sainz, a gesture that extended to their GT racing entries as well.[49]Tributes poured in from across the Formula 1 community, with Ferrari vice president Piero Ferrari describing the day as "very sad" and praising Forghieri as an "amazing person" whose impact on the team's history was profound.[50] Team principal Mattia Binotto echoed this sentiment, noting Forghieri's appointment as team manager at age 27 and his enduring legacy in Ferrari's successes.[50] Former Ferrari driver Jody Scheckter, the last to win a drivers' title in a Forghieri-designed car, called him "the one who got things done," highlighting his hands-on genius in pushing the team toward victory.[44]Memorial events took place in Modena shortly after his death, including a funeral attended by racing figures and locals, reflecting his deep ties to the city where he was declared an honorary citizen earlier in 2022.[51] Media coverage was extensive, with obituaries in The Guardian underscoring his 27-year tenure at Ferrari as the last engineer to design a complete championship-winning F1 car, and in Motorsport Magazine portraying him as an inimitable force in the sport's engineering evolution.[1][13]Forghieri's family, including his second wife Elisabetta and sons Alessandro and Michele from his first marriage, maintained privacy throughout the proceedings. He was cremated, with the disposition of his ashes kept private.[1][52]
