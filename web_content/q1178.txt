The Gerbrandy Tower (Dutch: Gerbrandytoren) is a radio tower in IJsselstein, the Netherlands. It is also known as Lopik tower after the nearby town. It was built in 1961.

Standing tall and proud in the Dutch landscape, the Gerbrandy Tower, known locally as the Gerbrandytoren, is a remarkable feat of engineering and a significant landmark in the Netherlands. Located in IJsselstein, this towering structure serves as a vital hub for radio and television broadcasts, mobile telecommunications, and data storage. With a height of over 366 meters, the Gerbrandy Tower is the tallest structure in the Netherlands, and its history and purpose are as fascinating as its impressive stature. The story of the Gerbrandy Tower begins in the early 20th century when the area south of Utrecht was identified as an ideal location for radio coverage. This led to the construction of various radio transmission towers in the region, including the Jaarsveld radio tower in 1937 and the medium wave transmitter Lopik in 1941. The advent of television in the Netherlands in the 1950s necessitated the construction of a new television mast in IJsselstein, which eventually led to the creation of the Gerbrandy Tower. The construction of the Gerbrandy Tower began in 1958, replacing an earlier television mast built in 1951. The new tower was designed to accommodate both radio and television transmissions, and it officially commenced operations on January 17, 1961. The tower was ceremonially opened by Queen Juliana on May 9, 1961, marking a significant milestone in Dutch broadcasting history. The Gerbrandy Tower is an architectural marvel, consisting of a 100-meter-high concrete tower topped with a 266-meter-tall guyed steel tube mast. The base of the steel mast is positioned at a height of 90 meters within the concrete tower, resulting in a total height of 356 meters above ground level. An additional 10-meter steel column with antenna boxes on top brings the total height to over 366 meters. The tower's design has evolved over the years to accommodate new technologies and broadcasting requirements. Originally, the total height of the tower was 350 meters. In 1962, antennas for the second network and the paging network were added, increasing the height to 382 meters, making it the tallest structure in Western Europe at the time. In 1987, the steel mast was extended by six meters to make room for paging antennas, and a red-and-white fiberglass-reinforced plastic cylinder was added, bringing the height to approximately 375 meters. Subsequent modifications, including the replacement of the old analog television antenna with a new digital DVB-T antenna in 2007, resulted in the current height of 366 meters. The Gerbrandy Tower comprises 25 floors and is supported by 132 foundation piles, each 12 meters long. The concrete section of the tower weighs 8,000 tons and has an internal diameter of 10.3 meters. The steel tube mast, with a diameter of 2 meters and a weight of 235 tons, is stabilized by guy wires anchored to three 1,000-ton guy blocks. The transition to digital broadcasting has also been a significant development for the Gerbrandy Tower. One of the most beloved traditions associated with the Gerbrandy Tower is its transformation into a giant Christmas tree during the holiday season. The illuminated tower can be seen from the A2, A12, and A27 highways, and in favorable weather conditions, it is visible from over 30 kilometers away. The Christmas tree on the Gerbrandy Tower is the largest in the world, second only to the tree created by the residents of Gubbio in Umbria, Italy. Over the years, the tradition has faced challenges, including technical issues, high costs, and adverse weather conditions, but the community's determination has ensured its continuation. In recent years, the lighting system was upgraded to energy-efficient LED lights, and light shows synchronized with Christmas music have added to the festive atmosphere. The Gerbrandy Tower is used for directional radio services and for FM- and TV-broadcasting, the Gerbrandy Tower consists of a concrete tower with a height of 100 meters on which a guyed aerial mast is mounted. Its total height was originally 382.5 meters, but in 1987 it was reduced to 375 meters.On August 2nd, 2007 its analog antenna was replaced by a digital one reducing its height by another 9 meters. Its height is now 366.8 meters.Towers of this type do not fit well in existing classification hierarchies of free standing tower antennas or guyed masts since they incorporate elements from both. If the structure is counted as a tower, it is the tallest tower in Western Europe. The Gerbrandy Tower is not the only tower which consists of a concrete tower on which a guyed mast is set. There are at least two similar but smaller towers with the same structure. One is the radio tower of Zendstation Smilde (Netherlands), which consisted of an 80 meter high concrete tower, on which a 223.5 meter high guyed mast was mounted. This structure collapsed after a fire on July 15th, 2011. It is unclear whether the tower is to be restored to its original height and design. Another example is Waldenburg TV Tower with a total height of 145 meters. During the Christmas season lamps are put on the guys and make the tower the biggest Christmas tree in the world. There were plans to limit these decorations to once every 5 years, but sponsoring has allowed the seasonal lighting to be put up every year so far. However, during Christmas 2006 there were no decorations on the mast.Due to the repairs after the fire on July 15th 2011, the lights were limited to the white light on the top and searchlights at the base.The tower is named after Pieter Gerbrandy, prime minister of the Netherlands during World War Two.Nearby, there are two other remarkable masts, the mast of mediumwave transmitter Lopik and KNMI-mast Cabauw, a mast used for meteorological measurements. On July 15th, 2011 there was a small fire in the Gerbrandy tower. Only hours later, a similar tower in Smilde caught fire and collapsed, after which all transmitters in the Gerbrandy tower were shut down as a precaution, leaving large parts of the Netherlands without FM-radio and digital TV (DVB-T) reception.
