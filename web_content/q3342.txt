Romesh Thapar (1922–1987) was an Indian journalist, editor, and political commentator renowned for founding and leading the influential monthly journal Seminar, which from 1959 offered thematic explorations of India's evolving social, political, and economic challenges through commissioned essays and debates.[1][2] Born in Lahore to Lieutenant-General Daya Ram Thapar, he began his career editing the left-leaning Cross Roads journal in the late 1940s, whose circulation ban by the Madras government prompted the landmark 1950 Supreme Court case Romesh Thapar v. State of Madras, which expanded protections for freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a) by striking down pre-censorship orders not directly threatening public order.[3][4][5]Thapar's editorial tenure at Seminar—for which he received the Press Foundation of Asia's Magazine Editor of the Year award in 1977—positioned it as a platform for alternative journalism, fostering critical discourse amid India's post-independence nation-building, including critiques of policy failures in security, science, and development.[2][6] As a left-wing intellectual, he contributed to public committees, such as one under Indira Gandhi evaluating media ethics, and commented on strategic issues like India's nuclear debates and China relations, often advocating pragmatic realism over ideological rigidity.[7][8] His communist affiliations, including early ties to the undivided Communist Party of India, drew scrutiny during Cold War-era intelligence assessments, though his work emphasized empirical analysis of India's modernization struggles rather than partisan dogma.[9] Thapar also served as director of the India International Centre, amplifying intellectual exchanges until his death.[10] Early Life and Education Family Background and Formative Years Romesh Thapar was born in 1922 in Lahore, then part of British India's Punjab province, into a Punjabi Hindu family of the Khatri caste.[3] His father, Lieutenant GeneralDaya Ram Thapar (1894–1965), held the CIE and OBE honors for service in the British Indian Army, eventually rising to Director-General of the Indian Medical Services after independence.[3][11] The family's circumstances reflected the mobility of military households, with postings across British India influencing early relocations and stability. Thapar had siblings including a sister Bimla and younger sister Romila Thapar (born 1931), whose own childhood involved schooling in multiple Indian cities tied to their father's assignments.[11]As a child in Punjab during the 1920s and 1930s, Thapar experienced the intensifying Indian independence movement, with Lahore serving as a hub for political agitation including non-cooperation campaigns and later communal tensions.[3] The 1947 Partition of India, which divided Punjab and led to mass migrations, directly impacted his family's Lahore origins, contributing to displacement effects on Hindu communities from the region and reshaping familial networks in the newly formed India.[12] These events, occurring as Thapar entered adulthood, underscored the era's disruptions to regional stability and personal ties for Punjabi Hindu families like his own. Initial schooling occurred in India amid these transitions, prior to overseas education.[12] Journalistic Career Early Publications and Crossroads Romesh Thapar founded and edited the weekly English-language journal CrossRoads, launching its first issue on 29 April 1949 from Bombay as a venue for incisive political commentary with pronounced communist leanings, despite Thapar and his wife Raj not being formal members of the Communist Party.[13][14] The publication emphasized Marxist-influenced critiques of the Nehru-led Congress government's policies, including sharp condemnations of incidents such as the 1949 killings of 22 communist prisoners in custody, positioning it as an early voice against perceived authoritarian tendencies in the post-independence state.[15][16]Content in CrossRoads targeted the administration's foreign policy and domestic handling of left-wing dissent, with articles like "Criminal"—penned by historian Romila Thapar and published on 10 June 1949—exemplifying the journal's bold scrutiny of official actions, which prompted a ban on its circulation in Bombay shortly thereafter.[14][17] This reflected Thapar's intent to foster unrestricted debate on governance failures amid India's fragile democratic transition, where communist activities faced suppression following the party's outlawing in several states.[18]In response to escalating regional restrictions, the Madras government enacted a ban on CrossRoads' entry and circulation on 1 March 1950 under Section 9(1-A) of the Madras Maintenance of Public Order Act, 1949, justifying it as necessary to avert threats to public tranquility arising from the journal's policy critiques.[19][20] Thapar countered in a subsequent editorial titled "Madras Ban on Cross Roads must go!", labeling the measure a "fascist attack" by Congress authorities and underscoring the irony of censorship in a newly sovereign nation committed to fundamental rights.[20] This confrontation highlighted nascent post-Partition efforts by provincial governments to curb dissenting publications, setting the stage for Thapar's broader journalistic resistance to state overreach without formal party affiliation.[21] Founding and Role at Seminar Romesh Thapar co-founded the monthly magazine Seminar in September 1959 alongside his wife Raj Thapar, launching it initially in Bombay before relocating operations to New Delhi for a less commercial intellectual environment.[22][23] The venture began as a modest, family-run enterprise modeled partly on earlier alternative journals, aiming to create a dedicated space for probing India's evolving challenges through curated symposia rather than routine news coverage.[23]As founder-editor, Thapar provided the editorial vision and operational drive, structuring each issue around a singular theme framed by a provocative question and addressed via commissioned essays from intellectuals, academics, politicians, and experts.[1][23] He maintained hands-on control over content selection and production, serving as a rigorous taskmaster who demanded high standards, rejected inadequate submissions without hesitation, and actively scouted emerging talent to contribute.[1] Under his leadership, the magazine operated from a small office in Delhi's Connaught Place, with family members assisting in distribution, and sustained itself through a lean budget backed by consistent advertising from long-term supporters.[1]Thapar's oversight emphasized aggregating contrasting perspectives to stimulate debate among India's elite thinkers during the early phases of post-independence nation-building, positioning Seminar as an independent forum insulated from mainstream media pressures.[1][23] This approach relied on his persuasive recruitment of contributors and Raj Thapar's organizational tact to navigate logistical hurdles, ensuring the publication's continuity until their deaths in 1987.[22][23] Editorial Style and Key Themes Thapar's editorial approach in Seminar emphasized thematic depth through curated collections of long-form essays, fostering debate on contemporary Indian issues rather than superficial reporting.[1] Each monthly issue centered on a singular motif, drawing contributions from intellectuals to explore facets such as caste politics and social transformation, with an underlying commitment to alternative perspectives outside mainstream narratives.[24] This style privileged discursive pluralism, often prioritizing interpretive consensus among progressive contributors over rigorous empirical scrutiny of policy outcomes, as evident in compilations like Tribe, Caste and Religion in India, which framed tribal integration as aligning with urban liberal attitudes while downplaying data on persistent cultural divergences.[25][26]Recurring themes in Thapar's oversight of Seminar included secular frameworks for social cohesion, portraying tribe-caste dynamics as barriers to modernization resolvable through state-orchestrated progressive reforms rather than market incentives or decentralized solutions.[27] His columns in the Economic and Political Weekly, such as "Capital View," critiqued economic policies from a statist vantage, advocating interventionist models amid India's developmental challenges while critiquing peripheral distractions under governments like Gulzarilal Nanda's in 1965.[28] This reflected a preference for centralized planning, as in references to India's "forced march" to modernity, which underscored accelerated state-led industrialization over liberalized alternatives, though such views aligned with the periodical's left-leaning editorial slant prone to overlooking fiscal inefficiencies.[29][30]Thapar's prose maintained a formal, exhortative tone, urging intellectual engagement with dissent and free speech as bulwarks against authoritarianism, yet often embedded assumptions of elite-guided consensus on secular progressivism without countering them with quantitative assessments of social policy efficacy.[13] In economic discourse, he favored critiques of policy missteps—such as foreign interventions diverging from non-alignment—while endorsing domestic statism, eschewing endorsements of private enterprise reforms that empirical data later validated for growth acceleration.[31] This thematic consistency shaped Seminar as a forum for causal narratives linking underdevelopment to structural inequities addressable via ideological statecraft, though sources like EPW archives reveal selective emphasis on critiques over balanced causal analysis.[28] Political Views and Engagements Ideological Affiliations Thapar's ideological leanings were rooted in Marxism, with early associations to the Communist Party of India (CPI) through his editorship of Crossroads from 1949 to 1951, which functioned as a CPI-aligned weekly despite his lack of formal membership in the party.[32] By the mid-1950s, he had broken from strict communist party discipline, transitioning toward a more independent stance that retained leftist sympathies without organizational commitment to the CPI.[33]Post-1964, following the CPI split, Thapar affiliated with the Communist Party of India (Marxist) (CPI(M)), aligning with its emphasis on orthodox Marxism and criticism of revisionism within the parent CPI, though records indicate no formal membership in either communist faction.[33] This affiliation reflected his enduring preference for radical leftist frameworks over moderate socialism, as evidenced by his known Marxist orientation in intellectual circles.[34]Thapar also engaged informally with Nehru-era elites and proponents of India's non-aligned movement, including policymakers and intellectuals who advocated pragmatic foreign policy independence from Cold War blocs, positioning him within broader discussions on global strategy during the 1950s and 1960s.[35] These ties, forged through journalistic networks rather than party structures, underscored his role in leftist discourse without binding partisan loyalty. Commentary on Indian Governments Thapar expressed qualified support for Jawaharlal Nehru's post-independence vision of democratic socialism and secular nation-building, yet consistently opposed encroachments on civil liberties under his administration. In 1949, his Bombay-based journal Crossroads published articles critiquing the Congress Party and the Madras government's handling of communal tensions, prompting the state to ban its circulation under the Madras Maintenance of Public Order Act to prevent threats to public order.[19] This action exemplified Thapar's broader contention that Nehru's government prioritized state security over free expression, as evidenced by the Supreme Court's 1950 ruling in Romesh Thapar v. State of Madras, which invalidated the ban and emphasized that prior restraints on speech required explicit constitutional limits rather than vague public order concerns.[13]Thapar's critiques intensified during Indira Gandhi's premiership, particularly amid the centralization of power and economic policies that he viewed as eroding institutional autonomy. He suspended publication of Seminar in July 1976 during the Emergency (1975–1977), citing imposed pre-censorship as incompatible with independent discourse, a move that halted the magazine after 17 years of operation despite its prior tolerance under less restrictive conditions.[36] Post-Emergency and following Gandhi's 1984 assassination, Thapar assessed her tenure as leaving "nothing of redeeming value," attributing this to autocratic governance that prioritized personal rule over sustainable reforms, including the 1971 bank nationalizations and 1975 Emergency declarations which, while aimed at curbing opposition, resulted in widespread arrests (over 100,000 detentions) and suppressed dissent without resolving underlying economic stagnation—evidenced by India's GDP growth averaging 3.2% annually from 1966 to 1977 amid persistent food shortages and inflation spikes exceeding 20% in 1973–1974.[37][38] In 1985, he noted public relief at the departure of her confrontational political style, which had fostered division rather than consensus.[39]The 1977 Janata Party victory elicited initial optimism from Thapar, who resumed Seminar's publication amid nationwide euphoria over the defeat of Congress authoritarianism, with voter turnout reaching 62% and Janata securing 295 Lok Sabha seats on promises of restoring democratic norms.[40] However, his "Capital View" columns soon documented the coalition's internal fractures, such as ideological clashes between socialist factions led by George Fernandes and market-oriented elements under Charan Singh, which empirically undermined governance—manifest in policy paralysis, with the government failing to repeal key Emergency-era laws like the Maintenance of Internal Security Act until mid-1978 and collapsing by July 1979 after 28 months due to withdrawal of support by 71 MPs from the Bharatiya Lok Dal.[41] Thapar highlighted these divisions as causal to the Janata's inability to translate anti-Congress sentiment into coherent reforms, contrasting the regime's early symbolic gains (e.g., release of political prisoners) with its operational disarray.[42] Legal Battles Romesh Thapar v. State of Madras Case In September 1949, the Government of Madras issued an order under Section 9(1-A) of the Madras Maintenance of Public Order Act, 1949, prohibiting the entry and circulation of the journal Cross Roads, edited by Romesh Thapar, on the grounds that a specific issue containing criticism of the central government's Kashmir policy was likely to disturb public order.[4][13] Thapar filed a petition in the Supreme Court of India in March 1950 under Article 32, contending that the ban infringed his fundamental right to freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a), as the right encompassed not only publication but also dissemination and circulation of ideas.[4][5]The state defended the order by arguing that the Act's provisions for maintaining public order fell within the permissible restrictions under Article 19(2), which at the time limited curbs on speech to those "in the interests of the security of the State."[4] Thapar countered that "public order" as invoked by the Act extended beyond the narrower constitutional ground of state security, amounting to an overbroad prior restraint without legislative sanction under Article 19(2).[5][13]On 26 May 1950, a five-judge bench of the Supreme Court, led by Chief Justice Hiralal Kania, unanimously quashed the ban, holding that freedom of speech under Article 19(1)(a) includes the right to propagate ideas through circulation, and that the state's action constituted unconstitutional pre-censorship.[4][5] The Court narrowed the scope of "public order" to align strictly with threats to the security of the State, reasoning that only disruptions involving grave and immediate dangers—such as those endangering the state's existence—could justify restrictions, not mere tendencies to incite disorder or affect tranquility without existential peril.[5][13] This interpretation invalidated the Act's application, as the impugned article posed no such fundamental threat.[5]The ruling prompted a legislative override: on 18 June 1951, Parliament enacted the Constitution (First Amendment) Act, which expanded Article 19(2) to explicitly permit "reasonable restrictions" on speech in the interests of, inter alia, public order, thereby restoring state authority to impose bans for lesser disturbances like those alleged in Thapar's case.[13][4] This amendment established a precedent for constitutional alterations to counter judicial limits on executive power over expression, influencing subsequent sedition and public order jurisprudence by broadening permissible grounds beyond existential security threats.[13] Institutional Roles Leadership at Indian International Centre Romesh Thapar assumed the directorship of the India International Centre (IIC) in 1967, succeeding D.L. Mazumdar and serving until 1973.[43] The IIC, founded in 1959 as an autonomous institution to promote international understanding through cultural and intellectual exchanges, operated under Thapar's guidance as a neutral forum for diplomatic dialogues and academic discourse, distinct from governmental influence. His appointment by the Board of Trustees capitalized on his prior experience in journalism and international relations, enabling the Centre to attract distinguished visitors and organize events that bridged Indian perspectives with global viewpoints, particularly emphasizing East-West interactions amid Cold War dynamics.[43]Thapar's leadership emphasized programs on international affairs, including seminars and lectures that explored themes of economic development, geopolitical strategy, and non-alignment, aligning with India's foreign policy of strategic autonomy while prioritizing pragmatic assessments over ideological dogma. These activities hosted policymakers, scholars, and envoys from diverse nations, fostering causal analyses of development challenges such as industrialization and resource allocation, though 1970s, though precise attendance figures for Thapar's tenure remain undocumented in primary archives.[43] Personal Life Marriage and Family Dynamics Romesh Thapar married Raj Malhotra in 1945; both Thapar's death in 1987, with Raj outliving him by over a decade, during which she documented aspects of their shared experiences in her memoir.[48] Writings and Bibliography Major Publications and Contributions Thapar authored India in Transition in 1956, a 272-page analysis of India's post-independence political and governmental shifts, published by Current Book House in Bombay.[49][50] This work drew on his experiences, including as secretary to the Indian delegation at the United Nations, to examine early challenges in nation-building.[51]Through his role as founder-editor of the --- [A Supreme Court ruling that continues to guardrail free speech in India] ---
In the summer of 1950, across Madras, copies of the weekly CrossRoads were quietly pulled off shelves — an article on police violence that killed 22 Communists in a Salem prison was deemed too subversive by the State. The Madras government had invoked the Madras Maintenance of Public Order Act to ban the sale and distribution of the magazine in the state. The editor of CrossRoads, Romesh Thapar, moved the Supreme Court, perhaps bullish about the new Constitution that guaranteed a remedy for the enforcement of fundamental rights, including the right to freedom of speech and expression. His faith in the new Republic and the ideals on which the Constitution was framed wasn’t misplaced. On May 26, 1950, a full bench of the Supreme Court ruled in his favour and Romesh Thapar v State of Madras became the first landmark ruling protecting free speech and limiting arbitrary use of State power. It is, till today, a widely recognised precedent for protection of a free press against State censorship. A crucial aftereffect of the ruling was the First Amendment to the Constitution that placed more limits on free speech. Five of the six judges of the Supreme Court — who in those days sat together for every case — held the Madras Maintenance of Public Order Act, under which Thapar’s magazine was banned, as “wholly unconstitutional and void”. The court said that unless a law was specifically conceived to address “security of the state”, a general, wide law could not obstruct free speech on such grounds. Justice Fazl Ali was the sole dissenter who said that the law was constitutional. Born in 1922 in Lahore, Thapar was the son of Lieutenant-General Daya Ram Thapar, CIE, Order of the British Empire, who served as the Director-General of the British Indian Armed Forces Medical Services. Thapar initially worked with The Times of India and was one of only two Indians, along with Frank Moraes, at editorial rank at that time. He then moved to London to set up the India offices of the Free Press Journal, the first Indian news agency abroad, before returning to India, a few months before Independence. On 29 April, 1949, the month his daughter Malavika was born, Thapar started CrossRoads. The first edition of the weekly had articles by some of the biggest names of the day, including Mulk Raj Anand, Balraj Sahni and Pablo Neruda. Although Thapar’s association with the Communist Party was well known, along with his weekly dinners at the party’s headquarters at Sandhurst Road in Bombay, the magazine itself had no formal association with the party. It was, however, “strongly anti-Congress”, writes his wife Raj Thapar in her memoir, All These Years. (Express Archives) With its “anti-Congress” and “civil liberties” articles, CrossRoads soon ran into trouble with Congress governments in states. Months before the Madras ban, the magazine was banned in Bombay, where it was published under the Bombay Public Security Measures Act. Then too, Thapar had moved court. In September 1949, the Bombay High Court took a sympathetic view of his case, with then Chief Justice of the High Court M C Chagla holding that “two views are possible” on the issue — the government’s cross-objections against CrossRoads were “dismissed with costs”. In her book, Raj notes that the Bombay ban came partly due to the article’s title, ‘Criminal’, which, she says, was given by Thapar’s sister, the historian Romila Thapar, who was then a college student helping her brother. When summoned by the Press Advisory Board, Thapar had refused to tender an apology. “He stood his ground on the right of a journalist to use whatever language he chose,” his wife writes. Story continues below this ad However, the ban in Madras, the second for the magazine in five months, pushed Thapar to move the Supreme Court. Thapar’s case was that powers under the Madras Maintenance of Public Order Act to ban his magazine on the grounds of maintaining “public order” were an excessive restriction on the freedom of expression guaranteed under Article 19 of the Constitution. He did not see it merely as an attack on his publication but as a direct assault on India’s fledgling democracy. Well-known Madras-based lawyer C R Pattabhi Raman argued for CrossRoads before the top court. The six-judge bench that delivered the ruling in Romesh Thapar v State of Madras said, “So long as the possibility of its (the Madras law) being applied for purposes not sanctioned by the Constitution cannot be ruled out, it must be held to be wholly unconstitutional and void.” The court’s view was that while a restriction on free speech is constitutionally sanctioned on the grounds that it impinges on the “security of the state”, public order cannot be construed as a ‘security of the state’ issue. Romesh Thapar vs State of Madras, 1950 Incidentally, Communist ideology found an unlikely comrade that day. On the same day as the Thapar verdict, the Supreme Court delivered a verdict in Brij Bhushan v State of Delhi, over a similar censorship of the Organiser, the mouthpiece of the RSS. It was an article in the magazine on riots in East Pakistan that had led to its ban in Delhi. Veteran lawyer N C Chatterjee, father of former Lok Sabha Speaker Somnath Chatterjee, argued for the RSS. The verdict was short since the judges had given their reasoning in Thapar’s case. Stung by these verdicts that went against the State’s power to curb free speech, the government eventually amended the law on free speech, leading to The Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951, which came into effect on June 18, 1951. Besides other consequential changes to the Constitution — exempting land reforms from scrutiny, providing protections for backward classes, among others — the amendment introduced ‘reasonable restrictions’ on the right to free speech, including “public order”, “incitement to an offence” and “friendly relations with foreign states” as grounds for restricting free speech. On the significance of the ruling, former Supreme Court Justice B N Srikrishna told The Indian Express, “If you look at the early days of the Supreme Court, especially in the 50s and 60s, the Supreme Court led the civil liberties movement in India. The Romesh Thapar ruling is from that era, where the court does not grandstand but simply stands guard.” Thapar’s sister Romila, Professor Emeritus at Jawaharlal Nehru University, said that though her “memories of events at that time are not too clear”, her brother took the “courageous decision to go to court”. Valmik Thapar, the man who chronicled India's Tigers, passes away Thapar dedicated his life to the study and conservation of tigers, particularly in Rajasthan's Ranthambore National Park Valmik Thapar, a towering figure in the field of tiger conservation in India, passed away at the age of 73 at his Kautilya Marg residence in New Delhi on Saturday morning (May 31). According to reports, he was diagnosed with cancer last year. Thapar was born in New Delhi in 1952, to Romesh and Raj Thapar, journalists and co-founders of the political journal Seminar. He is survived by his wife, Sanjana Kapoor and son, Hamir Thapar. He dedicated his life to the study and conservation of tigers, particularly in Rajasthan's Ranthambore National Park. He co-founded the Ranthambhore Foundation in 1988, a non-governmental organisation focused on community-based conservation efforts. Early life and education Thapar's father, Romesh Thapar, was a well-known journalist. His aunt is historian Romila Thapar, and his cousin is journalist Karan Thapar. Till his last days, Thapar remained passionate about tiger conservation.
