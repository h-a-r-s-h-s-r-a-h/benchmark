--- [91 Aegina - Wikipedia] ---
91 Aegina (from Latin Aegīna, Aegīnēta) is a large main-belt asteroid. It was discovered by French astronomer Édouard Jean-Marie Stephan on 4 November 1866. It was his second and final asteroid discovery. The first was 89 Julia. The asteroid's name comes from Aegina, a Greek mythological figure associated with the island of the same name.
This body is orbiting the Sun with a period of 4.17 years and an eccentricity of 0.105. The orbit of this object brings it to within 4.9 Gm of the dwarf planet Ceres, and the resulting gravitational interaction has been used to produce mass estimates of the latter. The cross-section size of the asteroid is 110 km and it has a rotation period of six hours. The surface coloring of 91 Aegina is very dark and this C-type asteroid has probably a primitive carbonaceous composition. Observation of absorption bands at wavelengths of 0.7 and 3 μm indicate the presence of hydrated minerals and/or ice grains on the surface.

--- [Aegina – Phoibe] ---
Aegina is asteroid #91 and of the type CP. Aegina’s orbit is characterized by a semi-major axis of 2.59 Astronomical Units, an eccentricity of 0.10, a period of revolution of 1 523 days, and an inclination of 2.1 degrees. Aegina was discovered by the French astronomer Édouard Jean-Marie Stephan at the Marseilles Observatory (Observatoire de Marseille). Aegina was his second and last asteroid discovery. His first was the asteroid Julia. Aegina is named after Aígina (Αἴγινα), a naiad from Greek mythology. Naiad means a nymph, who guards a source of freshwater. Aígina (Αἴγινα) is a daughter of the nymph Metṓpē (Μετώπη) and her husband, the river god Asōpós (Ἀσωπός). Zeús (Ζεύς) fell in love with her. So he did what lovers do: He turned into an eagle and abducted Aígina (Αἴγινα). Zeús (Ζεύς) took her to an island, that was known as Oinone, but later named after Aígina (Αἴγινα). It is located in the Saronic Gulf between Attica and the Peloponnesos. Sisyphos (Σίσυφος) watched the abduction and told Asōpós (Ἀσωπός) about it. Although this only calmed the worries of a father, Zeús (Ζεύς) disliked this and this could be the reason why death came to visit Sisyphos (Σίσυφος). But the tradition is unclear in this point. Asōpós (Ἀσωπός) at least rewarded Sisyphos (Σίσυφος) with a freshwater well on his land. Aígina (Αἴγινα) by Zeús (Ζεύς) became the mother of Aiakós (Αἰακός), who then became the king of the island. His people were the Myrmidons, who were created by Zeús (Ζεύς) from ants because Aiakós (Αἰακός) grew up in loneliness because the island was uninhabited previous to the abduction. Aígina (Αἴγινα) had more children, although different authors have different opinions about this. Possible candidates for more children of Aígina (Αἴγινα) are Damokrateia (Δαμοκράτεια), a daughter of Zeús (Ζεύς), Menoítios (Μενοίτιος), a son of Áktōr (Ἄκτωρ), and least likely a Sinope (Σινώπη), who is a daughter of Árēs (Ἄρης). Least likely because Sinope (Σινώπη) is the name of a sister of Aígina (Αἴγινα), who has several siblings. At least two brothers and eleven sisters, maybe up to nineteen sisters. Aígina (Αἴγινα) is translated as goat island. Aígina (Αἴγινα) seems to be quite irrelevant except in connecting other tales. Her name could remind at the past of the island, that originally was uninhabited. Maybe only goats were known to live there before some people settled down there. Aegina at least is a place name asteroid, that hints to the island Aígina (Αἴγινα) in the Saronic Gulf. Aegina also could hint to uninhabited islands in general or to flocks of goats (this too for determining a place). Aegina could represent first settlers in a lonely place.


--- [Édouard Stephan] ---
Édouard Jean-Marie Stephan (31 August 1837 – 31 December 1923) was a French astronomer. His surname is sometimes spelled Stéphan in some literature, but this is apparently erroneous.
He was born in Sainte Pezenne (today one of the districts of the town of Niort) and attended the Ecole Normale Superieure, and graduated at the top of his class in 1862.
He was the director of the Marseille Observatory from 1864 to 1907 (until 1872 he was subordinate to Urbain le Verrier). In the early part of his career there, he had limited opportunities to do observations because he was preoccupied with improving the observatory. He discovered the asteroid 89 Julia in 1866. In 1867 he used the new telescope to observe a transit of Mercury.
Between 1870 and 1875, Stephan systematically studied nebulae, precisely recording their positions and discovering many new ones. His goal was to enable the exact measurement of stellar proper motions by creating a reference system of fixed objects.
In 1873, Stephan was the first person to attempt to measure the angular diameter of a star using interferometry, converting the 80 cm telescope at Marseille Observatory into an interferometer. He did this by obscuring the reflector with a mask containing two vertical slits.
The star he chose to perform this experiment was Sirius. He did not succeed in resolving any stellar disks, but by 1874 had obtained an upper limit to stellar diameters of 0.158" (the true angular diameter of Sirius is 0.0059 arcseconds, and for comparison, the angular diameter of Alpha Centauri and Betelgeuse are 0.0145 and 0.05 arcseconds respectively).
In 1881 he discovered NGC 5, and he discovered the galaxy NGC 6027 the following year using the 80 cm reflector.
Among others, he discovered Stephan's Quintet, also known as "Arp 319", a group of five galaxies. Stephan made this discovery with the first telescope equipped with a reflection coated mirror.
In 1884 the French Academy of Sciences awarded him the Valz Prize (Prix Valz). His name is associated with the periodic comet 38P/Stephan-Oterma, although Jérôme Coggia saw it first.
He became a Chevalier of the Légion d'honneur in 1868 and an Officier of the Légion d'honneur in 1879.
