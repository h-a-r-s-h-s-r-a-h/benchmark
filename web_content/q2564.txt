--- [In England, fire damages Blenheim Palace and destroys a priceless ...] ---
A morning fire at Blenheim Palace, the stately home of the Dukes of Marlborough near Oxford, broke out in the bakehouse in the north-eastern wing and quickly spread to the upper floors causing significant damage. No serious injuries were reported. The upper section of the destroyed wing contained the art gallery and an irreplaceable and complete casualty of the fire was the thirteen feet by seven feet Rubens masterpiece, "Rape of Prosperine." (By John Osborne)

FIRE AT BLENHEIM PALACE. Great excitement was caused in the city of Oxford early on the morning of the sth February, from a report that the magnificent palace of Blenheim was being destroyed by fire, and that many valuable curiosities that were known to be located in the various rooms were irretrievably lost. Of course rumour with her busy tongue had considerably increased the damage that was done, and we were not a little gratified upon visiting the spot to find that the fire had been wholly confined to the north-east portion of the building, containing part of the porter’s lodge, the bake-house, the land clerk’s room, the steward’s room, and the Titian-room, this portion of the building being separated from the palaoe by a fireproof room, and an open space used as a yard. The loss of the Titian pictures will be much regretted by all admirers of this celebrated master. They were a present from Victor A 'madeus, King of Sardinia, to John Duke of Marlborough, and remained unhung and hidden from public view, till the visit of Sir Joshua Reynolds to Blenheim in 1788. He was so forcibly struck with their beauty and value, that he recommended their being placed in the room they occupied, where they were displayed for the sake of amateurs in the pictorial art. The room, about twenty by thirty feet, was hung all round with them, and it was not known that around the walls were several recesses over which these pictures were covered, and a green curtain before them facilitated their destruction. The remains of some ancient inscriptions on the plaster of the recesses, commemorative of the hang, ing, have escaped the fury of the element. The following is a list of the pictures destroyed : —Mars and Venus, Cupid and Psyche, Apollo and Daphne, Pluto and Proserpine, Hercules and Dejanira, Vulcan and Ceres, Bacchus and Ariadne, Jupiter, Juno and 10, Neptune and Amphitrite, all painted on leather by Titian. The only other painting is one by Peter Paul Rubens —the Rape of Proserpine, 8 feet 8 inches high and 2 feet 1 inch wide, and was considered one of Rubens’ masterpieces. The whole family, xvith the exception of the duke, who had gone to London to attend to his parliamantary duties, were resident in the palace.
