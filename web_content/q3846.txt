--- [Manasbal Lake - Wikipedia] ---
Manasbal Lake is the deepest freshwater lake found in the Safapora area of Ganderbal district of  Jammu and Kashmir, India. Named after the sacred Manasarovar. The lake is encircled by four villages, viz., Jarokbal, Kondabal, Nesbal (situated on the north-eastern side of the lake), and Gratbal. It's renowned for its lotus blooms (Nelumbo nucifera) at the periphery of the lake (blooms during July and August) adds to the beauty of the clear waters of the lake. The Mughal garden, called the Jaroka Bagh (meaning bay window), built by Nur Jahan, overlooks the lake.
The lake is a good place for birdwatching as it is one of the largest natural spawning grounds of Aquatic birds in Kashmir and has the epithet "supreme gem of all Kashmir Lakes". The rootstocks of the lotus plant, which grows extensively in the lake, are harvested and marketed, and also eaten by the local people.

--- [Manasbal Lake: The Supreme Gem of Kashmir's Lakes - About] ---
Nestled in the serene folds of the Himalayas, Manasbal Lake shines like a jewel in the crown of Jammu and Kashmir’s natural beauty. Located in Ganderbal District, this majestic water body has earned the title of the deepest lake in India and serves as an ecological, historical, and cultural landmark that continues to captivate tourists, scholars, and nature lovers. Location and Surroundings Manasbal Lake lies at an elevation of approximately 1,585 meters (5,200 feet) above sea level, cradled between the Pir Panjal Range and the Greater Himalayas. Its geographical setting not only provides breathtaking views but also places it within the third series of Himalayan high-altitude lakes alongside Dal and Anchar Lakes. It is bordered by the Baladar Mountains in the east, the elevated Karewa Plateau in the north, and the Ahtung hills to the south, the latter known for limestone extraction. Surrounding the lake are three picturesque villages: Jarokbal, Kondabal (also called Kiln place), and Ganderbal. These villages add to the rustic charm and local cultural experience of the area, making Manasbal an immersive destination for travellers. Origin and Name The lake’s name is thought to be derived from the sacred Lake Manasarovar in Tibet, suggesting ancient spiritual connections that blend local belief with wider Himalayan traditions. Though exact dating is still uncertain, local lore points to Manasbal as a lake of antiquity, rooted deeply in Kashmir’s medieval landscape. Historical Significance Historical ruins along its shore tell stories of imperial grandeur and strategic importance. On the northern edge of the lake sits Jarogabagh, a fort built during the 17th century by Mughal rulers. This structure is said to have provided resting grounds for caravans travelling between Punjab and Srinagar. Overlooking the lake is the Mughal garden called Jaroka, commissioned by Empress Nur Jahan. With its bay-window design and manicured terraces, Jaroka reflects the aesthetic and architectural legacy of Mughal landscaping. Ecological Richness and Geography Manasbal Lake exemplifies the biodiversity and geological diversity of the Himalayan region. The lake’s ecosystem thrives amid lacustrine and fluviatile sediments and loessic deposits. Being part of the series of valley lakes, it occupies an ecological niche between the lower-altitude wetlands like Nilnag and glacial lakes higher up the range. In summer, the lake transforms into a tapestry of colours with the blooming of Nelumbo nucifera commonly known as the lotus. These flowers, concentrated around the lake’s edges in July and August, not only beautify the landscape but are integral to the local economy. The lotus rootstocks are harvested, sold in regional markets, and consumed in traditional Kashmiri cuisine. Birdwatching Paradise For bird lovers, Manasbal Lake is nothing short of a paradise. It is one of the largest natural habitats for aquatic birds in Jammu and Kashmir. Designated as the supreme gem of All Kashmir Lakes. it attracts a variety of migratory and native species, making it a hotspot for ornithological study and leisure birdwatching. The quiet shores and open waters create a sanctuary for species like herons, kingfishers, grebes, and cormorants. How to Visit Manasbal Lake is accessible by road from multiple directions. It’s a scenic 30-kilometre (19-mile) drive from Srinagar via Shadipora and Ganderbal, making it a convenient day trip for visitors to the capital. The route from Sonmarg also passes through the district, offering stunning mountain views. Additionally, the road to Wular Lake Kashmir’s largest lake goes via Safapora and includes Manasbal as a picturesque detour. Whether you’re an eco-tourism, a backpacker, a bird enthusiast, or someone seeking solace in nature, Manasbal Lake delivers a vivid experience. Conservation and Future Like many Himalayan ecosystems, Manasbal Lake faces environmental challenges due to tourism, pollution, and urban encroachment. Conservation efforts are underway to maintain water clarity, protect bird species, and manage lotus harvesting sustainably. The lake stands as a reminder of nature’s delicate balance and the need to safeguard it. Conclusion Manasbal Lake is not just a geographical entity it’s a living mosaic of history, ecology, and local culture. From Mughal ruins and poetic lotus blooms to the chorus of aquatic birds, the lake offers a kaleidoscope of experiences. Its location, beauty, and significance make it an essential chapter in the story of Kashmir’s natural wonders. If you ever find yourself near Ganderbal with a few hours to spare, make your way to Manasbal Lake. Whether you choose to sit quietly by the water’s edge or immerse yourself in birdwatching and photography, the lake will leave an impression that’s as deep as its waters.
