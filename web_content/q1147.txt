--- [India national cricket team - Wikipedia] ---
The India men's national cricket team represents India in international cricket. It is governed by the Board of Control for Cricket in India (BCCI) and is a full member nation of the International Cricket Council with Test, One Day International and Twenty20 International status. India are the current holders of the T20 World Cup, the ICC Champions Trophy and the Asia Cup.
The team has played 598 Test matches, winning 185, losing 188, with 224 draws and 1 tie. As of August 2025, India is ranked fourth in the ICC Men's Test Team Rankings with 107 rating points. India have played in two of the three World Test Championship finals, finishing runners-up in 2021 and 2023.
The Indian team has played 1,075 ODI matches, winning 571, losing 450, tying 10 and with 44 ending in a no-result. As of September 2025, India is ranked first in the ICC Men's ODI Team Rankings with 124 rating points. India have appeared in the World Cup final four times and have won the title twice. They have also won the Champions Trophy a record three times.
The national team has played 275 Twenty20 International matches, winning 185, losing 75, tying 7 and with 8 ending in a no-result. As of August 2025, India is ranked first in the ICC Men's T20I Team Rankings with 271 rating points and also won the T20 World Cup twice.

India’s cricketing history is so rich. The Britishers brought along the sport to our shores, and we hung on to it like it was ours more than anyone else. And no wonder we have a mad following for the gentleman’s game. Cricket has seen an astronomical rise in India from pre-independence days to now. In between, we witnessed India’s first-ever match, first-ever win, first-ever centurion, and many such firsts. These firsts are so special, which are now part of history. So, in this article, we’ll look at the firsts for India’s men’s team in cricket history. First Match Test: India played their first-ever Test match in June 1932, when the country was still under British rule. The debut Test unfolded at the iconic Lord’s Cricket Ground against England, though India lost the match by 158 runs. ODI: Thirty-two years later, India made their ODI debut against the same opposition. The match took place on July 13, 1974, at Headingley, with England beating India by four wickets. T20I: India’s first T20I appearance came against South Africa on December 1, 2006. In T20Is, though, the Men in Blue started on a winning note, securing a six-wicket victory in Johannesburg. First Cap Test: Amar Singh was the first Indian player to get the first Test cap. He was part of India’s debut Test, where he shone with the ball and bat, taking four wickets and scoring India’s first Test fifty. ODI: Syed Abid Ali was the first Indian to receive the first ODI cap during India’s maiden match. T20I: In the shortest format, Ajit Agarkar donned the first cap in the match against South Africa in 2006. It was a short stint for Agarkar in T20Is. First Win (Match) Test: India had to wait almost 20 years to taste their first Test win. And it came against England at the Chepauk Stadium in Chennai in February 1952. It was the last Test of the five-match series, with England leading it by 1-0. But India stunned the visitors by wrapping them twice on 266 and 183 while declaring their innings on 457 for nine. Vinoo Mankad (12 wickets), Polly Umrigar (130*), and Pankaj Roy (111) were the chief architects as India beat England by an innings and eight runs. Vijay Hazare became the first captain to register India’s first Test win. ODI: In the 50-over format, though, the wait was pretty short. After losing their first three ODIs against England, India sneaked their first win against now-defunct East Africa. The match was part of the inaugural ODI World Cup, with East Africa opting to bat first. India bowled them out for 120 and then handed them a drubbing by 10 wickets. S. Venkataraghavan was the man-in-charge of India then. T20I: India started their T20I journey straight away with a win. They defeated South Africa in the one-off match played in 2006. South Africa mustered 126 for nine in their 20 overs, which India chased down with a ball and six wickets to spare. Virender Sehwag guided India to their first T20I win. First Captain Test: Colonel C. K. Nayudu became India’s first Test captain after a lot of back and forth. In fact, he wasn’t supposed to lead India in their debut Test, as only members from Royal families used to captain the team. But the designated captain, Maharaja of Porbandar, and vice-captain, K. S. G. Limbdi, backed out and let Nayudu lead the team. ODI: Ajit Wadekar was the first captain of India in ODI cricket. He stood as skipper in India’s first two matches. T20I: Virender Sehwag was India’s first T20I captain, leading the team in their debut match. That was the only T20I match Sehwag stood in as a skipper. First Series Win (Home & Away) Test: 1952 was a remarkable year for India as they not only won their first Test but also their first-ever Test series. India won the five-match series against Pakistan by 2-1 at home. India won the first and third matches, with Pakistan winning the second and the last two ending in draws. India had to further wait 16 years to taste their first overseas Test series victory. New Zealand hosted India for a four-match series in 1968, which the visitors started with a win. New Zealand staged a comeback in the second Test before India cleaned up the next two to register their first away series win. ODI: India played their first ODI series at home in 1981/82 and emerged as the winners. They hosted England for a three-match series, with each game scheduled a month apart. The visitors won the first match, but then India bounced back in the next two games to take the series 2-1. Two years later, India won the first overseas ODI series. This time they toured England for a two-match series, which ended 1-1. But India clinched the series based on the scoring rate. T20I: India’s first home T20I series win (involving two or more matches) came in 2016. The Men in Blue hosted Sri Lanka for a three-match series. Sri Lanka opened the series with a win, but then India dominated the visitors with two comprehensive victories to win the series 2-1. Before their home series victory, the Indian men had secured their first overseas series win back in 2010. India toured Zimbabwe for a two-match series and returned with a 2-0 victory. First World Cup Title Win ODI: India’s performance in the first two ODI World Cups was lacklustre. And they weren’t favourites either in the third edition in 1983. But the Indian men led by Kapil Dev shocked the whole world by defeating the West Indies in the summit clash. T20I: India won their first T20 World Cup back in the inaugural edition played in 2007. A young MS Dhoni led an inexperienced squad to the title victory by beating Pakistan in the final. ODI: India played their maiden ODI in 1974, but it took almost nine years for a batter to cross the 100-run barrier in an innings. Kapil Dev smashed the first ODI century for India in the 1983 World Cup. It was a whirlwind knock of 175 not out, which took India’s total from 9/4 to 266/8 against Zimbabwe. T20I: Suresh Raina scored the first T20I century for India. The southpaw batter brought up the three figures against South Africa in Gros Islet in the 2010 T20 World Cup. First Triple Century Virender Sehwag was the first-ever triple centurion for India in Test cricket. The former explosive opener surpassed the mark against Pakistan in 2004. Sehwag took 364 balls to reach the 300-run mark. First Five-Wicket Haul Test: Mohammad Nissar was a top performer in India’s debut Test match against England. In that match, the former pacer bagged the first five-wicket haul (5/93) for India in Test cricket. Nissar struck earlier in the first innings, reducing England to 11 for two. He then dismissed a set Les Ames, followed by Walter Robins and Freddie Brown to complete his five-for. ODI: Kapil Dev was the first one to take a fifer for India in ODIs, though it came before his hundred. The former captain registered the figures of five for 43 against Australia in the 1983 World Cup. Dev drew the first blood on 11 by uprooting Kepler Wessels’ stumps. He then removed Rod Marsh, Ken MacLeay, Tom Hogan, and Geoff Lawson towards the end. T20I: India’s first five-wicket haul in T20Is came after a decade of their debut in the format. Yuzvendra Chahal got the honour against England in Bengaluru in 2017. India posted 202 runs in the first innings, and then Chahal strangled the visitors by taking six wickets for 25 runs. First Hat-trick Test: Harbhajan Singh became the first bowler to take a Test hat-trick for India. He picked up three consecutive wickets against Australia in the memorable 2001 Kolkata Test. The former off-spinner removed Ricky Ponting, Adam Gilchrist, and Shane Warne in the 72nd over of Australia’s innings. Later, Harbhajan helped India win the match by 171 runs. ODI: Before Harbhajan, it was Chetan Sharma who first achieved the feat in international cricket. The former pacer bagged the first-ever hat-trick for India in ODI cricket. New Zealand were struggling 182 for five, and then Sharma shattered the stumps not once but three times in a row. He clean-bowled Ken Rutherford, Ian Smith, and Ewen Chatfield, registering the first hat-trick in the ODI World Cups. T20I: Deepak Chahar picked up the first T20I hat-trick for India. He completed the feat across two overs against Bangladesh in 2019. Chahar dismissed Shafiul Islam on the last ball of the 18th over. Cricket, often referred to as India’s religion, holds a special place in the hearts of millions across the nation. It is a sport that transcends boundaries, unites people, and fosters a sense of national pride. The journey of cricket in India has been dotted with remarkable milestones, one of which stands out prominently – the formation of the first-ever All-Indian cricket team under the leadership of Colonel CK Nayudu. This historic event not only marked a turning point in Indian cricket but also played a pivotal role in breaking down divisive barriers that had long plagued the sport. Divisions in Early 20th Century Cricket The early 20th century witnessed the nascent stages of Indian cricket, which was primarily organised and played along regional and communal lines. Teams were divided based on demography, religion, and ethnicity, reflecting the societal divisions prevalent at the time. These divisions not only hindered the growth of cricket as a sport but also perpetuated social cleavages. The lack of unity within the cricketing fraternity mirrored the broader disunity that India was grappling with on its path to independence. Colonel CK Nayudu: The Visionary Leader Amidst this backdrop of disunity emerged a visionary leader who would redefine the course of Indian cricket — Colonel CK Nayudu. A charismatic cricketer, Nayudu not only possessed exceptional skills on the field but also an unwavering belief in the power of unity. Born in 1895 in the princely state of Indore, Nayudu was an embodiment of the rich cultural diversity that India harboured. Nayudu recognised that the true potential of Indian cricket could only be unlocked if the divisions were set aside. He envisioned a united team that would represent the entire nation, transcending the confines of regionalism and religious biases. His dream was to bring together players from diverse backgrounds and create a team that would symbolise a united India. Challenges and Triumphs Creating the first All-Indian cricket team was no small feat. Nayudu faced a plethora of challenges, ranging from cultural prejudices to logistical hurdles. The Indian cricket landscape was riddled with scepticism and reluctance, as players were initially hesitant to abandon their local and communal teams. Moreover, colonial attitudes and a prevailing sense of inferiority had seeped into the minds of Indian cricketers, making the task of uniting them even more daunting. Nayudu’s efforts, however, proved to be relentless. He embarked on a journey to convince players of different backgrounds that the real victory lay in uniting for a common cause. His impassioned speeches, combined with his own personal example of rising above divisive lines, began to inspire cricketers to look beyond their differences. The turning point came in 1926 when Nayudu organised the Bombay Pentangular tournament — a competition that included teams based on religious lines. The tournament not only showcased Nayudu’s prowess as a cricketer but also highlighted the shortcomings of a divided cricketing structure. This event prompted a growing realisation among players that unity was the key to success, both on and off the field. Nayudu’s United Front: The Birth of All-Indian Cricket Team In 1932, Nayudu’s relentless pursuit of unity bore fruit as the first-ever All-Indian cricket team took the field. The team comprised players from various regions, religions, and backgrounds, thus symbolising the diverse fabric of the Indian subcontinent. This historic moment marked the end of an era defined by divisions and the beginning of a new chapter in Indian cricket. The All-Indian cricket team faced its maiden challenge against a formidable England side at Lord’s on June 25, 1932. India became the sixth team to be granted international test cricket status. The match was a resounding success, not just in terms of the cricket played but in the spirit it embodied — a spirit of unity, determination, and a collective aspiration for a better future. Although the match ended in a loss for India by 158 runs, the Indian team emerged victorious, showcasing the power of unity and resilience. The All-Indian cricket team’s journey didn’t end with that historic match; it marked the beginning of a legacy that would inspire generations of cricketers. The team’s unity set a precedent that future players would follow, and it played a significant role in shaping the narrative of Indian cricket as a force to be reckoned with on the global stage. Beyond the realm of cricket, the unity exhibited by the All-Indian cricket team had a profound impact on the collective consciousness of the nation. It shattered the illusion of divisiveness and sowed the seeds of national integration. Nayudu’s efforts transcended sports and contributed to the larger movement towards a united India that would eventually attain independence. The story of the first All-Indian cricket team led by Colonel CK Nayudu is more than just a sports tale; it is a testament to the power of unity in transcending barriers. Nayudu’s determination and leadership bridged the gaps that had divided Indian cricket along regional and religious lines. The journey from division to unity was not just about cricket; it was a microcosm of India’s struggle for cohesion and independence. As cricket continues to captivate the hearts of millions in India, the legacy of Nayudu and his team stands as a beacon of inspiration. The story reminds us that even in the face of deep-seated divisions, a shared vision and a resolute leader can unite a nation, not just on the cricket field but in the broader context of nation-building. The first All-Indian cricket team’s journey is a reminder that sports have the unique power to catalyse social change and sow the seeds of a better, more united future.
