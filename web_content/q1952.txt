--- [2019 Busy Bee Congo Dornier 228 crash - grokipedia.com] ---
The 2019 Busy Bee Congo Dornier 228 crash occurred on 24 November 2019, when a Dornier 228-200 twin-turboprop aircraft, registration 9S-GNH, operated by the Congolese airline Busy Bee Congo, crashed shortly after takeoff from runway 17 at Goma International Airport in Goma, Democratic Republic of the Congo.[1][2] The plane, en route to Beni Airport, failed to gain sufficient altitude, struck electrical wires, and impacted buildings in the densely populated Birere neighborhood, bursting into flames and causing widespread destruction.[1][3] Of the 22 people on board—20 passengers and 2 crew members—21 were killed, with only one passenger, Muma Emmanuel, surviving with minor injuries; additionally, 6 people on the ground perished, bringing the total death toll to 27.[1][4][5]The aircraft involved was a 35-year-old Dornier 228-200 (manufacturer serial number 8030), originally delivered in 1984 and previously operated by airlines including Olympic Aviation under the registration SX-BHC, during which it had sustained damage from striking power lines on approach to Athens-Ellinikon International Airport on 9 January 1994.[2][6][7] Busy Bee Congo, a small regional carrier based in Goma, primarily served short domestic routes in eastern Congo using a fleet of turboprops amid the country's challenging aviation infrastructure, marked by poor runways, security issues, and frequent incidents.[7] The flight had departed at approximately 09:08 local time (UTC+2) under visual meteorological conditions, with the crew reporting no initial emergency but requesting a turn back due to unstable flight characteristics, including wing rocking.[1][2]The accident prompted an immediate investigation by the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Bureau Permanent d'Enquêtes d'Accidents d'Aviation (BPEAA), with assistance from international experts; a preliminary report indicated a loss of control during the initial climb phase, possibly due to technical malfunction, overloading, or environmental factors such as the high-altitude airport (elevation 5,020 feet) and surrounding terrain, but no definitive cause was established at the time.[1] In the aftermath, the DRC Civil Aviation Authority suspended Busy Bee Congo's operations for safety audits, highlighting ongoing concerns about aviation safety in the region, where multiple crashes have occurred due to aging aircraft and inadequate maintenance.[7][3] The incident underscored the risks of air travel in conflict-affected areas like North Kivu province, where road alternatives are often unsafe.[5] Background Busy Bee Congo Busy Bee Congo was established in 2007 as a domestic charterairline headquartered at Goma International Airport in the Democratic Republic of the Congo.[8] The carrier primarily served regional routes within eastern DRC, connecting key locations such as Goma to Beni and Butembo amid the area's challenging infrastructure and security environment.[7] These operations catered to passengers and limited cargo needs in North Kivu province, where road travel is often restricted.[9]Prior to the 2019 accident, the airline's fleet comprised three Dornier 228 turboprop aircraft, including variants like the 228-100 and 228-200, suited for short-haul flights on unprepared airstrips common in the region.[10] Busy Bee Congo operated under ICAO airline code RHT and held Air Operator's Certificate (AOC) number AAC/DG/OPS-09/04, issued by the DRC's Autorité de l'Aviation Civile (AAC).[11][12]The airline faced regulatory scrutiny internationally, including a full ban on flights to European Union airspace due to deficiencies in safety oversight and airworthiness standards identified by the European Commission.[13] Domestically, Busy Bee Congo had encountered operational challenges; notably, on 21 June 2016, during a flight from Goma to Butembo, a Dornier 228-202 (registration 9Q-CSL) returned to Goma after its left main landing gear failed to extend, resulting in a runway excursion during landing at Goma International Airport with substantial damage but no injuries among the 13 occupants (11 passengers and 2 crew).[14] No fatal accidents were recorded prior to 2019.[15] Aircraft The aircraft involved in the crash was a Dornier 228-201, a twin-turboprop short takeoff and landing (STOL) utility transport manufactured by Dornier GmbH in Oberpfaffenhofen, Germany. Constructed in June 1984 with manufacturer's serial number 8030, it was originally delivered to Olympic Aviation under registration SX-BHC, during which it sustained damage from striking power lines on approach to Heraklion Airport in January 1994; it later operated under the Kenyan registration 5Y-BTU before being acquired by Busy Bee Congo and re-registered as 9S-GNH.[16][17]It was powered by two Garrett TPE331-5-252D turboprop engines. At the time of the accident on November 24, 2019, the airframe had accumulated 23,991 flight hours over 36,980 cycles.[1] The left engine recorded 21,458 hours and 22,800 cycles since new, including 3,331 hours and 3,560 cycles since its last overhaul; the right engine had 18,386 hours and 28,330 cycles since new, with just 0.3 hours and 1 cycle following a recent overhaul.[1]The Dornier 228-201 was certified for up to 19 passengers in a high-density configuration, though this aircraft was fitted with 18 seats, and its maximum takeoff weight was 5,760 kg against a basic empty mass of 3,496 kg.[1] It was not equipped with a flight data recorder or cockpit voice recorder.[1]Maintenance records indicated the right propeller had undergone overhaul in 2017, accruing 975 flight hours by the time of the accident; no prior defects or modifications specific to this airframe were documented in the lead-up to November 2019.[1] Crew The flight crew consisted of two pilots, with no cabin crew assigned to the short domestic route.[18][3]A witness at the crash site identified one of the pilots as Didier, who was heard calling for help amid the flames following impact.[3]No further public details are available regarding the crew members' ages, flight experience, type ratings on the Dornier 228, licensing status, medical certifications, training records, or rest and duty periods prior to the flight. Accident Flight details The flight was a domestic scheduled flight operated by Busy Bee Congo from Goma International Airport (FZNA) to Beni Airport (FZNP) in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, with a scheduled departure time of 09:08 local time on November 24, 2019.[19][1] The aircraft carried 17 passengers and 2 crew members, along with light cargo, and the total load was within the aircraft's operational weight limits of 5,760 kg maximum takeoff mass.[19][1]Weather conditions at Goma International Airport were generally favorable for departure, featuring CAVOK conditions with visibility of 9,000 meters, light winds of 8 knots from 180 degrees, scattered cumulonimbus clouds at 1,800 feet, and broken clouds at 30,000 feet, with temperatures around 25°C.[1]Air traffic control issued standard clearance for the flight to depart from Runway 17, and no delays were reported in the pre-departure sequence.[1] Pre-flight inspections, including fuel loading and aircraft systems verification, were conducted without noted anomalies, and the fuel truck was prepared routinely.[19][1] Takeoff and loss of control The Dornier 228-200 aircraft, operated by Busy Bee Congo, departed from runway 17 at Goma International Airport at 09:08 local time (07:08 UTC) on 24 November 2019, bound for Beni. The takeoff roll was reported as longer than normal by observers. The plane became airborne but achieved only a shallow initial climb, failing to gain significant altitude.[1]Shortly after liftoff, the air traffic controller in the tower observed the wings rocking as the aircraft struggled to maintain stability. The crew transmitted a radio call to air traffic control requesting permission to return to runway 35, but did not specify the reason for the emergency. Sources close to the incident indicated that the aircraft experienced an engine failure less than one minute after takeoff, leading to a sudden loss of power in one engine. This resulted in the plane yawing and banking uncontrollably, with the aircraft exhibiting an unstable attitude marked by rolling movements to the left and right.[1][2][3]Eyewitnesses on the ground described the aircraft as flying at very low height shortly after liftoff, appearing to spin or stall as it emitted smoke. One witness reported seeing the plane rotate three times in the air before it began descending toward the surrounding residential area. Ground observers noted the unstable rolling motion and the plane's inability to climb, consistent with the loss of control observed from the tower.[3] Crash sequence Following the loss of control, the Dornier 228-200 descended in an unstable attitude, rolling left and right while failing to gain significant altitude after departing runway 17 at Goma International Airport.[1] The aircraft's trajectory carried it southward over the densely populated Birere neighborhood, approximately 1 km from the runway end, where it became entangled with electrical wires at very low height before impacting the ground.[1][2]The plane struck an unfinished concrete house slab in a nose-low attitude at coordinates 1°41'16"S 29°14'38"E, with propeller wash also damaging a nearby wooden hut, resulting in a high-speed collision that caused the airframe to break apart upon impact.[1] This structural breakup scattered debris across the residential area near the airport perimeter.[1]Immediately after the impact, the aircraft burst into flames, with an intense post-crash fire igniting from the ruptured fuel tanks and engulfing the main wreckage in the Birere neighborhood.[1][2] The entire sequence from takeoff to the final impact lasted less than two minutes.[1] Investigation Official inquiry The official investigation into the 2019 Busy Bee Congo Dornier 228 crash was led by the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Bureau Permanent d'Enquêtes d'Accidents d'Aviation (BPEA), the national aviation accident investigation authority under the Ministry of Transport. The inquiry was formally opened on November 27, 2019, three days after the accident occurred on November 24. The crash site in the Birere neighborhood of Goma was secured by authorities on the same day to preserve evidence and restrict access.[1][19]International cooperation was incorporated from the outset, with technical assistance provided by Germany's Federal Bureau of Aircraft Accident Investigation (BFU), in line with International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) Annex 13 protocols for accidents involving aircraft of foreign design and manufacture. The BPEA coordinated these efforts to leverage expertise on the Dornier 228's systems. The scope of the investigation centered on determining the sequence of events, including potential engine performance issues, crew responses, and adherence to maintenance standards, while adhering to standard aviation safety protocols.Evidence collection formed a core part of the process, involving the recovery of wreckage from the impact site, where the aircraft had been largely destroyed by collision forces and a subsequent post-crash fire. Investigators gathered air traffic control recordings, which captured the aircraft's clearance for takeoff and initial climb, as well as interviews with eyewitnesses in the vicinity. Notably, the aircraft lacked a cockpit voice recorder (CVR) and flight data recorder (FDR), limiting data availability on internal cockpit activities and flight parameters. The engines were recovered and quarantined for examination, though further analysis depended on available funding. The preliminary report was released by the BPEA on January 10, 2020, providing an initial overview of the gathered evidence. As of November 2025, no final report has been published.[1][20][19] Preliminary findings The preliminary report released by the Democratic Republic of the Congo's Bureau Permanent d'Enquêtes d'Accidents d'Aviation (BPEA) in January 2020 described the sequence of events, noting a prolonged takeoff roll, shallow initial climb, wing rocking, and a request to return to the airport without declaring an emergency. The report suggested possible performance issues, such as engine malfunction, overloading, or crew actions, but did not identify a definitive cause.[1][2]The crew responded by attempting to turn back, but the low altitude at the time prevented a successful recovery. Further investigation was limited by the absence of recorders and funding constraints for detailed engine analysis.[1] Aftermath Casualties and survivors The Dornier 228-201 aircraft carried 20 passengers and 2 crew members, totaling 22 people on board. Of these, 21 perished in the crash, including all 2 crew members and 19 passengers, leaving only 1 survivor from the aircraft.[19][1]On the ground, the crash impacted a residential area in Goma, killing 6 members of a local family in their home, with no survivors reported from the ground impact.[19][1]The total death toll from the accident reached 27, comprising travelers en route from Goma to Beni among the passengers and local residents affected on the ground.[19]The sole survivor was passenger Muma Emmanuel, a telecommunications worker originally from Cameroon, who escaped the wreckage with minor injuries after being extracted from the burning aircraft.[19][21]Emmanuel received treatment for his injuries at Charité Maternelle hospital in Goma, where he was reported to be in stable condition following the incident.[21] Regulatory response Following the crash on November 24, 2019, the Democratic Republic of Congo's Civil Aviation Authority (AAC) immediately suspended all operations of Busy Bee Congo on November 25, 2019, pending a comprehensive safety review of the airline's fleet and procedures.[7] This action was taken to address potential risks identified in the incident involving the aging Dornier 228-201 aircraft.[7]Busy Bee Congo was cleared to resume limited flight operations on December 12, 2019, approximately three weeks after the suspension, following lobbying by local representatives and an implied audit of compliance measures.[9] The airline recommenced services connecting Goma, Beni, and Butembo in North Kivu province with its remaining fleet of one Dornier 228-100 and two Dornier 228-200s.[9] As of October 2025, Busy Bee Congo remains active, operating a single Fokker 50 aircraft acquired earlier that year for enhanced regional connectivity.[11][22]The incident contributed to heightened regulatory scrutiny on the use of aging aircraft in eastern DRC, where ongoing conflict in regions like North Kivu exacerbates aviation safety challenges, including poor maintenance and lax oversight.[23] Due to broader, long-standing safety concerns in the DRC, the European Union and United Kingdom maintain a blanket ban on all Congolese air carriers, including Busy Bee Congo, prohibiting operations into, within, or over their airspace due to systemic deficiencies in regulatory oversight.[24] While no specific mandatory inspections for Dornier 228 operators were publicly mandated solely from this event, the AAC's actions underscored efforts to enforce compliance amid persistent risks in conflict-affected areas.[7]
