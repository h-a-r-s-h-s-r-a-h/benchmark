NEW DELHI: After she retired from Supreme Court, Justice Ranjana Desai has had no time to relax, having been asked to head six commissions on a range of issues - from electricity to delimitation and uniform civil code to revision of pay scales for central government employees. Justice Desai, 76, who was part of the bench that upheld the death sentence for one of the 26/11 Mumbai terror attack actors, Ajmal Kasab, is highly regarded in legal circles for her integrity and understanding of criminal law. Justice Desai was part of the bench led by Justice Aftab Alam which had in Sept 2012 dismissed CBI's plea for cancellation of Amit Shah's bail in the Sohrabuddin fake encounter case while transferring the trial from Ahmedabad to Mumbai. She retired from Supreme Court on October 29, 2014. A little over a month after her three-year stint, she was appointed as chairperson of appellate tribunal for electricity, a position she held till Nov 30, 2017. Seven months later, she was asked to chair the advance ruling authority (income tax). She held this position till Oct 29, 2019. While heading ARA, she was made chairperson of the eight-member Lokpal search committee in 2018. The committee was reconstituted in Aug 2023 with Justice Desai as chairperson. After scrapping Article 370, Union govt had in March 2020 appointed her as chairperson of the delimitation commission. She continued in that position till May 2022, when the commission submitted the final report for J&K, based on which assembly polls were held. In May 2022, Uttarakhand govt constituted a committee of experts under chairpersonship of Justice Desai to draft the UCC for the state. A month later, Union govt appointed her as chairperson of Press Council of India. After she submitted her report on Uttarakhand UCC, Gujarat govt in Feb this year made her the head of a five-member committee to assess the necessity of UCC in the state. Even as she continues as PCI chairperson, Union govt has appointed her as chairperson of the Eighth Central Pay Commission. It has been given 18 months to make its recommendations on pay scales, pay revision and pensionary benefits of central govt employees.

A judge and an executor: Why Justice (retd) Ranjana Desai is Govt’s go-to person Lokpal selection panel and Press Council head for several years now, with UCC committees and Delimitation Commission behind her, the 76-year-old now has Central Pay Commission on her table She retired as a Supreme Court judge in October 2014, with a career marked by several important judgments and a self-effacing image. In the 11 years since, however, the spotlight has unerringly found Justice Ranjana Prakash Desai. In the latest of a series of high-profile assignments, the government has picked the 76-year-old to head the Eighth Central Pay Commission, which will recommend changes to the salaries and benefits of Central government employees. The three-member panel is to submit its recommendations – covering about 50 lakh Central government employees, including defence personnel, and around 69 lakh pensioners – in 18 months. The Seventh Central Pay Commission had recommended a 23.55% increase in pay, allowances and pension, which had led to an additional annual outgo of Rs 1.02 lakh crore for the Central government. If the scale of the task at hand is enormous, the government has repeatedly shown its faith in Justice Desai for such matters. Elevated to the Supreme Court in 2011 from the Bombay High Court, Justice Desai’s first major post-retirement role was as chairperson of the Appellate Tribunal for Electricity from 2014 to 2017. This was followed by a tenure as the head of the Advance Ruling Authority for Income Tax. Since 2018, Justice Desai has been chairing the Lokpal Search Committee, which recommends names for positions of chairperson and other members of the anti-corruption ombudsman. The Modi government has also fallen back on her for issues with more complex social and legal questions. In 2020, a year after the abrogation of Article 370 and scrapping of Jammu and Kashmir’s special status, the Narendra Modi government named Justice Desai the head of Delimitation Commission for the new Union Territory – a politically sensitive assignment with a lot at stake for the Centre. For J&K, it was to be its first delimitation exercise since 1995. The time given to the panel headed by Justice Desai was one year, but this was extended several times as the National Conference’s three MPs initially boycotted its proceedings. The commission submitted its final report in May 2022, increasing the Assembly seats in J&K (with Ladakh now out) by 6 in Jammu to 43, and 1 in Kashmir to 47, and made changes within the structure of the existing Assembly seats as well. In the case of the Lok Sabha seats of J&K, the commission redrew the boundaries of Anantnag and Jammu. All political parties in J&K, barring the BJP, contested the findings of the panel. However, these ultimately paved the way for the first Assembly polls in J&K after the Article 370 changes, held last year. Justice Desai next headed an expert committee set up by the BJP government in Uttarakhand in 2022 to examine the implementation of a Uniform Civil Code (UCC), in keeping with the promise made by the party in its manifesto for the state Assembly elections. The same year, Justice Desai was appointed as Chairperson of the Press Council of India, and continues in that role. In July 2023, The Indian Express reported that the Union government was expected to use the Uttarakhand UCC panel’s findings as a template for drafting its own UCC Bill. In February 2024, the Justice Desai panel submitted its final report to the Uttarakhand government, and the state soon became the first in the country to implement a UCC. Earlier this year, BJP-ruled Gujarat announced a five-member committee headed by Justice Desai to “assess the need” for a UCC and prepare a draft law for the same. Chief Minister Bhupendra Patel said the panel was expected to submit its report “within 45 days”, though it has not surprisingly taken longer. In July, the Gujarat High Court rejected a petition to include people from the minority community in the panel. A month later, the UCC panel met the Gujarat government for an extension. Those who know Justice Desai or have worked with her on various panels are not surprised at the government’s faith in her. Justice Permod Kohli, who was a member of the Uttarakhand UCC committee, described her as “a fine human being” who “created a conducive atmosphere and facilitated complete coordination among members”. Another member, social activist Manu Gaur, pointed to her legal knowledge, adding: “In spite of her age, she was the most active member of the committee.” Otem Dai, a member of the Lokpal Search Committee, said: “Justice Desai knows procedure well… She allows each of the other nine members on the committee to have their say and believes in building consensus on issues.” This approach to functioning was also highlighted by Senior Advocate R C Kodekar, a member of the Gujarat UCC committee. “She meticulously presents her position but at the same time consults everyone and takes their opinions.” Professor J S Rajput, who served on the Press Council of India with Justice Desai, described her as “considerate, compassionate and competent”. “She went deep into every issue under consideration… She is extremely hard-working and dexterous and provides incisive analysis – which is essential for arriving at the right conclusion in complex cases.” Born in Mumbai, Justice Desai studied law at Elphinstone College, followed by Government Law College, and joined the legal profession in 1973. She worked in the chambers of Justice S C Pratap, before his elevation as a judge of the Bombay High Court, as well as alongside her father, eminent criminal lawyer S G Samant. In 1979, Justice Desai was appointed a Government Pleader on the Appellate Side of the Bombay High Court. She steadily rose through the ranks, becoming a Special Public Prosecutor for preventive detention matters in 1986 and eventually the Chief Government Pleader in 1995. Her elevation to the judiciary came on April 15, 1996, when she was made a judge of the Bombay High Court. Her tenure in the High Court is best remembered for her confirmation of the death sentence for Ajmal Kasab, the lone surviving terrorist from the 26/11 Mumbai attacks. The Division Bench she headed termed the case “rarest of rare”, observing that the 24-year-old was beyond the scope of rehabilitation. In September 2011, she was elevated to the Supreme Court under the Congress-led UPA government. During her three-year tenure, she was part of Benches that delivered several landmark verdicts. In 2013, a Bench including Justice Desai upheld the voter’s right to reject all candidates in an election, paving the way for the ‘None of the Above’ or NOTA option on EVMs. That same year, she was part of a Constitution Bench that made it mandatory for police to register an FIR upon receiving a complaint about a cognisable offence – a significant step towards police accountability. Justice Desai was also part of the Supreme Court Bench that rejected pharmaceutical giant Novartis’s patent claim for its cancer drug Glivec, a decision celebrated for making life-saving generic drugs more accessible. Her latest role at the Pay Commission, one of India’s most challenging legal and administrative tasks, will require all of her skills.

Ranjana Prakash Desai (born 30 October 1949) is a retired judge of the Supreme Court of India who served from 8 September 2010 until her retirement on 29 October 2014.[1] Educated at Elphinstone College, where she earned a Bachelor of Arts in 1970, and Government Law College in Mumbai, Desai enrolled as an advocate of the Bombay High Court on 16 June 1976 and practiced in civil, criminal, labour, cooperative, and constitutional matters.[1][2] Appointed an additional judge of the Bombay High Court on 3 February 1991 and confirmed as a permanent judge on 3 February 1993, she handled significant cases in constitutional and criminal law before her elevation to the Supreme Court.[1] Following her retirement, Desai chaired the Delimitation Commission of India starting 13 March 2020, overseeing the redrawing of electoral boundaries, and served as Chairperson of the Press Council of India.[3][4] In February 2025, she was appointed head of a five-member panel by the Gujarat government to draft guidelines for implementing the Uniform Civil Code in the state.[5][6] Early life and education Family background and upbringing Ranjana Prakash Desai was born on October 30, 1949, in Mumbai, Maharashtra, into a family with deep roots in the legal profession.[1] Her father, S. G. Samant, was an eminent criminal lawyer practicing in Bombay, while her mother, Sharayu Samant, held a degree in Western Philosophy from Wilson College and served as a homemaker who actively supported Desai's pursuit of a legal career despite opposition from some relatives.[1][7] On her maternal side, Desai descended from a third-generation legal lineage; her grandfather, T. N. Walavalkar, maintained a civil practice at the Bombay High Court, and her maternal uncles were successful lawyers, though one uncle, V. T. Walavalkar, initially opposed her entry into the profession.[7]Desai was the middle of three sisters, with no brothers; her elder sister, Dr. Rekha Pandya, resides in the United Kingdom, and her younger sister is Kirti Gupte.[7] Raised in a liberal household in Mumbai, she and her sisters were brought up in a free environment that encouraged open expression and unrestricted movement, fostering independence from an early age.[7] This upbringing, influenced by her parents' progressive outlook, emphasized education and personal agency, with her mother affirming that no one could prevent Desai from practicing law if she chose to do so.[7] Desai's early exposure to the courtroom through her father's practice further shaped her aspirations toward a judicial career.[1] Academic qualifications and early influences Ranjana Prakash Desai completed her early schooling at the Convent of Jesus and Mary in Mumbai.[8] She obtained a Bachelor of Arts degree from Elphinstone College, Mumbai, in 1970, followed by a Bachelor of Laws (LL.B.) from Government Law College, Mumbai, in 1973.[1][9][2]Desai was born on October 30, 1949, into a family that placed significant emphasis on education and intellectual pursuits.[8] Her father, S. Pratap, later a judge, handling a range of civil and criminal cases, and assisted her father, S.G. regional equity.[27] Leadership in regulatory bodies Following her retirement from the Supreme Court of India on October 30, 2014, Ranjana Prakash Desai was appointed Chairperson of the Appellate Press Council Act, 1978, adjudicates complaints against print media, promotes journalistic ethics, and safeguards press freedom while addressing violations of norms.[29][2] Jurisprudence and notable decisions Approaches to constitutional rights Justice Ranjana Prakash Desai's interpretations of constitutional rights emphasized an expansive reading of fundamental freedoms, particularly under Articles 14, 19, and 21 of the Indian Constitution, while insisting on procedural fairness and empirical justification over moralistic overreach. In evaluating state restrictions on livelihoods, she prioritized individual autonomy against arbitrary regulations, as seen in her 2006 Bombay High Court judgment in the Mumbai dance bars case, where the court invalidated Maharashtra's ban on dance performances in eating houses. The decision held that the prohibition violated Article 21's right to life and livelihood by depriving thousands of women of employment without viable alternatives, and contravened Article 14's equality guarantee by irrationally targeting a specific profession under the guise of public morality. Desai reasoned that constitutional rights could not be subordinated to subjective ethical standards absent compelling evidence of harm, underscoring a first-principles approach that demands laws be rationally connected to legitimate state objectives.[30][8]Her Supreme Court jurisprudence extended this to Article 21's protections of personal liberty and dignity, advocating for context-specific applications that safeguard vulnerable individuals. In a 2013 ruling on medical termination of pregnancy, Desai participated in a bench permitting the procedure beyond the 20-week limit for a mentally challenged rape survivor, interpreting Article 21 to encompass bodily integrity and mental health as integral to the right to life. The court weighed the woman's severe cognitive impairment and trauma against fetal viability, concluding that denying termination would impose irreversible harm, thus reflecting a causal focus on outcomes rather than rigid statutory adherence. This aligned with her broader view that Article 21 evolves to address real-world exigencies, provided decisions are grounded in medical evidence and humanitarian considerations.[8]Desai also advanced transparency as a constitutional imperative intertwined with accountability, without eroding institutional independence. In 2013, as part of a bench addressing the Right to Information Act's scope, she affirmed that the Chief Justice of India's office qualified as a "public authority," subjecting it to disclosure requests under Article 19(1)(a)'s freedom of expression framework. The ruling balanced this by exempting sensitive judicial information, emphasizing that openness fosters public trust in rights adjudication while preventing abuse. Her approach critiqued unchecked discretion, insisting that constitutional offices must demonstrate fidelity to enumerated rights through verifiable processes.[8]In electoral rights, Desai's 2013 participation in the "None of the Above" (NOTA) judgment expanded Article 19(1)(a) to include voters' expressive choice against all candidates, mandating electronic voting machines display a NOTA option. The bench rejected the Election Commission's initial reluctance, holding that anonymity preserves voter autonomy and counters negative voting suppression, thereby enhancing democratic integrity without altering electoral outcomes mechanically. This decision exemplified her method of deriving rights from foundational principles of choice and non-coercion, supported by the absence of data showing NOTA's invalidation deterred participation.[17]Overall, Desai's framework privileged evidence-based scrutiny of state actions, often dissenting or concurring to refine balances between rights and order, as in corporate accountability cases like Sahara-SEBI (2012), where she enforced Article 14's equality by mandating refunds of over ₹24,000 crore to investors defrauded through opaque schemes. She consistently attributed interpretive latitude to judicial review's role in upholding causal links between laws and protected interests, wary of legislative overreach unsubstantiated by facts.[8] Criminal law and individual freedoms Justice Ranjana Prakash Desai's decisions in criminal matters consistently prioritized the protection of personal liberty and fundamental rights under Article 21 of the Indian Constitution, viewing arbitrary deprivations as antithetical to constitutional guarantees of life and liberty. She emphasized procedural fairness, rejecting state overreach in investigations, detentions, and punishments that lacked proportionality or due process. Her approach balanced societal interests in security and justice with individual safeguards against misuse of coercive powers, as evidenced in rulings on bail, investigative techniques, and capital sentencing delays.[8][31]In bail jurisprudence, Desai co-authored a 2013 Supreme Court judgment deprecating the imposition of onerous conditions on anticipatory bail under Section 438 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973. The court held that such conditions—such as requiring the accused to remain in custody upon surrender or report daily to police—defeat the provision's purpose of preventing unjustified arrests and protecting liberty pending investigation. The ruling clarified that bail conditions must be reasonable, tailored to prevent absconding or tampering without nullifying the relief granted, thereby reinforcing Article 21's mandate against presumptive incarceration.[32][33]Desai also addressed investigative intrusions on personal autonomy, examining compelled voice samples in criminal probes. In a 2011 ruling extended in subsequent cases, she scrutinized provisions under the Identification of Prisoners Act, 1920, the Evidence Act, 1872, and CrPC, holding that such procedures must respect constitutional protections against self-incrimination and unreasonable searches. While permitting voice analysis as non-testimonial evidence akin to fingerprints, her analysis underscored limits to avoid violating privacy and dignity under Article 21, distinguishing it from prohibited compelled testimony.[31][34]On capital punishment's intersection with liberty, Desai participated in the 2014 Shatrughan Chauhan v. Union of India bench, which commuted 15 death sentences due to prolonged delays in mercy petitions—averaging over five years—inflicting "incapacitative" mental agony equivalent to torture. The judgment established that such delays, attributable to state inaction, violate Article 21's right to life and dignified death, mandating reconsideration of sentences on humanitarian grounds without undermining deterrence. This reflected her view that procedural lapses erode the retributive rationale of extreme penalties.[8]Her rulings extended Article 21 protections to vulnerable contexts, such as permitting medical termination of pregnancy beyond the 20-week limit for a mentally challenged rape survivor in a 2013 case, prioritizing reproductive autonomy and health over rigid statutory bars when continuation posed grave risks. Similarly, in State of Maharashtra v. Indian Hotel and Restaurant Association (2013), the court struck down provisions of the Maharashtra Prohibition of Obscene Dance Act banning dance performances in bars, holding them discriminatory against women workers and violative of livelihood rights under Articles 14, 19(1)(g), and 21. Desai's concurrence rejected paternalistic morality as justification for curtailing consensual adult expression and employment, affirming equal liberty absent public nuisance or exploitation.[8] Gender justice and societal reforms Justice Ranjana Prakash Desai's judicial approach to gender justice highlighted the need for empathy in handling cases involving women's rights, particularly in criminal matters related to domestic violence and suicide. In Vajresh Anvekar v. State of Karnataka (2013), she co-authored the Supreme Court judgment upholding the conviction of a husband under Section 306 of the Indian Penal Code for abetment to suicide, where relentless dowry demands and harassment drove his wife to self-immolation on June 15, 2006.[35] The ruling critiqued the trial court's insensitivity, emphasizing that judges must recognize the psychological trauma inflicted on women in such scenarios to ensure fair adjudication.[36]Desai underscored systemic shortcomings in protecting women from violence, observing in 2012 that the criminal justice framework frequently fails victims of gender-based discrimination, a phenomenon she described as global in scope.[37] Her opinions advocated for heightened judicial sensitivity without compromising evidentiary standards, as seen in her caution against the misuse of protective statutes like Section 498A IPC, which she noted could be exploited despite their intent to safeguard women from cruelty.[38]In addressing societal reforms, Desai's tenure reflected a commitment to advancing women's access to justice through precedent-setting interpretations that prioritized individual dignity over rigid proceduralism. She participated in all-women benches, such as the one formed on September 5, 2013, with Justice Gyan Sudha Misra, signaling institutional progress toward gender-inclusive adjudication.[39] These efforts contributed to evolving norms on equality, though her decisions maintained a evidence-driven restraint to prevent overreach in reformist applications.[8] Controversies and public scrutiny Delimitation Commission outcomes The Delimitation Commission for Jammu and Kashmir, chaired by retired Supreme Court Justice Ranjana Prakash Desai and constituted under the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019, submitted its final report on May 5, 2022, after receiving presidential approval.[40] The report delineated 90 assembly constituencies excluding Pakistan-occupied areas, allocating 43 to the Jammu region and 47 to the Kashmir region, an increase from the previous 83 effective seats (37 in Jammu and 46 in Kashmir).[40][41] It also reorganized the five parliamentary constituencies, each comprising 18 assembly segments, while confining constituencies within district boundaries and preserving patwar circles as the lowest administrative units.[40]Among the key provisions, the commission recommended nine reserved seats for Scheduled Tribes (six in Jammu and three in Kashmir) and seven for Scheduled Castes, reflecting updated demographic considerations including the 2020 extension of ST status to Pahari-speaking communities in Jammu.[40] Additional recommendations included at least two nominated seats in the assembly for Kashmiri migrants (one reserved for a woman) and provisions for representation of displaced persons from Pakistan-occupied Jammu and Kashmir through nomination rather than territorial seats.[40] Justice Desai emphasized that the exercise treated Jammu and Kashmir as a single unit, prioritizing administrative contiguity, geographical compactness, and public consultations over strict population proportionality alone.[42]The outcomes drew significant scrutiny from Kashmir-based political parties, including the National Conference and Peoples Democratic Party, who boycotted consultations and alleged the redrawing fragmented Muslim-majority constituencies in the Valley while consolidating Hindu-majority areas in Jammu, potentially tilting electoral balance toward the Bharatiya Janata Party.[43] Critics, including petitions filed in the Supreme Court, argued the commission deviated from population-based norms under Article 170 of the Constitution, with Jammu's seat share rising from approximately 45% to 48% despite the Kashmir Valley's higher 2011 census population (6.89 million versus Jammu's 5.38 million).[44][43] The Supreme Court declined to stay the report in May 2022, affirming the commission's statutory independence, though subsequent challenges questioned the Centre's notification overriding Election Commission jurisdiction.[44] Justice Desai defended the process as transparent and cooperative, rejecting claims of partisanship.[42] Uniform Civil Code contributions and debates In June 2022, the Uttarakhand government established a five-member expert committee chaired by retired Supreme CourtJustice Ranjana Prakash Desai to prepare a draft Uniform Civil Code (UCC), marking the state's commitment to implementing uniform personal laws across religions in areas such as marriage, divorce, succession, and adoption.[45] The committee, which included retired judges Permod Kohli and Pramod Kohli alongside other experts, conducted extensive consultations, receiving over 143,000 public suggestions and holding 72 meetings to address diverse stakeholder inputs. Desai emphasized the need for citizen participation, stating in March 2025 during Gujarat deliberations that "it is essential to know the citizens' opinions before implementing a UCC" to ensure broad acceptance.[46]The committee completed its draft by June 30, 2023, with Desai announcing that the proposed code had been finalized after rigorous analysis, expressing "immense pleasure" at the outcome.[47] Submitted to the Uttarakhand Chief Minister on February 2, 2024, the draft formed the basis for the Uttarakhand UCC Bill, 2024, enacted on February 7, 2024, making Uttarakhand the first Indian state to adopt such legislation.[48] Key provisions under Desai's oversight included prohibiting polygamy, setting a uniform marriage age of 21 for men and women, mandating registration of live-in relationships, and equalizing inheritance rights, aimed at advancing gender equality while exempting Scheduled Tribes to respect customary practices.[49]Building on this, in February 2025, the Gujarat government appointed Desai to head another five-member panel to draft UCC guidelines, leveraging her prior experience amid ongoing national discussions on constitutional uniformity under Article 44.[5] The panel's formation followed Gujarat's electoral promises, with initial meetings focusing on public consultations similar to Uttarakhand's model.[50]Debates surrounding Desai's contributions center on the UCC's balance between secular uniformity and religious pluralism. Proponents, including BJP-led state governments, credit her committees with empirical grounding in equality, citing data on discriminatory practices like child marriages and unequal divorce rights under personal laws as justification for reform.[49] Critics, primarily from Muslim organizations and opposition parties, argue the codes impose majoritarian norms, potentially violating Article 25's freedom of religion; for instance, provisions on halala and triple talaq abolition were hailed for curbing abuses but contested as eroding Sharia-based family structures without adequate minority safeguards.[51] Desai's panels faced scrutiny for limited tribal exemptions in Uttarakhand, with tribal groups decrying cultural erosion, though empirical reviews post-implementation in 2025 have shown low litigation rates, suggesting practical viability despite ideological divides.[45] Mainstream critiques often reflect institutional biases favoring multiculturalism over uniformity, yet Desai's process-oriented approach—prioritizing data-driven consultations—has been defended as constitutionally pragmatic.[5]
