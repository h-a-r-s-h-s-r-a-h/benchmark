--- [William Ramsay - Wikipedia] ---
Sir William Ramsay  (; 2 October 1852 – 23 July 1916) was a Scottish chemist who discovered the noble gases and received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1904 "in recognition of his services in the discovery of the inert gaseous elements in air" along with his collaborator, John William Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh, who received the Nobel Prize in Physics that same year for their discovery of argon. After the two men identified argon, Ramsay investigated other atmospheric gases. His work in isolating argon, helium, neon, krypton, and xenon led to the development of a new section of the periodic table.

--- [Pyridine] ---
Pyridine is a basic heterocyclic organic compound with the chemical formula C5H5N. It is structurally related to benzene, with one methine group (=CH−) replaced by a nitrogen atom (=N−).  It is a highly flammable, weakly alkaline, water-miscible liquid with a distinctive, unpleasant fish-like smell. Pyridine is colorless, but older or impure samples can appear yellow. The pyridine ring occurs in many commercial compounds, including agrochemicals, pharmaceuticals, and vitamins. Historically, pyridine was produced from coal tar. As of 2016, it is synthesized on the scale of about 20,000 tons per year worldwide.

William Ramsay Early Life and Education Family Background and Childhood William Ramsay was born on 2 October 1852 in Glasgow, Scotland, to William Ramsay, a civil engineer, and his wife Catherine (née Robertson).[2][4] As the only child in the family, Ramsay grew up in an environment steeped in intellectual pursuits, with his father's profession in engineering providing early glimpses into practical applications of science. His mother, who had worked as a governess, contributed to a household that valued education and curiosity. Notably, Ramsay was the nephew of the renowned geologist Sir Andrew Crombie Ramsay, whose pioneering work in British geology and leadership of the Geological Survey of Great Britain exemplified the family's scientific heritage.[2][5] From a young age, Ramsay displayed a keen interest in the natural sciences, influenced by familial discussions on engineering principles and geological formations shared during family gatherings. His uncle's explorations and publications on rock structures and landforms particularly captivated him, fostering an appreciation for empirical observation and experimentation in the earth sciences. Complementing this intellectual stimulation, Ramsay engaged in hands-on chemistry at home, conducting simple experiments with equipment and chemicals sourced by his father. In his early teens, these pursuits extended to creating fireworks, which honed his skills in handling reactive substances and ignited a passion for chemical reactions.[6][5] Ramsay received his initial education at the Glasgow Academy, where he excelled in classical studies alongside emerging scientific subjects. By age 14, his aptitude for sciences was evident, as demonstrated by his readiness to pursue advanced coursework, leading him to enroll at the University of Glasgow in 1866. This early promise in analytical thinking and problem-solving laid the groundwork for his future contributions to chemistry.Academic Training Ramsay enrolled at the University of Glasgow in November 1866 at the age of 14, pursuing the standard arts curriculum with additional coursework in chemistry and natural philosophy. Over the next three years, he completed his junior honors, gaining a foundational understanding of scientific principles that sparked his interest in chemistry.[7] After completing his junior honors in 1869, Ramsay began hands-on training in practical chemistry in the laboratory of Robert Tatlock, the Glasgow city analyst, for approximately 18 months. This period emphasized laboratory techniques and experimental methods, bridging his theoretical knowledge with applied skills essential for advanced research.[7] In October 1870, Ramsay traveled to Germany, initially joining Robert Bunsen's laboratory at the University of Heidelberg for six months before transferring to the University of Tübingen in April 1871 to work under the organic chemist Rudolf Fittig. There, he conducted research leading to his PhD, awarded in August 1872 for the thesis Orthotoluic Acid and Its Derivatives, which explored the properties and reactions of these organic compounds.[2][7] During his student years, Ramsay began publishing in organic chemistry, demonstrating early analytical prowess. Notable among these was collaborative work with J. T. Dobbie on picric acid and the nitro-substitution products of phenol, contributing insights into their chemical behavior and synthesis around this formative period.[2]Professional Career Early Positions and Research After completing his doctorate at the University of Tübingen in 1872, Ramsay returned to Glasgow, where he served as an assistant in chemistry at Anderson's College from 1872 to 1874 before taking up the position of tutorial assistant in the chemistry department at the University of Glasgow in 1874.[2][8] In this role under Professor John Ferguson, he taught chemistry to undergraduates and conducted early research in organic synthesis, publishing his first independent paper in 1874 on the chemical composition of certain substances.[9] His work during this period focused on the preparation and properties of organic compounds, laying the groundwork for his later contributions.[6] In 1880, Ramsay was appointed professor of chemistry at University College, Bristol, where he also became principal in 1881, a position he held until 1887.[2][5] At Bristol, he expanded the chemistry curriculum to emphasize physical chemistry, introducing studies on topics such as molecular volumes of elements at boiling points, which marked his transition from pure organic chemistry to physicochemical investigations beginning around 1879.[10] During this time, he continued organic synthesis, notably achieving the first laboratory synthesis of pyridine in 1877 by passing acetylene and hydrogen cyanide through a heated tube, a method that produced the compound from inorganic precursors.[7][11] Collaborating with student James Dobbie in the early 1880s, Ramsay synthesized pyridine carboxylic acids from quinine alkaloids via oxidation and conducted early spectroscopic studies on their absorption spectra, revealing structural relationships between these derivatives and their parent compounds.[12][9] These efforts highlighted pyridine's role as a fundamental heterocyclic compound and advanced understanding of alkaloid degradation products.[10] In 1887, Ramsay was appointed to the chair of general chemistry at University College London, succeeding Alexander Williamson and focusing on broadening the department's scope in inorganic and physical chemistry.[13][2] This move positioned him to further develop his expertise in chemical education and research, transforming UCL's facilities into a leading center for chemical studies.[5]Discovery of Noble Gases In 1894, William Ramsay collaborated with physicist Lord Rayleigh to investigate an anomaly in the density of atmospheric nitrogen compared to nitrogen prepared from chemical sources, which Rayleigh had noted in his earlier measurements.[14] Their joint experiments confirmed the presence of a heavier, inert constituent in air, leading to the isolation of argon through repeated chemical removals of oxygen, carbon dioxide, and water vapor, followed by fractional distillation techniques. Argon was identified as a monatomic gas with a relative atomic mass of approximately 40, and its inert nature was demonstrated by its failure to react with common reagents like hot copper or ammonia. This breakthrough earned Ramsay and Rayleigh the 1904 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, shared for their discovery of argon and its placement in the periodic table. In 1895, Ramsay isolated helium (atomic mass ~4) from the mineral cleveite by heating it with acid and absorbing other gases, confirming its terrestrial presence beyond solar spectra.[2] Building on this success, Ramsay, now at University College London, partnered with his student Morris William Travers in 1898 to extend the search for similar gases.[2] Using liquefied air, they fractionated the residue after argon removal via low-temperature distillation and spectroscopic analysis, isolating neon (atomic mass ~20), krypton (~82), and xenon (~128). In 1908, Ramsay detected radon as a radioactive emanation from radium bromide, isolating sufficient quantities to record its spectrum and determine its properties, including an atomic mass of ~222 and extreme radioactivity. Collaborating with Robert Whytlaw-Gray, he named it niton (later radon) and linked it to the noble gas family through its inertness and spectral lines. These discoveries revealed the noble gases' chemical inertness, attributed to their stable electron configurations, prompting Ramsay to propose a new Group 0 at the head of the periodic table in 1900, which resolved gaps in Mendeleev's system and accommodated their zero valence. This expansion highlighted the gases' role as a distinct family of non-reactive elements comprising about 1% of the atmosphere.Later Work and Retirement Following the isolation of the noble gases, Ramsay extended his research into radioactive phenomena, focusing on the emanations from radium and thorium. Between 1908 and 1913, in collaboration with Robert Whytlaw-Gray, he determined key physical properties of radon—the gaseous emanation from radium—including its density, vapor pressure, liquid volume, critical constants, and atomic weight, using minute samples as small as 0.034 mg. These studies confirmed radon's status as a noble gas and advanced the understanding of radioactive decay chains by linking emanation behaviors to alpha particle emission and helium production. Ramsay also advised on extraction techniques for radium emanation, such as dissolution in hydrochloric acid and use of a Toepler pump, which facilitated experiments on thorium emanations and contributed to the quantitative description of their half-lives and decay patterns.[15][16] In the early 1900s, Ramsay endorsed a process promoted by the Industrial and Engineering Trust Ltd. for extracting gold from seawater, conducting trials at a small plant on Hayling Island capable of processing 40–50 tons of water daily. He publicly expressed conviction in its scientific and commercial soundness based on initial evidence, but the scheme proved fraudulent, yielding no viable gold recovery despite seawater's trace gold content. The failure led to significant financial losses for Ramsay and other investors, as well as reputational criticism for his involvement in what was later exposed as a swindle.[17][18] Ramsay resigned from his professorship of chemistry at University College London in 1913 amid declining health, marking the end of his active academic career. In the years following, he assumed advisory roles, including consultations for industrial chemical processes and contributions to scientific policy discussions.[2] During retirement, Ramsay devoted time to writing and advocacy for chemical education reform, emphasizing practical teaching methods and university curriculum improvements to foster scientific literacy. His efforts included essays and reports promoting accessible chemistry instruction, building on his lifelong interest in educational innovation.[19]Personal Life Marriage and Family In 1881, while serving as professor of chemistry at University College, Bristol, William Ramsay married Margaret Johnstone Marshall Buchanan, the daughter of George Stevenson Buchanan from Glasgow.[2][20] The couple's union provided a stable personal foundation amid Ramsay's rising academic career, with Margaret playing a key role in maintaining their household and facilitating social connections that complemented his professional network.[5] Ramsay and Margaret had two children: a daughter, Catherine Elizabeth (also known as Elska), born in 1884, and a son, William George, born in 1887.[21][22] The son pursued interests in science but tragically died in 1927 at the age of 40.[4] Catherine Elizabeth later married H. L. Tidy.[4] Following Ramsay's appointment at University College London in 1887, the family relocated to 12 Arundel Gardens in Notting Hill, where they resided for the next 15 years in what was described as a happy home.[5][23] These moves aligned with Ramsay's career progression, and the family occasionally traveled together to North America and Europe, sharing his interests in languages and exploration as recreations.[2][20]Health Issues and Death In the later stages of his career, William Ramsay began experiencing severe health problems around 1912, culminating in a diagnosis of nasal cancer. He had a history of recurring nasal polyps, first noted and surgically treated as early as 1886. The cancer caused significant physical misery and forced him to resign from his professorship at University College London in 1913; some sources suggest a possible link to his work with radioactive substances, though this remains speculative.[24][25][26][27] Ramsay underwent several treatments in England, including surgical removal of polyps from his left nostril in mid-1916 and operations on a tumor in his left antrum, followed by precautionary X-ray irradiation.[27] These interventions offered temporary relief and partial recovery, allowing him to complete some work into early 1916, but his condition steadily deteriorated over the ensuing months, rendering further research impossible.[27] Ramsay passed away quietly in the early morning of 23 July 1916 at his home in High Wycombe, Buckinghamshire, England, at the age of 63.[26][27] He was buried in Hazlemere Parish Church, with his family mourning the loss but drawing solace from the relatively swift end to his suffering and reflecting on his remarkable patience and dignity during the ordeal.[28][27] The illness left several projects unfinished, though his final paper on a hypothesis of molecular configuration—dated 1 April 1916 and based on prior experiments—was published posthumously on 1 July 1916.[27][29]Legacy Awards and Honors Ramsay was awarded the Davy Medal by the Royal Society in 1895 in recognition of his contributions to chemical research, particularly his role in identifying argon and other gaseous elements from minerals.[2] In 1902, he was appointed Knight Commander of the Order of the Bath (KCB) for his distinguished scientific achievements.[2] Ramsay received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1904 for his services in the discovery of the inert gaseous elements in air and their placement in the periodic system, building on his work with noble gases.[1] Among other notable honors, he was awarded the Longstaff Medal by the Chemical Society, the A.W. Hofmann Gold Medal by the German Chemical Society in 1903, and the Barnard Medal for Meritorious Service to Science.[2] Ramsay held numerous foreign memberships in prestigious academies, including the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences, the Royal Academy of Sciences in Berlin, the Institut de France, and the Academy of Sciences in Petrograd.[2]Scientific Impact and Commemorations Ramsay's discovery of the noble gases—argon, helium, neon, krypton, and xenon—completed a key gap in Mendeleev's periodic table by establishing a new group of inert elements, previously unaccounted for in the structure of atomic weights and properties.[6] This addition not only validated and expanded the periodic law but also provided early evidence for the stability of electron configurations, influencing the development of quantum theory by demonstrating the existence of a complete outer electron shell in these elements.[30] The isolation of noble gases by Ramsay laid foundational work for their practical applications, particularly helium, which he first obtained terrestrially in 1895. Building on this, post-1930s advancements utilized liquid helium's cryogenic properties—achieved through liquefaction techniques enabled by its low boiling point—for cooling superconducting magnets in magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) machines, a technology now essential for medical diagnostics worldwide.[31][32] Similarly, helium's role in cryogenics supports low-temperature physics research, underscoring the long-term industrial legacy of Ramsay's isolations.[33] In astrophysics, noble gases like helium, first spectroscopically detected in the sun before Ramsay's earthly confirmation, inform models of stellar composition; measurements of helium abundance in stars and cosmic microwave background radiation help trace the universe's primordial nucleosynthesis, extending the implications of his discoveries to cosmology.[6] Ramsay's later investigations into radon, a radioactive noble gas he isolated in 1908, contributed to early understandings of radioactivity, though modern research has highlighted radon's decay products as a significant health risk, causing lung cancer through alpha particle emission in enclosed spaces.[34][35] Ramsay's contributions are commemorated through various tributes, including a blue plaque erected by English Heritage in 2011 at his former residence, 12 Arundel Gardens in Notting Hill, London, recognizing his role in discovering the noble gases.[5] The Sir William Ramsay School in Hazlemere, Buckinghamshire, a secondary institution serving students aged 11 to 18, bears his name in honor of his scientific legacy.[36] In 2019, Google issued a Doodle for his 167th birthday, illustrating the periodic table elements he helped identify to celebrate his impact on chemistry.[37] Additionally, University College London established the Ramsay Memorial Laboratory of Chemical Engineering in 1923, funded in his memory, which evolved into the department's namesake professorship, the first such chair in the UK for the field.
