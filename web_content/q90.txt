The International 2016 was the sixth iteration of Dota 2's flagship annual championship. Hosted by Valve Corporation, it took place at the KeyArena Center in Seattle, Washington. The tournament began on 21 June 2016 with the Open Qualifiers and closed on 13 August 2016 with the Grand Final, where Wings Gaming became the third Chinese team to be crowned champions after defeating Digital Chaos. The 2016 edition of The International featured sixteen Dota 2 professional teams from all over the world.

--- [The International 2016 - Dota 2 Wiki] ---
The International 2016 was the sixth iteration of Dota 2's flagship annual championship. Hosted by Valve Corporation, it took place at the KeyArena Center in Seattle, Washington. The tournament began on 21 June 2016 with the Open Qualifiers and closed on 13 August 2016 with the Grand Final, where Wings Gaming became the third Chinese team to be crowned champions after defeating Digital Chaos. The 2016 edition of The International featured sixteen Dota 2 professional teams from all over the world. With a prize pool of over US$20 million, The International 2016 is the largest tournament to ever take place in eSports, breaking the record held by its previous iteration, The International 2015, which accumulated over U$18 million. The majority of this sum is collected through several in-game items released by Valve, including The International Compendium 2016, a digital book tied to the event that came with many activities and cosmetic items. 2016 marks the third consecutive year that The International's prize pool outgrows its previous-year record. The International 2016 became the second not to feature all previous champions, as Invictus Gaming was unable to qualify for the event. 2011 and 2014 winners Natus Vincere and Newbee were the only ones directly invited; 2013 winners Alliance secured their spot through the European qualifiers, whereas the defending champions Evil Geniuses were forced to play through the open qualifiers due to a last-minute roster change. Valve's roster stability rules also affected other teams, which resulted in only 6 direct invites being issued, the lowest ever for the tournament. In the All-Star match of 2016, two teams, Team Kaci and Team Slacks, faced each other in a best of one, playing a 10 vs 10 match (All Pick). Kaci and SirActionSlacks picked 5 random people from the audience in turn. After that, each of the 10 randomly picked persons then picked random pro players to join their teams. Just like in the previous years, there was no secrecy between the teams, mainly making fun picks. This is also the match in which Underlord got revealed in Dota 2. The match was not played in the booths, but was instead on the center of the main starge, on 2 rows of tables facing each other, meaning both teams could hear each other, the casters and the crowd.


--- [The International 2016 - Wikipedia] ---
The International 2016 (TI6) was the sixth iteration of The International, an annual Dota 2 esports championship tournament. Hosted by Valve Corporation, the game's developer, the tournament began in June 2016 with the qualifier phase and ended after the main event at the KeyArena in Seattle in August. The tournament awarded the biggest prize pool in esports history at over $20 million, surpassing the record set at the previous International, with the champion team, Wings Gaming, winning over $9 million.




--- [The International 2016 - grokipedia.com] ---
The International 2016 Event Overview Dates and Venue The International 2016 was held from August 2 to 13, 2016 (Wild Card: Aug 2; Group Stage: Aug 3–5; Main Event: Aug 8–13).[2] The tournament took place at KeyArena in Seattle, Washington, United States, marking the fifth consecutive year that the event was hosted in Seattle and the third time at this specific venue.[4][6] KeyArena, a multi-purpose arena in the Seattle Center, accommodated approximately 17,000 spectators for the main event, drawing a global audience that traveled internationally to attend in person.[4] This location aligned with Valve Corporation's tradition of hosting The International in Seattle, facilitating logistics for the North American-based developer while attracting fans from around the world.[6]Prize Pool The prize pool for The International 2016 was funded through a combination of a base contribution from Valve Corporation and crowdfunding via in-game Battle Pass sales. Valve provided an initial $1,600,000 USD to the pool.[2] Additionally, 25% of all proceeds from Battle Pass purchases, which were available from May 2016 leading up to the August event, were directed to the prize pool, contributing a record $19,170,460 USD from the community.[2][7] This brought the total prize pool to $20,770,460 USD, marking the largest in esports history at the time and surpassing the previous record set by The International 2015's $18,429,613 USD pool.[2][8] The pool reached a significant milestone of over $18 million USD by late July 2016, when it officially exceeded the prior year's total, driven by surging Battle Pass sales that highlighted the growing fan investment in Dota 2 esports.[9] The funds were distributed among the 16 participating teams according to a fixed percentage structure, with the winner receiving 44% of the total pool—a higher share than the 36% allocated to the champion in the previous year—to emphasize rewards for top performance while ensuring shares for all qualifiers.[10] This model underscored Valve's innovative approach to esports financing, relying heavily on direct community contributions to scale the event's financial stakes.[7]Background and Qualification Tournament Background The International 2016 marked the sixth edition of Valve Corporation's premier annual Dota 2 esports championship, a series that originated in 2011 as a showcase for the newly developed multiplayer online battle arena game.[11][12] Established to highlight top professional teams and foster competitive growth following Dota 2's transition from its mod roots in Warcraft III, the tournament quickly became a cornerstone of the esports industry, drawing global attention with escalating production values and community involvement.[13] By 2016, it had evolved into a highly anticipated event, building on the success of prior iterations that emphasized high-stakes competition and innovative event formats.[14] Anticipation for The International 2016 surged in early 2016, fueled by the series' rapid expansion and the record-breaking $18.4 million prize pool of the previous year's event, which had shattered esports fundraising benchmarks through in-game Compendium sales.[15] Valve announced the tournament details on April 1, 2016, including dates and ticket sales for the event at Seattle's KeyArena, which had been the main-event venue since 2014.[16][6] This hype was amplified by the growing Dota 2 player base and professional scene, with expectations of another massive prize pool driven by community contributions, positioning TI6 as a pivotal moment in the game's competitive calendar.[17] Pre-event preparations faced significant hurdles, particularly visa challenges for Southeast Asian teams seeking entry to the United States. Philippine squads TNC Pro Team and Execration encountered delays in securing U.S. visas, prompting intervention from Senator Bam Aquino, who leveraged his position as chairman of the Senate Committee on Trade, Commerce, and Entrepreneurship to expedite approvals through coordination with the U.S. Embassy in Manila.[18][19] These issues highlighted broader logistical barriers for international esports participants, but were ultimately resolved, allowing full regional participation.[20] The tournament's format reflected an ongoing evolution toward greater inclusivity and regional diversity, departing from the fully invite-only structure of the inaugural edition in 2011 to incorporate open and closed qualifiers starting prominently from TI2 in 2012, with TI3 in 2013 featuring similar regional qualifiers and a wild card round, and the format evolving further in 2014.[21][22][23][24] For TI6, Valve allocated six direct invites to established top teams: three to European teams (OG, Team Liquid, Natus Vincere), two to Chinese teams (Newbee, LGD Gaming), one to a Southeast Asian team (MVP Phoenix), and none to Americas teams, while reserving additional slots for qualifiers across multiple regions, enhancing global representation and providing pathways for emerging talent from areas like Southeast Asia and Eastern Europe.[25][2] Each of the four regional qualifiers (Americas, China, Europe, Southeast Asia) advanced the top two teams directly to the main event group stage, with the third-place team from each advancing to the Wild Card round.[2] This shift aimed to balance prestige with merit-based access, sustaining the event's reputation as the most competitive gathering in Dota 2.[26]Regional Qualifiers The Regional Qualifiers for The International 2016 took place in June 2016 across four regions: the Americas, China, Europe, and Southeast Asia, serving as the primary pathway for non-invited teams to earn spots in the main event. These qualifiers were structured in two phases—open and closed (main)—to ensure broad participation while prioritizing competitive merit. The open qualifiers ran from June 21 to 24, 2016, featuring eight single-elimination brackets (two per region), where thousands of teams registered globally, though regional participation exceeded 100 teams in total across the structured brackets. Winners from these open events advanced two teams per region to the main qualifiers, emphasizing grassroots competition and allowing underdog squads to challenge established organizations.[27] The main qualifiers occurred online from June 25 to 28, 2016, with the Americas hosting 7 teams and China, Europe, and Southeast Asia each hosting 10 teams.[28][5][29][30] The format consisted of a best-of-one round-robin group stage to determine initial standings, followed by a double-elimination playoff bracket with best-of-three series (best-of-five for the grand final). This setup tested team adaptability and depth, as the winner of each regional main qualifier advanced directly to The International, while the runner-up proceeded to the Wild Card round for a chance at an additional slot in regions sending one team, or both top two advanced directly in regions sending two. Seeding helped balance matchups, rewarding consistent performance from prior international events without favoring direct invites.[2][31][32] Key outcomes underscored the varying competitive landscapes across regions. In the Americas, Evil Geniuses qualified directly by winning the group stage tiebreaker, while Digital Chaos defeated compLexity Gaming 3-2 in the grand final, with both EG and DC securing direct spots in the main event due to the region's two available slots; compLexity Gaming advanced to the Wild Card round. In China, Wings Gaming qualified directly as the group stage winner via tiebreaker over EHOME, while Vici Gaming Reborn won the playoffs to qualify directly, and EHOME proceeded to the Wild Card round, highlighting the region's intense depth with multiple top-tier squads.[5] In Europe, Team Secret topped the group stage while Alliance won the playoffs to qualify directly, with Escape Gaming advancing to the Wild Card round, reflecting the area's talent pool bolstered by major winners.[29] Southeast Asia's TNC Pro Team qualified directly as the group stage winner, while Fnatic defeated Execration 3–1 in the grand final, with both teams advancing directly to the main event; Execration advanced to the Wild Card round, showcasing rising regional prowess. Overall, these qualifiers filled eight direct slots to the main event (two each from the four regions) and four Wild Card entries, from which two teams advanced to the main event, fostering global diversity amid logistical challenges like visa processing for international travel.[33][34][28][2]Direct Invites and Wild Cards Six teams received direct invitations to The International 2016 based on their strong performances in prior Dota 2 events, such as the majors earlier in the year. These teams were OG and Team Liquid from Europe, Newbee and LGD Gaming from China, MVP Phoenix from South Korea, and Natus Vincere from the Commonwealth of Independent States.[35] The wild card round served to allocate the final two slots in the 16-team group stage of the main tournament—which precedes the main event bracket held from August 8–13, 2016, at the KeyArena in Seattle—to additional qualifiers, providing an opportunity for lower-seeded teams from the regional closed qualifiers to compete. Held on August 2, 2016, at the KeyArena in Seattle, the event featured four teams—the Wild Card representatives from each region's closed qualifiers—the runner-ups from China (EHOME) and Europe (Escape Gaming), and the third-place finishers from the Americas (compLexity Gaming) and Southeast Asia (Execration).[36][37] The participants competed in a double-elimination bracket using best-of-three series, with EHOME defeating Escape Gaming 2–0 in the upper bracket final and Escape Gaming defeating Execration 2–0 in the lower bracket final to secure advancement, after Execration eliminated compLexity Gaming 2–0 earlier. Both winners joined the group stage, bringing the total to 16 teams.[38][39]Participating Teams Team Roster The International 2016 featured 16 teams representing five regions, with China contributing the largest contingent of five teams, followed by six from Europe, two from North America, two from Southeast Asia, and one from South Korea. These teams were determined through a combination of direct invitations based on prior major tournament performances, regional closed qualifiers, open qualifiers, and a wild card round, with seeding assigned to balance the two groups of eight for the group stage. The core rosters consisted of five players per team; positions varied but typically included one carry, one midlaner, one offlaner, and two supports. each regional qualifier.[38] This format allowed for a competitive elimination process among underdogs, with the winners earning entry into the Group Stage (August 3–5), which comprised six direct team in the group once, with all matches played as best-of-two series.[57] In these series, a 2–0 victory awarded two points to the winner and zero to the loser, while a 1–1 draw granted one point to each team.[57] The group stage took place from August 3 to August 5, 2016, at the KeyArena in Seattle, Washington, marking the beginning of the on-site competition for all participating teams.[2] Each
