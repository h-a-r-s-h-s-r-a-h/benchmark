--- [Orchestra of the Age of Enlightenment - Wikipedia] ---
The Orchestra of the Age of Enlightenment (OAE) is a British period instrument orchestra. The OAE is resident orchestra at Southbank Centre and Glyndebourne Festival Opera, Artistic Associate at Kings Place, and has its headquarters at Acland Burghley School. The leadership is rotated between four musicians: Matthew Truscott, Kati Debretzeni, Huw Daniel and Margaret Faultless.
A group of period instrumentalist players formed the OAE as a self-governing ensemble in 1986, and took its name from the historical period in the late 18th century where the core of its repertoire is based. The OAE does not have a principal conductor, but chooses conductors individually. The current Principal Artists are Sir Simon Rattle, Vladimir Jurowski, Iván Fischer, John Butt, Sir Mark Elder and András Schiff.  William Christie is Emeritus Conductor, as were the late Frans Brüggen, Sir Roger Norrington, and Sir Charles Mackerras.  Other conductors to have worked with the OAE at its invitation include Marin Alsop, Anu Tali, Yannick Nézet-Séguin, Sian Edwards, Edward Gardner, Suzi Digby, Robin Ticciati, Joanna Tomlinson, Philippe Herreweghe, Gustav Leonhardt, René Jacobs, Harry Bicket, Christopher Hogwood, Sigiswald Kuijken, Ivor Bolton, Monica Huggett, and Bruno Weil.
Sir Martin Smith was instrumental in establishing the OAE, securing crucial private funding for its inaugural work and serving on the Board for many years.  He is Life President of the orchestra.

The Orchestra of the Age of Enlightenment (OAE) is a British period-instrument ensemble founded in 1986 by a group of London-based musicians who sought to challenge and redefine traditional orchestral structures.[1] Specializing in music composed between the 17th and 19th centuries—primarily by figures such as Bach, Handel, Haydn, Mozart, and Beethoven—the OAE performs on original instruments or period replicas to evoke the sounds and revolutionary ideas of the Enlightenment era.[2] It operates as a self-governing collective, artistically directed by its players without a permanent conductor, emphasizing collaboration and innovation in historical performance practices.[3][4]The orchestra's mission centers on making "old music new" by exploring the radical, transformative spirit of Enlightenment-era compositions, often extending beyond strict period boundaries when artistically compelling.[2] Rejecting conventional orchestral hierarchies, the OAE prioritizes ongoing experimentation, deep scholarship, and risk-taking to continually refine its interpretations rather than adhering to fixed traditions.[1] This approach has established it as a world-renowned ensemble, known for its vivid tonal contrasts to modern symphony orchestras and its commitment to research-driven authenticity in sound production.[3]Headquartered at Acland Burghley School in north London since 2020, the OAE integrates arts and education through its unique residency partnership, hosting rehearsals and performances on school premises to foster community engagement.[5] It delivers extensive outreach initiatives, including regional tours across the UK, school-based programs, and digital projects aimed at broadening access to classical music for diverse audiences, from students to underserved communities.[6] As a registered charity, the ensemble collaborates with prominent conductors, soloists, and institutions while nurturing emerging talent through mentorship and performance opportunities, ensuring its artistic and educational impact endures.[3] History Founding and early development The Orchestra of the Age of Enlightenment (OAE) was founded in 1986 in London by a group of idealistic period instrumentalists seeking to create a self-governing ensemble distinct from traditional orchestral structures. This initiative arose from discussions among musicians frustrated with conventional practices in established ensembles, with the idea first conceived in 1985 by cellist Timothy Mason and bassoonist Felix Warnock. Key founding figures included violinist Catherine Mackintosh, alongside other instrumentalists such as viola player Jan Schlapp, violinist Marshall Marcus, clarinettist Tony Pay, horn player Tony Halstead, and flautist Lisa Beznosiuk.[7][1]The ensemble's inaugural public concerts took place in June 1986, marking its debut as a performer-led group using period instruments. The first performance occurred on 26 June at Oxford Town Hall, followed shortly by a London appearance at the Queen Elizabeth Hall, both conducted by Belgian violinist Sigiswald Kuijken. These programs featured an Overture Suite by Georg Philipp Telemann, excerpts from Jean-Philippe Rameau's opera Dardanus, a symphony by François-Joseph Gossec, and Joseph Haydn's Symphony No. 83 in G minor, "La Poule."[1]From its inception, the OAE embodied a mission to reject routine orchestral playing, commercial pressures that prioritized recordings over live performances, under-rehearsed executions, and the dominance of a single permanent director. Instead, the founders emphasized creative freedom, allowing the ensemble to select its own repertoire, collaborators, and interpretive styles while fostering innovation in historically informed performance. This ethos was realized through the establishment of a democratic governance model, centered on an elected Players’ Artistic Committee responsible for guiding artistic decisions and concert planning, with members also serving on the orchestra's Board of Directors.[1][7] Key milestones since 2000 In the early 2000s, the Orchestra of the Age of Enlightenment expanded its international presence with significant tours, including visits to South America and the United States in 2002, followed by its first tour to South East Asia in autumn 2003.[8]The ensemble launched its innovative late-night These include gut strings on string instruments for a warmer, more resonant tone; natural horns, which lack valves and produce a distinctive, less uniform sound through hand-stopping techniques; and wooden flutes, offering a gentler, more intimate articulation compared to modern metal variants.[36][2] This instrumentation enables a lighter, more transparent orchestral texture suited to late 18th-century music, contrasting sharply with the brighter, more powerful sound of contemporary symphony orchestras.[2]The orchestra's core repertoire centers on works from the Age of Enlightenment, encompassing symphonies by Joseph Haydn, operas by Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, and early compositions by Ludwig van Beethoven, which exemplify the era's emphasis on clarity, balance, and emotional expressivity. including strings, woodwinds, brass, timpani, and harpsichord, with applicants submitting recordings and attending interviews.[56] The program is generously supported by the Henocq Law Trust and has consistently attracted high-caliber candidates, enabling participants to form professional networks and extracurricular chamber groups.[58]Outcomes for alumni are significant, with many graduates broader outreach efforts at residencies, providing young musicians with complementary community engagement opportunities.[59] Residencies and outreach initiatives The Orchestra of the Age of Established in 2002, the scheme is consistently over-subscribed and offers its participants the opportunity to be mentored by OAE musicians, play in rehearsals alongside the OAE’s roster of guest conductors and also perform with the OAE, as well as sometimes giving concerts as an ensemble in itself.[5]
