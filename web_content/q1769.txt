Evi Nemeth (June 7, 1940 – presumed dead June 2013) was an American computer scientist, author, professor, and sailor best known for her pioneering contributions to Unix and Linux system administration.[1] She authored the influential UNIX System Administration Handbook (first published in 1989), which became a foundational reference—often called the "bible" of system administration—for managing computer networks and servers.[2] Nemeth's work emphasized practical, real-world guidance on topics like networking, security, and performance tuning, influencing generations of IT professionals.[1]Nemeth earned a bachelor's degree in mathematics from Pennsylvania State University in 1961 and a PhD in mathematics from the University of Waterloo in 1971 before transitioning to computer science in 1980.[3][2] She taught at institutions including Florida Atlantic University and the State University of New York at Utica (SUNY Tech) prior to joining the University of Colorado Boulder's Department of Computer Science as an associate professor in 1980, where she remained until her retirement in 2001.[1] During her academic career, she developed courses on data structures, operating systems, and internet technologies, and mentored numerous students in applied computing.[4] Nemeth also collaborated with the Cooperative Association for Internet Data Analysis (CAIDA) at the University of California, San Diego, starting in 1998, contributing to research on domain name systems (DNS) and internet infrastructure.[1]Beyond academia, Nemeth co-authored subsequent editions of her handbooks, including the Linux Administration Handbook (2002 and 2007), and presented at conferences like the USENIX Large Installation System Administration (LISA) events.[2] She advocated for global internet access through work with the Internet Society and the United Nations Development Programme.[1] In 1999, she was recognized as one of the Top 25 Women on the Web by San Francisco Women on the Web for her contributions to internettechnology.[5] Nemeth received the NCWIT Pioneer in Tech Award posthumously for her lasting impact on computing education and practice.[1]In her later years, Nemeth pursued sailing, crossing the Atlantic Ocean multiple times and serving as a crew member on voyages.[6] She was presumed lost at sea in late June 2013 when the schoonerNina, on which she was sailing from New Zealand to Australia, vanished during a storm; despite extensive searches, no trace was found.[7] Her legacy endures through her writings, which continue to guide system administrators worldwide, and her role as a mentor and trailblazer for women in technology.[2] Early Life and Education Birth and Upbringing Evi Nemeth was born on June 7, 1940.[8]She grew up in Vermont, where she spent her childhood in a rural New England environment that shaped her formative years.[9]Details on her family background remain limited, with no public --- [Evi Nemeth - Wikipedia] ---
Evi Nemeth (born June 7, 1940 – missing-at-sea June or July 2013) was an engineer, author, and teacher known for her expertise in computer system administration and networks. She was the lead author of the "bibles" of system administration: UNIX System Administration Handbook (1989, 1995, 2000), Linux Administration Handbook (2002, 2006), and UNIX and Linux System Administration Handbook (2010, 2017). Evi Nemeth was known in technology circles as the matriarch of system administration.
Nemeth was best known in mathematical circles for originally identifying inadequacies in the "Diffie–Hellman problem", the basis for a large portion of modern network cryptography.

CU-Boulder Honors Distinguished Engineers For Contributions The University of Colorado at Boulder will honor four of its distinguished engineering alumni along with a retired faculty member at the 42nd annual Engineering Awards Banquet on April 20. Recipients of the 2007 Distinguished Engineering Alumni Award in the industry and commerce category are Scott Donnelly, president and chief executive officer of GE Aviation, and Pamela Drew, vice president and general manager of Boeing's Integrated Defense and Security Solutions.
