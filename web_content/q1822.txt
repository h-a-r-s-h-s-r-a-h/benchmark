Kow Nkensen Arkaah (14 July 1927 – 25 April 2001) was a Ghanaian politician and traditional chief who served as Vice President of Ghana from 1993 to 1997 under President Jerry Rawlings.[1][2] Born in Senya Beraku in Ghana's Central Region, Arkaah attended Mfantsipim School and pursued a career in business and public service before entering politics.Arkaah initially aligned with opposition groups, serving as the running mate for the New Patriotic Party in the lead-up to the 1992 elections before switching to support Rawlings' National Democratic Congress through a political alliance.[3] As the first Vice President of Ghana's Fourth Republic, he contributed to governance during a period of economic stabilization and democratic transition, drawing on his experience as a businessman and leader of the revived Convention People's Party, formed by merging the National Convention Party and People's Convention Party.His vice presidency ended amid strained relations with Rawlings, highlighted by a 1995 cabinet meeting altercation that reportedly involved physical confrontation, though accounts differ on the details and severity.[4][5] Arkaah's political career was characterized by opportunism and controversy, including later attempts to align with opposition forces in 1996, reflecting his fluid ideological shifts in Ghana's multiparty era.[2][6] Early life Upbringing and family background Kow Nkensen Arkaah was born on July 14, 1927, in Senya Beraku, a coastal town in Ghana's Central Region, then part of the British colony known as the Gold Coast.[2] His origins in this Fante-speaking area immersed him early in the region's matrilineal traditions, fishing-based economy, and communal governance structures centered on chieftaincy, fostering a strong identification with local heritage that later manifested in his role as chief of Senya Beraku.[1]Arkaah married Marian Ribeiro-Ayeh, who accompanied him in public life and survived him after his death.[7] The couple had at least one son, Kofi Arkaah.[8] Details on his parents or siblings remain sparse in available records, with no documented accounts of specific familial influences shaping his formative years beyond the cultural milieu of Senya Beraku. Education and early influences Arkaah received his secondary education at Mfantsipim School in Cape Coast from 1941 to 1946, a prominent Methodist institution established during the colonial era that emphasized rigorous academic discipline and produced numerous Ghanaian leaders in the post-independence period.[1] He subsequently attended Achimota College until 1948, Ghana's leading secondary school at the time, known for blending European curricula with local cultural elements under the influence of figures like James Kwegir Aggrey, fostering a sense of national identity amid the push for self-governance.[2]In 1949, Arkaah traveled to the United States for higher education, briefly studying at Lincoln University before earning a Bachelor of Arts in Economics from Tufts University in 1952.[9] This period aligned with expanding opportunities for African students abroad following World War II, where exposure to American economic theories and market-oriented systems likely shaped his later focus on efficient resource management in public enterprises. He then pursued a Master of Business Administration at Harvard Business School, completing it in the mid-1950s, which provided training in corporate strategy and finance during Ghana's transition from colony to independent nation.[1] These qualifications positioned him for administrative roles, reflecting an early orientation toward pragmatic, economics-driven public service rather than ideological activism prevalent among some contemporaries.[2] Professional career Roles in state enterprises Arkaah held the position of General Manager at the Ghana National Trading Corporation (GNTC), a state-owned entity established to manage imports and distribution of essential goods such as food, textiles, and consumer products amid post-independence supply constraints.[2][10] In this role, spanning part of the late 1960s to 1970s, he directed trading operations, negotiated bulk procurement deals, and coordinated logistics for national distribution networks.[10]He progressed to Managing Director of the Ghana Airways Corporation in 1973, overseeing the state airline's commercial flights, aircraft maintenance, and international route expansions during a period of fuel shortages and operational strains.[9][2] His responsibilities included fleet management, passenger and cargo services, and compliance with aviation regulations under economic volatility.[9]From 1978 to 1979, Arkaah served as Chief Executive Officer of the Ghana National Procurement Agency, centralizing government purchases of supplies, equipment, and materials to streamline state expenditures and mitigate import bottlenecks.[9][2] This involved vendor selection, contract administration, and inventory control for public sector needs across ministries.[9] These appointments formed a sequence of leadership in key state trading, aviation, and procurement bodies from 1969 to 1979.[10] Key achievements and challenges in management Arkaah directed the Ghana National Trading Corporation (GNTC), a state monopoly handling bulk imports and distribution of essential commodities, during Ghana's post-colonial era of heavy state intervention in trade.[2] The GNTC's expansive operations under his leadership facilitated national supply chains amid import dependency, though specific performance metrics remain undocumented in available records. Similarly, as Managing Director of Ghana Airways Corporation, Arkaah oversaw the national carrier's expansion of routes in the 1960s and 1970s, including international services that supported connectivity despite limited infrastructure.[11]In the Ghana National Procurement Agency (GNPA), where Arkaah served as Managing Director from 1978 to 1979, the entity centralized purchasing of strategic goods to counter shortages, operating under the Supreme Military Council Decree amid economic volatility.[9] This role exemplified attempts at streamlined state procurement, yet the agency's effectiveness was constrained by broader fiscal pressures on public entities.Challenges in Arkaah's management reflected systemic flaws in Ghana's state-owned enterprises during the 1970s, including over-reliance on centralized control without competitive incentives, leading to inefficiencies and corruption.[12] Political instability, fuel shortages, and kalabule—widespread black-market smuggling—undermined operations across SOEs like GNTC and Ghana Airways, fostering fiscal drains through subsidies and non-commercial pricing.[13] By the late 1970s, these factors contributed to enterprise losses and market distortions, as state dominance stifled innovation and accountability, a pattern evident in subsequent reforms.[14] Political involvement Entry into politics and 1992 election Arkaah first entered elective politics in the 1979 Ghanaian general election, securing a seat in Parliament as the Member of Parliament for Senya Breku in the Central Region under the People's National Party (PNP), a Nkrumahist successor party. His tenure lasted until the 1981 military coup led by Jerry Rawlings' Provisional National Defence Council (PNDC), which dissolved the Third Republic and suspended parliamentary functions. During the PNDC era, Arkaah largely withdrew from active politics, focusing on business interests in state enterprises.[15]In the lead-up to Ghana's return to multiparty democracy in 1992, Arkaah initially affiliated with the opposition New Patriotic Party (NPP), contesting its presidential primaries but failing to secure the nomination, which went to historian Albert Adu-Boahen. Undeterred, Arkaah founded the National Convention Party (NCP) and negotiated an electoral alliance with Rawlings' National Democratic Congress (NDC) and the Every Ghanaian Living Everywhere (EGLE) group, forming the Progressive Alliance (PA). This pact positioned Arkaah as Rawlings' vice-presidential running mate, leveraging his business acumen and regional influence in the Central Region to broaden the alliance's appeal beyond NDC core supporters.[15][16]The PA's campaign centered on sustaining the PNDC's Economic Recovery Programme, highlighting achievements in stabilizing inflation from over 100% in the 1980s to around 10% by 1992, boosting cocoa exports, and implementing structural adjustments under IMF guidance, while promising democratic consolidation without radical upheaval. In opposition, the NPP emphasized private sector-led growth, deregulation, and accountability for PNDC-era human rights abuses, portraying Rawlings' rule as a continuation of military authoritarianism despite civilian trappings.[17][15]Presidential voting occurred on November 3, 1992, with Rawlings and Arkaah winning 58.3% of the approximately 3.5 million valid votes against Adu-Boahen's 30.4%, amid a turnout of about 45%. The NPP and other opposition parties contested the results, citing irregularities such as delayed polling materials, voter register discrepancies, and alleged intimidation in rural strongholds, though some international monitors, including from the Commonwealth, reported procedural issues but validated the overall outcome as credible. These disputes prompted the main opposition to boycott the December 29 parliamentary elections, enabling the NDC to secure a supermajority in the legislature.[18][15] Vice Presidency under Rawlings (1993–1997) Kow Nkensen Arkaah was sworn in as Vice President of Ghana on January 7, 1993, alongside President Jerry Rawlings, inaugurating the Fourth Republic following the 1992 constitution's adoption and multiparty elections won by the National Democratic Congress (NDC).[19] This marked Arkaah's transition from running mate to a key figure in the new democratic framework, where the vice presidency was established to support executive functions under the 1992 Constitution.[20]In his role, Arkaah presided over cabinet meetings in Rawlings' absence and acted as interim head of state during presidential travel or incapacity, as stipulated by constitutional provisions for the office. He also contributed to transitional governance by representing the administration in official capacities, including oversight of policy implementation amid the shift from provisional rule to elected democracy. Arkaah's business background informed his involvement in economic coordination, where vice presidents typically chaired the Economic Management Team to align fiscal and structural reforms.[21]During Arkaah's tenure, the administration sustained the Economic Recovery Programme (ERP), emphasizing macroeconomic stabilization, privatization, and private sector growth to address inherited fiscal imbalances. Ghana's real GDP grew at an average annual rate of approximately 4.3% from 1993 to 1997, with yearly figures of 4.9% in 1993, 3.3% in 1994, 4.1% in 1995, and 4.7% in 1996, reflecting progress in inflation control from over 30% to single digits and increased foreign investment.[22] These efforts built on prior structural adjustments, fostering export diversification beyond cocoa through mining and agricultural incentives, though challenges like public debt persistence remained.[23] Arkaah's support for these policies leveraged his prior experience in state enterprises, aiding cabinet-level decisions on trade and procurement efficiency.[24] Controversies Party switching and political opportunism Arkaah served as the running mate to New Patriotic Party (NPP) presidential candidate Albert Adu-Boahen in Ghana's 1992 general election, which the opposition NPP lost to incumbent Jerry Rawlings' National Democratic Congress (NDC) amid allegations of electoral irregularities and low turnout due to partial boycotts.[25] Following this defeat on November 3, 1992, Arkaah defected to the NDC and was appointed Vice President on January 7, 1993, becoming the first and only Ghanaian politician to contest as running mate for both major parties in subsequent elections, including his renomination by the NDC for the 1996 presidential race alongside Rawlings.[3] This shift positioned him within Rawlings' administration, which had originated from the 1981 military coup and emphasized populist policies contrasting with the NPP's center-right emphasis on liberal-conservative principles and multiparty democracy.[26]The defection drew sharp criticisms of political opportunism, with NPP supporters and independent observers accusing Arkaah of abandoning ideological commitments for personal gain, particularly the vice presidential office in a dominant NDC regime.[27] Prominent voices within the NPP framed the move as a betrayal of the party's foundational values, rooted in the "Danquah-Busia-Dombo" tradition of property-owning democracy and opposition to revolutionary authoritarianism, arguing that aligning with Rawlings undermined the principled resistance against one-party dominance established post-1992 transition.[28] Such views highlighted Arkaah's rapid pivot from criticizing NDC governance in the 1992 campaign to endorsing it, interpreting the switch as prioritizing power over consistency in Ghana's polarized two-party system, where the NDC secured 57.4% of the presidential vote in 1996.[15]Defenders, including some aligned with Arkaah's chiefly role as Omanhene of Senya Beraku, countered that the decision reflected pragmatic adaptation to Ghana's electoral realities, where the NDC's incumbency and institutional control made opposition bids structurally disadvantageous after 1992.[1] Arkaah reportedly justified the alignment by invoking his traditional neutrality as a stool holder, unbound by partisan dogma, and pointed to the NDC's electoral dominance—evidenced by Rawlings' 1996 victory margin of over 1.2 million votes—as validation of cross-aisle collaboration for national stability over futile ideological purity.[29] This perspective posited the switch as a calculated response to bipolar politics, where alternating major-party candidacies maximized influence absent a viable third force, though empirical data on voter perceptions remains anecdotal, with no formal polls documenting widespread approval. Conflict with Jerry Rawlings On December 28, 1995, during a cabinet meeting at the Castle, Osu, a heated altercation erupted between Vice President Kow Nkensen Arkaah and President Jerry Rawlings, culminating in Arkaah's temporary removal from cabinet duties. Arkaah publicly alleged that Rawlings struck him forcefully on the shoulder, causing him to fall to the floor, an account he detailed in a subsequent news conference attended by supporters.[30] Rawlings' administration, however, issued an official statement confirming a physical confrontation but describing Arkaah's ejection as an orderly "escorting out" by security personnel, denying any assault by the president himself.[31] Supporters including Nana Konadu Agyeman-Rawlings later reiterated this, insisting no beating occurred and attributing reports to opposition exaggeration.[32]The incident reflected deeper frictions in their working relationship, characterized by mutual distrust and policy divergences, as recounted by contemporaries. Professor Kwamena Ahwoi, a long-serving minister under Rawlings, described the pair's interactions as marked by "bad blood," with Arkaah's independent streak clashing against Rawlings' dominant style, though Ahwoi explicitly rejected claims of physical violence, framing the event as an escalation of verbal sparring where aides intervened to separate them.[33] Arkaah, a former businessman with ties to market-oriented reforms, reportedly advocated faster economic liberalization, including privatization pushes that grated against lingering statist elements in Rawlings' National Democratic Congress (NDC) framework, which had originated in revolutionary populism before adopting IMF-backed structural adjustments in the 1980s.[4] These tensions underscored Arkaah's marginalization, as Rawlings sought his ouster from cabinet roles while Arkaah retained the vice presidency until the end of their term in 1997, refusing resignation amid threats to his position.[5]In the broader context of Rawlings' administration, the episode highlighted authoritarian undercurrents persisting from his military rule era, where dissent—even from a running mate—was met with forceful containment rather than institutional resolution. Eyewitness accounts and official narratives diverged sharply, with no independent corroboration of assault claims beyond partisan recollections, suggesting a pattern where power consolidation prioritized loyalty over collegial governance.[4] Ahwoi noted the "Fante caucus" within NDC circles' reluctance to intervene decisively, reflecting ethnic and factional dynamics that amplified Rawlings' unchallenged authority despite Ghana's democratic transition.[34] This rift eroded the facade of unified leadership, exposing causal realities of personal ambition and control in a regime balancing electoral legitimacy with revolutionary habits.[35] Later life and death Post-vice presidency activities Following the end of his vice presidency on January 7, 1997, Kow Nkensen Arkaah maintained a low public profile, abstaining from political activities and commentary for approximately four years.[36]Arkaah resurfaced on January 7, 2001, attending the inauguration of President John Agyekum Kufuor and the swearing-in of Members of Parliament at the Parliament House in Accra, accompanied by his wife, Marian. He arrived late to the event, stood for about 10 minutes until seating was arranged, and observed the proceedings, which included the election of Speaker Peter Ala Adjetey. No substantive political statements from Arkaah were reported, though he was described in coverage as referring to himself as a "stubborn cat," alluding to his reputed resilience.[36] Circumstances of death and surrounding theories Kow Nkensen Arkaah, aged 73, sustained severe injuries in a motor vehicle accident on March 12, 2001, in the Cantonments area of Accra, Ghana, when the Mercedes Benz he was traveling in collided with another vehicle.[37][38] The incident involved his driver attempting to avoid a head-on collision, after which the other vehicle reportedly fled the scene, characterizing it as a hit-and-run. Arkaah was initially treated at the 37 Military Hospital in Accra, where he remained under observation for several days before being airlifted to Atlanta, United States, for advanced medical care.[37][11]He died on April 25, 2001, in Atlanta from complications arising from these injuries, as announced by a family spokesman.[11][2] Official accounts attribute the death directly to the accident's aftermath, with no public disclosure of an autopsy confirming alternative causes such as pre-existing conditions or poisoning.[39] Contemporary reports from Ghanaian media outlets, including state-affiliated sources, consistently frame the event as a tragic road mishap without evidence of sabotage.[40]Speculative theories of foul play have circulated in Ghanaian political discourse, primarily alleging orchestration by figures from the Rawlings era due to Arkaah's prior fallout with President Jerry Rawlings, including a 1995 public dispute where Arkaah claimed physical assault by Rawlings during a cabinet meeting.[41] These narratives, often amplified in opinion pieces questioning the hit-and-run's unresolved nature and the unidentified perpetrator, posit political assassination to silence a potential critic amid the transition to President John Kufuor's administration.[42] However, such claims lack empirical support, including forensic evidence, witness corroboration, or official investigations implicating state actors; they remain unsubstantiated conjectures from partisan commentators rather than verifiable data.[39] No credible medical records or independent probes have emerged to contradict the injury-related causation endorsed by Arkaah's family and initial reporting.[11] Legacy and assessment Contributions to Ghanaian governance Arkaah's prior experience in state-owned enterprises facilitated the integration of business acumen into public administration during Ghana's transition from the PNDC era to multiparty democracy. As Managing Director of the Ghana National Procurement Agency, he oversaw centralized purchasing processes that enhanced efficiency in government acquisitions, supporting economic stabilization by streamlining resource allocation amid post-military recovery efforts in the late 1980s and early 1990s.[1] Similarly, his tenure as Managing Director of Ghana Airways from 1973–1979 and 1976–1978 contributed to aviation sector management, fostering connectivity vital for trade and national integration during economic reforms.[9][1]In his capacity as Vice President from January 7, 1993, to January 7, 1997—the first under the 1992 Constitution—Arkaah helped institutionalize the office's role in the Fourth Republic, providing continuity in executive functions and balancing factional interests within the NDC government.[43] Acting as President during Jerry Rawlings' absences, he resolved the 1993 University Teachers Association of Ghana (UTAG) strike by compelling the Education and Finance Ministers to meet lecturers' demands, averting disruptions to higher education and demonstrating pragmatic crisis management.[1]As Tufuhene of the Senya Beraku Traditional Area prior to entering active politics, Arkaah contributed to local governance by upholding customary dispute resolution and community leadership, which bolstered social cohesion in the Central Region before his abdication to pursue national roles.[1] Criticisms and historical evaluations Arkaah faced accusations of political expediency that undermined any claim to principled conservatism, particularly through his rapid shifts in allegiance. Initially leading the National Convention Party (NCP), a grouping aligned with more traditionalist elements, he forged a Progressive Alliance with Jerry Rawlings' National Democratic Congress (NDC) for the 1992 elections, securing the vice presidency despite the NDC's revolutionary origins clashing with conservative values. By 1996, amid a fallout, Arkaah pivoted to the opposition Great Alliance—partnering with the New Patriotic Party (NPP)'s Albert Adu Boahen and later John Kufuor—positioning himself as running mate against his former allies, a move critics labeled as self-serving opportunism that eroded trust in ideological consistency.[44][45] The phenomenon even inspired the term "Arkaahism" to describe the pinnacle of treacherous political maneuvering in Ghanaian history.[46]Evaluations of his vice presidency highlight Arkaah's constrained influence under Rawlings' assertive style, which limited substantive reforms. Despite Arkaah's business background promising market-oriented changes, his role yielded minimal autonomous impact, as Rawlings centralized decision-making; this dynamic escalated to a reported physical confrontation on December 28, 1995, during a cabinet meeting, where Rawlings allegedly struck Arkaah, underscoring the vice president's marginalization. Promised economic reforms, such as accelerated privatization, stalled with only partial implementation— for example, key state enterprises like Ghana Telecom remained under partial government control by 1997, reflecting unfulfilled liberalization targets amid bureaucratic resistance.[4][47]Later historical assessments, particularly from perspectives skeptical of NDC dominance, portray Arkaah's tenure as complicit in perpetuating regime shortcomings rather than mitigating them, countering partisan narratives that gloss over the era's flaws. Right-leaning analyses argue his alliance accommodated Rawlings' authoritarian leanings and policy inconsistencies, diluting conservative opposition to statist interventions. Governance under the 1993–1997 administration saw corruption endure despite rhetorical crackdowns, with scandals emerging in public procurement and elite enrichment—contradicting initial anti-corruption pledges and contributing to stagnant transparency, as evidenced by early 1990s reports of unchecked nepotism and contract irregularities.[48][49] Such critiques emphasize that Arkaah's expediency failed to deliver structural accountability, leaving a legacy of unaddressed institutional weaknesses in Ghana's nascent democracy.[50]

Kow Nkensen Arkaah (14 July 1927 – 25 April 2001) was a Ghanaian politician who was Vice-President of Ghana from 1993 to 1997. He was also a chief of Senya Beraku. Early life Kow Arkaah was born on 14 July 1927 in Senya Beraku in the Central Region of the Gold Coast (now Ghana). He attended Mfantsipim School between 1941 and 1946, then Achimota College. He proceeded to the United States of America, where he obtained his first degree at Tufts College, after which he attended Harvard University for an MBA between 1952 and 1954. Career Arkaah was an Assistant Sales Manager of Secony Oil Corporation of New York City. He later returned to his homeland. From 1954 to 1957, Arkaah worked as a Marketing Executive of Mobil Oil Ghana Limited. For the next 10 years up to 1968, Arkaah worked with the civil service, rising to become Principal Secretary between 1966 and 1968. He was head of the Ghana National Trading Corporation (GNTC), a huge national trading franchise at the time, the Ghana Airways airline and the Ghana National Procurement Agency. During 1965, he was the Chief Commercial Officer for foreign trade. He has also worked as a consultant in the Gambia, Sierra Leone, Yugoslavia and Ethiopia. Politics Arkaah became the leader of the National Convention Party (NCP) prior to the 1992 presidential elections. His party formed an alliance with the National Democratic Congress (NDC) of Jerry Rawlings, and Every Ghanaian Living Everywhere (EGLE). As part of the deal, Arkaah became the Vice-Presidential candidate on Rawlings’ ticket. Rawlings and Arkaah had a difficult working relationship throughout their four-year term. The high point was an alleged punch-up between them at a cabinet meeting on 28 December 1995. Rawlings alluded that there had been some form of misunderstanding. Arkaah styled himself the “stubborn cat” after that incident. Arkaah became the leader of the Convention People’s Party formed by the merger of the NCP and the People’s Convention Party. The merger was announced on 29 January 1996. Arkaah, who continued as vice-president of Ghana, stood as a candidate in the 1996 presidential elections and lost. He was replaced in the Rawlings government by Professor John Atta Mills, a law lecturer, as Rawlings’ deputy. Death Arkaah was involved in a road traffic accident at Cantonments, Accra. He died of his injuries in Atlanta in the United States on 25 April 2001.

--- [What (and When) Is V-J Day? - The National WWII Museum] ---
On September 1, 1945, President Harry S. Truman addressed the nation by radio. “The thoughts and hopes of all America—indeed of all the civilized world—are centered tonight on the battleship Missouri,” Truman said. “There on that small piece of American soil anchored in Tokyo Harbor, the Japanese have just officially laid down their arms. They have signed terms of unconditional surrender.” The president went on to proclaim the following day, Sunday, September 2, “to be V-J Day—the day of formal surrender by Japan.” The United States still marks each September 2 as V-J Day with ceremonies honoring the sacrifices of the men and women who served in the Pacific war. Yet by September 2, 1945, celebrations of Japan’s surrender had already been taking place around the United States for weeks. Some of the most iconic photographs of V-J Day celebrations, if not the war itself, were actually taken on August 14, 1945, not September 2. Why can the date of V-J Day vary so widely? The answer lies in the complicated events of August to September 1945 in the Pacific theater. From Hiroshima to Tokyo In July 1945, Allied leaders met in the German city of Potsdam to discuss the strategy for ending the war against Imperial Japan. Germany had already been defeated and had accepted the Allied terms of “unconditional surrender.” Among other critical decisions, the Allied leaders—Soviet leader Joseph Stalin, US President Harry S. Truman, and British Prime Minister Clement Attlee, who replaced Winston Churchill during the conference—agreed that the Soviet Union would join the war against Japan by invading the occupied region of Manchuria. The day before the conference began, however, the United States successfully detonated the world’s first atomic bomb in the Trinity test. While the results of the test were not made public, Truman knew of its success. With both the Soviet commitment to join the war and the existence of the atomic bomb, the Allies issued the Potsdam Declaration on July 26 calling on Japan to accept terms of unconditional surrender, or face “prompt and utter destruction.” Two days later, Japanese Prime Minister Kantarō Suzuki appeared to reject the terms of the Potsdam agreement during a press conference. On July 31, Truman authorized preparations for the use of atomic weapons against Imperial Japan.1 On August 6, 1945, three American B-29 bombers, including the Enola Gay, appeared over the skies of Hiroshima, Japan. At 8:15 a.m., the Enola Gay dropped a single atomic bomb over the city. Less than a minute later, it detonated, killing at least 80,000 people instantly. Despite the destruction, the Japanese government made no official response, and a militant faction in Emperor Hirohito’s cabinet argued that the war should continue. Three days later, on August 9, another B-29 named Bockscar dropped a second atomic bomb, this time on the southern Japanese city of Nagasaki. That same day, the Soviet Union invaded Manchuria. Hirohito now stepped into the decision-making process personally. In the early hours of August 10, Tokyo time, the emperor announced to his war cabinet that he supported accepting the terms of the Potsdam Agreement. The cabinet then assembled a statement in accordance with the emperor’s decision. The agreement was transmitted to the Allies by letter through Switzerland.2 In it, the Japanese government conveyed that it was prepared to accept the terms of the Potsdam proclamation, but with Hirohito retaining his prerogatives as a sovereign ruler. News of the decision reached Washington, D.C., on the morning of August 10 local time; discussions began within Truman’s cabinet and with Allied leaders later that day. The following day, a tentative statement of agreement with these terms was sent by Washington to Tokyo with the stipulation that the authority of the emperor and the Japanese government would be subject to the Supreme Commander of the Allied powers. However, no official response was immediately received and, in the meantime, B-29 raids using conventional bombs continued against Japanese cities.3 On the night of August 13–14, American B-29s dropped leaflets on Japanese cities announcing that the emperor had transmitted possible terms of surrender to the Allies. This shocking public revelation precipitated a new crisis in the Japanese war cabinet. Hirohito reiterated his intention to end the war and recorded a message announcing his decision to the Japanese people later that night. Hours later, a rejectionist faction within the government launched an attempted military coup to destroy the emperor’s recording and prevent surrender. By the morning of August 15, however, the plot had been defeated and the conspirators committed suicide. 4 At noon Tokyo time on August 15, the emperor’s recording was broadcast over Japanese national radio. Never using the term “surrender” itself, Hirohito stated that his government would accept the terms of the Potsdam Declaration. This was the first time the emperor had ever spoken directly to the Japanese people. 5 Celebrations at Different Times News of Japan’s intention to surrender reached Washington, D.C., on the evening of August 14 due to the time zone difference with Tokyo. At 7 p.m., Truman addressed the press in the Oval Office and announced the news from Tokyo. Celebrations erupted across the country, most iconically in Times Square, New York. As a result, many of the images Americans associate with the end of the war were taken on August 14. In the United Kingdom, the following day—August 15—was formally declared as V-J Day and remains so to the present day. Japan commemorates August 15 as the end of the war as well. In his press conference, however, Truman stated explicitly that “proclamation of V-J Day must wait upon the formal signing of the surrender terms by Japan.” The formal surrender of Imperial Japan was scheduled for two weeks later, on August 31, but because of severe weather, it was delayed until September 2, 1945. The surrender took place on the deck of the USS Missouri as a Japanese delegation signed formal surrender documents with General Douglas MacArthur, the Supreme Commander of Allied forces for the occupation of Japan. Due to the time difference with Washington, Truman announced the signing of the formal surrender documents on the evening of September 1, 1945. He then proclaimed the following day as the United States’ official V-J Day. Crowds again gathered around the country to celebrate Japan’s formal surrender. Commemoration Dates Vary Due to the complexities of the events surrounding the end of the Pacific war, V-J Day has been, and is, commemorated on a variety of dates: August 15, the anniversary of Hirohito’s announcement in local time; August 14, the date of Hirohito’s announcement in the United States; and September 2, the anniversary of Japan’s formal surrender The United Kingdom, for instance, officially commemorates V-J Day on August 15 of each year. Australia recognizes August 15 as Victory in the Pacific Day (V-P Day). V-J Day is typically seen as the final end of World War II. Adding complexity, however, is another date that receives little recognition today: December 31, 1946, more than a year after Japan’s surrender. This was the date of Truman’s announcement of “a proclamation terminating the period of hostilities of World War II.” By doing so, Truman formally ended 53 “war and emergency statutes” that had been enacted to assist the war effort and, later, facilitate demobilization. With Truman’s announcement, the United States formally ended its involvement in the largest conflict in human history. Bradley W. Hart, PhD Bradley W.
