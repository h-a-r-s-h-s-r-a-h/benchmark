Muhammad VII al-Munsif, (Arabic: محمد السابع المنصف; 4 March 1881 in La Manouba – 1 September 1948 in Pau) commonly known as Moncef Bey (Arabic: المنصف باي) was the Bey of Tunis between 19 June 1942 and 14 May 1943. He was the penultimate ruler of the Husainid dynasty.

Muhammad VII al-Munsif, commonly known as Moncef Bey (4 March 1881 – 1 September 1948), was the Bey of Tunis who ruled from 19 June 1942 to 14 May 1943 as the penultimate monarch of the Husainid dynasty under French protectorate.[1][2] His brief tenure, amid World War II, marked a pivotal challenge to colonial authority as he appointed a ministry sympathetic to Tunisian nationalists, including members of the Destour party, aiming to assert greater local control.[3][4] French authorities deposed him on 14 May 1943, citing alleged collaboration with Axis forces following the German occupation of Tunisia, though subsequent accounts highlight his resistance to Nazi demands and explicit efforts to protect the Jewish population from deportation and persecution.[5][2][6] Exiled to France until his death, Moncef Bey's nationalist stance and defiance earned him enduring popularity among Tunisians as a symbol of resistance against colonial rule, contrasting with the more compliant policies of his successors.[7][8] Early Life Birth and Family Background Muhammad VII al-Munsif, commonly known as Moncef Bey, was born on 4 March 1881 at La Manouba, a residence near Tunis in present-day Tunisia.[9] He was the eldest son of Muhammad V an-Nasir Pasha, who ruled as Bey of Tunis from 1902 until his death in 1922.[9]Moncef Bey belonged to the Husainid dynasty, which had held power in Tunisia since 1705, when Husayn ibn Ali, an Ottoman officer of Turkish origin, seized control from the Muradid dynasty and established hereditary rule with Ottoman nominal suzerainty. The dynasty maintained semi-independent governance over Tunisia, blending Ottoman administrative traditions with local Arab-Berber influences, until the French protectorate was imposed in 1881—the year of Moncef's birth—effectively subordinating the Beys to French oversight while preserving their ceremonial role. As a prince within this lineage, Moncef received a traditional education suited to the royal family, though details of his early upbringing remain sparse in historical records.[9] Education and Early Influences Muhammad VII al-Munsif, commonly known as Moncef Bey, was born on 4 March 1881 in Tunis to Naceur Bey, a prince of the Husainid dynasty.[10] His father actively opposed French colonial encroachments, notably attempting to resist Resident General Lucien Saint's overreach in 1922 by supporting claims of the nationalist Destour party and challenging administrative impositions on the Bey's authority.[11]From an early age, Moncef served as a close advisor to his father, immersing himself in the political tensions of the French protectorate era, where the Husainid rulers faced diminishing sovereignty.[11] This familial involvement exposed him to resistance against colonial policies, fostering a reputation as an upright and pious figure sympathetic to Destourian constitutionalism, which advocated for Tunisian self-rule within a reformed framework.[11] Unlike more palace-isolated princes, his youth was marked by direct engagement in nationalist undercurrents outside the court, shaping his later advocacy for reforms during his brief reign.[11]Specific details on Moncef Bey's formal education remain sparse in historical records, likely reflecting the traditional princely upbringing common among Husainid heirs, which emphasized Islamic scholarship, dynastic duties, and rudimentary exposure to European administrative norms under the protectorate.[11] His early political acumen, evident in advisory roles by the 1920s, suggests practical influences from observing his father's confrontations rather than structured academic training, aligning with the era's constraints on native elite formation amid French dominance.[11] Ascension to Power Predecessor’s Rule and Transition Ahmad II Bey (1862–1942) ruled Tunisia from February 11, 1929, until his death, during a period dominated by the French Protectorate, where the Bey's authority was ceremonial and overshadowed by the French Resident-General's control over foreign affairs, defense, and administration.[12] His reign, marked by political insignificance and courtly decadence, saw no significant challenges to French dominance amid rising nationalist sentiments in the interwar years.[12]Following France's capitulation in June 1940 and the imposition of Vichy rule, Ahmad II cooperated with Vichy officials, including Resident-General Admiral Jean-Pierre Esteva, in enforcing discriminatory policies such as anti-Jewish regulations decreed by Vichy on October 3, 1940, which extended to Tunisia despite the Bey's nominal sovereignty.[13] This alignment maintained the protectorate's status quo amid Axis advances in North Africa, with Ahmad II avoiding any overt resistance as German and Italian forces approached in late 1942.Anticipating Ahmad II's failing health at age 80, the Vichy French administration designated Muhammad VII al-Munsif—known as Moncef Bey, a distant cousin and grandson of the deposed Ali III Bey—as Bey al-Mahalla (crown prince) on April 30, 1942, selecting him over other Husainid heirs for his perceived pliancy and lack of strong nationalist ties. Ahmad II died on June 19, 1942, at Dar al-Taj Palace in La Marsa, enabling Moncef Bey's immediate accession without disruption, as the French viewed the transition as a means to ensure continued collaboration during the escalating war.[14] Circumstances of Succession in 1942 Ahmad II ibn Ali, the Bey of Tunis since 1929, died on June 19, 1942, at the age of 80 in La Marsa.[15][16] His death occurred amid the uncertainties of World War II, with Tunisia remaining under the Vichy French protectorate, where the French Resident-General held effective control over key decisions, including dynastic succession.[17] Ahmad II left ten sons, but none were positioned as immediate successors; instead, the throne passed to his cousin, Muhammad VII al-Munsif, known as Moncef Bey.[18]Moncef Bey had been formally invested as Bey al-Mahalla (heir apparent) on April 30, 1942, less than two months prior, in a move that reflected the Bey's authority to designate a successor from within the Husainid family, subject to French concurrence.[18] This appointment bypassed Ahmad II's direct heirs, likely due to a combination of Moncef's seniority, his prior military and administrative roles, and the Vichy authorities' preference for a figure perceived as maintainable under protectorate oversight. The swift enthronement of Moncef on the day of Ahmad II's death ensured continuity, avoiding a power vacuum during a period when Vichy France was aligning with Axis powers while facing Allied advances in North Africa.[19]The selection surprised many Tunisians, as Moncef had earlier demonstrated sympathy toward nationalist aspirations, including defending the Destour Party in 1922, contrasting with Ahmad II's more compliant stance toward French rule.[20] Nonetheless, the French viewed the transition as stabilizing, unaware that Moncef would soon pursue reforms challenging protectorate dominance. No evidence suggests foul play in Ahmad II's death, which historical records attribute to natural causes given his advanced age and long rule marked by limited autonomy under colonial oversight. Reign (1942–1943) Domestic Reforms and Nationalist Initiatives Upon acceding to the throne on 19 June 1942, Moncef Bey pursued nationalist initiatives to assert greater Tunisian autonomy within the French protectorate, focusing on the Tunisianization of administrative roles and the reduction of French oversight in domestic governance.[21] He appointed Tunisian nationals to ministerial positions, challenging the established French-influenced structure and aligning with aspirations of local nationalists.[21]In early August 1942, Moncef Bey presented French Resident-General Admiral Jean-Pierre Esteva with a series of demands aimed at restoring Tunisian control over internal affairs, including administration, education, and justice, while maintaining the protectorate framework.[1] These initiatives positioned him as a focal point for nationalist sentiments, drawing support from groups like the Destour party, though they provoked resistance from French authorities wary of eroding their authority.[21]Moncef Bey further advanced these reforms by selecting M'hamed Chenik, a prominent Tunisian figure, as Grand Vizir, forming a cabinet that emphasized local leadership and indirectly bolstered nationalist momentum amid wartime pressures.[6] Despite the brevity of his rule, these actions symbolized a push for self-determination, contributing to his popularity among Tunisians but ultimately fueling conflicts leading to his deposition.[21] Relations with the Vichy Regime Upon his ascension to the throne on 14 June 1942, Moncef Bey inherited a French protectorate structure under Vichy control, where the Resident-General, Admiral Jean-Pierre Esteva, held effective authority over Tunisian affairs despite the Bey's nominal sovereignty.[4] Moncef quickly sought to assert greater Tunisian influence by demanding Esteva appoint local officials to key government positions, a request Vichy Paris rejected, exacerbating frictions over administrative autonomy.[13]Relations deteriorated further amid Vichy's enforcement of anti-Jewish statutes, enacted across North Africa since October 1940, which included quotas on Jewish civil servants, professionals, and students.[22] Moncef Bey resisted these measures by refusing to countersign discriminatory decrees requiring his approval, effectively stalling their implementation in Tunisia and signaling opposition to Vichy's racial policies.[23] In a public gesture of solidarity, he awarded the Nishan Iftikhar, Tunisia's highest honor, to approximately 20 Jewish subjects during this period of escalating persecution.[24]Despite these conflicts, Vichy formally recognized Moncef's status on 2 July 1942 by granting him the Grand Cross of the Légion d'honneur, a ceremonial honor that masked underlying tensions.[6] Moncef's broader nationalist push for Tunisian self-governance clashed with Vichy's maintenance of colonial control, leading to accusations from French authorities of subversion, though his actions prioritized local sovereignty over alignment with Vichy collaborationist aims.[25] These strains persisted until the Allied landings in November 1942 shifted regional dynamics, but Vichy's pre-invasion resistance to Moncef's initiatives foreshadowed his later deposition.[4] Policies Toward Jewish Subjects Upon ascending the throne on May 14, 1942, Moncef Bey demonstrated sympathy toward Tunisia's Jewish subjects by awarding the Nishan Iftikhar, the kingdom's highest distinction, to approximately 20 prominent Jews, including doctors and businessmen, just eight days into his reign.[24][6] This gesture occurred amid Vichy France's enforcement of anti-Jewish statutes in the Tunisian protectorate, which included professional bans, property restrictions, and quotas limiting Jewish access to education and public roles.[22]Moncef Bey leveraged his authority to stall the implementation of these discriminatory laws, as all legislation required his formal approval or signature, allowing him to delay decrees on evictions, "Aryanization" of Jewish businesses, and other economic penalties.[26][22] In coordination with Resident-General Admiral Jean-Pierre Estéva, he heeded appeals from Jewish community leaders, postponing financial impositions and mitigating hardships during the initial phase of his rule.[24] He also exploited loopholes in Vichy statutes to grant exemptions for native Tunisian Jews deemed to have rendered exceptional service to the state, continuing a practice initiated under his predecessor.[6]Following the Axis occupation of Tunisia in November 1942, Moncef Bey's administration provided targeted protections despite German demands for deportations and forced labor. His court sheltered Jews fleeing German camps, and Prime Minister Mohamed Chenik relayed warnings of impending roundups, enabling some to evade arrest.[6] Interventions by cabinet ministers under his direction prevented certain deportations, though broader enforcement of anti-Semitic measures persisted under occupation until Allied liberation in May 1943.[6] These actions earned him enduring recognition among Tunisian Jews as a protector, evidenced by oral testimonies recounting personal interventions and communal gratitude.[6] Interactions with Axis and Allied Forces Following the Axis landings in Tunisia on November 16, 1942, in response to Operation Torch, Moncef Bey maintained a policy of official neutrality between the occupying German and Italian forces and the advancing Allies, using the turmoil to advance Tunisian nationalist interests without committing to belligerency on either side.[21] German and Italian troops established a defensive bridgehead in eastern Tunisia, bolstered by Vichy French collaboration under Resident-General René Estéva, but Moncef Bey avoided declarations of allegiance, instead prioritizing the protection of Tunisian autonomy amid the escalating Tunisian Campaign.[27]On November 9, 1942—one day before the full Allied landings in Algeria and Morocco—U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt dispatched a direct message to Moncef Bey, requesting permission for American forces to transit through Tunisia to enable a swift confrontation with Axis armies, framing it as a mutual opportunity to defeat shared enemies without intent to impair Tunisian sovereignty.[28][29] The Bey did not accede to this overture, citing the need to navigate Vichy demands for loyalty to France while resisting full subjugation to Axis commands; concurrent pressures from Marshal Philippe Pétain urged resistance to the Allies, placing Moncef Bey in a precarious diplomatic bind.[21]Under Axis occupation, which persisted until the Allied capture of Tunis on May 7, 1943, Moncef Bey took limited but symbolic steps against discriminatory policies, notably awarding the Nishan Iftikhar—Tunisia's premier order of merit—to roughly 20 prominent Jewish physicians and businessmen in late 1942, a gesture of solidarity amid Italian and German enforcement of Vichy-era anti-Semitic statutes like forced labor and property seizures.[27] This act, alongside delays in implementing anti-Jewish decrees, positioned him as a protector of Jewish subjects despite Axis presence, though it did not extend to military resistance or sabotage.[30]Post-victory, on May 14, 1943, French authorities deposed Moncef Bey, charging him with compromising Tunisia's security through alleged Axis collaboration—a claim unsubstantiated by evidence and widely viewed as a pretext to eliminate his nationalist reforms, with Allies tacitly endorsing the move to stabilize French colonial rule under GeneralHenri Giraud.[31][32] No documented transfers of resources or intelligence to Axis powers occurred under his brief tenure, contrasting with Estéva's more overt accommodations, such as airfield access; the accusations served primarily to justify exile amid fears of Moncef Bey leveraging wartime chaos for greater independence.[21][31] Deposition and Exile Allied Occupation and French Accusations Following the Axis defeat in the Tunisian Campaign, Allied forces, including American, British, and Free French troops, occupied Tunisia in early May 1943, marking the end of hostilities in North Africa and the establishment of provisional Allied military administration.[33] The French Resident-General Jean Estéva fled as Axis forces retreated, leaving General Alphonse Juin as acting Resident-General under the authority of General Henri Giraud, the co-leader of the Free French Forces. This occupation shifted control from Vichy-aligned authorities to the Allies, who sought to restore French colonial oversight while addressing local governance amid rising Tunisian nationalist sentiments.[34]On May 14, 1943, Giraud ordered the deposition of Moncef Bey, accusing him of compromising the regency's internal and external security by maintaining his position during the Axis presence and allegedly collaborating with German and Italian forces.[32] French investigations cited his refusal to abdicate earlier and the awarding of decorations to Axis generals on April 12, 1943, as evidence, though these were minor protocol gestures under duress rather than active support.[31] No formal trial occurred; the deposition was abrupt, with Moncef Bey replaced by his cousin Lamine Bey, a more compliant figure favored by French colonial interests.[35] Pressure from French settlers, who viewed Moncef's nationalist reforms and popularity as threats to colonial stability, influenced Giraud's decision, framing the bey's neutral stance amid Vichy-Axis-Allied pressures as disloyalty.[33]The collaboration charges lacked substantiation, as Moncef Bey had navigated wartime demands without providing material aid to Axis operations, instead protecting Jewish subjects and engaging in cautious diplomacy with Allied envoys like U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt, who had urged troop passage in November 1942.[31] Historians note the accusations served primarily to eliminate a ruler whose brief reign had empowered Tunisian autonomy, exacerbating French fears of independence movements; Giraud himself later expressed doubts about the charges' validity.[33] The deposition triggered protests and arrests of nationalists, underscoring Allied complicity in perpetuating French protectorate rule despite Moncef's non-aggressive policies.[34] Exile Conditions and Legal Challenges Following his deposition on 14 May 1943 by order of General Henri Giraud, who accused him of collaborating with Axis forces during the North African campaign, Muhammad VII al-Munsif (Moncef Bey) was immediately exiled from Tunisia. Initially confined to Laghouat in the Algerian desert under French military oversight, he formally abdicated on 8 July 1943, after which he was relocated to Ténès, a coastal town in Algeria, where living conditions were described as austere and restrictive.[36] On 17 October 1945, he was transferred to Pau in southwestern France, where he resided in relative isolation until his death on 1 September 1948, subject to ongoing surveillance that limited his political activities and contacts.[10]The removal of Moncef Bey proceeded without any formal trial or judicial process, as French authorities invoked wartime exigencies and unsubstantiated collaboration claims—despite evidence that he had maintained neutrality by refusing explicit alignment with either Axis or Allied forces—to bypass legal norms under the protectorate treaty.[31] This extrajudicial action, ordered directly by Giraud to neutralize a ruler seen as enabling nationalist governance through his appointment of Tunisian ministers, lacked due process or opportunity for defense, prioritizing colonial control over evidentiary review. No independent legal challenges succeeded during his lifetime, though Tunisian nationalists later protested the deposition as an illegitimate suppression of sovereignty, highlighting the absence of recourse mechanisms for Husainid rulers under French dominance.[31] Attempts at Return and International Appeals Following his deposition on May 14, 1943, Moncef Bey remained in French-imposed exile, initially in Algeria before being transferred to southern France, where he was held under surveillance until his death.[37] Tunisian nationalists, particularly from the Neo-Destour party, viewed his removal as an affront to national sovereignty and mobilized campaigns for his reinstatement, portraying him as a legitimate ruler sympathetic to reformist aspirations.[35]In August 1946, Tunisian nationalist groups formally petitioned French authorities for Moncef Bey's restoration to the throne, emphasizing his popularity among the Muslim population and his brief tenure's alignment with Destourian goals.[32] Neo-Destour leader Habib Bourguiba incorporated demands for recognition of Moncef Bey as Tunisia's legal sovereign into broader independence appeals submitted to France, arguing that autonomy should proceed under his rule rather than the imposed successor, Muhammad al-Amin Bey.[38]Internationally, the Council of the Arab League on November 23, 1946, urged Paris to free Moncef Bey alongside other detained North African figures, framing his detention as unjust within the context of Tunisia's protectorate status.[32][39] Supporters escalated efforts by bringing his case before the United Nations in 1948, seeking diplomatic pressure on France to end the exile, but these appeals yielded no concessions as French officials maintained the deposition on grounds of alleged Axis sympathies and administrative necessity.[32] Moncef Bey died in Pau, France, on September 1, 1948, without returning to Tunisia, though his exclusion fueled ongoing nationalist sentiment.[40] Death and Legacy Final Years and Death in 1948 Following his deposition and initial exile to Laghouat in southern Algeria, where he formally abdicated on 8 July 1943, Moncef Bey was transferred to Pau in southwestern France during the summer of 1945. He resided at Villa Cadaval under ongoing French surveillance, accompanied by his son Raouf Bey, who had been permitted to join him.[18][41]Throughout these years, Moncef Bey symbolized resistance to French colonial rule for Tunisian nationalists, who viewed his ouster as an illegitimate act by the Free French under General Henri Giraud, predicated on unproven charges of compromising regency security through alleged Italian contacts. Supporters, including Destour party affiliates, escalated international appeals, preparing to submit his case to the United Nations Economic and Social Council just prior to his death, arguing for recognition of his legitimacy and potential restoration.[32][40]Moncef Bey died on 1 September 1948 in Pau at age 66.[1][18] Funeral and Public Response Moncef Bey died of uremia on 1 September 1948 in Pau, France, where he had been held under surveillance since 1945.[42] His remains were repatriated to Tunisia aboard the French torpedo boat Somali and arrived in Tunis shortly thereafter.[43]The official funeral took place on 5 September 1948 on the hillside of Sīdī b. al-Ḥasan in Tunis, drawing an estimated 300,000 mourners in a display of public veneration despite ongoing French colonial oversight.[1] Neo-Destour activists prominently participated by laying wreaths at his tomb, framing the event as a nationalist affirmation of Moncef Bey's legacy as a reformist ruler sympathetic to Tunisian independence aspirations.[1] He was interred with full honors in the Jellaz Cemetery, distinguishing his burial from those of other Husainid dynasty rulers who received lesser ceremonies under colonial restrictions.[1]The massive attendance and organized nationalist involvement highlighted Moncef Bey's enduring popularity among Tunisians, particularly as a figure who had briefly justify his 1943 deposition.[1] This response contrasted with the limited official recognition during his exile, signaling growing anti-colonial sentiment that influenced subsequent negotiations between nationalists and the French protectorate.[1] Historical Evaluations and Controversies Historical evaluations of Moncef Bey portray him as a pivotal figure in Tunisian nationalism, credited with advancing proto-independence sentiments during World War II through reforms that challenged French colonial oversight, though his brief reign invited sharp divisions in interpretation.[44] French colonial authorities and their postwar narratives framed him as a security risk whose contacts with Axis representatives compromised Allied interests, justifying his 1943 deposition as a necessary measure to restore stability under Vichy-aligned governance.[32] This perspective, advanced by Resident-General René Étienne and General Henri Giraud, emphasized alleged secret meetings with German envoys in late 1942, portraying such interactions as evidence of disloyalty amid the Axis occupation of Tunisia from November 1942 to May 1943.[1]The collaboration accusations, however, remain unsubstantiated by declassified records or Axis documentation, with French motivations appearing rooted in Moncef's refusal to fully implement Vichy anti-Jewish statutes and his appointment of a nationalist-leaning ministry on 8 September 1942, which included Destour party sympathizers and eroded protectorate control.[1] General Alphonse Juin, who oversaw the transition post-deposition, privately expressed skepticism toward the charges, noting insufficient proof of active Axis aid despite Moncef's nominal allegiance to the Beylical throne under French suzerainty.[35] Postwar French Nations in 1947–1948 as subversive agitation.[45]In contrast, Tunisian collective memory and emerging independence-era scholarship rehabilitate Moncef as a symbol of resistance against colonial pressures for full compliance with racial laws.[6] This view, echoed in oral histories from Tunisian Jewish communities, attributes his exile not to Axis affinity but to French exploitation of wartime colonial injustice, influencing Habib Bourguiba's Neo-Destour campaigns.[32] Balanced assessments, drawing from Allied intelligence reports, affirm Moncef's non-aggression toward invaders while underscoring French bias in equating nationalism with collaboration, a pattern seen in parallel Moroccan cases under Sultan Mohammed V.[35] Personal Life Marriages and Family Muhammad VII al-Munsif, known as Moncef Bey, married his second cousin, Princess Lalla Traki Beya, the eldest daughter of Muhammad IV al-Hadi, in October 1900 at Sidi Bou Saïd.[18] Lalla Traki died on 18 April 1919 at the --- [Tunisia] ---
Tunisia, officially the Republic of Tunisia, is a country in the Maghreb region of North Africa. It is bordered by Algeria to the west and southwest, Libya to the southeast, and the Mediterranean Sea to the north and east. Tunisia also shares maritime borders with Italy through the islands of Sicily and Sardinia to the north and Malta to the east. It features the archaeological sites of Carthage dating back to the 9th century BC, as well as the Great Mosque of Kairouan. Known for its ancient architecture, souks, and blue coasts, it covers 163,610 km2 (63,170 sq mi), and has a population of 12.1 million. It contains the eastern end of the Atlas Mountains and the northern reaches of the Sahara desert; much of its remaining territory is arable land. Its 1,300 km (810 mi) of coastline includes the African conjunction of the western and eastern parts of the Mediterranean Basin. Tunisia is home to Africa's northernmost point, Cape Angela. Located on the northeastern coast, Tunis is the capital and largest city of the country, which is itself named after Tunis. The official language of Tunisia is Arabic. The vast majority of Tunisia's population is Arab and Muslim. Vernacular Tunisian Arabic is the most spoken language, and French serves as an administrative and educational language in some contexts, but has no official status.
Beginning in early antiquity, Tunisia was inhabited by the indigenous Berbers. The Phoenicians, a Semitic people, began to arrive in the 12th century BC, settling on the coast and establishing several settlements, of which Carthage emerged as the most powerful by the 7th century BC. The descendants of the Phoenician settlers came to be known as the Punic people. Ancient Carthage was a major mercantile empire and a military rival to the Roman Republic until 146 BC when it was defeated by the Romans who occupied Tunisia for most of the next 800 years. The Romans introduced Christianity and left architectural legacies like the Amphitheatre of El Jem. In the 7th century AD, Arab Muslims conquered Tunisia and settled with their tribes and families, bringing Islam and Arab culture. A later large-scale Arab migration of Banu Hilal and Banu Sulaym tribes in the 11th-12th centuries accelerated this process. By around the 15th century, the region of modern-day Tunisia had already been almost completely Arabized. Then, in 1546, the Ottoman Empire established control, holding sway until 1881, when the French conquered Tunisia. In 1956, Tunisia gained independence as the Tunisian Republic. Today, Tunisia's culture and identity are rooted in this centuries-long intersection of different cultures and ethnicities.
In 2011, the Tunisian Revolution, which was triggered by dissatisfaction with the lack of freedom and democracy under the 24-year rule of President Zine El Abidine Ben Ali, overthrew his regime and catalyzed the broader Arab Spring movement across the region. Free multiparty parliamentary elections were held shortly thereafter; the country again voted for parliament on 26 October 2014, and for president on 23 November 2014. From 2014 to 2020, it was considered the only democratic state in the Arab world, according to The Economist Democracy Index. After democratic backsliding under President Kais Saied, Tunisia was rated a hybrid regime in 2022 and as of 2025 it no longer has free and fair elections according to Freedom House. It is one of the few countries in Africa ranking high on the Human Development Index, with one of the highest per capita incomes on the continent, ranking 129th in GDP per capita income.
Tunisia is well integrated into the international community. It is a member of the United Nations, Organisation internationale de la Francophonie, the Arab League, the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation, the African Union, the Common Market for Eastern and Southern Africa, the Non-Aligned Movement, the International Criminal Court, the Group of 77, among others. It maintains close economic and political relations with some European countries, particularly with France and Italy, due to their geographical proximity. Tunisia also has an association agreement with the European Union and has attained the status of a major non-NATO ally of the United States.
