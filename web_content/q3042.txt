--- [Jerry Lee Lewis discography - Wikipedia] ---
This is a detailed discography for American rock and roll, country, and gospel singer-songwriter Jerry Lee Lewis (1935–2022). One of the pioneers of rockabilly, Lewis recorded over 40 albums in a career spanning seven decades. Lewis was a versatile artist, and recorded songs in multiple genres. Lewis, in 1986, was one of the first inductees into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, and was the last surviving rock and roll pioneer of Sun Records. Some of his best known songs are "Great Balls of Fire", "Whole Lotta Shakin' Goin' On", and "High School Confidential". His album, Live at the Star Club, Hamburg, is widely considered one of the greatest live concert albums ever. In his lengthy career in music, Lewis had 30 songs reach the top ten on the "Billboard Country-and-Western" chart. Lewis was regarded as one of the greatest and most influential pianists of the rock and roll era, and was ranked number 24 on Rolling Stone magazine's list of the "100 Greatest Artists of All Time".

These releases often curate selections from his original singles and albums, focusing on thematic or chronological retrospectives to highlight his rock 'n' roll, country, and gospel contributions. Early compilations emphasized his 1950s breakthrough tracks, while later box sets and remasters incorporate rare material, alternate takes, and high-fidelity audio upgrades. Sales success for select anthologies underscores their enduring popularity, with certifications reflecting strong consumer interest in his legacy. Posthumous reissues since 2022 have continued to archival material. The first major compilation, Original Golden Hits, Vol. 1, was released in 1969 by Sun International, featuring 12 tracks primarily from his early Sun singles like "Whole Lotta Shakin' Goin' On" and "Great Balls of Fire," curated to recapture his explosive rockabilly sound for a new audience. This was followed by Original Golden Hits, Vol. 2 in 1970 and Original Golden Hits, Vol. 3 in 1971, each expanding on Sun-era material with additional hits and B-sides. In 1974, The Session...Plus compiled London sessions from 1973, including outtakes and live cuts, marking an early effort to revisit his 1970s output. The 1980s saw a surge in Sun-focused retrospectives, such as Rockin' with Jerry Lee (1980) and Milestones (1983), both drawing from his foundational recordings to appeal to collectors. A pivotal release, 18 Original Sun Greatest Hits (1984, Rhino Records), assembled 18 key tracks from his Sun tenure, emphasizing raw, unpolished demos and masters that defined his career launch; it peaked at No. 177 on the Billboard 200. The 1990s brought expansive anthologies, including Greatest Hits: Jerry Lee Lewis (1991, Mercury), which blended rock and country selections and reached No. 124 on the country albums chart. All Killer, No Filler: The Anthology (1993, Rhino), a two-disc set spanning 1957–1974, curated 50 tracks with liner notes detailing his evolution; it was certified platinum by the RIAA in 1994 for over one million U.S. shipments. Other notable 1990s entries include The Collection (1996, Mercury), focusing on 1960s Mercury hits, and Breathless: The Very Best of Jerry Lee Lewis (1998, EMI), which highlighted vocal-driven rock tracks. Into the 2000s, box sets provided comprehensive overviews. All the Best (2000, Chronicles) compiled 50 tracks across his rock, country, and gospel phases, achieving gold certification in the UK. A Half Century of Hits (2006, Time Life), a three-disc set covering 1956–2005, included rare demos and collaborations, curated by Lewis himself for biographical depth. The Complete London Sessions box set (2007, Salvo), reissuing his 1970s Decca/London material across six CDs, featured remastered tracks and unreleased outtakes from sessions with producer Owen Bradley. Thematic compilations emerged, such as Christmas Time with Jerry Lee Lewis (2009, incomplete but expanded in later releases) and gospel-focused The Gospel Side of Jerry Lee Lewis (2011, SPR), selecting hymns like "I'll Fly Away" from his religious roots. Recent reissues and remasters have revitalized interest, particularly post-2020. The Killer Keys of Jerry Lee Lewis on the Air in Hollywood (2022, Sun Records, for the label's 70th anniversary) reissued a 1958 radio transcription disc with eight tracks, remastered for clarity to showcase his early live energy. In 2023, Killer in Stereo: Good Rockin' Tonight (Sun Essentials) presented stereo remixes of Sun classics, while the Live at the Star Club, Hamburg reissue (Bear Family Records) offered a deluxe edition with expanded liner notes on the 1964 recording. At the Palomino Club (2023, Cleopatra) compiled unreleased 1970s live tapes from the Los Angeles venue, focusing on country-rock hybrids. His early singles captured the raw exuberance of Southern American music, propelling him to national fame alongside contemporaries like Elvis Presley. However, personal controversies, including his 1957 marriage to 13-year-old Myra Gale Brown—his first cousin once removed—exploded into public scandal in 1958, leading to tour cancellations, radio blacklisting, and a sharp decline in popularity that forced a reevaluation of his commercial path.[25][26] Despite the backlash, Lewis continued releasing singles on Sun until 1963, experimenting with covers and original material amid diminishing rock 'n' roll success. "Breathless" (1958), with its suggestive lyrics about romantic urgency, became another top-10 hit but faced resistance from some radio stations wary of its sensuality following the scandal. After departing Sun, he signed with Smash Records (a Mercury subsidiary), gradually pivoting toward country music in the mid-1960s as rock opportunities waned; this shift culminated in a string of country chart-toppers by the late 1960s, revitalizing his career with heartfelt ballads and uptempo numbers that showcased his versatile vocal style.[27] Key examples include his energetic cover of Ray Charles's "What'd I Say" (1961), which bridged his rock roots, and later country staples like "What's Made Milwaukee Famous (Has Made a Loser Out of Me)" (1968).
