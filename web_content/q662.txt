--- [Kier Eagen Family Tree in ‘Severance’: Explained - FandomWire] ---
Have you ever felt like you’re stuck between two worlds? Like your mind is divided, caught in a tug-of-war between who you really are and who you’re told to be? That’s the literal and metaphorical essence of Dan Erickson’s Severance—a show where the human soul is split, family legacies are tangled, and power is often held by those whose names we can barely pronounce. When you think about the Eagan family in Severance, it’s like staring at an iceberg—there’s so much more beneath the surface than we’re ever told. The family is practically a dynasty in their own right, controlling the enigmatic empire of Lumon Industries and shaping not just the present but the very foundation of the future. But as we dive deeper into the Eagan family tree, it’s clear that their hold on power is as precarious as it is potent. But what do we really know about them? Let’s take a closer look at the complex web of Eagans, their dark pasts, and their increasingly divided present. Kier Eagan At the very top of the Eagan family tree sits Kier Eagan (played by Marc Geller), the founding patriarch and the mind behind Lumon Industries. If there’s one thing you should know about Kier, it’s that he’s treated like a god within the confines of Lumon—his portraits, writings, and philosophies are practically revered. This man built an empire, but at what cost? His soul, perhaps, or more fittingly, the souls of those who’ve worked under him. Kier founded Lumon in 1865, a biotech company that grew to dominate an entire city. He’s not just a businessman, though; he’s a visionary—someone who believed that the truest form of service and love came from the relationship between employer and employee. His beliefs were almost cult-like, preaching that a person could achieve their full potential by ‘taming’ human emotions. But let’s not sugarcoat it—this wasn’t some warm, fuzzy philosophy. Kier’s love for his company and workers was framed as a selfless act, yet his high expectations seemed more like manipulation than compassion. As he wrote in his Compliance Handbook (via Elle): This paradoxical philosophy became the backbone of Lumon and carried on through the generations. Imogene Eagan Kier Eagan’s wife, Imogene, is a figure who doesn’t get nearly enough attention. Her name makes a brief yet intriguing cameo in the first season of Severance. Their romance, set against the backdrop of an ether factory, is a love story marked by ambition, power, and the spirit of industry. The Lumon handbook, the company’s sacred text revered by all employees, tells a tale of Imogene and Kier’s early years, describing how they were “bonded by the spirit of industry” as they built Lumon from the ground up. Though she never held the CEO title, Imogene is held in such reverence that she’s seen as an almost mythic figure, one of the company’s foundational pillars. Baird Eagan Then comes Baird Eagan, the enigmatic successor to Myrtle Eagan, who is a bit of a shadowy figure in the Eagan family legacy. While the specifics of their relationship are never directly revealed, we can piece it together from the Eagan family tree like a cryptic puzzle. Given the history of family succession, it’s a safe bet that Baird was Myrtle’s son—his name fits neatly into the family’s power-hungry mold. He held the reins as CEO until his death, though we never really get a peek behind the curtain of his tenure. Baird’s name graces the subdivision of Lumon housing where Mark, the ever-perplexed protagonist, resides. A subtle nod to his influence, perhaps? It’s not a stretch to assume that Baird was, in fact, a successful CEO—after all, you don’t get your name on housing developments for nothing. But, like most Eagans, the specifics of his reign remain shrouded in mystery. There’s something oddly poetic about that, don’t you think? Myrtle Eagan Ah, Myrtle Eagan—the second-born child of Kier Eagan, and let’s just say, she had a bit of a chip on her shoulder about it. Although her brother Ambrose snatched the CEO title right from under her nose, Myrtle was never one to settle for second best. Ambition coursed through her veins like a raging river, and she was convinced, from the get-go, that she was meant to run the show. Irving, during their jaunt through the Perpetuity Wing in Severance’s first season, gave us the scoop: Myrtle always knew she was the rightful heir to the throne. She wasn’t going to let a little thing like being second-born stop her. But Myrtle wasn’t just a corporate dreamer. No, no—she was a trailblazer. In 1952, she put her name on the map, literally, by founding the Myrtle Eagan School for Girls. It’s almost like Myrtle had a knack for planting seeds of influence everywhere she went—whether she got the CEO seat or not. Now, let’s talk about her statue. Myrtle didn’t just want to be remembered; she wanted to be seen. While the other Eagan statues strut around in muted colors, Myrtle’s statue pops out in bright red—loud, proud, and unmissable as a neon sign. Jame Eagan The latest Eagan to sit in the CEO chair is Jame Eagan, and let us tell you, this man is far from a saint. Sure, he might look the part, but he’s hardly the visionary that his ancestors were. In fact, it’s revealed that Jame didn’t even invent the severance procedure—Harmony Cobel did. But Jame was quick to slap his name on her groundbreaking work. He’s not exactly beloved, and his strained relationship with his daughter, Helena, only adds to the drama. His brief cameo in the ninth and final episode of the season leaves viewers with one tantalizing revelation: Jame is the father of Helly R., one of the show’s most enigmatic characters. Helena Eagan Helena Eagan, or “Helly R”, is Jame’s daughter, and she might just be the most interesting member of the Eagan clan. Though she doesn’t hold the title of CEO, she wields an unsettling amount of power within Lumon. She underwent the severance procedure herself, which makes her the first Eagan to do so. Despite her outwardly high status, Helena doesn’t seem to think highly of the “Innies”—the severed personalities that exist in the Lumon workforce. And yet, there’s more to her than meets the eye. Her involvement in the company’s shady operations makes her a fascinating—and terrifying—character to watch. Throughout the first season, she relentlessly tries to break free from the oppressive confines of the severance floor. Still, it’s only in the finale that she uncovers the shocking truth about her identity. The revelation forces Helly to confront the chilling reality of her family’s involvement in Lumon’s disturbing work, leaving her to reckon with her own complicity in the company’s sinister operations. So, there you have it—the Eagan family tree in all its complex, multi-generational glory. From the god-like Kier Eagan to the manipulative Jame and the mysterious Helena, this family is anything but ordinary. In Adam Scott’s Severance, the Eagans are not just a family—they’re a symbol of power, control, and the unshakable legacy of a business empire. The idea for this show struck creator Dan Erickson while he was daydreaming about the elusive work-life balance on his daily commute. What started as a simple thought has since morphed into a magical ride that takes viewers down unexpected paths. Season 2 of Severance premiered on January 17, with new episodes rolling out every Friday thereafter. Stream all episodes on Apple TV+.


--- [‘Severance’ Eagan Family Tree and Character Guide, Explained] ---
If the joke of Severance is that modern corporations act like cults, then it makes sense that Lumon Industries is led by what is essentially a family cult: the Eagans. The clan at the center of the Apple TV+ drama runs the mysterious business that requires many of its employees to surgically sever their minds from their personal lives. Not only that, but the family also owns the town in which Lumon’s founding office is located (Kier, PE)—and wields enough power to enrapture followers with their bizarre scripture. No one knows exactly what the Eagans are really up to at Lumon Industries. Severance trickles out information episode by episode while viewers inch closer to an answer. All we can say for now is that it likely won’t amount to much good. I just don’t trust that goat room! In the name of searching for more clues, let’s trace the Eagan family tree to find out just how far back the mystery goes. Kier Eagan Kier is the founder of Lumon Industries and arguably the most important character in Severance’s backstory. He served as the company’s first CEO for 75 years, from 1865 to 1939. He left many writings behind, written in a blend of biblical and corporate tones. His teachings—and very likely the creation of the Eagans as a new-age family cult—now make up most of the chapters of the employee Compliance Handbook. In some of the handbook’s passages, Kier describes scenes from his life, including suffering from tuberculosis as a child and noticing a “close biological relationship” between his mother and father. His bout with tuberculosis is also depicted in The Youthful Convalescence of Kier—which is a painting loved by Irving (John Turturro). Kier eventually married a woman named Imogene, whom he met while working at an ether factory. Severance fans glimpse the first time they met in another painting, titled The Courtship of Kier and Imogene. They had two known children together, Ambrose and Myrtle. Sometime in his adulthood, Kier became the leader he’s known as today. He began to spread his beliefs, including his plans to “tame” the four tempers of the human soul: Woe, Frolic, Malice, and Dread. Dieter Eagan Dieter was Kier’s twin brother, whom we only recently learned about in season 2, episode 4. According to the Fourth Appendix, it sounds as if Kier murdered his brother in Woe’s Hollow for masturbating in front of him. You tell me, dear reader, but I’m not buying the fairy tale that Dieter turned into a tree after he “spilt his lineage” all over Mother Nature. Ambrose Eagan Ambrose is Kier’s son and his first successor. However, he led the company only for a short time, from 1939 to 1941. Not much is known about Ambrose or his short tenure as Lumon CEO, so it’s possible that Kier’s son was a bit of a failure. There’s a square on the group’s bingo game during the Perpetuity Wing episode in season 1 that reads, “Passive-Aggressive Shit-Talking of Ambrose.” Another square reads, “Unexplained Eagan death.” Myrtle Eagan Myrtle is Kier and Imogene’s daughter. She was Lumon’s first female CEO, running the company from 1941 to 1959. After whatever happened to Ambrose, Myrtle took over. She also likely founded the Myrtle Eagan School for Girls, which Harmony Cobel (Patricia Arquette) attended. “I think that to be an Eagan, either a true Eagan or anyone working in this Lumon family, what you are is the keeper of an ethos, a compact of values that we have long held as precious, and which I do believe will one day save this world,” her bust in the Perpetuity Wing says. “That ethos goes all the way back through my blood to where we all started, with Kier. When I was a girl, my father would make me whisper … Vision. Verve. Wit. Cheer. Humility. Benevolence. Nimbleness. Probity. Wiles.” Baird and Gerhart Eagan Baird was the fourth Lumon CEO, holding the position from 1959 to 1976. Gerhardt followed Baird as CEO from 1976 to 1987. Not much is known about either Eagan. They are only known as “descendants of Eagan” in the Perpetuity Wing, so it’s unknown exactly how the family tree continues after Myrtle. The neighborhood in Kier, PE, where Mark (Adam Scott) lives is named Baird Creek Manor. Phillip Eagan Known as “Pip” Eagan, the sixth CEO shepherded Lumon Industries from 1987 to 1999. In episode 1, Mark receives a gift card from the company to a restaurant in town named Pip’s Bar & Grille. “Of course they gave you Pip’s,” his sister remarks. Leonora Eagan Lumon’s second female CEO is Leonora Eagan, who led the company from 1999 to 2003. She died relatively young, at 48, but her cause of death is not revealed. Irving’s neighborhood of Leonora Lake is likely named after her. Jame Eagan Jame Eagan (Michael Siberry) is the current CEO, operating since 2003. He bears a resemblance to the founder, Kier, and is the first Lumon CEO to employ the severance technology. Whatever Lumon did before Jame didn’t involve severed employees at all. With this new tech at their disposal, the Eagans and Lumon are supposedly closer to their goal than ever before. Their success all hinges on Mark S.’s completion of a file named “Cold Harbor.” Helena Eagan Presumably next in line as Jame’s successor, Helena Eagan (Britt Lower) doesn’t seem to have a great relationship with her father. Jame tells her that “one day you will sit with me at my revolving.” Despite this, Helena agrees to undergo the severance procedure herself and work on the severed floor to prove its efficacy to the public. Her Innie, Helly R., doesn’t share the same goals. When she discovers that she’s an Eagan in the season 1 finale, Helly R. attempts to tell the world that Lumon is lying to them about what really occurs within their company. In season 2, Helena attends work on the severed floor pretending to be her Innie, Helly R. Mr. Milchick (Tramell Tillman) seems aware of this when he’s forced to save her life at the end of season 2, episode 4. Despite this, her motivations remain unclear. If she was spying on the group for Lumon, why did she sleep with Mark S.? It’s possible that Helena was more interested in why her Innie fell in love with Mark S. than anything else. Who knows with this family! Check back here after the next episode. We’ll likely learn more about what the Eagans are up to at Lumon as the season goes on.
