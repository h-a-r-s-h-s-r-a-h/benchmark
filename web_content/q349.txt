--- [Harrison Schmitt - Wikipedia] ---
Harrison Hagan "Jack" Schmitt (born July 3, 1935) is an American geologist, former NASA astronaut, university professor, and former U.S. senator from New Mexico. He is the most recent living person—and only person without a background in military aviation—to have walked on the Moon.
In December 1972, as a crewmember of Apollo 17, Schmitt became the first member of NASA's first scientist-astronaut group to fly in space. As Apollo 17 was the last of the Apollo missions, he also became the twelfth and second-youngest person to set foot on the Moon and the second-to-last person to step off of the Moon (he boarded the Lunar Module shortly before commander Eugene Cernan). Schmitt also remains the only professional scientist to have flown beyond low Earth orbit and to have visited the Moon. Before training for Apollo 17, he was influential in the geology field for supporting the Apollo program and had helped train Apollo astronauts chosen to visit the lunar surface.
Schmitt resigned from NASA in August 1975 to run for election to the United States Senate as a member from New Mexico. As the Republican candidate in the 1976 election, he defeated Democratic incumbent Joseph Montoya. In the 1982 election, Schmitt was defeated by Democrat Jeff Bingaman.

Harrison Schmitt Harrison Hagan "Jack" Schmitt (born July 3, 1935) is an American geologist, astronaut, and former politician who served as a United States Senator from New Mexico from 1977 to 1983.[1] Selected as a scientist-astronaut by NASA in 1965, Schmitt earned a Ph.D. in geology from Harvard University in 1964 and contributed to early Apollo missions by training crews in lunar field geology and navigation.[2] As the lunar module pilot and resident geologist on Apollo 17—the final crewed mission to the Moon in December 1972—he became the twelfth and last person to walk on the lunar surface, conducting three extravehicular activities that yielded over 240 pounds of rock and soil samples, including the discovery of vivid orange regolith indicative of volcanic activity.[3][4] Schmitt remains the only professional geologist to have explored another celestial body, providing firsthand empirical data that advanced understanding of the Moon's geological history and formation processes.[3][4] After resigning from NASA in 1975, he won election as a Republican to the Senate, where he chaired subcommittees on energy resources and advocated for expanded domestic resource extraction and space exploration initiatives during a period of economic challenges from energy shortages.[1][5] Early Life and Education Early Life and Family Background Harrison Hagan "Jack" Schmitt was born on July 3, 1935, in Santa Rita, New Mexico, a company town centered on copper mining operations.[6][7] He was the only son of Harrison A. Schmitt, a mining geologist whose professional activities in the region's extractive industries provided young Jack with early exposure to practical geological fieldwork and earth sciences.[6][8] His mother was Ethel Schmitt.[9] The family soon relocated to nearby Silver City, New Mexico, another mining community in the southwestern part of the state, where Schmitt spent his formative years amid rugged terrain and active mineral extraction sites.[7][6] This environment, combined with his father's career and occasional assistance in geological tasks, cultivated Schmitt's initial fascination with rocks, minerals, and natural processes, often through hands-on outdoor exploration in the arid landscapes of Grant County.[8] His father's additional pursuits, such as amateur meteorology, further sparked curiosity about atmospheric and environmental phenomena during childhood activities near Silver City.[10] Schmitt attended local public schools in Silver City, demonstrating aptitude in science and mathematics from an early age, which aligned with the practical STEM influences of his surroundings but without formal specialization until later.[4]Academic Training and Influences Schmitt received a Bachelor of Science degree in geology from the California Institute of Technology in 1957.[11] The Caltech geology program at the time stressed intensive field training, including mapping expeditions to regions like the Grand Canyon, which instilled in students a hands-on approach to stratigraphy, structural geology, and mineral identification under professors such as Leon Silver.[12] This quantitative and empirical foundation equipped Schmitt with skills in analyzing rock formations and mineral compositions, foreshadowing applications in planetary contexts.[13] Following his undergraduate studies, Schmitt conducted research as a Fulbright Scholar at the University of Oslo, focusing on Norwegian geology, before pursuing graduate work at Harvard University.[11] He earned a Ph.D. in geology from Harvard in 1964, with a dissertation titled "Petrology and Structure of the Eiksundsdal Eclogite Complex, Hareidland, Sunnmore, Norway," which examined high-pressure metamorphic rocks through detailed petrographic analysis and structural mapping.[14] This work highlighted influences from Harvard's emphasis on metamorphic petrology and field-based tectonic studies, contributing to Schmitt's expertise in mineralogical processes and rock deformation relevant to deep crustal dynamics.[15] During his academic career, Schmitt published early works on mining geology terminology and regional stratigraphy, such as contributions to studies of the Central Mining District in New Mexico, demonstrating proficiency in economic geology and sedimentary layering analysis.[16] These efforts reflected mentorship in applied geological surveying, bridging academic theory with practical mineral exploration techniques.Scientific Contributions Pre-NASA Geological Work Following completion of his Ph.D. in geology from Harvard University in 1964, Schmitt joined the U.S. Geological Survey's (USGS) Astrogeology Branch in Flagstaff, Arizona, where he participated in photogeologic and telescopic mapping of the Moon's surface features.[15] There, he served as project chief for the Lunar Field Geological Methods initiative, developing training protocols and simulation techniques for extraterrestrial fieldwork based on terrestrial volcanic and structural analogs, such as those observed in the American Southwest and Alaska.[17] This work emphasized the adaptation of standard geological mapping to low-gravity, airless environments, drawing on empirical data from Earth-based field expeditions to predict lunar stratigraphy and resource distribution.[15] Earlier, during his graduate studies, Schmitt conducted extensive fieldwork as a Fulbright Scholar with the Norwegian Geological Survey along the west coast of Norway from 1957 to 1958, mapping Precambrian and Paleozoic rock sequences in tectonically active terrains that provided insights into orogenic processes potentially analogous to planetary crust formation.[18] This research formed the basis of his Harvard dissertation on eclogite and related high-pressure metamorphic rocks, analyzing samples from Norwegian localities to model deep crustal dynamics.[18] Complementing this, Schmitt spent two summers (1955 and another prior to his Caltech bachelor's) as an exploration geologist examining ultramafic intrusions on Duke Island in southeastern Alaska, documenting mineral compositions and alteration patterns in mafic complexes that later informed theories on volcanic and intrusive lunar analogs.[7] At Harvard, Schmitt held a teaching fellowship in 1961, instructing on ore deposit geology and integrating field data from his Norwegian and Alaskan surveys to illustrate economic geology principles applicable to extraterrestrial resource assessment.[19] These pre-NASA efforts established Schmitt's expertise in bridging terrestrial volcanism—particularly in regions like Alaska's volcanic fields and Norway's Caledonian orogeny—with emerging planetary science, prioritizing direct observation over speculative models.[20] His USGS mapping contributions, for instance, refined stratigraphic interpretations of lunar maria using Arizona's basaltic terrains as proxies, yielding preliminary assessments of potential lunar regolith resources.[15]NASA Astronaut Selection and Preparation Harrison Schmitt was selected in June 1965 as one of six scientists in NASA's Astronaut Group 4, the agency's first dedicated group of scientist-astronauts, chosen from over 1,000 applicants emphasizing advanced degrees in fields like geology to support Apollo's scientific goals over pure piloting experience.[15][21] This selection marked a shift from prior groups of military test pilots, prioritizing Ph.D. holders capable of conducting in-situ lunar observations and sample collection to maximize geological data return.[22] Schmitt's expertise in lunar and planetary geology, gained through his doctoral research at Harvard and work with the U.S. Geological Survey's Astrogeology Branch, positioned him to advocate within NASA for integrating geologists into flight crews rather than relying solely on pilot-astronauts with abbreviated field training.[15][4] Lacking prior aviation experience, Schmitt completed a 53-week Undergraduate Pilot Training course at Williams Air Force Base in Arizona, earning his wings as a jet pilot on December 7, 1966, alongside Air Force cadets to build proficiency in aircraft handling essential for spacecraft operations.[15][4] His preparation extended to specialized astronaut training, including centrifuge simulations for high-g reentry, zero-gravity parabolic flights, and water survival exercises in the Gulf of Mexico, alongside standard protocol for all Apollo candidates to ensure operational resilience in extraterrestrial environments.[23] A core element of Schmitt's preparation involved bridging scientific and operational roles through geological field training, where he designed and led simulations replicating lunar terrains, such as excursions to volcanic sites in Iceland, Hawaii, and the American Southwest, to instruct Apollo crews on rock identification, stratigraphic mapping, and sample documentation techniques.[22][23] This advocacy for rigorous, geologist-led pre-mission fieldwork addressed early Apollo limitations, where pilots' brief geology primers yielded suboptimal sample contexts, thereby enhancing the causal linkage between surface observations and laboratory analyses for deriving lunar formation models.[15] Schmitt also contributed to experiment design, including the Apollo Lunar Surface Experiments Package, integrating geophysical instruments to complement manual sampling during extravehicular activities.[23]Apollo 17 Mission and Lunar Geology Apollo 17 launched on December 7, 1972, from Kennedy Space Center, carrying commander Eugene Cernan, command module pilot Ronald Evans, and lunar module pilot Harrison Schmitt as the mission's trained geologist.[15] The spacecraft entered lunar orbit on December 10, and Cernan and Schmitt piloted the lunar module Challenger to a landing in the Taurus-Littrow valley on December 11 at 19:54 UTC, selected for its diverse geological features including ancient massifs, valley fill deposits, and evidence of potential late-stage highland volcanism.[24][25] Primary scientific objectives focused on sampling these features to understand lunar highland evolution and impact processes.[26] Cernan and Schmitt conducted three extravehicular activities (EVAs) totaling 22 hours and 4 minutes on the lunar surface, traversing approximately 30 kilometers using the Lunar Roving Vehicle to visit nine geological stations.[24][26] EVA-1 on December 12 explored the immediate landing site and Shorty Crater, EVA-2 on December 13 targeted the North Massif and Sculptured Hills, and EVA-3 on December 14 examined the South Massif and Camelot Crater, allowing systematic documentation of stratigraphy and rock distributions in real time.[24] Schmitt provided verbal descriptions to Mission Control, identifying breccias indicative of impact melting and boulder tracks revealing mass wasting from the massifs.[26] During these EVAs, the crew collected 110.5 kilograms of lunar material using tools such as tongs, rakes, scoops, and core tubes to depths of up to 2.3 meters, capturing documented samples from specific stations.[24] Notable collections included vivid orange soil at Shorty Crater, interpreted on-site as possible volcanic fire fountain ejecta due to its glassy beads and lack of weathering, and anorthositic troctolite 76535 from a rake sample at Station 6 near the North Massif base, recognized immediately for its coarse-grained primitive composition suggesting crystallization from a deep magma source.[27][28] Core samples preserved stratigraphic layers of valley fill, aiding inferences about depositional history from massifs and impacts.[26] Schmitt's real-time assessments emphasized the valley's complex geology, with light mantle deposits on slopes indicating recent mass movements and dark mantling at craters suggesting pyroclastic origins, while large boulders from highland massifs displayed megaregolith breccias with clasts from multiple lunar epochs.[26] These observations, transmitted live via television and audio, highlighted the site's value in testing hypotheses of lunar crustal differentiation and secondary cratering chains.[24]Post-Mission Scientific Analysis Schmitt contributed to the post-mission processing and petrographic analysis of Apollo 17's 110.5 kilograms of lunar regolith, rocks, and core samples at NASA's Lunar Receiving Laboratory, emphasizing stratigraphic correlations observed during extravehicular activities.[27][4] These efforts yielded empirical evidence of the site's geological history, including light mantle deposits overlying ancient highland breccias and mare basalts, with sample compositions revealing chemical gradients consistent with fractional crystallization in a differentiated lunar interior.[29] Anorthositic fragments and troctolites from the South Massif, such as those in the 700-series samples, confirmed early lunar differentiation around 4.4 billion years ago, where plagioclase-rich melts ascended to form a flotation crust amid a partially molten mantle, as traced through trace element partitioning and isotopic ratios in the returned materials.[30] High-titanium basalts from the Sculptured Hills, dated via argon-argon methods to 3.7–3.9 billion years, indicated localized mare volcanism sourced from ilmenite-bearing cumulates, providing causal links to mantle heterogeneity rather than uniform global melting.[31] In key publications, including contributions to the 1973 Apollo 17 Preliminary Science Report and proceedings of the Fourth Lunar Science Conference, Schmitt integrated field documentation with laboratory data to model planetary accretion dynamics, arguing that basin-scale impacts like Tycho (dated to ~108 million years ago) exposed deep crustal sections, thereby evidencing punctuated rather than gradual geological evolution.[29] These analyses refuted strict uniformitarian interpretations by quantifying the role of hypervelocity collisions in regolith generation and volatile retention, with glass spherules in the orange soil (sample 74220) demonstrating explosive fire-fountain eruptions tied to degassing of juvenile magmas.[4] Schmitt's involvement extended to curating preliminary sample allocations for global investigators and training geologists on handling extraterrestrial materials, leveraging his mission-derived insights to refine protocols for contamination-free study and contextual interpretation.[4] His frameworks influenced subsequent models of lunar interior evolution, prioritizing causal mechanisms like impact-induced melting over steady-state diffusion, and informed resource assessments for volatiles in polar regoliths.[32]Political Involvement Entry into Politics and 1976 Senate Victory Schmitt resigned from NASA on August 30, 1975, after serving as Assistant Administrator for Energy Programs since January 1974, to launch a bid for the U.S. Senate seat representing New Mexico as a Republican candidate.[33][2] His decision reflected a desire to apply scientific and geological expertise to national policy challenges, particularly in energy and natural resources, amid growing concerns over U.S. dependence on foreign oil following the 1973 embargo. In the Republican primary held on June 1, 1976, Schmitt easily defeated challenger Eugene Peirce, securing the nomination to challenge incumbent Democratic Senator Joseph Montoya, who had held the seat since 1964.[34] The general election campaign, spanning over 14 months, leveraged Schmitt's Apollo 17 astronaut credentials—earning him the nickname "Spaceman Jack" in contrast to Montoya's "Little Joe"—to underscore the practical benefits of space exploration for earthly technologies and economic growth.[35] Schmitt's platform centered on energy independence through expanded domestic production, emphasizing his background in lunar geology to advocate for balanced policies favoring free enterprise over excessive government intervention in resource extraction and development. He positioned himself against regulatory overreach that he argued hindered innovation and self-reliance in New Mexico's mineral-rich economy, including uranium, oil, and gas sectors. Montoya, criticized for downplaying Schmitt's scientific achievements, focused on traditional Democratic themes but struggled to counter the appeal of Schmitt's forward-oriented vision.[36] On November 2, 1976, Schmitt won the election with 234,681 votes, capturing 56.82% of the popular vote to Montoya's 43.18%, a decisive margin despite the post-Watergate environment that favored Democrats nationally and contributed to their net gain of two Senate seats overall.[37] This victory highlighted the electoral value of Schmitt's unique qualifications in a state dependent on extractive industries, enabling Republicans to retain influence in Senate resource committees.[33]Senate Tenure and Policy Positions Harrison Schmitt served as a U.S. Senator from New Mexico from January 3, 1977, to January 3, 1983, representing the Republican Party.[5] During this period, he focused on energy independence and technological advancement amid the ongoing 1970s energy crises triggered by OPEC embargoes and supply disruptions. Schmitt served on the Senate Committee on Commerce, Science, and Transportation, chairing its Subcommittee on Science, Technology, and Space during his final two years (1981–1983), where he advocated for sustained federal funding for NASA programs to maintain U.S. leadership in space exploration and related technologies.[38] Schmitt prioritized policies promoting domestic energy production, including support for nuclear power expansion and decontrol of oil prices to incentivize exploration and reduce reliance on foreign imports. In 1977, he introduced S. 2011, a bill aimed at regulatory reforms to streamline energy sector operations and bolster domestic oil production.[39] He participated in Senate hearings on the National Energy Conservation Policy Act (NECPA) of 1978, which built upon and amended provisions of the earlier Energy Policy and Conservation Act, emphasizing practical conservation measures alongside market-driven incentives rather than stringent mandates.[40] In bipartisan efforts, Schmitt collaborated on legislation addressing energy security, such as amendments facilitating strategic petroleum reserves and efficiency standards, while critiquing overly prescriptive regulations that he argued lacked sufficient empirical grounding and hindered economic recovery. His committee work contributed to debates on balancing environmental protections with the need for reliable, affordable energy sources, including uranium mining and nuclear facilities critical to New Mexico's economy.[40]

Edgar Mitchell Edgar Mitchell (born September 17, 1930, Hereford, Texas, U.S.—died February 4, 2016, West Palm Beach, Florida) was an American astronaut who was a member, with Commander Alan B. Shepard, Jr., and Stuart A. Roosa, of the Apollo 14 mission (January 31–February 9, 1971), in which the uplands region north of the Fra Mauro crater on the Moon was explored by Mitchell and Shepard. Mitchell entered the U.S. Navy after earning a B.S. in industrial management (1952) from the Carnegie Institute of Technology in Pittsburgh. He was awarded a B.S. in aeronautics (1961) by the U.S. Navy Postgraduate School and a doctorate in aeronautics and astronautics (1964) by the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. In 1966 Mitchell joined the manned space program. During the 1970 Apollo 13 mission, when an oxygen tank explosion damaged the spacecraft, Mitchell worked in the lunar module simulator at the Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center in Houston to develop procedures for the safe return of the spacecraft and its crew. He received (1970) a Presidential Medal of Freedom for his work. During the Apollo 14 mission, Mitchell, Shepard, and Roosa, set records for the most time (33 hours) and longest distance traversed on the lunar surface. They also collected 42.6 kg (94 pounds) of rock and soil samples to be studied. - In full: - Edgar Dean Mitchell - Died: - February 4, 2016, West Palm Beach, Florida (aged 85) - Awards And Honors: - Presidential Medal of Freedom (1970) In 1972 Mitchell retired from the navy and the space program. His interest in parapsychology led him to found (1973) the Institute of Noetic Sciences in Palo Alto, California. He wrote The Way of the Explorer (1996), about his experiences as an astronaut and his spiritual journey.

After obtaining a degree in Industrial Management from the Carnegie Institute of Technology in 1952, Mitchell joined the Navy and completed his basic training at the San Diego Recruit Depot. In May 1953, Mitchell completed instruction at the Officer's Candidate School at Newport, Rhode Island and was commissioned as an Ensign. From 1957 - 1958, Mitchell flew A3 aircraft while assigned to Heavy Attack Squadron Two deployed aboard the USS Bon Homme Richard (CV-31) and the USS Ticonderoga (CV-14). Mitchell earned a Doctor of Science degree in Aeronautics and Astronautics from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1964. Selected by NASA to be an astronaut in April 1966, Mitchell subsequently served as a member of the astronaut support crew for Apollo IX and served as a backup lunar module pilot for Apollo X. On 31 January 1971, serving as lunar module pilot, Mitchell embarked on a journey over 500,000 miles through space aboard the Apollo XIV spacecraft. Following Apollo XIV, Mitchell was the backup lunar module pilot for Apollo XVI. In 1972, Mitchell retired from both NASA and the U.S. Navy and founded the Institute for Noetic Sciences in 1973. Mitchell devoted himself to the exploration of the human mind, physics, and the unexplained. He passed on 4 February 2016; his death coincided with the 45th anniversary of the Apollo XIV mission.
