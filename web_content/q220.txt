--- [Emperor Xizong of Jin - Wikipedia] ---
Emperor Xizong of Jin (28 February 1119 – 9 January 1150), personal name Hela, sinicised name Wanyan Dan, was the third emperor of the Jurchen-led Jin dynasty of China. He reigned for about 15 years from 1135 to 1150. During his reign, the Jin dynasty launched several military campaigns against the Han-led Southern Song dynasty in southern China.

A comprehensive overview of the 119-year history of the Jin dynasty, from its founding by Wanyan Aguda to its conquest by Mongol cavalry(from:坚剩柔0j) 06:49 The Jin Dynasty (1115–1234) was a feudal dynasty in Chinese history established by the Jurchen people, ruling over northern and Northeastern China . It was founded by Wanyan Aguda (Wanyan Min). It bordered the Western Xia and the Mongols to the west, and faced the Southern Song to the south. The dynasty saw a succession of ten emperors and lasted for 119 years. 1 After unifying the various Jurchen tribes, Emperor Taizu of Jin, Wanyan Aguda (Wanyan Min), raised an army in rebellion against the Liao Dynasty . In 1115, he established the state, named Jin, in Shangjing Huining Prefecture (present-day Acheng District, Harbin City , Heilongjiang Province), adopting the era name "Shouguo". The Jin Dynasty conquered the Liao Dynasty in 1125; in 1127, it further conquered the The Northern Song Dynasty , an event historically known as the Jingkang Incident . In 1130, Emperor Gaozong of Song, Zhao Gou , submitted a letter of surrender, acknowledging the Jin Dynasty as his sovereign. Emperor Xizong of Jin began a gradual process of Sinicization. In 1153, Prince of Hailing, Wanyan Liang , moved the capital to Zhongdu (present-day Beijing). During the reigns of Emperor Shizong of Jin and Emperor Zhangzong of Jin, the political situation in the north was stable, and politics and economy reached their peak, a period historically referred to as the "Great Peace of Dading and Mingchang". In the later years of Emperor Zhangzong's reign, the dynasty rapidly declined from its peak. During the reign of Emperor Weishaowang of Jin, it was defeated by the Mongols, and internal troubles and external threats became frequent. After Emperor Xuanzong of Jin ascended the throne, internal political corruption and widespread hardship among the people, coupled with southern invasions by the Great Mongol State, forced the dynasty to move its capital to Bianjing (present-day Kaifeng, Henan). In 1234, the Jin Dynasty fell at Caizhou under the joint attack from the Southern Song in the south and the Mongols in the north. 2 At its height, the territory of the Jin Dynasty included Northeastern China , North China, the Guanzhong region, the Central Plains, the Huang-Huai region, and the Russian Far East. Its southern boundary extended to a line from the Sanguan Pass to the Huaihe River , facing the Southern Song ; it bordered the Western Xia to the northwest; its northeastern territory reached the Outer Khingan Range, with its eastern border adjacent to the Sea of Japan. Externally, Goryeo and the Western Xia acknowledged its suzerainty. 95 In its early period, the Jin Dynasty adopted the Boji Lie system, a form of aristocratic council, later gradually transitioning from a dual political structure to a singular Han Chinese system. Militarily, it implemented the Meng'an Mouke system, which integrated military and civilian organization. The Jin Dynasty's ceramic and iron smelting industries flourished, and its border markets for foreign trade also controlled the economic lifeline of the Western Xia . Culturally, the Jin Dynasty rapidly Sinicized; variety play and traditional opera developed significantly during this period, with the Jin Dynasty's Yuanben laying the foundation for the Zaju of Yuan Qu . When Wanyan Aguda (Wanyan Min) established the state, he said: "The Liao took hard iron as its name, signifying its strength. Although hard iron is strong, it will eventually deteriorate. Only gold does not change or decay. The color of gold is white, and the Wanyan tribe esteems the color white." Thus, the state name "Great Jin" was decided, which is pronounced "amba-an antʃu-un" in the Jurchen language. One theory suggests that the Jurchen rose from the Jin River (Anchuhu River or Anchun River), hence the state was named Jin, and thus "Jinyuan" became an alternative name for the Jin Dynasty. Another view holds that initially the Jin Dynasty had no state name; when they formed the Alliance on the Sea with the Song Dynasty , the Song, considering itself to possess the virtue of Fire, suggested that the Jurchen adopt "Jin" (Gold, representing the Metal element) as their state name, hoping to suppress the Jurchen (as Metal overcomes Wood, which was associated with the Khitan Liao). The Jurchen, not yet familiar with Han culture, unwittingly adopted it as their state name. 2 One perspective argues that at the beginning of its founding, the Jin Dynasty did not designate its state name as Great Jin, but was simply called the "Jurchen State," and it was not until 1117 or 1118 that it formally established a state upon the suggestion of the Bohai people's advisor Yang Pu. The later state name Jin was derived from the Anchuhu River, known for producing gold. Emperor Taizu of Jin's statement about "Liao taking hard iron as its name..." is considered a later fabrication, and even the entire founding history of the Jin Dynasty was a constructed pseudo-history by later Jin officials to prove the Jin Dynasty's Mandate of Heaven. Initially, Aguda merely sought an independent status for the Jurchen people; later, seeing the Khitan repeatedly defeated in battle and signs of their demise apparent, he then proclaimed himself emperor and established a state, harboring ambitions to contend for the realm. The Jin Dynasty tampered with its founding history to conceal the historical fact that it initially sought investiture from the Liao, aiming to portray that the Jurchen had established the ambition to overthrow the Liao and replace it soon after their uprising, thereby reflecting an orthodox concept of Jin succeeding the Liao. Another viewpoint refutes this interpretation, arguing that the founding history recorded in the History of Jin is accurate, Emperor Taizu of Jin indeed had ambitions to contend for the realm when establishing the state, and there was no state name "Jurchen State." 12 History Rise of the Jurchen The Jin Dynasty was established by the Jurchen people from the northeastern region, who lived by fishing and hunting. Chinese historical documents consistently state that the Jurchens were one part of the Mohe tribes. The most culturally advanced among the Mohe, the Sumo Mohe, established the Bohai Kingdom in the early 8th century. Another part of the Mohe consisted of seven tribes known as the "Heishui Mohe" (Black Water Mohe), inhabiting the middle and lower reaches of the Heilongjiang River. The first ancestors of the Jurchen people originated from the Heishui Mohe. 13 During the Five Dynasties period, the Mohe had tribes such as the Wanyan clan, who were subjects of the Bohai Kingdom. After the Liao Dynasty conquered and destroyed the Bohai Kingdom, it incorporated the Jurchens from the southern regions, calling them the "Ripe Jurchen" (Shu Nüzhen), while those in the north were called the "Raw Jurchen" (Sheng Nüzhen). The Khitan policy towards the Jurchen people was one of "divide and rule." They enticed powerful clans and major surnames to the Liaodong Peninsula , enrolling them into Khitan nationality, calling them the "Hesuguan" (also written as Hesuguan or Hesugun, meaning "fence" or "palisade" in the Jurchen language). These people became the "Ripe Jurchen." Another part remained north of the Sumo River (the northern-flowing section of the Songhua River ) and east of Ningjiangzhou (present-day Fuyu City , Jilin); these were the "Raw Jurchen." The descendants of the Heishui Mohe formed the main body of the Raw Jurchen. The Wanyan clan resided in the upper reaches of the Yalu River and the Tumen River basin. The Jurchen ethnic group was composed of the Zhulizhen people from this area, who were associated with pearl-gathering communities. As a branch of the Heishui Mohe, the Wanyan clan migrated from the banks of the Wanyan River to the shores of the Ashi River . The Wanyan surname was the royal clan among the Jurchen people. The Jurchen people migrated from the Changbai Mountains to the middle reaches of the Heilongjiang River, and the Wanyan clan settled in the Wanda Mountain . 14 Emperor Taizu of Jin, Wanyan Aguda By the time of Wanyan Hanpu, the Jurchen had already entered the late stage of a patriarchal clan society. In the early 11th century, during the time of Wanyan Suike, they migrated again to the Haigu River, and society underwent significant changes. They began smelting iron, cultivating land and planting trees, constructing houses, building boats, and settled by the Anchuhu River (present-day Ashi River , southeast of Harbin City, Heilongjiang Province). His son, Shilu, after becoming chieftain, conquered nearby tribes and established a tribal confederation. Shilu's son, Wanyan Wugunai, merged many more tribes. The large Jurchen military tribal confederation took shape during the period of Wanyan Wugunai, but the alliance was not united and was quite fragile internally. Subsequently, struggles mainly revolved around the alliance chieftain and the Guoxiang (State Chancellor). 15 In the late Liao Dynasty, court administration was chaotic. Emperor Tianzuo of Liao was incompetent and foolish. The Liao court continuously demanded tribute and oppressed the Jurchen people. In 1112, when Emperor Tianzuo went to Changchunzhou to meet with chieftains of various Jurchen tribes, he wantonly insulted the chieftains, which made Wanyan Aguda (Wanyan Min) determined to rebel against the Liao court. In 1113, Aguda succeeded his elder brother Wuyashu as the alliance leader, assuming the title of Du Bojilie. 16 Founding the State and Destroying Liao In September 1114, Wanyan Aguda (Wanyan Min) ordered Jurchen forces from various tribes to pledge an oath at the Laishui River (present-day Lalin River), initiating a decade-long war against the Liao Dynasty. Subsequently, the Jurchen army defeated Liao forces at the Victory at Ningjiangzhou and the Battle of Chuhedian. 106 The Rise of the Jin Dynasty On the first day of the first lunar month in the summer calendar of 1115, Aguda declared himself emperor at the "Huangdizhai" (Emperor's Camp, present-day Harbin, Heilongjiang), founding the state with the name "Great Jin" (Da Jin) and the era name "Shouguo." He is known as Emperor Taizu of Jin. It was only then that Emperor Tianzuo of Liao began to take the matter seriously and ordered a personal expedition. However, the Liao army was defeated by the Jurchen forces, while simultaneously, rebellions led by Yelü Zhangnu and Gao Yongchang broke out within the Liao state. 17 After founding the state, Emperor Taizu of Jin targeted the five capitals of Liao, dividing his armies into two routes to launch the Jin conquest of the Liao. In May 1116, the eastern route army captured Dongjing Liaoyang Prefecture. In 1120, the western route army captured Shangjing Linhuang Prefecture, causing the Liao Dynasty to lose half of its territory. During the war, the The Northern Song Dynasty successively sent envoys Ma Zheng and Zhao Liangsi to establish the Alliance on the Sea with the Jin Dynasty, agreeing to jointly attack the Liao Dynasty. In 1122, the eastern route army captured Zhongjing Dading Prefecture, and Emperor Tianzuo fled to the desert. Simultaneously, the western route army also captured Xijing Datong Prefecture. Yelü Dashi and others enthroned Yelü Chun at Nanjing Xijin Prefecture, establishing the Northern Liao. The Northern Song also sent Tong Guan and others to lead armies multiple times to attack Liao's Nanjing and other areas of the Sixteen Prefectures of Yan and Yun, but they were all repelled by Liao forces. The Northern Song finally requested the Jin army to capture Liao's Nanjing, leading to the fall of the Northern Liao. By this point, all five Liao capitals had been captured by Jin forces. After negotiations between the Song and Jin, the Jin army ceded some cities of the Sixteen Prefectures to Song and received annual tribute. However, the Northern Song ultimately received only a collection of empty cities that had been looted by the Jin army. 17 In 1123, Emperor Taizu of Jin died and was succeeded by his younger brother Wanyan Wuqimai (Wanyan Sheng), known as Emperor Taizong of Jin. Emperor Taizong continued the campaign against Liao forces in the Datong area. In the first month of 1124, in order to ally with the Western Xia to destroy Liao, Emperor Taizong ceded Liao territory north of Xiazhai and south of the Yin Mountains to Western Xia. Western Xia then switched its allegiance and became a vassal to the Jin Dynasty. In 1125, Emperor Tianzuo of Liao was captured, marking the end of the Liao Dynasty. Meanwhile, Yelü Dashi led his troops westward and established the Western Liao in the Western Regions. 17 Entering the Central Plains Main articles: Jingkang Incident, Treaty of Shaoxing After destroying the Liao Dynasty, the Jin Dynasty intended to move south to conquer the The Northern Song Dynasty . Using the Northern Song's harboring of the Liao general Zhang Jue during the Pingzhou Incident as a violation of prior Song-Jin agreements as a pretext, Emperor Taizong of Jin launched the Jin conquest of the Song in 1125. He appointed the Bojilie Wanyan Xieye ( Wanyan Gao ) as the Grand Marshal, dividing the army into two routes advancing south from Shanxi and Hebei, aiming to converge at the Northern Song capital, Kaifeng. With the Song general Li Gang defending Kaifeng tenaciously, both sides declared a truce and began peace negotiations. 18 Encyclopedia x Hunzhi: Illustrated Jingkang Incident In 1126, Emperor Taizong of Jin again used the Song court's breach of the treaty as a reason to send Wanyan Zongwang and Wanyan Zonghan to attack Kaifeng via two separate routes. This event is historically known as the Jingkang Incident, which led to the fall of the Northern Song Dynasty. However, Prince Kang of Song, Zhao Gou, managed to escape by chance. He declared himself emperor and re-established the Song Dynasty at the Southern Song's provisional capital, Yingtian Prefecture (present-day Shangqiu, Henan), becoming Emperor Gaozong of Song, historically known as the Southern Song. At this time, to govern the vast newly conquered Han territories, the Jin Dynasty successively established puppet states such as Zhang Chu and Liu Qi. It also repeatedly sent Jin generals like Wanyan Zongbi to lead southern expeditions against Emperor Gaozong, who had fled to the Jiangnan region. However, through the efforts of Southern Song generals such as Yue Fei , Han Shizhong , and Zhang Jun, the Southern Song repeatedly turned peril into safety. Ultimately, the Jin Dynasty forced the Southern Song to submit as a vassal and compelled states like Western Xia and Goryeo to become subjects, achieving hegemony in East Asia. 17 In 1135, Emperor Taizong of Jin died and was succeeded by Emperor Taizu's grandson, Wanyan Dan, known as Emperor Xizong of Jin. At that time, some meritorious officials assisting the Jin court were called "Yancheng meritorious officials." They were mainly divided into a hawkish faction and a dovish faction. Emperor Xizong abolished the Liu Qi puppet state in 1137. Subsequently, following the advice of the dovish faction leader Wanyan Talan (Wanyan Chang), he negotiated peace with the Southern Song's dovish faction, represented by Emperor Gaozong and Qin Hui . 21 The cession of the Henan and Shaanxi territories dissatisfied the pro-war faction leader Wanyan Zongbi. In the tenth year of the Shaoxing era (1140), Wanyan Zongbi led his army to recapture the Henan and Shaanxi lands. The following year, Wanyan Zongbi launched another southern expedition but was defeated by Yue Fei and Liu Qi . After the Battle of Yancheng, Yue Fei advanced north again, approaching Bianjing. After ascending the throne, he and Wanyan Zongbi promoted reforms to adopt Han institutions and heavily employed Han Chinese. The following year, he appointed the three Yancheng meritorious officials, Wanyan Zongpan, Wanyan Zonggan, and Wanyan Zonghan, to jointly oversee government institutions, "jointly managing the affairs of the Three Departments." The Jin Dynasty's bureaucratic system was largely sinicized by this time, establishing a Three Departments system centered on the Department of State Affairs. Emperor Xizong was controlled by the Yancheng meritorious officials and the empress, and being excessively repressed, he neglected state affairs in his later years, wantonly killing innocent people. He was finally assassinated in 1150 by the Right Chancellor, Prince of Hailing Wanyan Liang, who then declared himself emperor, historically known as Prince of Hailing. 23 Emperor Wanyan Liang, in order to conquer the Southern Song and unify the Chinese region, implemented many measures: he moved the capital to Yanjing, which became Zhongdu (present-day Beijing), and intended to move south to Bianjing (present-day Kaifeng, Henan); he reorganized the administrative divisions into fourteen circuits for easier management; he brought the armies stationed in Shangjing Huining Prefecture (present-day Baicheng Village, Ashe Street, Acheng District, Harbin City, Heilongjiang Province) under the jurisdiction of Emperor Taizong of Jin and the Yancheng meritorious officials Wanyan Zonggan and Wanyan Zonghan under the direct control of the Jin court, laying the foundation for the centralization of power in the Jin Dynasty. 24 Wanyan Liang, who moved the capital to Yanjing However, Emperor Wanyan Liang harbored deep suspicions towards the imperial clan, and nearly all descendants of Emperor Taizong of Jin were killed by Wanyan Liang. After this, the Jin Dynasty no longer attempted to conquer the Song. 24 The Reigns of Shizong and Zhangzong In 1161, Emperor Shizong of Jin raised an army to proclaim the crimes of Emperor Wanyan Liang, unified North China with his forces, and halted the southern expeditions to conquer the Song. However, the war between Song and Jin did not end. In 1162, using the Southern Song's refusal to submit as a vassal as a pretext, he dispatched the chief commander Pusanzhongyi to station at Bianjing and Geshilie Zhining to guard the front line, preparing to recapture the Huainan region. At this time, Emperor Xiaozong of Song intended to recover lost territories and sent the chief commander Zhang Jun, leading Li Xianzhong and Shao Hongyuan, to launch a northern expedition, historically known as the Longxing Northern Expedition. At that time, uprisings by various ethnic groups were frequent. To maintain his rule, he used systems like the imperial examinations and schools to win the support of the Han Chinese nobility, while also strengthening the power of the Meng'an Mouke and expanding the land occupied by the Jurchen people. All these measures led to a certain degree of recovery and development in the Jin Dynasty's economy and culture, a period historically known as the Dading Prosperity. 26 Lugou Bridge built during the Dading era of Emperor Shizong Besides resisting the Southern Song's northern expeditions, Emperor Shizong of Jin also dispatched troops to intimidate Western Xia and Goryeo, forcing these two states to submit to the Jin Dynasty. He was called the "Little Yao and Shun" in the History of Jin. After Emperor Shizong's death in 1189, since the Crown Prince Wanyan Yungong had died early, Yungong's son Wanyan Jing was enthroned, becoming Emperor Zhangzong of Jin. 24 In the early period of his reign, Emperor Zhangzong's politics were deeply sinicized, and culture flourished greatly, a period historically known as the Mingchang Prosperity. At this time, the Jin military gradually fell into neglect, while the Mongol tribes in the north rose. In 1206, the Mongol chieftain Genghis Khan unified the vast territories north and south of the Gobi Desert, establishing the Great Mongol State. 26 At that time, the Mongols harbored serious hostility towards the Jin Dynasty, intending to break free from its control. Genghis Khan also knew that Wanyan Yongji was incompetent, considering it a good opportunity to attack and destroy the Jin Dynasty. Genghis Khan first attacked Western Xia to dismantle the Jin-Xia alliance and avoid being constrained by it during the campaign against Jin. When Western Xia sought aid from the Jin Dynasty, Emperor Wanyan Yongji took pleasure in his neighbor being attacked and watched without helping. Eventually, Western Xia submitted to the Mongols and turned to assist them against the Jin. After eliminating this concern, Genghis Khan severed relations with the Jin Dynasty in 1210. The following year, he launched the Mongol-Jin War, decisively defeating the 400,000-strong Jin army led by Chancellor Wanyan Chengyu and general Duji Sizhong at Yehuling (located at the border of Zhangbei County and Wanquan District , Zhangjiakou, Hebei Province). Emperor Wanyan Yongji replaced the chancellor with the strategically skilled Tudan Yi. The Mongol army then invaded North China, plundering extensively, and finally besieged the Jin capital, Zhongdu (present-day Beijing), but withdrew due to the city's strong defenses. In 1212, Genghis Khan launched another southern expedition against the Jin, once besieging the Jin's western capital, Xijing Datong Prefecture. That same year, the Khitan Yelü Liuge rebelled against the Jin and submitted to the Mongols in the northeast, defeating Jin troops at Dijinao'er (near present-day Changtu, Liaoning). The Mongol army again approached Zhongdu. In 1213, the general Hushahu (Geshilie Zhizhong) killed Emperor Wanyan Yongji and installed Emperor Zhangzong's elder half-brother Wanyan Xun as emperor, becoming Emperor Xuanzong of Jin. 31 Situation of the Battle of Yehuling In 1213, shortly after Emperor Xuanzong of Jin ascended the throne, Hushahu (Geshilie Zhizhong) held the power of the Jin Dynasty. Hushahu (Geshilie Zhizhong) threatened Shuhu Gaoqi, the defender of Zhongdu, for his ineffective military performance, but was ultimately killed by him. In the autumn of the same year, Genghis Khan launched a three-pronged attack on the Jin. He sent his son Jochi to campaign in Shanxi, his brother Hasar to Hebei, while he and his youngest son Tolui advanced into Shandong. The Jin Dynasty lost control of all areas except for eleven cities, including Zhongdu, Zhending, and Daming. The following year, Emperor Xuanzong of Jin sued for peace, offering gold and Qi Country Princess to Genghis Khan, leading to a peace agreement between the Mongols and the Jin. 32 Southern Move to Bianliang After the Mongol army withdrew, Emperor Xuanzong of Jin, disregarding the opposition from ministers like Tudan Yi and together with Shuhu Gaoqi, moved the capital to Nanjing Bianjing (modern-day Kaifeng, Henan) in 1214, leaving only the crown prince to defend Zhongdu. This move caused unease among the military and civilians in Hebei. In 1215, citing the Jin emperor's southern relocation as a pretext, the Mongols again led their army to capture Zhongdu, thereby occupying the Hebei region. In October of the same year, Puxian Wannu declared independence in Liaodong, establishing the Dongzhen State. At this time, the Jin's ancestral homeland of Liaodong was divided between Puxian Wannu and Yelü Liuge, while Shandong and Hebei were under the control of the In the red jacket rebels. The Jin court could only control the regions of Henan, northern Huai, and Guanzhong. Concurrently, the Yellow River flooded extensively, changing its course to flow southeastward after the Jin court's southern move, causing widespread disaster. 33 After Emperor Xuanzong of Jin moved south, the state's power weakened further. The Mongols had already replaced the Jin as the dominant power in East Asia. However, as Genghis Khan became embroiled in a dispute with the Khwarazmian Empire and launched his western expedition, he appointed Muqali to govern the Han territories, bestowing upon him the title "Taishi Guowang" (Grand Preceptor King), who continued to threaten the Jin. However, this proved ineffective. He also stipulated that those who sold themselves into slavery could redeem themselves with a person of equal labor capacity. However, in practice, the possibility of such redemption was minimal. The Han residents forced to migrate could not help but become slaves in large numbers. 55 The Jin court used forced relocation to move people from surrendered and attached areas to the interior. For example, residents of Shanxi prefectures and counties were relocated en masse to the Jin Shangjing (Upper Capital) and even the Hunhe Road area. Residents from the Shangjing region were, in turn, moved to Ningjiang Zhou. After the people of Pingzhou rebelled and were suppressed, they, along with the people of the four prefectures of Runzhou, Xizhou, Laizhou, and Qianzhou, were relocated to the Eastern Capital, Shenyang. These residents, struggling to survive, were forced to sell themselves into slavery, leading to deep-seated hatred among the Han people. The Jurchens seized the most fertile farmland from the Han. To cover increasing living and military expenses, they also continuously increased the taxes and corvée labor imposed on the Han. Culture Script The Jurchen script and the Chinese script were the official scripts in common use during the Jin Dynasty. The Jurchen script was created by modifying Khitan characters, which were themselves based on Chinese characters, to phonetically represent the Jurchen language. Originally, the Jurchen people used Khitan script. With the establishment of the Jin Dynasty, Wanyan Xiyin, obeying the order of Emperor Taizu of Jin, created the Jurchen script by referencing the Chinese and Khitan scripts, and it was officially promulgated in the eighth month of 1191. In 1165, Tushan Ziwen, by referring to Khitan-script editions, translated works such as "Zhengguan Zhenyao" (Essentials of Government from the Zhenguan Period) and "Bashi Celi" (Bai's Forest of Policies). During the reign of Emperor Shizong of Jin, the court established a Translation Bureau to translate Chinese Confucian classics and historical texts into Jurchen script, and subsequently translated many other Chinese books. Emperor Shizong told his ministers: "The reason I ordered the translation of the Five Classics is so that the Jurchen people will know where benevolence, righteousness, morality, and virtue reside." 73 However, at that time, translation between Jurchen script and Chinese script required an intermediate step: first translating into Khitan script and then converting. During the reign of Emperor Zhangzong of Jin, a special Hongwen Institute was established for translating and transcribing Confucian classics, and officials were appointed to lecture on them. In 1191, the use of Khitan script was abolished, and it was stipulated that henceforth, Jurchen script should be translated directly into Chinese characters. However, with the increasing prevalence of the Chinese language, many Jurchen nobles became literate in Chinese characters. Chinese-character books became widely popular among the Jurchen people. 70 Intellectual Thought The Jin Dynasty adopted Confucianism as its fundamental ideology, while Daoism, Buddhism, and Legalism also circulated and were applied quite widely. Jin Dynasty thinkers engaged in discussions and critiques of the neo-Confucianism and classical exegesis of the Song Dynasties (Northern and Southern), leading to a renewed flourishing of neo-Confucianism in the north and the development of Chinese thought. In terms of scholarly thought, Zhao Bingwen was known as the "Master of Orthodox Confucian Principle." He criticized the tradition of textual commentary and annotation that had existed since the Han Dynasty, and fully affirmed the neo-Confucianism established by Zhou Dunyi and the Cheng brothers (Cheng Hao and Cheng Yi) of the Northern Song. He also integrated Buddhist and Daoist ideas with neo-Confucian thought, earning a reputation in the Jin for defending the Confucian orthodoxy. 65 Wang Ruoxu criticized the tradition of commentary and annotation, stating its shortcomings were too numerous to mention, and affirmed Northern Song neo-Confucianism. However, he also criticized Northern Song neo-Confucianism and took great pains to comment on and evaluate the annotations of Song Dynasty neo-Confucians, though he did not establish a distinct school of thought himself. Li Chunfu authored works such as "Zhongyong Jijie" (Collected Explications of the Doctrine of the Mean) and "Mingdao Jijie" (Collected Explications on Elucidating the Way). His intellectual journey shifted first from Confucianism to Daoism, and finally to Buddhism, "known as the School of Mind in China and Western cultural teachings." He said: "When learning reaches Buddhism, there is nothing left to learn," believing that Song Confucians like Cheng Yi "all stealthily took from our Buddhist books." Aiming for a Buddhist-centered unity of Confucianism, Daoism, and Buddhism, he boldly challenged Song neo-Confucianism. 66 Regarding political thought, Zhao Bingwen believed that the relationships between the royal court and feudal states, between the Hua (civilized) and the Yi (barbarians), and between the Central States (China) and border regions were all mutable. He considered anyone with a mind for the public good of the world to be "Han" (civilized), and believed that compared to the people, the people were precious while the state altars (sheji) were of lesser importance. He opposed the view that the calamities of the late Kaiyuan era of the Tang Dynasty "began with imperial consorts, were accomplished by eunuchs, and ended with regional military governors," arguing the root cause was "Emperor Minghuang." Wang Ruoxu believed that unifying China must consider the "principle of right and wrong (quzhi)." He argued that Ouyang Xiu 's advocacy for unification ignored this principle of right and wrong, and was "bowing flatteringly to his own dynasty, falsely embellishing the ruler's faults." He believed a state's survival or demise could be left to fate, but one must not commit the crime of cannibalism in the name of preserving loyalty and integrity. He also praised Sima Guang 's criticism of the traditional view of legitimate dynastic succession (zhengrun), stating, "As for the theory of legitimate succession, I follow Sima Guang." 71 Like the Song Dynasty, the Jin Dynasty revered Confucianism and Confucius. Early on, when Jin troops advanced to Qufu, soldiers intended to destroy Confucius's tomb but were stopped by Wanyan Zonghan. Reverence for Confucius began from the reign of Emperor Xizong of Jin, who established a Confucian temple in the Upper Capital (Shangjing) and enfeoffed a descendant of Confucius as the Duke of Fulfilling the Sage (Yanshenggong). Although Emperor Wanyan Liang of Jin looked down on Confucianism, Emperors Shizong and Zhangzong vigorously revived the reverence for Confucius and Confucianism, repairing Confucian temples and temple schools, and promoting texts like the "Book of Documents" and " Mencius ." 73 Literature Literature in the early Jin Dynasty was relatively simple and unadorned, with most literary figures being Liao and Song individuals like Han Fang. It was not until the emergence of Cai Gui that literature was recognized as having a true orthodox transmission within the Jin; other figures included Dang Huaiying , as well as Zhao Feng , Wang Tingyun, Wang Ji, Liu Congyi, and others. Notable literary figures during the reign of Emperor Zhangzong of Jin included Zhao Bingwen, Yang Yunyi, Li Chunfu, and Yuan Haowen . Among the Jurchen people, notable figures included Emperor Wanyan Liang, Emperor Shizong, and Emperor Zhangzong. When Emperor Wanyan Liang of Jin invaded the Southern Song, he composed a poem in Yangzhou containing the lines: "Leading a million troops by the side of West Lake, / I halt my steed atop the highest peak of Mount Wu." The Prince of Hailing, aspiring to destroy the Song and unify China, expressed his ambition in poetry, which was powerful in style and grand in vision. Notable Jin Dynasty literary scholars included Wang Ruoxu and Yuan Haowen. Wang Ruoxu authored the "Collection of the Old Man of Hunan," excelling in poetry, prose, and the textual criticism of classics and histories. He preliminarily established grammar and rhetoric. In discussing history, he attacked Song Qi; in discussing poetry and prose, he esteemed Su Shi while belittling Huang Tingjian . He was an authoritative critic in the Jin Dynasty, and later Pan Shengxiao's "Examples of Inscriptions on Metal and Stone" was influenced by him. Whether Buddhism or Daoism in the Jin period, both advocated a trinity of Buddhism, Daoism, and Confucianism centered on their own doctrines, as seen in figures like Wansong Xingxiu and Li Chunfu, who had profound achievements in Buddhist theoretical development. Buddhism had already spread during the Jurchen tribal period. After the Jin Dynasty conquered the Liao Dynasty and The Northern Song Dynasty , and was influenced by Central Plain Buddhism, faith in Buddhism developed further. Buddhist schools such as Huayan, Chan, Pure Land, Esoteric Buddhism, and Vinaya all developed considerably. Among these, the Chan School was particularly prevalent, which can be said to be entirely due to the influence of Northern Song Buddhism, significantly impacting the Jin Dynasty's society, economy, politics, culture, and customs. After the Jurchen occupation of the Central Plains, Daoxun succeeded Jingru in propagating the Dharma at Lingyan Temple, authoring works such as "Extensive Sayings for the Assembly," "Investigations and Discriminations on Pilgrimage," and "Eulogies on Ancient Cases and Hymns of Praise." 78 In Bianliang, there was a great propagation of Buddhist teachings during the 'Buddha Sun' period. The dharma-transmitting disciple Yuanxing, at the invitation during the Dading era, presided over the Tanzhe Mountain Temple in Yanjing, vigorously reviving Chan Buddhism. He authored three collections of recorded sayings which circulated widely. Wansong Xingxiu was a particularly renowned Chan master of the Jin Dynasty. He transmitted the Chan lineage of the Qingyuan branch of the Caodong school, succeeding Master Xueman of the Daming Temple in Cizhou. Although he specialized in Chan studies, he regularly practiced based on the Avatamsaka Sutra. He once commented on and chanted Tiantong's One Hundred Old Cases at the Congrong Hermitage, compiling the Congrong Lu (Record of the Congrong Hermitage), which became a famous Chan work. He also held syncretic ideas integrating the Three Teachings, often advising the important minister Yelü Chucai to govern the state with Confucianism and govern the mind with Buddhism, earning high praise from Chucai, who said he "possessed the bloodline of Caodong, the skillful means of Yunmen, and the sharpness of Linji," which was widely acclaimed at the time. 78 Taoism The Jin Dynasty saw the emergence of three major new Taoist schools: the Quanzhen School, the Great Tao School (Da Dao Jiao), and the Taiyi School. The founder of the Quanzhen School was Wang Zhe, who established it in 1167. It was later successively led by his seven disciples. Besides inheriting traditional Chinese Taoist thought, the Quanzhen School reorganized elements beyond talismans, registers, and elixirs, laying the foundation for modern Taoism. The founder of the Great Tao School was Liu Deren in the early Jin period, who began preaching around 1142. It advocated "preserving qi and nurturing the spirit," promoted self-reliance, having few desires and little thought, did not discuss ascension, refinement, or immortality, and incorporated Confucian thought into its system. Furthermore, the Great Tao School had a monastic system. The founder of the Taiyi School was Xiao Baozhen, who established it in 1138. It focused on talismanic and ritual methods, and also had inner cultivation practices emphasizing softness and weakness. It venerated Taiyi. The Taiyi School imitated the esoteric transmission principle of the Way of the Celestial Masters, requiring each generation's leader to change their surname to "Xiao." Its founding purpose was "to deliver sentient beings from suffering and distress," and it respected human relations. 78 Wang Zhe, the founder of the Quanzhen School, always named the associations he established with references to the Three Teachings. As stated in Wanyan Shou's Stele of the Quanzhen Patriarch: "This sufficiently shows its profound emptiness, clear subtlety, tranquil silence, and perfect harmony; it does not exclusively belong to one teaching." Proceeding from his advocacy of the unity of the Three Teachings, Wang Zhe urged people to recite Taoist, Buddhist, and Confucian classics such as the Tao Te Ching (Classic of the Way and Virtue), the Heart Sutra, and the Classic of Filial Piety. 78 Shamanism The Jurchen people believed in Shamanism, a primitive religion encompassing beliefs such as nature worship, totemism, animism, ancestor worship, and witchcraft. Shamans served as intermediaries between humans and gods, participating in or officiating sacrifices during major ceremonies, events, and festivals. Activities such as dispelling disasters and curing illnesses, helping people bear children, and cursing others to suffer misfortune or calamity almost all fell within the scope of shamanic practices. 78 Arts Painting The development of art during the Jin Dynasty also achieved high accomplishments in various aspects. Emperor Zhangzong of Jin established a painting and calligraphy academy, collecting famous paintings from the common people and the Southern Song collections. Wang Tingyun and the court gentleman Zhang Rufang appraised 550 scrolls of painting and calligraphy collected by the Jin Dynasty, and determined their respective grades of quality. In 1127, when Jin troops captured the The Northern Song Dynasty capital Bianliang, they plundered paintings from the Song court collection and took captive painters north. The Jin court emphasized the collection of famous painting and calligraphy works, building upon the paintings obtained from the Northern Song imperial collection, and further enriching it by soliciting works from the common people. Under the influence of Han culture, Jin Dynasty painting was more prosperous than Liao Dynasty painting, especially during the period from Emperor Shizong of Jin to Emperor Zhangzong of Jin, when painting activities became increasingly active. Emperor Zhangzong of Jin was skilled in poetry, literature, and calligraphy, and also had a fondness for painting. He established a painting and calligraphy bureau under the government's Directorate of Palace Libraries to appraise the collected paintings, and also imitated Emperor Huizong of Song's calligraphic style to inscribe signatures and affix seals on famous works. 76 Zhao Lin's "Portrait Scroll of the Six Steeds of Zhaoling" (detail) The Jin Dynasty also established a Painting Office under the Directorate for Imperial Manufactories, "in charge of painting and gold-inlaying craftsmen." Famous paintings of the time included Yu Zhongwen's "Galloping Steed," Wang Tingyun's "Withered Tree," Zhang Gui's "Divine Turtle," and Zhao Lin's "Portrait Scroll of the Six Steeds of Zhaoling," among which Zhang Yu's "Wenji Returning to Han" was considered the finest. The landscape, bamboo, and rock paintings by Wu Yuanzhi, Li Shan, and Wang Tingyun, compared to the works of contemporary Southern Song academy painters, seemed to exhibit more of a "literati" taste. 76 Calligraphy Jin Dynasty calligraphy was most deeply influenced by Yan Zhenqing , Su Dongpo, Huang Tingjian, and Mi Fu . Emperor Zhangzong of Jin studied Emperor Huizong of Song's Slender Gold style and achieved considerable success. Wang Jing excelled at cursive and clerical script, particularly skilled in large characters; the inscribed plaques for the palaces in both capitals were all written by Wang Jing. Dang Huaiying was proficient in seal and large seal script, revered by scholars. Zhao Feng was skilled in regular, running, and cursive script, and also excelled at small seal script. His regular script combined the styles of Yan and Su; his calligraphy and painting were vigorous and elegant, considered superior to Shi Manqing. His running and cursive script encompassed various masters' styles. Contemporaries paired Zhao Feng's small seal script with Dang Huaiying's, calling them "Dang and Zhao." Wu Ji grasped the brushwork essence of his father-in-law Mi Fu. Among those who studied Mi Fu at the time, Wang Tingyun attained the deepest mastery; his calligraphy was unmatched even by the likes of Kangli Naonao in the early Yuan Dynasty. Ren Xun possessed multifaceted talents, and his calligraphy was considered the best of his time. The "Collected Poems of the Central Plains" stated of him: "His painting surpasses his calligraphy, his calligraphy surpasses his poetry, and his poetry surpasses his prose." 76 Emperor Zhangzong of Jin's Slender Gold Style Calligraphy Music and Dance In the early years of the Jin Dynasty, the musical instruments of the Jurchen people consisted only of drums and flutes, and their singing had only the "Partridge" tune, "with just two notes, high and low, long and short, from the partridge." After entering Song territory, the Jin army seized musicians, instruments, and music books from the Song court's Music Bureau, and Han Chinese music was integrated into Jurchen music. When Emperor Shizong of Jin held banquets for envoys from the Southern Song and Western Xia, the musicians imitated the Song style, but their attire was different. Jin Dynasty dance originated from the Mohe music of their Mohe ancestors. After establishing the state, it was largely absorbed directly from Northern Song dance, while also promoting Jurchen music and dance culture. In terms of theater, the Zhu Gongdiao (All Keys and Modes), popular in the Northern Song, became the primary form of storytelling and singing in the Jin Dynasty. Only Dong Jieyuan's "The Western Chamber in All Keys and Modes" and "Liu Zhiyuan" have survived to the present day. Due to the richness of practical experience, many physicians conducted in-depth research into ancient medical classics, combined them with their own clinical experiences, and developed their own theories to interpret the theories of their predecessors, gradually forming different schools. Liu Wansu founded the Hejian School, Zhang Yuansu founded the Yishui School, and Zhang Yuansu's disciple Li Dongyuan independently created the theory of the spleen and stomach. These three schools, together with Zhu Zhenheng 's theory of nourishing yin from the Yuan Dynasty, are collectively known as the Four Great Schools of the Jin and Yuan Dynasties , exerting a significant influence on the development of traditional Chinese medical theory. 80 Agronomy The Jin Dynasty absorbed agricultural techniques from the Northern Song Dynasty, which improved agricultural yields in the area around its Upper Capital in the northeast. Archaeologists have excavated many iron agricultural tools used during the Jin period in the modern-day northeastern region, such as plowshares and seed-drill tubes. At that time, famous agricultural texts from the Jin and Western Xia regions included Wuben Xinshu and Shinong Biyong, but unfortunately, these are now lost. Techniques for sericulture (silkworm breeding) and horticulture were also highly developed during this period; for example, the method of "covering sheds with cow dung" was used to cultivate watermelons in the colder northeastern regions. 80 Calendar and Mathematics The most important advancement in mathematics at the time was the development of the " Tianyuan Shu " method, which was the ancient Chinese method for establishing higher-degree equations, where "tianyuan" was equivalent to an unknown variable. In 1248, the mathematician Li Ye of the Jin-Yuan period systematically introduced the use of the tianyuan method to establish quadratic equations in his works Ceyuan Haijing and Yigu Yanduan. The Jin court learned from the Northern Song by establishing the Directorate of Astronomy to observe celestial phenomena. Mathematics was also highly developed during this period, leading Jin scholars to be enthusiastic about compiling calendars. The Jin court promulgated the Daming Calendar compiled by Yang Ji in 1137 (different from the Daming Calendar by Zu Chongzhi). Later, Zhao Zhiwei revised and compiled the more accurate Revised Daming Calendar in 1180, whose precision surpassed that of the superior Song Dynasty calendar, the Jiyuan Calendar. If any Jurchen fell into debt slavery or sold themselves into servitude, the government would redeem them to free status. On the other hand, they went to great lengths to demote Han people to the status of slaves. 81 The Jin rulers referred to the land of their origin as the "interior region," namely the Shangjing region. During the reigns of Emperor Taizu of Jin, Emperor Taizong, and Emperor Xizong of Jin, the interior region was treated as the political, military, and economic center and was developed with priority. During the wars to conquer the Liao and defeat the Song, in order to weaken the resistance of the Han, Khitan, and other peoples in the Yan region and the Central Plains, and also to compensate for the labor shortage in the "interior region," improve production skills, and develop the economy, they continuously forced "wealthy and skilled craftsmen and farmers" to migrate to the "interior region." After the Jin army captured Kaifeng, they "escorted 3,000 households of artisans and laborers" back north. 82 After Emperor Shizong of Jin took charge of governance, he continued the "populate the interior" policy for military purposes, relocating people to the northeastern region. After large numbers of Han people were forcibly relocated to the "interior region," except for a few leaders, most saw their status decline. The majority served as slaves for households under the Meng'an and Mouke system, undertaking agricultural, pastoral, handicraft, and domestic labor, becoming government slaves for the rulers. Some established independent households, but they often struggled to survive due to hardship; some "lacked food to the point of selling their children," and others "sold themselves into slavery due to poverty," frequently falling into slavery. 83 The Policy of Relocating People to the Interior caused a great disaster for large numbers of Han people from the Central Plains, who were uprooted from their homes and reduced to slaves. However, on the other hand, the influx of several hundred thousand relatively skilled laborers into the backward northeastern region led to a significant change in the composition of the workforce. Together with the Khitan, Xi, Jurchen, and other peoples, they developed the northeast, promoting the socio-economic progress of the northeastern region. Mongols From its founding, the Jin Dynasty was in opposition to the Mongolian Plateau. From 1124 to 1130, Yelü Dashi controlled the region north of the Gobi Desert. In 1130, during the Battle of Kedun City, the Jin Dynasty drove Yelü Dashi out of the Mongolian Plateau, but relations between the various tribes of the Mongolian Plateau and the Jin Dynasty did not ease. Precisely during the 1130 Battle of Kedun City, the Jin general Yelü Yudu, in pursuit of Dashi, conscripted troops from the tribes north of the Gobi Desert, but his request was refused, and the Jin troops returned without success. Among the tribes of the Mongolian Plateau, except for the Ongud tribe and the Tatar tribe south of the Gobi Desert, none were under Jin control. In 1135, Emperor Xizong of Jin ascended the throne. By this time, large-scale wars between Jin and Song had largely ended, and the Jin Dynasty concentrated its efforts on dealing with the Five Tribes of Mongol Tatars, engaging in years of continuous warfare with the Mengwu State, the Kereyid, and other tribes. 84 In 1135, "That winter, the Jin ruler, due to a Mongol rebellion, dispatched Songguowang (Wanyan) Zongpan, who oversaw the three departments, to lead troops and defeat them. The Mongols are located northeast of the Jurchens; their people are robust, brave, and skilled in warfare, using shark skin for armor, which can withstand stray arrows." In 1138 (or 1139), "The Jurchen commander Hushahu (also translated as Hushahu, i.e., Hesholie Zhizhong) attacked the Mongols to the north, but returned after exhausting his supplies. The Mongols pursued and ambushed him, reaching the northwest of Shangjing, where they heavily defeated his forces at Hailing." In 1143, "That month, the Mongols rebelled again. The Jin ruler ordered a general to campaign against them. Initially, after Luguo Wang (Wanyan) Chang was executed, his son, Shenghua Du Langjun, led his father's former troops in rebellion and allied with the Mongols. The Mongols thus grew strong, capturing over twenty fortified settlements, which the Jin people could not control." In 1146, "The Jin used 80,000 trained crossbowmen armed with divine arm crossbows to campaign against the Mongols, but could not subdue them for years. That month, they ordered Xiao Baoshounu, the head of the Bianjing Branch Department of State Affairs, to negotiate peace with the Mongols, ceding twenty-seven fortified settlements north of the Xiping River to them, and providing annual tribute of cattle, sheep, rice, and beans. They also intended to confer the title of 'Mongol King' upon their leader, but the Mongols did not accept." In 1147, "That month, the Jin and the Mongols finally made peace, with the Jin providing generous annual tribute of cattle, sheep, rice, beans, silk, and other goods. Thereupon, the Mongol leader Eluo (also translated as Elun or Aoluo) Beile proclaimed himself Emperor Zuyuan and changed the era name to Tianxing. The Jin had waged war for years but ultimately could not subdue them, merely dispatching elite troops to garrison key strategic points before withdrawing." Later, the alliance broke down. In 1127, the Jin Dynasty destroyed the Northern Song Dynasty, an event known in history as the "Jingkang Incident". Multiple northern expeditions by the Southern Song and multiple southern invasions by the Jin Dynasty were unsuccessful. Later, the two sides reached the "Treaty of Shaoxing". During this period, there were also official exchanges and border trade between them. After the rise of the Mongols, during the Mongol–Jin War, the Southern Song Dynasty allied with the Mongols to destroy the Jin Dynasty at Caizhou. Relations with Western Xia Initially, the Jin Dynasty was very cautious in dealing with Western Xia's aid to the Liao, consistently trying to pull Western Xia away from the Liao. In early 1124, through negotiations, the Jin Dynasty "ceded territory as a reward for merit" to Western Xia, and Western Xia agreed to become a vassal to Jin using the same rituals as it had for Liao, an event known in history as the "Tianyue Peace Agreement". However, Western Xia was also highly distrustful of Jin. From the accession of Emperor Renzong of Western Xia to the first Mongol siege of Zhongxingfu in 1209, a period of over seventy years, Western Xia and Jin maintained generally close and friendly relations, except for two localized conflicts during the late Zhenglong era of Prince Hailing and the early Mingchang era of Emperor Zhangzong of Jin. 85 Relations with Goryeo Before Aguda unified the Jurchen tribes, he fought the Battle of Helandian with Goryeo. Goryeo built nine fortresses in the Helandian region but was later defeated by Jurchen forces and had to return the nine fortresses. During Aguda's campaign to destroy Liao, he sent a letter to Goryeo proposing they refer to each other as brotherly states. In 1126, Goryeo voluntarily submitted as a vassal to Jin, and Jin granted Baozhou to Goryeo, establishing a suzerain-vassal relationship between the two. During the reigns of Emperor Weishaowang, Emperor Xuanzong, and Emperor Aizong in the late Jin period, Jin's national power declined and it could no longer control Goryeo. During Emperor Xuanzong's reign, there are no records of Goryeo sending envoys to Jin. Goryeo no longer regarded Jin as its suzerain, not only refusing Jin's commands but also frequently raiding the border. Jin could only adopt a policy of loose rein to maintain the nominal suzerain-vassal relationship. 95 Relations with Jiaozhi In 1133, Emperor Taizong of Jin dispatched over twenty envoys, along with envoys from its puppet state, the "Qi", to travel south from Dengzhou to Jiaozhi. They conferred the title of Prince of Guang upon its ruler, Li Yanghuan, while also contacting chieftains of the surrounding streams and caves in the Jiaozhi area, aiming to contain the Song Dynasty from the south. 100 Society Social Strata The expansion of the slave system under the Jin Dynasty inevitably encountered resolute resistance from the vast peasantry. The inhabitants of Shanxi who were forcibly relocated to Shangjing rose up in rebellion and fled one after another. During the Jin military's campaigns to plunder the Yan and Yun regions, the struggle of the masses against abduction and plunder raged like a prairie fire, dealing heavy blows to the Jurchen slave-owning class. While relying on the Han landlord class in the Yan and Yun regions to implement the feudal Han official system, Emperor Taizong of Jin repeatedly issued edicts prohibiting the capture of slaves in surrendered and subjugated areas. The feudal economic relationship where Han landlords exploited peasants actually continued to exist in the vast rural areas. The Jin Dynasty did not, and could not, extend its internal land-grant system to the prefectures and counties of Yan and Yun. 55 Bronze Seated Dragon of the Jin Dynasty In the Central Plains and Shaanxi regions, formerly under Northern Song rule and later under the control of Liu Yu's Qi state, there was even less possibility or capability to implement any measures altering the feudal socio-economic system. 55 The socio-economic system within the Jin Dynasty's domain presented a situation where the slave system and the feudal system coexisted simultaneously. Within the Jin heartland, the slave system was essentially promoted. The prefectures and counties of Yan and Yun inherited the feudal relations from the Liao Dynasty. In the territories ruled by the Qi state, the more developed feudal economic relations from the Northern Song period were largely preserved. The differing conditions of the Jin Dynasty's political and military systems in different regions were, in fact, a necessary reflection of the different socio-economic systems within the superstructure. Different systems, of course, could not develop in parallel within the unified state of the Jin Dynasty; an intense and sharp struggle was inevitable. This struggle erupted fiercely during the reign of Emperor Xizong of Jin. 55 Sinicization The Jin Dynasty also gradually moved towards Sinicization in cultural aspects. The Jin rulers emphasized the orthodox concept of "virtue phase." Initially designated as having the phase of Metal (Jin De), after two rounds of discussion on the virtue phase issue during the reigns of Emperor Zhangzong and Emperor Xuanzong, it was ultimately changed from Metal to Earth phase (Tu De), declaring that since the Jin had destroyed the Northern Song and the Zhao Song's Fire phase had ended, the Jin Dynasty should inherit the Song lineage as the Earth phase. The ceremonial attire of Jin emperors and officials was also largely the same as that of the Song Dynasty, with the sole remaining characteristic being the "left-lapelled" style. From the mid-period onward, it became increasingly common for Jurchen nobles to adopt Han surnames and wear Han clothing, despite repeated imperial bans. Emperor Shizong of Jin actively advocated for the study of the Jurchen script and language, but was still unable to reverse the trend of Jurchen Sinicization. Despite this, during the middle and late periods of the Jin Dynasty when the Jurchens had already become largely Sinicized, the rulers persistently enforced the policy of "shaving their heads and adopting left-lapelled garments" on the Han people, forcing them to accept Jurchen customs and etiquette. This indicates that they harbored a profound sense of crisis regarding the excessive Sinicization of the Jurchens. 70 The culture of the Jin Dynasty reached a high level of development in its time. It "transformed the declining and crude customs of the Five Dynasties and the late Liao period," and "after the Dading era, its literary style was vigorous and robust, directly continuing the legacy of the various Northern Song masters." In certain aspects, it even surpassed that of the Song Dynasty, heralding the development of later culture. The Jin Dynasty implemented policies of Sinicization, moving from "borrowing talent from other dynasties" to forming its own "national literary school," gradually developing a distinct style and character different from the Song Dynasty. However, its fierce and brave martial spirit gradually disappeared as the Jin regime stabilized, ultimately contributing to the dynasty's downfall. 87 Portraits of Jin Dynasty Emperors from *Jami' al-tawarikh* (copied from contemporary Chinese-language documents) During the Yuan Dynasty, surviving elders of the fallen Jin liked to say "the Jin perished because of Confucianism." While this statement may not be entirely correct, the fact that the Jurchens underwent comprehensive Sinicization is indisputable. Liu Qi said: "After the southern crossing, many Jurchen hereditary Meng'an and Mouke often appreciated literature and enjoyed associating with scholar-officials." The emperors from Emperor Xizong of Jin onward all possessed considerable proficiency in Han culture. A saying from the Yuan period states: "There were five emperors who understood music: Emperor Xuanzong of Tang, Emperor Zhuangzong of Later Tang, Li Houzhu of Southern Tang, Emperor Huizong of Song, and Emperor Zhangzong of Jin." From the mid-Jin period onward, the phenomenon of Jurchens adopting Han surnames and wearing Han clothing became increasingly common, with repeated imperial bans proving ineffective. Emperor Shizong of Jin consistently opposed the complete Sinicization of the Jurchens and actively promoted the study of the Jurchen script and language, but he could not slow down the pace of Jurchen Sinicization. 91 The first and deepest to accept Han culture and undergo Sinicization were the upper strata of Jurchen aristocratic society. Hao Jing stated that the Jin Dynasty's "splendid institutions of a generation rivaled those of the Tang and Han, far surpassing the capabilities of the Northern Wei or Northern Qi to rank alongside them." Zhao Yi also remarked that "the cultural achievements of the Jin source (i.e., Jin Dynasty) surpassed those of the Liao above and excelled over the Yuan below." The culture and arts of the Jin Dynasty continued to develop following the Liao and Northern Song, surpassing the Liao and, after the Northern Song, progressing in parallel with the Southern Song, constituting the two major northern and southern branches of Chinese cultural development at the time. In the history of Chinese cultural and artistic development, it played a role "surpassing the Liao above and excelling over the Yuan below." --- [Chinese Monarchs - Emperor Xizong of Jin (February 28, 1119-January 9 ...] ---
Emperor Xizong of Jin Emperor Xizong of Jin (February 28, 1119–January 9, 1150) reigned from February 10, 1135 to January 9, 1150 as an emperor of the jin dynasty which controlled northern China from 1115 to 1234. His birth name was Wányán Hélá (完顏合剌). His Han Chinese name was Wányán Dǎn (完顏亶). Wanyan Dan was the first son of the founder of the jin dynasty Wanyan Aguda. Although after Aguda's death in 1123 the Jin throne was inherited by Aguda's brother Wuqimai, Aguda's top general Nianhan and chancellor Xiyin convinced Wuqimai to declare young Dan his heir apparent (anban bojilie, 谙班勃极烈) in 1132. Accordingly, Dan inherited the Jin throne after the death of Wuqimai in 1135. He was murdered by Prince Hailing of Jin in a coup d'état on Julian calendar January 9, 1150 (皇統九年十二月丁巳日). Early life Hela was the eldest son of Shengguo (繩果; also known as Wanyan Zongjun 完顏宗峻), the eldest son of Aguda (Emperor Taizu), the founder and first emperor of the Jin dynasty. His mother was Lady Pucha (蒲察氏), whom he posthumously honoured as "Empress Huizhao" (惠昭皇后). When Emperor Taizu died in 1123, the throne was passed on to his younger brother, Wuqimai (Emperor Taizong). Wanyan Zonghan and Wanyan Xiyin, who used to be Emperor Taizu's chief advisers, convinced Emperor Taizong to designate Hela as his heir apparent (anban bojilie; 諳班勃極烈) in 1132, so Hela became the new emperor in 1135 when Emperor Taizong died. Wars against the Southern Song dynasty In 1137, Emperor Xizong abolished the Qi kingdom, a vassal state of the Jin dynasty ruled by Liu Yu (劉豫), a former Song dynasty official. The Jin dynasty started peace negotiations with the Southern Song dynasty. In 1139, the Jin and Song dynasties arrived at a treaty, with the latter agreeing to be a tributary vassal state under the former. In return, the Jin dynasty returned control of Henan and Shaanxi provinces to the Song dynasty. However, in 1140, Emperor Xizong decided to wage war against the Song dynasty so he ordered the general Wanyan Zongbi to lead Jin forces to attack and seize back Henan and Shaanxi. In 1141, Wanyan Zongbi and his army were defeated by Song forces led by Yue Fei and Han Shizhong; the Jin dynasty agreed to negotiate for peace again with the Song dynasty. Internal politics Emperor Xizong was very fond of Han culture because of the influence of his adoptive father, Wanyan Zonggan, so he reformed the political institutions and modelled them after Han Chinese ones, while at the same time encouraging talented Han Chinese to serve in his government. In 1136, Emperor Xizong ordered Wanyan Zonggan, Wanyan Zongpan (完顏宗磐) and Wanyan Zonghan to take charge of reforming the government and creating the Three Departments system. Emperor Xizong abolished the traditional bojilie (勃極烈) system that he inherited from his predecessors. The bojilie system allowed the Jurchen ruler to choose an heir apparent from among male relatives in the same generation as him, usually his brothers. However, Emperor Taizong, who succeeded his brother Emperor Taizu, made an exception when he chose Emperor Xizong (Emperor Taizu's grandson) as his heir apparent instead of choosing from among his sons. Wanyan Zongpan, the eldest son of Emperor Taizong, was very unhappy when the bojilie system was abolished because this meant that he would have no chance to become emperor. Between 1138 and 1139, Wanyan Zongpan tried to seize power and start a rebellion but was defeated and executed. Since then, the political arena was dominated by Wanyan Zonghan, Wanyan Zonggan and Wanyan Zongbi; Emperor Xizong had little or no say in politics. After Wanyan Zongbi died in 1148, Emperor Xizong gained an opportunity to participate in politics, but his wife, Empress Daoping, started to interfere in politics and she wielded significant influence. Emperor Xizong's two sons, Wanyan Ji'an (完顏濟安) and Wanyan Daoji (完顏道濟), died in 1143 and 1144 respectively. Emperor Xizong felt depressed by the loss of his sons that he developed an addiction to alcohol and started neglecting state affairs. He also became more violent and ruthless, and started killing people indiscriminately. One of his victims was Ambaghai, a Mongol chieftain and great-granduncle of Genghis Khan. Emperor Xizong was overthrown and murdered by his chancellor, Digunai, and other court officials in a ''coup d'état on 9 January 1150.
