The institution was moved again in 1933, to its current location: the former Pump House in Recoleta, remodeled by the architect Alejandro Bustillo. This period saw the addition of outstanding paintings, among them, Paul Gauguin Woman of the Sea, Vincent van Gogh's Le Moulin de la Galette, and El Greco's Christ on the Mount of Olives. The last decades of the 20th century took in major figures in international modern art. Among those added were Pablo Picasso, Amedeo Modigliani, Marc Chagall, Vassily Kandinsky, Paul Klee, Lucio Fontana, Jackson Pollock, Mark Rothko, and Henry Moore. The history of local production as well is related in the Museum's galleries, which exhibit a vast panorama of Argentine art, with works by its leading representatives, such as Cándido López, Prilidiano Pueyrredón, Emilio Pettoruti, Xul Solar, Raquel Forner, Grete Stern, Antonio Berni, Alicia Penalba, Gyula Kosice, Marta Minujín, Antonio Seguí and León Ferrari. In addition, the Museum has outstanding holdings of other Latin American art, with an assemblage of pieces by Pedro Figari, Joaquín Torres García, Tarsila do Amaral, Diego Rivera and Jesús Rafael Soto, among others. In its 130 years, Argentina's Museo Nacional de Bellas Artes has built up an important collection of more than 13,000 pieces from different periods, both national and international, housed in one of South America's most noteworthy cultural institutions. The Museo Nacional de Bellas Artes (MNBA) is Argentina's premier institution for fine arts, situated at Avenida del Libertador 1473 in the upscale Recoleta neighborhood of Buenos Aires.[1] Founded in 1896, it houses a vast collection exceeding 13,000 works, spanning European art from the 12th to 20th centuries, Argentine art primarily from the 19th and 20th centuries, and notable Latin American pieces, featuring masterpieces by international luminaries such as Goya, Renoir, Van Gogh, Picasso, and Modigliani, alongside key Argentine figures like Cándido López and Berni.[1] The museum serves as a cultural hub promoting both national and global artistic heritage through its permanent displays, temporary exhibitions, and educational programs.[1]The MNBA's history traces back to its inauguration on December 25, 1896, in the repurposed Bon Marché department store building on Florida Street, which today houses the Galerías Pacífico shopping arcade.[1] In 1911, it relocated to the Argentine Pavilion originally constructed for the 1889 Paris World's Fair and situated in Plaza San Martín.[1] The current neoclassical building, opened in 1933, occupies the site of a former municipal pump house and was extensively remodeled by renowned Argentine architect Alejandro Bustillo to accommodate the growing collection.[1]Organized across multiple floors, the museum's permanent collection is displayed in 30 rooms: the ground floor focuses on European art from the 12th to 19th centuries and 19th-century Argentine works, while the first floor highlights 20th-century international and Argentine art.[2] The second floor hosts rotating temporary exhibitions and features accessible terraces offering views of the surrounding Recoleta parks.[1] Admission is free, with the institution open Tuesday to Friday from 11 a.m. to 7:30 p.m. and weekends from 10 a.m. to 7:30 p.m. (as of November 2025), drawing visitors to explore its digital collection of over 2,500 high-resolution works available online.[2][3] History Founding and Early Development The Museo Nacional de Bellas Artes in Buenos Aires was established on December 25, 1896, following a presidential decree issued earlier that year, becoming the first public fine arts museum in Argentina.[4][5] Argentine painter, art critic, and educator Eduardo Schiaffino, a key figure of the Generation of '80, founded the institution and served as its first director from 1896 to 1910, overseeing its initial organization and operations.[5][6] His leadership was instrumental in transforming the museum into a cornerstone of cultural infrastructure during a period of national consolidation.The museum opened its doors in the repurposed building of the Bon Marché department store on Florida Street in central Buenos Aires, a site now occupied by the Galerías Pacífico shopping arcade.[5][7] From its inception, the institution emphasized the collection and exhibition of Argentine artworks, aiming to foster appreciation for local artistic production amid the country's evolving cultural landscape.[6]Early acquisitions relied heavily on donations from private collectors, whom Schiaffino actively encouraged to contribute works that would form the core of the collection.[4][8] The Argentine government supplemented these efforts by purchasing select European paintings, such as those acquired through international dealers in the late 1890s and early 1900s, to elevate the museum's international standing and provide comparative context for national art.[5][8] Schiaffino's broader vision positioned the museum as an educational instrument for cultivating cultural awareness and strengthening national identity in the decades following Argentina's independence.[6] Relocations and Institutional Growth In 1911, the museum relocated from its initial site at the Bon Marché building on Florida Street to the former Argentine Pavilion in Plaza San Martín, a structure originally constructed for the 1889 Paris Universal Exposition and subsequently transported to Buenos Aires, which provided significantly more space for expanded exhibitions and better accommodation of the growing collection.[9]The institution underwent another major relocation in 1933 to its current location at Avenida del Libertador 1473 in the Recoleta neighborhood, transforming the 1870-built Casa de Bombas—a former water pumping station originally used for purifying and distributing water to the city—into a cultural venue through remodeling by architect Alejandro Bustillo, thereby establishing a permanent and more suitable home for its operations.[9][10]Following further remodeling and expansions in 1943 that enhanced preservation and display capabilities, the museum experienced substantial institutional growth in the post-war period, particularly during the directorship of Jorge Romero Brest from 1955 to 1963, when modernization efforts added new galleries and culminated in the construction of a temporary exhibitions pavilion in 1960 for the "150 Años de Arte Argentino" show, which was later integrated into the permanent structure to support larger-scale programming.[9][11]Subsequent developments included the 1980 inauguration of a dedicated pavilion on the first floor for 20th-century Argentine and international contemporary art, funded in part by the Asociación de Amigos del MNBA, which expanded the facility's capacity for modern holdings. In 2004, the museum extended its reach with the opening of its Neuquén branch, a 2,400 m² facility designed by architect Mario Roberto Álvarez that houses over 200 works from the national collection on loan, marking the institution's first permanent outpost beyond Buenos Aires and promoting regional access to its resources.[9][12][13] Key Directors and Modernization Efforts Eduardo Schiaffino served as the first director of the Museo Nacional de Bellas Artes from 1896 to 1910, laying the foundational acquisition policies that shaped the institution's early collection. As a painter, critic, and art historian, Schiaffino prioritized the procurement of European masterpieces to elevate Argentine artistic standards, securing works by artists such as Francisco Goya and Pierre-Auguste Renoir through targeted purchases and donations. His vision emphasized the museum's role in fostering national cultural development, establishing protocols for curatorial growth that balanced international prestige with local relevance.[9]Jorge Romero Brest's tenure as director from 1955 to 1963 marked a pivotal shift toward modern curatorial practices and international collaborations. A prominent art critic, Brest professionalized the museum's operations by incorporating specialists like photographer Grete Stern and initiating educational programs to engage broader audiences. He expanded the institution's scope through partnerships with global entities, promoting contemporary art movements and creating specialized collections, such as the print cabinet, to diversify holdings. These efforts positioned the MNBA as a hub for modernist discourse in Latin America, influencing subsequent institutional strategies.[9]Subsequent directors in the 1960s and 1970s, such as Samuel Oliver, continued modernization amid political and cultural upheavals, focusing on administrative stability and collection management. By the 2010s, the museum's library underwent expansion to accommodate over 110,000 books, along with press articles, pamphlets, and periodicals.[14] Current director Andrés Duprat, appointed in 2015 following an international contest, has advanced inclusivity and digitization initiatives, emphasizing federal outreach to decentralize access beyond Buenos Aires.[15]Post-2020, amid global disruptions, the MNBA accelerated digital access programs, launching an online collection featuring over 2,500 high-definition works with accompanying texts, audio, and virtual itineraries to ensure continuity of public engagement. In 2025, under Duprat's leadership, the museum acquired key works such as Oscar Bony's La cruz del diablo at arteba and opened exhibitions including Museo secreto and Porter-Camnitzer, further integrating technology and expanding public access.[2][16] Building and Location Architectural Design and Adaptations The Museo Nacional de Bellas Artes occupies a building originally constructed in the 1870s as the Casa de Bombas de Recoleta, a pumping station for Buenos Aires' water supply system, which was ceded to the museum in 1931 following its relocation to the site.[9][10] The structure was extensively adapted and remodeled by Argentine architect Alejandro Bustillo, who transformed the industrial facility into a cultural venue with a neoclassical aesthetic, including a symmetrical facade and elegant marble interiors suited for art display.[9][17] The redesigned building was inaugurated in 1933, featuring clean, minimalist walls to emphasize artwork contemplation and spanning three floors across approximately 34 exhibition halls.[9]In 2004, the museum expanded with a branch in Neuquén, Patagonia, designed by architect Mario Roberto Álvarez in a modern style aligned with the Movimiento Moderno, characterized by minimalist lines, simple geometric forms, and functional spaces that integrate with the regional landscape.[12][18] This outpost, awarded at the 2004 National Architecture Biennale, represents a departure from the main building's classical influences, prioritizing openness and contemporary exhibition needs.[19]Subsequent adaptations to the main Recoleta building have focused on preservation and functionality, including renovations in 2005, 2011, and 2019 to restore Bustillo's original spatial configurations and enhance exhibition capabilities.[9] A major 2017 overhaul restored key halls while incorporating accessibility improvements, such as ramps for wheelchair entry and an upgraded elevator system to ensure equitable navigation across floors. The National Museum of Fine Arts The Building The National Museum of Fine Arts (MNBA) of Argentina is located on Avenida del Libertador 1473, Buenos Aires. It was inaugurated on 25 December 1896 and the opening was attended by numerous personalities. Among them was Nicaraguan writer, Rubén Darío, a referent of modernity. Its first Director was Eduardo Schiaffino. The Bon Marché on the Calle Florida, was the precursory site for Argentina’s first art museum and had been built to house a department store. The absence of public buildings undoubtedly led to the MNBA setting out in this commercial mall, built in the style of its namesake in Paris. In 1910 MNBA moved to its second premises in the Plaza San Martin, a palace made of iron and glass that had served as the Argentine Pavilion at the 1889 Universal Exhibition in Paris and was shipped to Buenos Aires afterwards. Since 1932, the MNBA has had premises of its own in the old pump building of Obras Sanitarias in Recoleta. Though not originally built to house a museum, it was adapted for its new role by the architect Alejandro Bustillo. The building currently consists of thirty rooms distributed over three floors. The Museum & its Permanent Rooms International Art: The permanent rooms on the first floor are entirely devoted to international art dating from the Middle Ages to the 20th century. The MNBA’s collection is considered to be the most important of European art in South America, and includes works by Tintoretto, El Greco, Rembrandt, Zurbarán, Tiepolo and medieval masters. The amount of space dedicated to the 19th century in our collections denounces its relevance. It withholds paintings of Goya, the landscapes of Courbet, Millet, Troyon and Daubigny of the Barbizon School, as well as of their academic contemporaries such as Bouguereau and Lefebvre, the dawn of impressionism in Manet’s emblematic Nymph Surprised, the vibrant colour in the views of Pissarro, Monet and Sisley, the post-impressionist search in Van Gogh, Gauguin, Tolouse-Lautrec and Degas. It can be seen the valuable local contributions, as well as Italian painting, the Macchiaioli, the Spanish version of impressionism, and a thematic core devoted to various different manifestations of late 19th century European symbolism. The last part of the tour through the permanent exhibition brings together various early 20th century avant-garde masters such as Klee, Kandinsky, De Chirico, Carrà, Modigliani, Picasso and Léger. Post-war trends are represented by European artists like Nicholson, Fontana, Vasarely, Dubuffet, Fautrier, Saura, Alechinsky and Henry Moore, while US section includes paintings by Rothko, Pollock and Nevelson. 19th and 20th Century Argentine Art: The first floor is devoted to Argentine and Latin American art. Next to the room of pre-Columbian Andean art, is a room displaying the Panels of the Conquest of Mexico, an emblematic example of colonial art in the crossing of cultures. Also on this floor María Luisa Bemberg’s former collection incorporates the River Plate avant-gardes. Exhibited as an extensive and comprehensive overview, the permanent exhibition of Argentine art offers a sequence ranging from the foreign painters who visited Argentina in the early 19th century to the latest artistic trends of the 20th century.
