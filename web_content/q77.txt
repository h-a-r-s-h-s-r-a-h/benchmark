--- [New York's congressional districts - Wikipedia] ---
The U.S. state of New York contains 26 congressional districts. Each district elects one member of the United States House of Representatives to represent it. 
The state was redistricted in 2022, following the 2020 U.S. census. It lost one seat in Congress. According to CNN, unnamed census officials stated that if 89 more people had been counted in New York's census results, and all other states' population figures had remained the same, New York would not have lost a congressional seat.

New York's congressional districts Overview Apportionment and current number of districts New York's apportionment of seats in the United States House of Representatives is determined decennially following each federal census, using the Huntington–Hill method to allocate the fixed total of 435 seats among the states based on their relative populations. This method, adopted in 1941, prioritizes additional seats to states via a geometric mean formula—specifically, the priority value for a state's (n+1)th seat is its population divided by the square root of n(n+1), where n is the number of seats already allocated—ensuring minimal relative representation disparities across states. The apportionment population includes resident census counts plus overseas federal employees and military personnel, excluding non-citizens on visas and certain other groups not tied to state residency.[8] As of the 2020 census, New York was apportioned 26 seats, a reduction from 27 following the 2010 census, effective with the 118th Congress in 2023.[8] This loss stemmed from the state's slower population growth—totaling about 0.5% over the decade compared to the national 7.4% increase—placing it below the threshold for retaining the 27th seat by 89 residents in the apportionment population. Had the count reached the minimum quota of approximately 20 million for the 27th seat under Huntington–Hill priorities, New York would have held steady, but Minnesota retained its delegation by a similar slim margin of 26 residents.[8][9] Historically, New York's seat count has fluctuated with its share of national population, rising from 6 seats in the 1st Congress (1789–1791) to a peak of 45 following the 1930 census (effective 1933), driven by industrialization and immigration-fueled growth in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.[10] Subsequent declines—to 37 seats after the 1950 census and further reductions in later decades—reflected relative stagnation compared to faster-growing Sun Belt states, amid out-migration and lower birth rates, halving the state's apportionment share from over 10% in 1930 to about 4.8% in 2020.[10][10] These shifts underscore the method's sensitivity to marginal population differences, where even small absolute changes can tip seat allocations when scaled against the uniform House size.Election mechanics and representation New York's congressional districts operate under the uniform federal framework for electing members of the United States House of Representatives, with each of the state's districts electing a single representative every two years.[11] Elections occur on the first Tuesday after the first Monday in November of even-numbered years, coinciding with federal midterm and presidential cycles. The voting system employs plurality rules, whereby the candidate receiving the plurality of votes—regardless of majority—secures the seat, with no provision for runoffs or ranked-choice mechanisms in general elections for these offices.[12] Primaries precede the general election, typically held in June or August depending on the cycle, and are conducted on a closed or semi-closed basis under New York state law, restricting participation to enrolled party members or allowing limited crossover.[13] Federal law mandates single-member districts for House elections, a requirement codified in 1967 to replace at-large and multi-member systems that had persisted in some states. This ensures one representative per district, with boundaries drawn to achieve substantially equal population across districts, as established by the Supreme Court's interpretation of Article I, Section 2 of the Constitution in Wesberry v. Sanders (1964), aiming for "one person, one vote."[14] Districts must also comply with Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act of 1965, which prohibits practices that dilute the voting strength of racial or language minorities, requiring mapmakers to avoid packing or cracking minority communities in ways that impair their ability to elect preferred candidates. New York additionally adheres to its state-specific John R. Lewis Voting Rights Act, enacted in 2021, which mirrors and extends federal protections against discriminatory districting.[15] Representatives serve two-year terms with no constitutional or statutory term limits, allowing indefinite reelection subject to voter approval. This structure, rooted in Article I of the Constitution, promotes frequent accountability but has drawn criticism for entrenching incumbency advantages, though proposals for limits require a constitutional amendment following the Supreme Court's ruling in U.S. Term Limits, Inc. v. Thornton (1995). The allocation of House seats from New York's districts indirectly influences the state's apportionment of presidential electors, calculated as the number of representatives plus two senators, thereby linking congressional representation to the Electoral College process. This mechanic underscores the districts' role in broader federal power distribution, with deviations in seat counts—such as New York's loss of one seat after the 2020 census—affecting both legislative influence and electoral weight.Historical Evolution Formation and early apportionment (1789–1900) New York's congressional districts were initially established by the state legislature in December 1788, prior to the first federal elections, dividing the state into six single-member districts to elect representatives to the First United States Congress (1789–1791). These districts grouped counties geographically, with the first encompassing New York City and surrounding areas, the second covering parts of Long Island and Westchester, and subsequent ones spanning upstate regions like Albany and the Hudson Valley, reflecting the state's sparse population distribution at the time, estimated at around 340,000 residents. Elections occurred on March 3–4, 1789, marking the state's entry into the federal system after ratifying the Constitution in July 1788.[16] Following the 1790 census, which enumerated New York's population at 340,120—second only to Virginia—Congress reapportioned House seats using the constitutional ratio of one representative per 30,000 persons, granting New York 10 seats effective for the Third Congress (1793–1795).[17] The state legislature promptly redrew boundaries in 1791, creating smaller districts to accommodate the increase, with urban centers like New York City gaining dedicated representation as its population concentrated in southern Manhattan. This shift from the initial broad districts highlighted early adjustments to emerging demographic patterns, though rural upstate areas retained multi-county configurations. New York continued electing by district rather than at-large, adhering to practices that ensured localized representation amid debates over electoral methods. The 19th century saw dramatic expansions driven by population surges from European immigration and internal migration to urban-industrial hubs, particularly New York City, which grew from 33,131 residents in 1790 to 813,669 by 1860 due to its port dominance and manufacturing rise. [18] Apportionments reflected this: 17 seats after the 1800 census (population 589,051), 27 after 1810 (959,049), and peaking at 40 after 1830 (1,919,361), as waves of immigrants fueled commerce and canal development like the Erie Canal (completed 1825), boosting upstate growth before urbanization concentrated gains in the metropolis.[17] District boundaries shifted accordingly, with frequent legislative redraws incorporating expanding boroughs—Brooklyn emerged as a separate district by the 1820s—and subdividing dense urban zones to balance voter loads, though gerrymandering allegations arose in competitive elections. By mid-century, however, relative growth slowed amid national westward expansion, leading to a net loss: from 40 seats post-1830 to 33 after 1840 (population 2,428,921), under the Webster apportionment method prioritizing equal proportions, as southern and midwestern states gained faster.[17] Renewed immigration—Irish fleeing the 1845–1852 famine and Germans post-1848 revolutions—spurred recovery, stabilizing at 33 seats post-1850 (3,097,394) before dipping to 31 post-1860 (3,880,735) amid Civil War disruptions. Urban districts in New York City underwent repeated refinements, splitting Manhattan into multiple seats by the 1850s to reflect tenement densities and ethnic enclaves, while upstate boundaries stabilized around agricultural counties. These changes causally linked to empirical census data, underscoring how immigration (foreign-born reaching 25% by 1855) and urbanization outpaced rural stagnation, elevating New York's House delegation to national prominence until later apportionments.[18] By 1890, with 6,003,174 residents, the state held 34 seats, setting the stage for further industrial-era adjustments.[17]20th-century expansions and contractions Following the 1920 census, New York's congressional representation expanded to 45 seats, driven by substantial population growth from immigration and urbanization, particularly in New York City, which accounted for over 40% of the state's residents by 1920.[19] This peak reflected the state's role as a primary entry point for European immigrants and internal migrants seeking industrial jobs, with the population reaching 10.4 million. The Permanent Apportionment Act of 1929 fixed the total U.S. House membership at 435 and adopted the method of equal proportions for allocating seats, basing the initial distribution on the 1920 census data, which preserved New York's 45 seats.[20] Despite the 1930 census showing slower relative growth—New York's population increased by only 8.3% compared to national trends—Congress enacted apportionment using 1930 data that retained the 45 seats, as gains in southern and western states offset losses elsewhere without immediate contraction for New York.[19] Similarly, after the 1940 census, delayed federal action kept the apportionment unchanged, maintaining stability amid World War II-era population dynamics, including temporary wartime influxes to urban defense industries. Postwar suburbanization and out-migration began eroding this stability, as families relocated from dense urban cores to peripheral areas like Long Island and the Hudson Valley, facilitated by federal highway funding and housing programs; simultaneously, net domestic migration to Sun Belt states accelerated due to milder climates, lower costs, and expanding opportunities in air-conditioned economies. By the 1950 census, New York's population stood at 14.8 million, but its growth lagged national averages, resulting in a sharp contraction to 37 seats under the Reapportionment Act of 1951, effective for the 83rd Congress in 1953—a loss of eight seats attributed to these demographic shifts.[19] In the ensuing state redistricting process, the New York Legislature adjusted district boundaries in 1952 to incorporate burgeoning suburban populations, extending lines into counties like Nassau and Suffolk while consolidating some rural upstate areas to approximate equal population distribution under prevailing standards; these tweaks aimed to mitigate disparities between declining urban cores and expanding exurban zones without fully addressing rural underrepresentation, as urban delegates retained influence in the drawing process.[21]Post-apportionment act reforms (1960s onward) The U.S. Supreme Court's decision in Wesberry v. Sanders (1964) required congressional districts to contain populations as equal as practicable, enforcing the "one person, one vote" principle under Article I, Section 2 of the Constitution to prevent dilution of urban voters' influence relative to rural areas.[22][14] Prior to this ruling, New York's 41 congressional districts following the 1960 census exhibited population variances that, while not as extreme as in some Southern states, still deviated by up to several percentage points, reflecting lax federal enforcement and state practices favoring traditional geographic units over strict equality. The decision compelled New York legislators to prioritize numerical parity in boundary adjustments, resulting in refined maps for the 1966 elections that reduced maximum deviations to under 1% in many cases, aligning with emerging national standards for deviations no greater than 0.5-1% to withstand judicial scrutiny.[23] Complementing these population mandates, the Voting Rights Act of 1965 prohibited electoral practices that diluted minority voting strength, with Section 2 later interpreted to bar districting that fragmented or overly concentrated racial groups in ways that impaired their ability to elect preferred representatives.[24] Although New York was not designated a covered jurisdiction requiring federal preclearance due to its relatively high Black voter registration rates, the Act's nationwide prohibitions influenced state redistricting by introducing racial fairness criteria alongside population equality, prompting evaluations of minority community cohesion in urban districts like those in New York City during the 1970 census cycle. This dual framework—equal population from Wesberry and anti-dilution protections—shifted New York's practices from politically driven line-drawing to data-verified compliance, correcting historical underrepresentation of growing urban and minority populations. By the 1970s, New York adapted to these reforms through technological innovation, employing early computer systems for redistricting to process census data, simulate boundary options, and achieve precise population balancing with deviations often below 0.1%.[25][26] These tools, utilized by the state's Legislative Task Force on Demographic Research and Reapportionment (LATFOR) precursors, facilitated the 1972 congressional maps by automating tallies across 39 districts, reducing manual errors and enabling compactness analyses that minimized elongated shapes favoring incumbents. Such methods marked a departure from hand-drawn maps, enhancing transparency and verifiability while enforcing causal links between demographic shifts and representational equity.Redistricting Framework State constitutional requirements The New York State Constitution, Article III, Section 5-c, requires congressional districts to consist of compact and contiguous territory while containing as nearly as may be an equal number of inhabitants, with reapportionment occurring every ten years following the federal census. This provision echoes federal equal population mandates under Article I, Section 2 of the U.S. Constitution and Section 2 of the 14th Amendment, which take primacy, but adds state-level emphasis on geographic cohesion to prevent arbitrary boundary drawing. Section 5-c further prohibits dividing towns, cities, or blocks except where strictly necessary to achieve population equality, maintain contiguity or compactness, or comply with the federal Voting Rights Act, thereby minimizing fragmentation of local political subdivisions and communities of interest. Compactness lacks a precise constitutional definition but is often evaluated empirically using metrics such as the Polsby-Popper score, which quantifies district shape by comparing area to perimeter squared (ranging ideally from 0 to 1, with higher values indicating greater regularity), as applied in redistricting analyses to assess adherence. Lower scores, such as those below 0.2 observed in some past New York districts, have signaled potential non-compliance prompting judicial scrutiny, though no fixed threshold is enshrined in the constitution. Historically, New York congressional maps have aimed for compliance with these mandates, with deviations—such as elongated districts crossing multiple counties—typically justified by population balancing needs, but persistent irregularities have led to repeated court evaluations of constitutional fidelity without outright invalidation absent federal violations.[27] The 2014 constitutional amendments reinforcing these criteria via the redistricting process underscore a commitment to structured geographic integrity over discretionary partisan adjustments.[28]Role of the Independent Redistricting Commission The Independent Redistricting Commission was created through Proposition 1, a constitutional amendment approved by New York voters on November 4, 2014, and effective January 1, 2015, to transfer primary authority for drawing congressional, state senate, and assembly districts from the legislature to an independent body.) [29] The reform sought to curb partisan entrenchment by insulating the process from legislative self-interest, where incumbents previously controlled lines affecting their own reelection prospects, often yielding maps accused of favoring the drawing party.[30][3] The commission comprises ten members selected for partisan balance: two appointed by the state senate majority leader, two by the senate minority leader, two by the assembly speaker, and two by the assembly minority leader, typically yielding four from each major party based on chamber control.[29] These eight then choose two independents unaffiliated with Democrats or Republicans for the prior five years, with selections requiring consultation from voting rights organizations and restricting appointees to New York registered voters without recent lobbying or legislative ties.[29] This composition aims to prevent dominance by any single party, fostering negotiation over extreme configurations that might otherwise maximize seats for the in-power faction. Following each decennial census, the commission develops maps through public hearings, data releases, and iterative drafts, submitting a plan upon seven affirmative votes, including at least one from each party's legislative leaders.[29][28] The legislature receives the proposal and may enact it verbatim or amend it via two-thirds supermajority in both houses, with changes capped at two percent population deviation per district to preserve commission intent; failure to achieve consensus prompts a second commission submission.[28] The governor retains veto power, subject to legislative override, adding executive oversight.[28] Proposed districts must be contiguous, compact where practicable, equally populated, respectful of municipal boundaries and communities of interest, compliant with federal voting rights laws, and free from favoring parties, incumbents, or suppressing electoral competition.[28] By design, the IRC erects procedural hurdles against unilateral gerrymandering, contrasting prior eras of unchecked legislative discretion that bred repeated deadlock and bias claims, with bipartisan thresholds and amendment limits compelling compromise toward maps aligning more closely with underlying electoral geography.[30][3] This structure prioritizes empirical criteria like population equality and compactness over discretionary partisan advantage, theoretically yielding less distorted representations of voter preferences.[28]Key legal precedents and processes The U.S. Supreme Court's decision in Wesberry v. Sanders (1964) established that congressional districts must contain substantially equal populations to comply with Article I, Section 2 of the Constitution, invalidating Georgia's apportionment where one district's population exceeded others by two to three times.[22] This federal mandate, rooted in the principle of equal representation, overrides state preferences for factors like compactness or contiguity when population deviations exceed minimal thresholds, typically under 1% total variance as clarified in subsequent cases like Karcher v. Daggett (1983).[14] In New York, this precedent has compelled judicial intervention in apportionment challenges, prioritizing empirical population equality derived from census data over discretionary state criteria, thereby enforcing causal accountability in vote dilution across districts.[31] New York's redistricting framework, amended via the 2014 constitutional reform creating the Independent Redistricting Commission, mandates structured processes including public hearings for input on proposed maps and requirements for data transparency in demographic and geographic analyses.[29] These steps, applicable to congressional lines, aim to incorporate verifiable evidence from stakeholders while subjecting proposals to scrutiny for compliance with federal equal-population standards, though courts retain authority to strike maps failing these tests.[32] Precise boundary delineation in New York relies on Geographic Information System (GIS) technology, which aggregates U.S. Census data at the smallest verifiable units—census blocks—to ensure contiguity, population precision, and minimal deviation without arbitrary adjustments.[33] This method facilitates first-principles verification of district integrity, reducing errors in causal factors like voter access disparities, and aligns with federal precedents by enabling courts to assess maps against objective metrics rather than subjective intent.[2]Recent Redistricting Cycles 2010 redistricting and its outcomes Following the 2010 census, which showed New York retaining 27 congressional districts after losing two seats, the state legislature—controlled by Democrats in the Assembly and Republicans in the Senate—attempted to redraw boundaries through the bipartisan Legislative Task Force on Demographic Research and Reapportionment (LATFOR). Deadlock ensued, with each chamber proposing partisan-favoring maps, leading to delays beyond the typical cycle. On March 15, 2012, the legislature passed a compromise map, but it faced immediate lawsuits alleging violations of compactness and process fairness; a federal court rejected it and appointed a special master, who finalized neutral-leaning maps on March 19, 2012, for use in the upcoming elections.[34][35] The resulting maps preserved incumbent protections across parties while incorporating subtle urban advantages aligned with New York's Democratic voter registration skew (approximately 6 million Democrats to 5 million Republicans as of 2010), but avoided aggressive gerrymandering. Analyses of partisan symmetry, such as simulated election outcomes under uniform swings, indicated a mild pro-Democratic tilt—estimated at 1-2 seats beyond proportional representation—far less pronounced than in states like North Carolina or Pennsylvania during the cycle, where efficiency gaps exceeded 10%. Compliance with the Voting Rights Act was prioritized, maintaining majority-minority districts in areas like the Bronx and Queens subject to Section 5 preclearance; the U.S. Department of Justice reviewed and effectively cleared the maps despite challenges from advocacy groups claiming dilution in districts such as the 14th, but no alterations were mandated before implementation.[34][36] In the 2012 elections under these maps, Democrats secured 21 seats to Republicans' 6, retaining their pre-redistricting dominance despite the national Republican wave that netted 8 House seats overall from redistricting advantages elsewhere. This outcome reflected the maps' status quo preservation rather than partisan engineering, as competitive races remained limited (only about 10% of districts saw margins under 10%), and flips were minimal: Democrats recaptured two Republican-held seats while losing one. The configuration helped insulate New York's delegation from broader GOP gains but drew criticism for entrenching uncompetitive urban strongholds pre-dating the 2014 Independent Redistricting Commission reforms.[36][37]2020 census adjustments and seat loss The 2020 United States Census, conducted as mandated by Article I, Section 2 of the Constitution, recorded New York's population at 20,201,249, reflecting a 2.6% increase from the 19,378,102 counted in 2010.[8] This growth rate lagged the national average of 7.4%, with the total U.S. apportionment population reaching 331,108,434.[38] Consequently, on April 26, 2021, the Census Bureau announced that New York would lose one of its 27 House seats, reducing its delegation to 26 members starting with the 118th Congress in 2023.[8] The apportionment adhered to the Huntington-Hill method, established by the 1941 Reapportionment Act, which allocates seats by prioritizing states based on the geometric mean of their current and next potential seat quotients to minimize relative representation disparities.[39] New York's seat forfeiture stemmed primarily from sustained domestic outmigration, with the state experiencing net losses of over 1 million residents to other states between 2010 and 2020, offset only partially by international immigration.[40] Upstate regions exhibited population stagnation or decline due to economic contraction in manufacturing and agriculture, while New York City saw significant outflows to suburban areas and Sun Belt states like Florida and Texas, driven by higher housing costs, taxation, and remote work opportunities post-2010.[41] These patterns reduced New York's relative share of the national population from approximately 6.25% in 2010 to 6.10% in 2020, rendering it vulnerable under the equal-proportions formula that favors faster-growing states.[42] In response to the lost seat, New York's congressional maps for the 2022 elections relied on an interim configuration derived from the 2010 districts, adjusted by court order to accommodate 26 districts while preserving compactness and contiguity pending comprehensive redistricting.[8] This approach ensured continuity amid the decennial process, with the U.S. Census Bureau's apportionment population—including overseas federal personnel—finalized without overseas military adjustments that might have marginally altered outcomes.[38]2022 and 2024 redistricting battles In the wake of the 2020 census, which apportioned New York 26 congressional seats rather than the previous 27, the Democratic-majority state legislature rejected a congressional districting plan submitted by the Independent Redistricting Commission (IRC) on January 13, 2022, and instead enacted its own map on February 1, 2022, signed into law by Governor Kathy Hochul.[3] This legislative map consolidated Democratic voters into fewer districts while extending influence into Republican-leaning areas, prompting Republican voters to challenge it in Harkenrider v. Hochul as violating the state constitution's Article III, §4-c requirements for compact, contiguous districts free from partisan bias.[43] On April 25, 2022, the Steuben County Supreme Court ruled the map unconstitutional, finding it deviated excessively from traditional criteria like compactness and county preservation to favor Democrats, and appointed special master Jonathan Cervas—a Carnegie Mellon University professor with prior redistricting experience—to draw a remedial plan. Cervas released his report and map on May 20, 2022, prioritizing non-partisan principles such as equal population, contiguity, and minimal county splits while avoiding extreme partisan outcomes.[44] The court adopted this map for the 2022 elections over Democratic objections, which argued for deference to the legislature; the resulting boundaries increased competitiveness, particularly on Long Island and in upstate New York, enabling Republicans to flip four districts (1st, 4th, 19th, and 22nd) and achieve net seat gains relative to outcomes under the invalidated legislative proposal.[45] The 2022 special master map faced ongoing challenges, leading to the New York Court of Appeals' 4-3 ruling on December 12, 2023, in Matter of Hoffmann v. New York State Independent Redistricting Commission, which determined the IRC had failed its constitutional obligation under Article III, §5-b to submit at least one compliant congressional plan in 2022 (having proposed only one rejected by the legislature's supermajority) and invalidated the remedial process for bypassing required IRC involvement.[30][46] The court mandated the IRC to reconvene and submit a new congressional plan by February 28, 2024, with the legislature permitted only limited amendments preserving compactness and rejecting overt gerrymanders. The IRC certified a revised congressional map on February 15, 2024, which modestly favored Democrats in aggregate but adhered more closely to neutral criteria than the 2022 legislative attempt.[47] Despite initial legislative rejection of the unamended IRC plan, the Democratic supermajorities passed a slightly modified version on February 28, 2024—incorporating minor boundary tweaks to enhance urban-suburban contiguity—signed by Governor Hochul that evening, averting further court intervention.[48][49] These maps, used for the 2024 elections, reduced extreme partisan skews compared to the 2022 legislative draft, fostering greater competitiveness in districts like the 3rd and 17th by balancing voter distributions without packing or cracking excesses.[50]Current Districts and Representatives District boundaries and demographic profiles New York's 26 congressional districts, as established by the 2022 court-approved map for the 119th Congress, divide the state's population of 20,201,249 from the 2020 Decennial Census into nearly equal parts, with each district averaging 776,979 residents and maximum population deviations under 1% to satisfy constitutional equal protection standards.[51][52] Geographically, the districts encompass urban cores in New York City (primarily Districts 5 through 16 across Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, and the Bronx), suburban expanses in Long Island's Nassau and Suffolk counties (Districts 2, 3, and 4), the Hudson Valley region (Districts 17, 18, and 19 spanning multiple counties like Westchester, Rockland, and Dutchess), and upstate areas blending suburban, exurban, and rural landscapes (Districts 20 through 26, extending from Albany to Buffalo and the Canadian border).[53][33] Rural elements are prominent in eastern Long Island (District 1) and northern districts, while urban density dominates the metropolitan axis. Demographic profiles highlight stark contrasts tied to geography. --- [New York City District #25 - Flushing, NY] ---
New York City District #25 is located at 30-48 Linden Place Flushing, NY and it provides for the educational needs in Queens County. District 25 in Queens had the highest average school score in New York City. Flushing was one of the original Dutch villages on Long Island. Today, Flushing has evolved into one of the largest neighborhoods in New York City. The public schools in Flushing are all supervised by the New York City Department of Education through Administrative District 25. There are numerous public Elementary and Junior High Schools in Flushing and students generally attend a school based on the location of their residence. Flushing High School which is the oldest public high school in the City of New York and there are 4 other high schools in the flushing area. The District staff members show a personal interest in developing students’ independent and creative thought, willingness to accept intellectual risks and challenges, forming superior work habits and cooperative, positive approaches to learning. While these attributes cannot always be precisely measured and adjusted, Teachers honor them and work energetically to foster their development. There is no doubt that the development of these skills brings with them not only a sense of academic achievement but also a feeling of pride and satisfaction.
