--- [Cape Town Cycle Tour - Wikipedia] ---
The Cape Town Cycle Tour, formerly known as the Cape Argus Cycle Tour, is an annual cycle race hosted in Cape Town, South Africa, usually 109 km (68 mi) long. It is the first event outside Europe to be included in the Union Cycliste Internationale's Golden Bike Series.
South Africa hosts some of the largest, by the number of entrants, sporting events in the world with three being the largest of their type. The Cape Town Cycle Tour, with as many as 35,000 cyclists taking part, is the world's largest individually timed cycle race. The other two are the world's largest ultra-marathon running event, the Comrades Marathon, and the world's largest open water swim, the Midmar Mile.
The Cycle Tour formed the last leg of the Giro del Capo, a multi-stage race for professional and leading registered riders which was last run in 2010.
It is traditionally staged on the second Sunday of March and has enjoyed well-known competitors such as Miguel Indurain, Jan Ulrich, Matt Damon, Helen Zille and Lance Armstrong.

In 2007, over R3- million was donated to the race's two official charities, the Pedal Power Association and the Rotary Club of Claremont in Cape Town.History The idea for the Cape Argus Pick 'n Pay Cycle Tour can be traced back to 1978, when Bill Mylrea and John Stegmann organised the Big Ride-In to draw attention to the need for cycle paths. The Ride-In, held under the auspices of the Western Province Pedal Power Association, was well received and drew hundreds of cyclists, including the Mayor of Cape Town. From this event the idea was planted that a long-distance race should take place and be known as the Peninsula Marathon. Before the race was run for the first time, the name was changed: the Cape Argus newspaper stepped in to sponsor the race, and thus the Argus Cycle Tour was born.1978 Five hundred and twenty-five starters lined up for the start of the 1978 race, and 446 cyclists made it across the finishing line as Lawrence Whittaker won the event in a shade over three hours, edging out W van der Merwe by one second for the victory. Janice Theis became the first women's winner, finishing in a time of 4:35:00. J and R Stegmann were the winners of the tandems, while 65-year-old JO Clough was the oldest finisher in a time of 6:25:00. It is interesting to note that a top-class bicycle at that time cost between R300 and R500!1979 At the request of Captour, the date for the 1979 Argus Cycle Tour was changed to coincide with the Cape Town festival, and the number of entrants almost doubled as 999 competitors set off on the 105-kilometre route. 239 failed to make it to the finish line. Hans Degenaar improved the men's record by three seconds short of 10 minutes, in a time of 2:52:28, while Janice Theis again won the women's event, bettering her winning time of the previous year by a staggering 58 minutes and 14 seconds as she lowered her record to 3:36:46. J Kennedy became the oldest competitor in the two- year history of the race when, at 74 years of age, he crossed the finish line in 5:19:45, while J and R Stegmann repeated as winners in the tandem category.1980 Having missed the 1 000 mark by a single rider in 1979, the Argus topped that milestone in 1980, with 1 398 cyclists entering the race. The Cape Town Cycle Tour is an annual one-day road cycling event held in Cape Town, South Africa, consisting of a challenging 109 km (68 mi) route that circumnavigates the Cape Peninsula, showcasing iconic landmarks such as Table Mountain, Chapman's Peak, and the Atlantic coastline.[1][2] Originating in 1978 as the "Big Ride-In"—a protest ride organized by Bill Mylrea and John Stegmann to advocate for better cycling infrastructure in the region—it evolved into a major international race under sponsorship from the Cape Argus newspaper, later renamed the Cape Argus Pick n Pay Cycle Tour in 1991 before adopting its current name in the 2010s.[2]Recognized as the world's largest individually timed cycling event, the tour takes place on the second Sunday in March and typically attracts around 30,000 participants from more than 30 countries, including elite professionals and recreational riders of all ages, with a peak of over 40,000 in 2001.[2][1] Since 2001, it has been part of the Union Cycliste Internationale (UCI) Golden Bike Series as the first event outside Europe to be included, featuring competitive categories with notable records such as the quickest time ever recorded on the 105 km course of 2:16:40 by Wimpie van der Merwe in his fully faired recumbent in 1993, averaging 46.1 km/h.[2] The route begins at the Grand Parade near Hertzog Boulevard in the city center and ends in Green Point, ascending climbs like Suikerbossie and descending coastal roads through areas including Muizenberg, Noordhoek, and Camps Bay, while emphasizing safety with electronic timing introduced in 1999.[2][1]Beyond its sporting significance, the event drives substantial economic benefits for the Western Cape, boosting tourism and local business, and supports charitable causes through initiatives like Cycle for a Cause, having donated millions of rand since its inception.[2] The 47th edition in March 2025 drew approximately 28,000 entrants despite weather challenges in prior years, underscoring its enduring appeal as a celebration of cycling, community, and Cape Town's natural beauty.[1] Overview Event Description The Cape Town Cycle Tour is an annual 109 km road cycling race held on the second Sunday of March in Cape Town, South Africa.[1] It serves as the world's largest individually timed cycling event, where participants ride a mass-participation course but are tracked via electronic timing chips for personal and competitive results.[1] Organized by the Cape Town Cycle Tour Trust, the event accommodates up to 35,000 riders, including elite athletes, recreational cyclists, and charity participants from around the globe. While historically accommodating up to 35,000, the 2025 edition saw nearly 28,000 participants.[3] The race begins at the Grand Parade Precinct in central Cape Town (near Hertzog Boulevard) and concludes at the Green Point Precinct near Cape Town Stadium, fostering a vibrant atmosphere with seeded starts to manage the large field efficiently.[4]Originally known as the Cape Argus Pick n Pay Momentum Cycle Tour, the event underwent a significant rebranding in 2014 to become the Cape Town Cycle Tour, reflecting its evolving international profile and alignment with global cycling standards following adjustments in long-standing sponsorship arrangements.[5] This name change emphasized the city's role as a premier cycling destination and simplified the title for broader appeal.[5]As a marquee event in the international calendar, the Cape Town Cycle Tour was the first outside Europe to be included in the Union Cycliste Internationale's (UCI) Golden Bike Series and, as of 2026, is part of the UCI Gran Fondo World Series, the first such event in Africa, highlighting its status among key global amateur and professional cycling competitions.[6] The series integration underscores the tour's role in promoting cycling accessibility and excellence on a worldwide stage.[7] Significance and Scale South Africa hosts several of the world's largest mass participation sporting events by number of entrants, including the Cape Town Cycle Tour, recognized as the world's largest individually timed cycle race, alongside the Comrades Marathon and the Midmar Mile, which hold similar distinctions in ultramarathon running and open water swimming, respectively.[8][9][10]The Cape Town Cycle Tour draws up to 35,000 participants annually in a non-competitive format that emphasizes personal achievement over head-to-head racing. This scale underscores its global prominence, with entrants hailing from 47 countries, including international cyclists comprising a significant portion of the field in recent editions (e.g., ~6,000 non-local riders in 2025), fostering a diverse community of riders from professional athletes to recreational enthusiasts.[11][12]The event significantly contributes to health promotion by encouraging widespread participation in cycling, a low-impact activity that supports physical fitness and community wellness initiatives, such as skin cancer awareness campaigns integrated into the race.[13] It bolsters tourism by showcasing Cape Town's iconic landscapes, attracting visitors who generate substantial economic activity in hospitality and related sectors, while reinforcing South African identity through its portrayal of the nation's scenic diversity and resilient spirit.[14] Extensive media coverage, including live broadcasts and digital tracking, amplifies its reach to a broad audience, highlighting cycling's role in sustainable lifestyles.[15]Over time, the Cycle Tour has evolved into a multifaceted series encompassing the Tricycle Tour for children under six, the Junior Cycle Tour for ages six to twelve, and the MTB Challenge for off-road enthusiasts, broadening accessibility across age groups and disciplines.[16] Following global disruptions in 2020, the event expanded virtual participation options, such as the Prelude Challenge allowing riders worldwide to complete the 109 km distance remotely and upload results for official recognition, which sustained engagement during restrictions and continued post-pandemic.[17]The 2025 edition exemplified post-pandemic recovery, with nearly 28,000 entrants completing the routes under ideal conditions, reaffirming the event's enduring appeal and capacity to unite participants in a celebration of endurance and scenic beauty.[1] Route and Terrain Primary Route The primary route of the Cape Town Cycle Tour is a 109 km circular path that begins at the Grand Parade in the Cape Town Central Business District (CBD) and proceeds south along the Cape Peninsula. Riders pass iconic landmarks such as Cape Town Castle and travel along Nelson Mandela Boulevard before navigating Hospital Bend and the University of Cape Town (UCT) grounds.[4]As the route continues southward, cyclists ride along the M3 to Muizenberg, then along the Main Road on the eastern side of the peninsula, hugging the coastline of False Bay toward Simon's Town. This segment passes through Smitswinkel Bay and Simon's Town. The route then crosses the peninsula in a westerly direction, passing the entrance to the Cape of Good Hope section of the Table Mountain National Park, within which Cape Point is situated.[4]The path then heads north along the Atlantic coast, passing through Scarborough, Kommetjie, Noordhoek, Chapman's Peak, and Hout Bay. Highlights include the dramatic ascent and descent of Chapman's Peak and the climb over Suikerbossie Hill on the way to Camps Bay.[4]The return leg continues via Camps Bay, Clifton, and Sea Point, with the finish line located next to the Cape Town Stadium in Green Point. The route remained consistent at 109 km for the 47th edition in March 2025, with the next event scheduled for March 8, 2026.[4] Variations and Challenges The Cape Town Cycle Tour's 109 km primary route features a demanding terrain profile with approximately 1,200 meters of total elevation gain, encompassing a mix of steep ascents, rolling hills, and coastal flats that test riders' endurance and climbing ability.[18] Key challenges include the Suikerbossie climb, a 1.5 km steep gradient near Hout Bay that demands significant power output amid spectator encouragement, and the multi-part Chapman's Peak ascent, which combines a short, sharp "Little Chappies" section with a longer, more gradual rise offering panoramic ocean vistas but requiring sustained effort.[4] These elevation shifts, combined with fast descents, create a physically taxing course that emphasizes pacing and recovery skills for the event's roughly 35,000 participants.[19]To enhance accessibility, the event offers shorter route variations, such as the 42 km option introduced in 2023, which provides a more approachable distance while still incorporating scenic coastal paths and moderate climbs for novice or family riders.[20] This variant starts at the same Grand Parade location but loops through Constantia and Tokai Forest with about 600 meters of elevation gain, allowing broader participation without the full peninsula circuit's intensity.[21] Earlier adaptations, like the 47 km solidarity ride in 2015, similarly adjusted the course for safety and inclusivity amid environmental constraints, underscoring the event's commitment to diverse rider levels.[22] In addition, the primary route has seen slight variations in certain periods; in 2009 and 2010, as well as in years prior to 1999, the route differed slightly from the recent standard, with lengths ranging between 104 km (65 mi) and 110 km (68 mi).Weather conditions add variable challenges, particularly the prevailing south-easterly winds—known as the "Cape Doctor"—which can gust up to 100 km/h and either assist on tailwind sections or hinder progress with headwinds, significantly impacting pacing and energy expenditure.[23] Potential rain, especially in early March, further complicates traction on descents and increases fatigue, as seen in past events where wet conditions slowed average speeds.[24] To mitigate these, organizers provide robust support infrastructure, including around 16 feed stations stocked with water, electrolytes, and snacks for hydration and nutrition, alongside 6 medical points for on-route assistance.[25] Traffic management is meticulously coordinated, with road closures, sweep vehicles, and cut-off times ensuring safe flow for the large peloton.[4]Iconic landmarks integrated into the route serve as motivational anchors, with sweeping views of Table Mountain from the start chutes and along the Atlantic seaboard providing visual inspiration during grueling segments like the return through Camps Bay.[20] These vistas, framed by the peninsula's dramatic cliffs and beaches, not only highlight the event's scenic allure but also boost psychological resilience amid physical demands.[4] History Origins and Founding The Cape Town Cycle Tour originated in 1978 as a protest initiative called the "Big Ride-In," organized by cyclists Bill Mylrea and John Stegmann to draw attention to the lack of safe cycling infrastructure in Cape Town. Frustrated by inadequate bike paths and the dangers faced by riders on busy roads, Mylrea and Stegmann, both prominent figures in the local cycling community, rallied support through the newly formed Western Province Pedal Power Association to stage a mass ride around the Cape Peninsula. The event was groundbreaking for its non-racial format, defying South Africa's apartheid-era segregation policies and attracting diverse participants, including the Mayor of Cape Town.[3][2]The inaugural race, sponsored by the Cape Argus newspaper and held on October 28, 1978, featured a route originally planned to be longer but reduced to 104 km due to refused authority to enter the Cape Point Nature Reserve, starting at the Castle of Good Hope on Strand Street and circling the peninsula. It drew 525 starters, with 446 completing the course under challenging conditions, including variable weather and rudimentary road markings. Local lawyer and cyclist Lawrence Whittaker claimed victory in the men's category in 3:02:24, while Janice Theis won the women's category in 4:35:00, marking the first of what would become an iconic timed event.[2][26] Early logistics relied on volunteers for route marshaling and basic manual timing, reflecting the informal, community-driven setup that characterized the founding era.[27]Participation grew rapidly in the subsequent years, fueled by word-of-mouth enthusiasm and media coverage from the Cape Argus, which helped formalize the event as the Argus Cycle Tour. For example, in 1979, Hans Degenaar won the men's category in 2:52:28, with 999 starters and 760 finishers. Organizational challenges persisted, including resistance from authorities wary of the event's inclusive nature and the need for ad-hoc volunteer coordination; these were partially addressed in 1983 when the Rotary Club of Claremont took over operations to channel proceeds toward community fundraising. By the mid-1980s, as numbers approached 10,000, the tour transitioned to more professional timing systems to handle the scale, ensuring accurate individually timed results for the growing field.[2][27][28] Major Developments Building on its establishment in 1978, the Cape Town Cycle Tour experienced steady growth in participation during the early 1980s. Entrant numbers rose from 999 in 1979 (with 760 finishers) to 1,398 in 1980 (1,119 finishers), 1,669 in 1981 (1,372 finishers), and 1,698 in 1982. To manage increasing congestion as the field grew, the start location was moved in 1981 to Hertzog Boulevard to reduce bottlenecks at the on-ramp to the Eastern Boulevard. The 1982 edition was impacted by a strong southeaster wind that disrupted bannering at the start and howled throughout the day.[2]In 1983, despite very windy conditions, 1929 cyclists finished the race. The first bicycle to cross the finish line was a tandem ridden by William Smith and Francois du Toit, while Robbie McIntosh won the men's category in 02:49:55.[2]In 1984, Theuns Mulder from South Africa won the men's category with a time of 02:55:07, Isavel Roche-Kelly from South Africa won the women's category in 03:19:14, and Lloyd Wright recorded the quickest overall time on an unconventional bike in 02:43:51.[2]In 1985, Hennie Wentzel from South Africa won the men's category, Louise van Riet Lowe from South Africa won the women's category in 03:04:36, with 3008 entrants and 2445 finishers.[2]During the 1990s, the Cape Town Cycle Tour underwent substantial expansion, with participant numbers surging from around 11,000 in 1990 to over 20,000 by the mid-decade, reflecting its growing popularity as a mass-participation event that blended recreational and competitive cycling. This era marked the formalization of professional racing categories, enabling elite athletes to vie for titles within the same timed structure as amateur riders, which helped attract international talent and boosted the event's competitive edge. In 1991, Pick n Pay joined as a sponsor, renaming the event the Cape Argus Pick n Pay Cycle Tour.[29][30][2]Sponsorship by the Cape Argus newspaper, which began in 1978 and continued until 2013, played a pivotal role in funding infrastructural expansions, including improved road closures, medical support, and marketing efforts that positioned the tour as a global spectacle. Following the end of the Argus partnership in 2013, the event rebranded to the Cape Town Cycle Tour in 2014 and achieved operational independence under the Cape Town Cycle Tour Trust, which reinvests surplus funds into community cycling initiatives and charitable causes, having distributed over R130 million since its inception.[5][3]To diversify its offerings, organizers introduced ancillary events in the early 2000s, including the Giro del Capo, a multi-stage professional road race held from 1992 to 2010 that culminated with the Cycle Tour as its final leg and attracted UCI-licensed teams from around the world. Complementing this, the Cape Town Cycle Tour MTB Challenge launched in 2001 as an off-road event, providing mountain bikers with a parallel competitive platform during the tour week.[31][32][33]Technological advancements enhanced the event's precision and spectator engagement starting in the late 1990s, with electronic chip timing transponders introduced in 1999 to provide accurate individual start, finish, and split times for all participants. By the 2010s, live tracking applications, such as the SportSplits Tracker integrated with mobile platforms, allowed real-time monitoring of riders' progress, leaderboards, and GPS routes, improving safety and accessibility for both competitors and remote viewers.[29][1]The 2020s brought adaptations in response to the COVID-19 pandemic, with the 2020 edition proceeding under strict health protocols amid early virus concerns, while the 2021 event was postponed and transformed into a hybrid virtual format on platforms like FulGaz, enabling global participants to ride simulated routes from home. Full-scale resumption occurred in 2022, with over 20,000 riders completing the in-person tour, signaling a return to its traditional mass-participation model while incorporating enhanced hygiene and digital registration systems.[34][35][36] Cancellations and Alterations The Cape Town Cycle Tour has been stopped three times due to extreme weather, significantly shortened once due to fire, and cancelled once. In the first two cases of stoppages due to extreme weather, many competitors had completed the race before the stoppage.The event has experienced several disruptions due to extreme weather and environmental factors throughout its history, leading to stoppages, shortenings, or full cancellations. Between 2000 and 2003, due to the closure of Chapman's Peak Drive, the event followed an alternative route consisting of a return trip via Ou Kaapse Weg and the Blue Route. In 2002, the event was halted prematurely at Ou Kaapse Weg around 14:45 when temperatures soared to 42°C (108°F), posing significant health risks to participants after many had already completed the route.[37] Similarly, the 2009 edition featured the strongest winds ever recorded for the event, with maximum gusts reaching 120 km/h (75 mph). Fences were blown down and banners ripped to shreds at the start, and the race was stopped at Chapman's Peak at 16:30 due to extreme weather conditions, with an extended cut-off time of 8 hours, though a substantial number of participants finished before the stoppage.[29]The 2015 race underwent a major alteration rather than a full cancellation, shortened to a 47 km circular route due to devastating wildfires that ravaged the southern peninsula and damaged key sections of the traditional path, including areas near Chapman's Peak Drive.[38] This rerouting avoided fire-affected zones and prioritized safety amid ongoing blazes that had burned for days. The 2017 event marked the first complete cancellation in the tour's then-40-year history. The cancellation occurred on the day of the race after the first group of cyclists had started, with those participants turned back at the end of the M3 highway, due to safety concerns from winds in excess of 100 km/h (62 mph) that toppled cyclists, fires on part of the route in Hout Bay requiring emergency access, and the threat of possible protest action along a section of the route.[39][40]These disruptions significantly affected participants, with organizers adhering to entry terms that prohibited refunds for cancellations occurring close to or during the event, as seen in 2017 when over 35,000 riders were impacted without financial reimbursement.[41] In response to such challenges, alternatives like virtual rides emerged, particularly during the COVID-19 pandemic when the 2021 edition was postponed to 17 October 2021 and effectively cancelled, allowing participants to complete a 109 km challenge via platforms such as FulGaz.[42] The ongoing Prelude Challenge offers a flexible, location-independent option to ride the distance within seven hours and upload results, serving as a contingency for those unable to join the main event.[17]From these incidents, organizers emphasized safety as paramount, implementing stricter weather assessments and contingency measures to mitigate risks, such as early monitoring of wind patterns and fire alerts.[43] Route stabilizations post-2017 included refined planning for wind-exposed sections, contributing to smoother operations in subsequent years. --- [Cape Town Cycle Tour - UCI Gran Fondo World Series] ---
The Cape Town Cycle Tour is the world’s largest individually timed cycling event, held every March on a scenic 109 km route around the Cape Peninsula. It began in 1978 as a protest ride advocating for safer cycling infrastructure and quickly evolved into a major race that welcomed riders of all races during apartheid, setting a precedent for inclusivity. Over the years, it has grown into a global spectacle attracting tens of thousands of participants and cycling legends, while supporting charitable causes and promoting cycling development. COURSE The Cape Town Cycle Tour is a 109 km road race with 1,200 m of climbing, starting in the city centre beneath the iconic silhouette of Table Mountain. Riders roll out onto a series of early ascents via Nelson Mandela Boulevard, Hospital Bend and Edinburgh Drive before settling into a fast, flowing coastal run through Muizenberg, Kalk Bay and Fish Hoek. The route rises again toward Smitswinkel and the edge of the Cape Point Nature Reserve, opening into some of the most spectacular shoreline riding anywhere in the world. The final test comes with the Chapman’s Peak and Suikerbossie climbs, two Cape Town landmarks that shape every race. From the top of Suikerbossie, it’s a triumphant return along the Atlantic Seaboard, sweeping through Bakoven, Camps Bay, Clifton and Sea Point toward a high-energy finish back in the city. This is road cycling at its most beautiful, a route as iconic as the place it calls home. PROFILE Elevation profile - Total Elevation Gain: Around 1,200–1,300 metres. - Key Climbs: - Edinburgh Drive (Wynberg Hill): Early in the ride, a short but sharp climb to warm up the legs. - Chapman’s Peak: A scenic climb of about 4.5 km with gentle gradients (average 4–5%) but exposed to wind. - Suikerbossie: The toughest climb near the end—2.6 km averaging 6%, with pitches up to 8%. This is the decisive point for many riders. - Terrain: Mostly rolling with long flat sections along the coast, interspersed with these three major climbs. Difficulty - The route is challenging due to its length, wind conditions, and the final Suikerbossie climb, but it’s achievable for most trained cyclists. The scenery—mountains, ocean views, and coastal roads—makes it one of the most beautiful rides in the world. CAPE TOWN The Mother City Cape Town is South Africa’s crown jewel, a vibrant coastal city where natural beauty meets rich culture and history. Nestled between the iconic Table Mountain and the sparkling Atlantic Ocean, it offers an unforgettable mix of adventure, relaxation, and world-class cuisine. Visitors can ride the cable car or hike to the summit of Table Mountain for panoramic views, explore the colourful streets of Bo-Kaap, and learn about South Africa’s past at Robben Island and the District Six Museum. For nature lovers, the Cape Point Nature Reserve and Kirstenbosch Botanical Gardens showcase breathtaking landscapes and unique flora, while Boulders Beach delights with its famous African penguin colony. Wine enthusiasts can savour award-winning vintages in the nearby Stellenbosch and Franschhoek winelands, and foodies will find endless culinary experiences at the V&A Waterfront and trendy city eateries. Whether you’re paragliding from Lion’s Head, enjoying a sunset cruise, or simply soaking up the sun on Clifton’s beaches, Cape Town promises a perfect blend of adventure and relaxation. It’s not just a destination—it’s an experience that stays with you forever.
