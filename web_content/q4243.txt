Qinhuangdao Beidaihe Airport (IATA: BPE, ICAO: ZBDH) is an airport serving the city of Qinhuangdao, Hebei Province, China. It is located in Longjiadian Town, Changli County, 47 kilometres (29 mi) from the city center and 34 kilometres (21 mi) from Beidaihe.[2] The Beidaihe Airport project was started in 2004, and received approval from the State Council in July 2009. The construction feasibility report was approved in May 2011, and construction officially began in April 2012. The airport was opened on 31 March 2016, replacing the old Shanhaiguan Airport, which was shared with the military, as Qinhuangdao's main airport. Shanhaiguan Airport reverted to sole military use.[2] The airport occupies an area of 2,195 mu. It has a runway that is 2,600 meters long and 60 meters wide, and a 10,592-square-metre (114,010 sq ft) terminal building. The Yellow River, anciently known as the "River Water" or "Great River," according to expert research and textual studies, flowed extensively through the heart of what is now Hebei for over 20,000 years before the Eastern Han dynasty and discharged into the sea in the area of present-day Cangzhou and Tianjin. The "Zhouli·Zhifang," compiled over 2,000 years ago, contains references to "the land north of the river." The "Strategies of the Warring States·Zhao Ce III," which dates to the Western Han dynasty period, states, "Zhao possesses the land north of the river, Qi possesses the land east of the river." The term "Hebei" in these records refers to the vast region north of the Yellow River, encompassing most of present-day Hebei. "Hebei" as a clearly defined administrative region began in the Tang dynasty. During the Zhenguan reign period of Tang, Li Shimin established the Hebei Circuit. By the time of Emperor Gaozong of Tang, Li Zhi, the territory of the Hebei Circuit largely covered the present-day Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region. During the Ming and Qing dynasties, most of Hebei belonged to Zhili Province, with its provincial capital in Baoding. "Zhili" signifies being directly subordinate to the central government. The Zhili region during the Qing dynasty was, in practice, a province, also called Zhili Province. The Zhili prefecture was reduced to the level of a county-level city. Jizhou, which once referred to the entire Hebei region governed by Yuan Shao as the Governor of Jizhou, ultimately became the Jizhou District of Hengshui City. In subsequent dynasties, the territory and administrative names of Hebei underwent several changes, but its general outline did not undergo significant alteration. 256 After the Northern Expedition in 1928, Beijing was renamed Beiping, and Zhili Province was renamed Hebei Province, marking the origin of "Hebei" as a modern geographical name. In July 1949, the Hebei provincial administrative system was reinstated. On August 1 of the same year, the People's Government of Hebei Province was formally established. Since then, Hebei has been a provincial-level administrative division unit. 256 Historical Development Hebei Province is one of the birthplaces of the Chinese nation. As early as over five thousand years ago, the three legendary progenitors of the Chinese nation—the Yellow Emperor , the Yan Emperor , and Chi You—engaged in warfare and eventual integration in Hebei, initiating the history of Chinese civilization. 72 Pre-Qin Period Archaeological findings from the Nihewan site cluster in Yangyuan, within the territory of present-day Hebei Province, reveal that as early as 1.76 million years ago, ancient human activities were already present in the Nihewan area of Hebei. Based on archaeological discoveries at Houjiayao and Xujiayao villages in Yangyuan, located at the border between present-day Hebei and Shanxi provinces, approximately 200,000 to 700,000 years ago, the Hebei region was home to Peking Man. 280 During the Neolithic period, the entire area of Hebei, particularly the piedmont zone of the Taihang Mountains and the Zhangjiakou region, was the most densely populated and active area for Neolithic inhabitants. In the early Neolithic period (10,000 to 7,500 years ago), early inhabitants lived in scattered locations such as Yujiagou in Hutouliang Village, Yangyuan County, Zhangjiakou in the north; northeast of Nanzhuangtou Village, Xushui District, Baoding in the central region; and on the terrace on the north bank of the Nanming River, 1 kilometer east of Cishan Village, Wu'an City, Handan in the south. By the middle Neolithic period (7,500 to 5,000 years ago), the Yangshao culture and the Hongshan culture had emerged within Hebei. In the late Neolithic period (5,000 to 4,000 years ago), Hebei engaged in deeper cultural exchanges with neighboring provinces such as Shandong, Henan, Shanxi, and Shaanxi, leading to the emergence of the Longshan culture. 280 Hebei was anciently called Jizhou. Shun divided the land into twelve provinces, with Hebei designated as Youzhou. The Tribute of Yu also records it as Jizhou. The Rites of Zhou ·Zhifang refers to it as Youzhou. 276 In the 15th century BCE, King Zu Yi of the Shang dynasty moved the capital to Xing (present-day Xingtai City). The capital remained there through the reigns of Zu Yi, Zu Xin, Wo Jia , Zu Ding, and Nan Geng, until Nan Geng moved it to Yan, a period spanning approximately 130 years. 276 In the 11th century BCE, King Wu of the Zhou dynasty enfeoffed the son of the Duke of Shao with the Yan State and the son of the Duke of Zhou with the Xing state. Consequently, Hebei became part of the territories of Yan and Xing. 276 During the Spring and Autumn period, the northern part of Hebei province belonged to the Yan state, the southern part belonged to the Zhongshan state, Zhao state, and The Wei State , and the northwestern part belonged to the Dai state. Handan and Xingtai were once the capitals of the Zhao state. In the late Spring and Autumn period, in 506 BCE, the Baidi Xianyu established the Zhongshan state at Zhongren City (northwest of present-day Tang County, Hebei). It was later destroyed twice and restored, and around 380 BCE, it established its capital at the ancient Lingshou City (present-day Sanji Village, Pingshan, Hebei). During the mid-Warring States period, the Zhongshan state gradually grew in strength. At its peak, its territory included the southern part of present-day Baoding City, most of Shijiazhuang City, the northern part of Xingtai City, and the western part of Hengshui City in Hebei, stretching approximately 200 kilometers north-south and 150 kilometers east-west. In 296 BCE, the Zhongshan state was destroyed by the Zhao state. 255 Qin, Han, and the Northern and Southern Dynasties Period During the reign of Qin Shi Huang, the region fell under the Julue Commandery, Handan Commandery, Hengshan Commandery, Qi Commandery, Jibei Commandery, Shanggu Commandery, Dai Commandery, Yuyang Commandery, and Youbeiping Commandery. 255 During the Western Han Dynasty , Hebei was part of Youzhou, Jizhou, and Bingzhou. In the late Eastern Han dynasty, most of Hebei was under the control of the northern warlord Gongsun Zan and the southern warlord Yuan Shao. In 200 CE, Cao Cao defeated Yuan Shao at the Battle of Guandu. Hebei was subsequently ruled by the Wei state. 255 During the Eastern Jin and Sixteen Kingdoms period, the area was successively occupied by the Later Zhao , Former Yan, Former Qin, Later Qin, Later Yan, Northern Yan , and Southern Yan. 255 During the Southern and Northern Dynasties period, it successively belonged to the The Northern Wei Dynasty , kingdom Of East Wei , Kingdom of Qi , and Northern Zhou . 255 Sui, Tang, Song, and Yuan Dynasties Period During the Sui dynasty, it belonged to Jizhou. 255 During the Tang dynasty, it belonged to the Hebei Circuit. Hebei as a distinct administrative region began in the Tang dynasty. During the Zhenguan era of Tang, Li Shimin divided the country into ten "circuits," including the Hebei Circuit. By the time of Emperor Gaozong of Tang, Li Zhi, the territory of the Hebei Circuit was already vast, essentially covering the present-day Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region. In subsequent dynasties, Hebei's territory and administrative names underwent several changes, but its general outline did not change significantly. 255 Hebei in the Tang Dynasty During the Later Jin of the Five Dynasties, Shi Jingtang ceded the Sixteen Prefectures of Yan and Yun to the Khitan Liao dynasty. Emperor Shizong of Later Zhou recovered the Three Passes, establishing the Baigou River as the border with the Khitans. During the Five Dynasties period, Yedu (present-day Daming Prefecture) became the focal point of Hebei. 259 During the Northern Song and Liao dynasties, the southern part belonged to the Hebei East Circuit and Hebei West Circuit of the Song dynasty, while the northern part belonged to the Liao Nanjing Circuit, Xijing Circuit, and Zhongjing Circuit. After Emperor Taizu of Song seized power from the Later Zhou, he continued the old military governor system in the Hebei region but implemented some reforms. In the second year of the Xianping era of Emperor Zhenzong (999 AD), the Khitans invaded south. The Song court consolidated the armies stationed in various prefectures of Hebei for wartime purposes, merging the former Zhending Circuit and Dingzhou Circuit into one, with the Military Commissioners of the Zhending Circuit and Gaoyang (Pass) Circuit commanding the remaining garrisoned armies in Hebei. Most rivers originate from or flow through the Yanshan Mountains and Taihang Mountains. Their lower reaches either converge before flowing into the sea or enter the sea individually, forming inland rivers on the Zhangbei Plateau in Zhangjiakou. 209 The Hai River system is the largest river system in Hebei Province, with an average annual runoff of 7.6 billion cubic meters and a drainage area of 260,000 square kilometers, of which 140,000 square kilometers are within Hebei Province, located in the central and southern parts of the province. The system is fan-shaped. The main stream of the Hai River is very short and is located in Tianjin Municipality. Rivers within the province, such as the North Canal, Yongding River, Da Qing River, Ziya River, and South Canal, are its tributaries, all converging into the Hai River, which flows through Tianjin to Tanggu before entering the sea. The most notable characteristics of this system are: first, the river gradient becomes gentle after entering the plains; second, the river channels are prone to siltation and shifting; third, the inflow from upstream far exceeds the discharge capacity of the downstream Hai River. During flood seasons, river levels rise and fall rapidly, often causing damage due to insufficient drainage. Furthermore, due to poor vegetation and severe soil erosion in the upper reaches of these rivers, floodwaters carry a high sediment load, leading to siltation, riverbed elevation, and the formation of semi-elevated or elevated "hanging" rivers. This is particularly severe for the Zhang River, Hutuo River, and Yongding River, all of which have historically been referred to as "Little Yellow Rivers." Taking the Yongding River as an example, the Guanting Station records an average annual sediment discharge of 60.3 million tons, with an average sediment concentration of 33.9 kilograms per cubic meter, second only to the Yellow River. Additionally, the Chaobai River and Ji Canal, located southwest of the Luan River, are also part of the Hai River system but discharge into the sea separately. 277 Yongding River The Luan River system has a drainage area of over 50,000 square kilometers, with 45,800 square kilometers within Hebei Province. It is the mother river of Chengde, a national-level tourist city in the province, and Tangshan, a major industrial base in the province. In the 8th year of the Kangxi era of the Qing dynasty (1669), the Governor of Zhili moved his seat to Baoding Prefecture, and thus the provincial capital was moved to Baoding, where it remained until the late Qing dynasty. 189 After the 9th year of the Tongzhi era of the Qing dynasty (1870), the Viceroy of Zhili concurrently held the position of Beiyang Minister. The Viceroy's yamen was located in Baoding until the fall of the Qing dynasty (1911), after which the position was abolished, spanning a period of 182 years. 299 In July of the 17th year of the Republic of China (1928), Zhili Province was renamed Hebei Province (the name Hebei Province first appeared), with the provincial capital remaining in Tianjin, but it was moved to Beiping in October of that same year. In October of the 19th year of the Republic of China (1930), the provincial capital moved back to Tianjin. In June of the 24th year of the Republic of China (1935), Tianjin was designated as a municipality directly under the central government, and the provincial capital of Hebei was moved back to Baoding. With the outbreak of the War of Resistance Against Japan, the Hebei Provincial Government became itinerant. Between September 1937 and 1945, its offices were successively located in Daming, Luoyang, Mei County, Beiping, Baoding, Xi'an, and other places. Present-day Daming County served as the provincial capital of Hebei twice during this period. In November 1945, in the initial period after the end of the War of Resistance, the provincial capital of Hebei was established in Beiping. In July 1946, the Hebei Provincial Government moved its offices from Beiping back to Baoding, and Baoding once again became the provincial capital. In November 1947, during the Civil War, the provincial capital moved from Baoding back to Beiping. In July 1949, with most of North China liberated, the North China People's Government decided to establish a unified Hebei Province. The Hebei Provincial Committee of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China and the Hebei Provincial People's Government were successively established in Baoding, making Baoding the provincial capital of Hebei. In April 1958, during the Great Leap Forward period, to promote mutual advancement between industry and agriculture and achieve a comprehensive leap forward, the 7th Session of the 1st Provincial People's Congress decided to move the provincial capital from Baoding to Tianjin. 190 In the 1960s, as Sino-American and Sino-Soviet relations deteriorated, in response to Comrade Mao Zedong's instruction to "prepare for war, prepare for famine," the Hebei Provincial Committee decided to move the provincial capital out of Tianjin and back to Baoding. In May 1966, the provincial capital of Hebei was moved from Tianjin back to Baoding. Shortly after the return of the provincial capital to Baoding, the "Cultural Revolution" erupted. The Party and government authority in Hebei province was seized by rebel factions, and the armed struggles among several factions made it impossible to form a unified provincial leadership core. Many provincial-level departments never entered Baoding city proper, instead scattering across surrounding counties. During this period, the situation in Shijiazhuang was relatively stable, with industrial and agricultural production showing steady growth. In January 1968, the Beijing Military Region convened a preparatory meeting for the Hebei Provincial Revolutionary Committee in Beijing. It was considered that Shijiazhuang's working class was relatively strong and that it was an important transportation hub; it was suggested that the provincial capital of Hebei be moved from Baoding city to Shijiazhuang city. On January 29 of the same year, the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China, the State Council, the Central Military Commission, and the Central "Cultural Revolution" Group approved the establishment of the Hebei Provincial Revolutionary Committee and agreed to move the provincial capital from Baoding to Shijiazhuang. After the move to Shijiazhuang, the situation was not calm, with further debate over "staying in Shijiazhuang" and "returning to Baoding." In March 1971, Premier Zhou Enlai instructed that the provincial capital of Hebei should not be moved again. Since then, the provincial capital has been located in Shijiazhuang. 189 On May 1, 2016, with the approval of the State Council, the organs of the Hebei Provincial People's Government moved from No. 46, Weiming South Street, Qiaoxi District , Shijiazhuang City to No. 113, Yuhua East Road, Chang'an District, Shijiazhuang City. During the late Ming and early Qing dynasties, local operas began to flourish. At that time, northern China experienced frequent warfare and social turmoil, and the lives of common people were very difficult. The slow-paced and gentle Kunqu opera could hardly satisfy the artistic needs of the northern populace. The vigorous Shanshan Bangzi, arriving at this opportune time, was introduced to the capital region (including Hebei) via Zhangjiakou area by Shanxi merchants. The orchestra of Hebei Bangzi consists of two sections: the civil section (wenchang) and the martial section (wuchang). In earlier times, the former included string and wind instruments such as the banhu and liudi, while the latter included percussion instruments such as the bangu, bangzi (wooden clappers), large gong, naobo (cymbals), and small gong. Later, other instruments were gradually added, but it still primarily relies on the banhu, liudi, and sheng. The wooden clappers (bangzi) used in this opera genre are mostly made of jujube wood. On May 20, 2006, Hebei Bangzi, as declared by Hebei Province, was approved by the State Council of the People's Republic of China to be included in the first batch of national-level intangible cultural heritage list. 170 Weixian County paper-cutting Weixian paper-cutting, also known as "window flowers," is a traditional folk decorative art in the local area with a history of over two hundred years. They represent the largest existing, most complete system, and most regular layout of an ancient imperial mausoleum complex in China. Construction began in the 18th year of the Shunzhi Emperor's reign (1661) and was completed in the 34th year of the Guangxu Emperor's reign (1908), spanning 247 years, almost coinciding with the entire duration of the Qing dynasty. The Eastern Qing Tombs consist of 15 mausoleums in total: 5 emperor mausoleums, 4 empress mausoleums, 5 concubine garden tombs, and 1 princess garden tomb. They interred 5 emperors of the Qing dynasty (Shunzhi, Kangxi, Qianlong, Xianfeng, Tongzhi), 15 empresses, 136 concubines, 3 princes, and 2 princesses, totaling 161 individuals. The various mausoleums of the Eastern Qing Tombs are arranged east to west on the southern foothills of Mount Changrui, centered on the Xiaoling Mausoleum of the Shunzhi Emperor. Mausoleums for those of lower generational status are situated farther from the Xiaoling Mausoleum, while the empress mausoleums and concubine garden tombs of the same reign period are built beside the emperor's mausoleum of that period. The layout of the Eastern Qing Tombs embodies both the traditional Chinese concept of centrality and reverence and the burial system that emphasizes respect for ancestors and lineal continuity. 121 Within the Eastern Qing Tombs are the longest spirit way of the Qing dynasty—the 6-kilometer-long Xiaoling Mausoleum Spirit Way; the largest stone statues of humans and animals of the Qing dynasty—the 18 pairs of statues along the Xiaoling Mausoleum Spirit Way; as well as the Yuling Mausoleum underground palace stone carvings, renowned as the "underground Buddhist hall" and "treasury of stone carvings," and the Cixi Mausoleum, hailed as the crown jewel of Qing mausoleums. The Eastern Qing Tombs are an outstanding representative of the final glorious period in the history of Chinese mausoleums and a crystallization of the wisdom of the Chinese nation. It has successively been awarded titles such as a UNESCO Global Geopark, a National Scenic Area, a National AAAAA-level Tourist Attraction, a National Forest Park, a National Ecotourism Demonstration Zone, a National Cultural Industry Demonstration Base, and a National Popular Science Education Base. Yesanpo is regarded as a tourist destination for adventure and exploration, as well as a natural classroom for scientific research and study tours. 268 Yesanpo derives its name from its terrain, which gradually rises from south to north. As the elevation changes, so does the climate, leading locals to traditionally divide it into the Upper Slope, Middle Slope, and Lower Slope. Within the area, the Juma River, Baili Gorge, and Yugudong Cave are located on the Lower Slope; Longmen Tian Pass is on the Middle Slope; and Baicaopan is situated on the Upper Slope. Among these, the Baili Gorge scenic area enjoys the reputation of being the "Number One Gorge Under Heaven." The gorge appears as if carved by a knife and split by an axe, with strange rocks standing upright, crabapple trees filling the ravines, and flowers and plants covering the mountains. The Juma River winds its way through the entire Yesanpo scenic area. The Yugudong Cave and Yugudong Spring in the Yugudong Spring scenic area are naturally formed. Yugudong Cave has a long history of its cave spring. Every year around the Grain Rain season, the spring here gushes out fish, a phenomenon listed among the world's wonders. This interesting spectacle typically lasts for 8 to 10 days, with the peak output reaching over 2,000 jin (approximately 1,000 kilograms), and each fish weighing up to about six or seven liang (approximately 300-350 grams). Locals call them "Shikou fish." The phenomenon of the spring gushing fish is regarded as an age-old mystery, and Yugudong Spring is also listed as one of the "Eight Strange Springs." Yesanpo's Baicaopan is known as the "Green Pearl in the Taihang Mountains." In May of the 37th year of the Republic of China (1948), Mao Zedong, Zhou Enlai, and Ren Bishi led the central organs here, making Xibaipo the seat of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China and the leading center of the Chinese revolution. In March 1949, the Party Central Committee and Chairman Mao left Xibaipo for Beijing to establish the new nation. In 1978, to commemorate the 30th anniversary of the relocation of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China and the General Headquarters of the People's Liberation Army to Xibaipo, the Xibaipo Site of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China and the Xibaipo Memorial Hall were officially opened to the public. On March 11, 1982, the State Council announced the Xibaipo Site of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China as a National Key Cultural Relics Protection Unit. In 2012, it was awarded the title of "National Advanced Unit in Cultural System Reform" by the Publicity Department of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China. 115 Due to its significant position in Chinese history, Xibaipo has become one of the national revolutionary memorial sites. Attractions open for visitors include the Xibaipo Site of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China, the Xibaipo Memorial Hall, the Xibaipo Stone Carving Garden, the National Security Education Hall, the Bronze Statues of the Five Secretaries, the Xibaipo Monument, and the Xibaipo Youth Civilization Garden.
