--- [Paul Henry and Prosper Henry] ---
Paul Pierre Henry (1848–1905) and Prosper Mathieu Henry (1849–1903) were French brothers who worked as astronomers and opticians at the Paris Observatory, pioneering astronomical photography and discovering a total of 14 asteroids between 1872 and 1878.[1][2] Born in Nancy, the siblings joined the observatory's meteorological service at age 16 and later transferred to the equatorial telescope section in 1871, where they conducted independent optical research from a shared workshop in Montrouge.[1] Their inseparable collaboration advanced 19th-century astronomy through innovative telescope designs and systematic sky mapping, earning them multiple prizes from the Academy of Sciences and associate membership in the Royal Astronomical Society in 1889.[1]The Henry brothers' early work focused on constructing a 30 cm reflector telescope in 1868, which they used to chart stars up to 13th magnitude along a 5-degree band of the ecliptic, cataloging 36,000 stars by 1884 before halting due to the Milky Way's density.[1] This project not only facilitated their asteroid discoveries—credited jointly under "P. P. Henry" and "P. M. Henry" by the Minor Planet Center—but also laid groundwork for broader celestial surveys.[2] Notable asteroids include 125 Liberatrix (1872), 164 Eva (1876), and 186 Celuta (1878), among others identified during their systematic observations.[2] Their efforts complemented major initiatives like the 1882 transit of Venus expedition to Pic du Midi, where they contributed precise measurements.[1]In astronomical photography, the brothers innovated by designing a 16 cm achromatized objective lens optimized for photographic wavelengths, paired with a guiding telescope that enabled exposures up to one hour by 1884.[1] They built the first large photographic equatorial telescope in 1885 (34 cm aperture, 3.4 m focal length), producing groundbreaking images of the Pleiades cluster and the nebula around Maia (NGC 1432), discovered on plates from November 16, 1885.[3] This instrument served as a prototype for the Carte du Ciel project, with the Henrys constructing over half of its 17 identical telescopes and fabricating the 76 cm refractor lens for the Nice Observatory.[1] Paul was appointed head of the Carte du Ciel service in 1893 and chief astronomer in 1897, while Prosper held the chief astronomer title from 1893 and led the service from 1900 until his death.[1] Their legacy endures in lunar and Martian craters named in their honor, such as Henry Frères on the Moon.[3] Early Lives Paul Henry Paul Pierre Henry was born on 21 August 1848 in Nancy, France.[1] Alongside his younger brother Prosper, he developed an early interest in astronomy and optics, becoming largely self-taught in these fields. In 1865, at the age of 17, Paul entered the Paris Observatory as a supernumerary in the meteorological service.[4] By 1871, he transferred to the equatorial telescope section, where he began conducting independent optical research. That same year, the brothers established a shared workshop in Montrouge to pursue their innovative designs in telescope construction.[1]Their early collaboration was marked by the construction of a 30 cm reflector telescope in 1868, which they used for initial stellar observations along the ecliptic. This project honed their skills and laid the foundation for their later astronomical contributions.[1] Prosper Henry Prosper Mathieu Henry was born on 10 December 1849 in Nancy, France.[1] Like his brother Paul, Prosper showed a precocious aptitude for science and joined the Paris Observatory in 1865 at the age of 16, starting as a supernumerary in the meteorological bureau.[4] He was formally appointed as an assistant astronomer in 1872. In 1871, Prosper followed Paul to the observatory's equatorial section, where the brothers began their joint optical experiments.[1]From their Montrouge workshop, established in 1871, Prosper contributed equally to their early telescope-building efforts, including the 1868 reflector that initiated their systematic sky surveys. Their inseparable partnership from youth propelled their rapid advancement in astronomical instrumentation.[1] Astronomical Careers Paul Henry Paul Henry died on 4 January 1905 in Montrouge, near Paris, France, at the age of 56, after a brief illness caused by cerebral thrombosis.[1] He had outlived his brother Prosper, with whom he had collaborated extensively in astronomical observations, by less than two years; Prosper's death in July 1903 left Paul to carry on their shared projects at the Paris Observatory alone during that period.[5] This continuation underscored Henry's dedication to their pioneering work in celestial photography and asteroid hunting, including their joint discovery of numerous minor planets.[6]Tributes to Henry's career appeared promptly in astronomical publications following his death. An obituary in the Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society (vol. 65, 1905, p. 349) commemorated the brothers' achievements, emphasizing their 1889 admission as Associates of the Society and their legacy in advancing photographic astronomy through instruments like the astrograph, as well as their discovery of 14 asteroids between 1872 and 1882.[2] Similarly, at Henry's funeral on 7 January 1905 in Montrouge, Pierre Puiseux, an astronomer at the Paris Observatory, delivered a discourse that lauded his engineering ingenuity in designing precision optical tools essential to the observatory's operations.[7] This eulogy, published in the Bulletin astronomique (vol. 22, 1905, pp. 97–102), portrayed Henry as a meticulous craftsman whose innovations in telescope construction and astrophotography had enduring impact on French astronomy.[7]These reflections highlighted Henry's personal resilience and professional solitude in his final years, as he persisted with observational duties amid grief over his brother's loss, contributing to the Paris Observatory's cataloging efforts until his health failed.[5] Prosper Henry Prosper Mathieu Henry died on July 25, 1903, in Pralognan, Savoy, France, at the age of 53, from cerebral congestion during a holiday journey in the French Alps.[8][1]An obituary published in Popular Astronomy in 1903, penned by Camille Flammarion, highlighted Henry's exceptional skill in calculating comet orbits, noting his precise and reliable contributions to astronomical ephemerides that aided observers worldwide.[4] Flammarion emphasized Henry's unwavering dedication to science, describing how he and his brother Paul formed an inseparable duo at the Paris Observatory, often appearing as a single entity in their collaborative efforts despite personal sacrifices.[4]Henry's sudden death profoundly impacted the brothers' ongoing joint projects, including their work on photographic astronomy and instrument construction, effectively halting their shared endeavors at the Paris Observatory; Paul Henry, deeply affected, passed away just 18 months later.[6][1]In posthumous recognition, the Nice Observatory honors the Henry brothers' legacy through the 76-centimeter refractor they constructed, a key instrument still associated with their innovative optical designs.[8] Joint Astronomical Work Development of Photographic Techniques In 1885, Paul and Prosper Henry constructed the first large photographic equatorial telescope at the Paris Observatory, a specialized instrument designed for wide-field astrophotography, including searches for comets and asteroids. This astrograph featured a 34 cm aperture objective lens with a 3.4-meter focal length, achieving an f/10 ratio that provided sharp images across a 2° field of view.[3][8]The Henrys employed gelatin dry plates—sensitized with gelatino-bromide emulsions—for their exposures, which could last up to one hour to capture faint celestial objects.[1] To identify moving bodies like asteroids against fixed stars, they developed star trail techniques, where imperfect equatorial tracking produced short streaks from stars while asteroids appeared as distinct points. These methods allowed for the detection of objects down to 14th magnitude, revolutionizing the identification of solar system transients.[9]Key innovations included custom mounting systems with equatorial drives for precise sidereal tracking, compensating for Earth's rotation during long exposures. The brothers collaborated closely with optician Paul Gautier on lens designs, optimizing achromatic objectives for blue-sensitive wavelengths to minimize aberrations and ensure image sharpness at the f/10 ratio. These advancements, tested extensively in 1884–1885 prototypes, enabled reliable photography over large sky areas.[8]This work at the Paris Observatory in 1884–1885 represented an early systematic application of photography to celestial surveys, laying the groundwork for international projects like the Carte du Ciel. The astrograph's design was later standardized, with the Henrys constructing over half of the 17 instruments produced for global observatories. Their techniques contributed to asteroid surveys through improved imaging, though their personal discoveries were visual.[10] Asteroid Discoveries Between 1872 and 1882, the brothers Paul Henry and Prosper Henry discovered a total of 14 asteroids while working at the Paris Observatory, with each credited for seven discoveries.[2] Their systematic visual searches along the zodiacal belt, using equatorial telescopes, allowed for rapid identification of faint moving objects against the stellar background.[3] This methodical approach contributed to the expanding catalog of minor planets during a period when the known asteroid population grew from around 50 in 1870 to over 200 by 1880.The asteroids discovered by Prosper Henry (P. M. Henry) were: 125 Liberatrix (1872 September 11), 127 Johanna (1872 November 5), 148 Gallia (1875 April 7), 153 Hilda (1875 November 2), 172 Baucis (1877 January 8), 175 Andromache (1877 March 1), and 199 Byblis (1879 July 8). Those by Paul Henry (P. P. Henry) were: 126 Velleda (1872 November 5), 136 Austria (1872 December 18), 164 Eva (1876 July 12), 176 Iduna (1877 October 14), 211 Helene (1879 August 27), 219 Thusnelda (1880 May 17), and 227 Philosophia (1882 August 12).[2][11] Notable examples include 125 Liberatrix, marking their first success; 148 Gallia, named after ancient Gaul in honor of their French origins; and 164 Eva, a main-belt asteroid approximately 102 km in diameter. These discoveries were confirmed through follow-up observations and orbital calculations, often in collaboration with other astronomers at Paris.The Henry brothers' work extended to photographic surveys after their involvement in the Carte du Ciel project from 1887 onward, where they pioneered astrographic techniques at Paris and contributed instruments to observatories including Nice. These methods captured asteroids on plates, facilitating identifications and orbital refinements.[3] Their contributions helped establish standardized photographic catalogs, enhancing the precision of minor planet studies and supporting the Carte du Ciel project. Overall, their 14 asteroids added significantly to the late 19th-century understanding of the asteroid belt's density and distribution, influencing subsequent systematic surveys.[3] Legacy and Recognition Awards and Honors The Henry brothers received numerous recognitions for their pioneering work in astronomical photography and instrument-making.Jointly, Paul and Prosper Henry were awarded the Prix Lacaze by the Académie des Sciences in 1887 in acknowledgment of their extensive contributions to astronomy.[8] In 1889, they were elected Associates of the Royal Astronomical Society, honoring their collaborative advancements in celestial observation.[12]Paul Henry was appointed a Chevalier of the Légion d'honneur, reflecting his distinguished service to French astronomy.[13] Prosper Henry similarly received the title of Chevalier de la Légion d'honneur in 1889.[14]Posthumously, the brothers' legacy was commemorated through several astronomical namings. The lunar impact crater Henry Frères (23.5°S, 58.9°W), with a diameter of 42 km, was named in their honor by the International Astronomical Union.[15] On Mars, Henry Crater (10.9°N, 23.3°E), measuring 170 km across, was designated after Paul Henry as a tribute to his 19th-century astronomical achievements.[16] Additionally, minor planet (1516) Henry was named for both brothers, recognizing their discoveries of 14 asteroids and their construction of key telescopes, including the 76-cm refractor at the Nice Observatory.[17] Influence on Astronomy The Henry brothers' innovations in astrophotography, particularly their design of the astrograph—a specialized telescope with a photographic objective and guiding system—enabled unprecedented long-exposure imaging of faint stars and nebulae, capturing details like 1421 stars in the Pleiades cluster during three-hour exposures in the 1880s.[8] This instrument's optical precision, praised by contemporaries such as David Gill of the Cape Observatory, facilitated the transition to systematic photographic surveys, shifting astronomy from subjective visual observations to objective, reproducible data capture.[8]Their astrograph required instruments.[8] This endeavor not only standardized astrophotographic practices worldwide but also laid the groundwork for 20th-century sky surveys, including the Palomar Observatory Sky minor planet elements for dynamical studies.At Nice Observatory, where Paul and Prosper Henry relocated in 1888 to advance Carte du Ciel work and asteroid searches, their hands-on expertise with photographic instruments influenced local astronomical practices and the training of observers in precise exposure and plate processing techniques.[8] Their publications, including reports on nebula discoveries in the Pleiades (1886), provided methodological guidance on photographic reduction—converting plate images to astrometric coordinates—helping standardize pre-digital data analysis across observatories.[3]Overall, the brothers' contributions accelerated the adoption of photography in astronomy, enabling faster accumulation of celestial catalogs and discoveries in the decades before --- [126 Velleda - Wikipedia] ---
126 Velleda is a main-belt asteroid. It is probably a rather typical, albeit sizable, S-type asteroid. Named for Veleda, a priestess and prophet of the Germanic tribe of the Bructeri.  It was discovered by Paul Henry on 5 November 1872, in Paris, France. It was his first credited discovery. He and his brother Prosper Henry discovered a total of 14 asteroids.
This body is orbiting the Sun with a period of 3.81 years and an eccentricity (ovalness) of 0.11. The orbital plane is inclined by 2.9° to the plane of the ecliptic. It has a cross-section diameter of ~45 km. This asteroid rotates once every 5.36 hours. During each rotation the brightness varies by 0.22 magnitudes.


--- [Paul Henry and Prosper Henry] ---
Paul-Pierre Henry (21 August 1848 – 4 January 1905) and his brother Prosper-Mathieu Henry (10 December 1849 – 25 July 1903) were French opticians and astronomers.
They made refracting telescopes and instruments for observatories, and were involved in the origin of the Carte du Ciel project.
Between the two of them, they discovered a total of 14 asteroids.  The Minor Planet Center credits their discoveries under "P. P. Henry" and "P. M. Henry", respectively. The lunar crater Henry Frères (Henry brothers) and the Martian crater Henry are named after them. They were jointly awarded the first Valz Prize in 1877 for their sky charts designed to facilitate the search for minor planets.
