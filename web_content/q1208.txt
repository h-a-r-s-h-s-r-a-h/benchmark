--- [Raquel Meller - Wikipedia] ---
Francisca Romana Marqués López (9 March 1888 – 26 July 1962), better known as Raquel Meller, was a Spanish diseuse, cuplé, and tonadilla singer and actress.
She was an international star in the 1920s and 1930s, appearing in several films and touring Europe and the Americas. A vaudeville performer, she sang the original versions of well known songs such as "La Violetera" and "El relicario", both written by José Padilla Sánchez.


--- [Spanish celebrity couple from last century embraced power of myth] ---
Spanish celebrity couple from last century embraced power of myth LAWRENCE — Think for a moment of the relationship between Arthur Miller and Marilyn Monroe — he a celebrated playwright and she a younger actress. That’s the rough equivalent of the union between Spain’s Raquel Meller and Enrique Gómez Carrillo in the early 20th century. The sparks of synergy — and conflict — generated by the (mis)match of two such strong personalities are the subject of a new scholarly journal article by Margot Versteeg, professor in the University of Kansas Department of Spanish & Portuguese. In “Myths of Stardom: Raquel Meller through the Eyes of Enrique Gómez Carrillo,” in the journal Anales de Literatura Española Contemporánea (ALEC), Versteeg sketches Gómez Carrillo’s contributions to Meller’s early career on the “variety stage” before her film and recording work that made her an international star. Versteeg said it’s part of a larger book project she’s working on about late 19th and early 20th century women performance artists in Spain. “That is a period that is obsessed with female artists,” Versteeg said. “A little earlier, the focus was mostly on prostitutes. Now it’s on performers. In literature, in poetry, in plays, there are so many dancers, singers and variety artists ... I read a lot of books with and about singers, dancers, opera stars, etc., and I came to the conclusion that these women were often used to address emergent societal questions. They were kind of a crucible for other issues that were going around, related to discussions about art and commerce, body and nation, because they represented modernity and change. They crossed borders like the private and the public sphere.” Meller was certainly a border-crosser, Versteeg said. She started off singing bawdy songs in small theaters, then changed her style to a more demure, serious one, but always with a sense of drama to which audiences responded, Versteeg said. Contemporaries believed Meller could have been a great stage actress, but she preferred to be a popular performer, Versteeg said, not least for the money — more than she would ever earn on the serious stage. “Star performers like Meller had an independent lifestyle, and they brought it to celebrity culture,” Versteeg said. “They endorsed products like perfumes and promoted all kinds of fake stories about their lives. Many such stars engaged in celebrity marriages. It's just like today.” Meller was already a star when she married Carrillo, 10 years her senior. “Carrillo did not really make it into the literary canon,” Versteeg said, “but he was a famous literary critic and journalist who promoted a great many of his fellow writers. He was smart and very well connected. He came from Guatemala. He lived in Paris. He lived in Madrid. When Carrillo married Meller, his literary career had already peaked. The marriage got him back in the spotlight.” Carrillo extolled his wife’s virtues in a book about her (he probably also ghost-wrote her autobiography, Versteeg said) and helped Meller gain important bookings in big theaters in Paris and London. And yet something about his prose rings false to Versteeg’s ear today. “Instead of saying that she's embodying everything that she's thinking about, how she ... carefully stages her songs, in his books he makes her like she is more than a woman. She's ethereal ... divine. He doesn't present her as a flesh-and-blood person who knows exactly what she does, but he makes her like a force of nature. This doesn’t give her the credit she deserves,” Versteeg said. In the autobiographical text, on the other hand, Carrillo took another route and portrayed his rather uneducated wife as far more erudite than she actually was, according to the KU professor. Perhaps it’s no surprise the marriage lasted less than five years (1919-1922). “He was an interesting person, and she certainly was, too,” Versteeg said. “But they were both very complicated. They were mismatched. That's what I read over and over again. That’s also what people said about them.” Nevertheless, by the time they split, Meller’s career had been energized and elevated to a new level, setting the stage for even greater fame to come.

- Born - Died - Birth nameFrancisca Marqués López - Raquel Meller was born in Tarazona, Zaragoza, Aragón (Spain) from a very poor family. Since her parents could not afford her education she was sent to live with an aunt who was a nun at a convent. At age 12 she returned with her parents who were then residing in Barcelona and shortly after began working as a seamstress in a shop that catered to many show business personalities. In 1907 she left the shop to start singing "cuplés" ("couplets") using the name "La Bella Raquel". Although these songs were considered indecent and were initially performed in venues attended by men only, Raquel with her beauty and charismatic presence raised the "cuplé" genre to a more respectable art making it acceptable to families. After a love affair with a German named Moeller, Raquel adopted his last name changing it to Meller to make it sound Spanish. The quality of her voice was a source of debate, but for her fans and theater-goers in general Meller could do no wrong. "Raquel Meller is a genius." exclaimed at one point Sarah Bernhardt. Songs such as "La Violetera", "El Relicario", "Flor de Te", "Mimosa", "Flor del Mal", etc., became standards thanks to her interpretation. She was the first Spanish popular singer to succeed in Europe and the Americas including the United States where her recordings enjoyed great popularity and her live concerts in 1926 broke box-office records in the most important American cities. At the height of her popularity she endorsed several products as well as backing many articles named after her from dolls, to perfume, all of which supplemented her already high income making her one of the wealthiest women in the world. She was also a hit on the big screen, starring in important films such as_Violettes impériales (1923)_and Carmen (1926). Meller filmed mostly in France but in 1927 she starred in a short sound feature for Movietone Fox in New York in which she sang four of her hits. Charles Chaplin, a big Meller fan, offered her the part of Josephine de Beauharnais in a film he was planning based on life of Napoleon Bonaparte but Raquel could not find time for the project. A few years later Chaplin wanted her for the part of the blind girl in _City Lights (1931)_but Raquel was now involved in a play written especially for her by Maurice Rostand in which she could achieve her dream of becoming a serious stage actress in a respectable Paris theater. Chaplin replaced her with Virginia Cherrill but he kept Raquel's theme "La Violetera" in the film music score taking credit for its composition which resulted in a law suit by the song's composer Jose Padilla. Raquel continued performing and was a big draw in vaudeville circuits well into the 1940s. Her private life was followed with great interest by the media and the public. She was imperious, ruthless (especially with the competition), lovable, funny, temperamental, generous, witty and totally egomaniac. Her love life was not as turbulent as many believed but among her many admirers there were royalty, heads of state, intellectuals, painters and assorted VIPs. In 1919 she married celebrated Guatemalan journalist diplomat Enrique Gomez Carrillo and adopted a baby girl in Buenos Aires naming her Elena. However after a couple of years of honeymoon bliss came a collision of both temperaments and they divorced in 1922. She had another short lived marriage in 1940 this time to a French businessman named Edmond Sayac and they were together long enough to adopt a baby boy whom they named Jordi Enric. By then Raquel Meller's career was over, only to surface again in 1957 in the wake of Sara Montiel's box-office success in the films_El último cuplé (1957)_ and_La violetera (1958)_,in which Montiel revived Raquel's greatest hits. As with other rivals in the past, Meller became Montiel's nemesis, but by then a new generation that simply did not know her just plain ignored her. She attempted several comebacks but all were critical and commercial failures. Bitterly, she retired and stayed out of the public eye until her death in Barcelona on July 26, 1962. A plan to film her story starring Sara Montiel (of course) was foiled by Meller's relatives, but some of her story made it to the big screen anyway in Montiel's vehicle La reina del Chantecler (1962). Most of Raquel's recordings, considered lost for years, have been trickling out on CDs. However her films remain unedited in home video formats and are seldom shown in silent films revivals or festivals. Raquel Meller has been the subject of many books and articles in Europe. There are streets named after her in France and Spain, while her statue commands a plaza with her name in Barcelona. It is inexcusable that film preservationists and the cinemateques of France, Spain and the United States have shown little or no interest in Meller's filmography. Without these films it is impossible to assess Raquel's personal charisma which was the source of her enormous success and fame.- IMDb mini biography by: M.O. ''Raquel Meller'' is a Spanish singer and actress known for her pioneering contributions to the cuplé genre and her international stardom in the early 20th century music hall scene. She captivated audiences with her dramatic interpretations of popular songs, blending vocal expressiveness, theatrical gesture, and visual flair, which made her one of the most celebrated performers of her era in Spain and France.Born Francisca Romana Marqués López in Tarazona, Aragon, on 9 March 1888, she adopted the stage name Raquel Meller and began her career in Barcelona's café-concert venues before achieving breakthrough success in the 1910s. Her signature pieces, including "La Violetera" and "El Relicario", became enduring classics, with "La Violetera" notably inspiring Charlie Chaplin's use of the melody in his 1931 film City Lights. Meller's performances at prestigious venues like the Olympia in Paris established her as a leading figure in European variety theater, where she was often known as the Queen of the Cuplé.Throughout the 1920s, she toured extensively across Europe and the Americas, recording numerous songs and appearing in several films, both silent and early sound productions. Her influence extended beyond performance to cultural impact, as she helped popularize Spanish song styles internationally during a time of growing global interest in European popular music. Meller continued performing into the mid-20th century and passed away in Barcelona on 26 July 1962. Early life Childhood and family background Raquel Meller was born Francisca Romana Marqués López on March 9, 1888, in Tarazona, a town in the province of Zaragoza, Aragón, Spain. [1][2] She grew up in a very poor family in rural Aragón, where economic hardships defined much of her early life. [3] Her parents struggled to care for her, leading to arrangements for her care outside the home due to their limited means. [2][4]As a child, she received part of her early education in Montpellier, France, under the care of an aunt who was a nun, remaining there until around 1900. [1][2] The family's poverty restricted her access to extensive formal education, shaping a childhood marked by financial constraints and modest circumstances. [3][4] Due to ongoing economic difficulties in her region, she later moved to Barcelona seeking work opportunities. [2] Move to Barcelona and early performances Raquel Meller, born Francisca Marqués López, returned to Barcelona around 1900 after spending her early education in Montpellier, France, under the care of her aunt, a nun. [5] Her family had settled in the city earlier, and upon rejoining them she contributed to the household by working as a seamstress in a workshop that made and repaired dresses for popular artists. [5]While sewing, her singing caught the attention of the artist Marta Oliver, who overheard her humming and encouraged her to pursue a stage career. [5] With Oliver's support, she made her debut in 1907 at a modest music hall in Barcelona under the name "La Bella Raquel." [5] Shortly afterward, she adopted the stage name Raquel Meller, reportedly inspired by a brief romance with a man of German origin bearing a similar surname. [5]She began performing in Barcelona's salas de fiesta and small venues, where her voice and presence gradually gained notice among local audiences. [5] This early activity in the city's modest entertainment scene built her initial reputation as a singer in the emerging cuplé tradition. [5] Her growing popularity in Barcelona soon led to broader opportunities beyond the city. [5] Musical career Breakthrough as a cuplé singer Raquel Meller achieved her breakthrough as a cuplé singer during the 1910s, elevating the genre from its origins in risqué, men-only venues to a more widely accepted form of popular entertainment in Spain. [6][1] After initial performances in Barcelona, she appeared in Madrid, debuting at the Trianón Palace in June 1911, where she shared the bill with established figures such as Amalia Molina, Pastora Imperio, and La Goya. [7] This engagement marked her entry into the capital's music halls and theaters, contributing to her growing reputation across Spanish vaudeville and cabaret circuits. [2]Although she lacked a powerful vocal range, Meller distinguished herself through exceptional expressiveness, dramatic interpretation, and theatrical delivery, acting out her songs like a diseuse rather than relying solely on singing. [1][2] She specialized in cuplé—often featuring risqué or sentimental themes—along with tonadilla, infusing them with finesse, spirituality, and subtle gestural language that conveyed intense emotion and attracted diverse audiences, including women and higher society. [2][1] Her charismatic presence and ability to elevate the genre's artistic level through elegance and good taste helped solidify her fame in Spain during this period. [7][1]Her success in Madrid and other Spanish cities during the 1910s eventually drew international attention, leading to invitations abroad. [2] Signature songs and repertoire Raquel Meller's repertoire primarily consisted of cuplés, a genre of Spanish popular song that she elevated through her distinctive blend of singing and acting. Her performances emphasized dramatic expression, storytelling, and emotional intensity, often incorporating theatrical gestures, facial expressions, and vocal dynamics to transform songs into miniature dramatic scenes. [2]Her most iconic and signature song was "La Violetera" (The Violet Seller), composed by José Padilla with lyrics by Eduardo Montesinos in 1914. Although premiered by another singer, Meller popularized it in Spain and France during her breakthrough years, rendering it inseparable from her image through her poignant and dramatic interpretation. [8][9] The song became so emblematic of her that Charles Chaplin drew influence from her rendition for his 1931 film City Lights, using it as a leitmotif. [9]Another cornerstone of her repertoire was "El Relicario" (also known as "My Toreador" or "The Locket"), composed by José Padilla in 1914. Meller's rendition captivated audiences, with her hypnotic delivery culminating in sobbing the final verse instead of singing it, heightening its emotional impact. [2][10] This song, like "La Violetera", was composed with her vocal and dramatic style in mind and became a staple of her performances. [11]Meller also made "Mala Entraña" one of her signature pieces, tearfully conveying themes of frustration and betrayal in a way that resonated deeply with listeners and reinforced her reputation as an actress-singer. [2] Other notable works in her repertoire included "Nena" and "La Perricholi", which she performed and recorded during the 1920s and 1930s, aiding the dissemination of her music through sheet music publications and early phonograph records. [12] Her recordings of these songs preserved her expressive style for wider audiences and contributed to their enduring popularity in Spanish popular music. Film career Entry into cinema and silent films Raquel Meller entered cinema in 1919, appearing in her first silent film in Spain while already established as a celebrated cuplé singer. Her debut was in Los arlequines de seda y oro (also known as La gitana blanca) (1919), in which she played La gitanilla blanca.[13] This early appearance allowed her to translate her dramatic stage presence and expressive performance style to the screen, marking the beginning of her transition from musical theater to film.[13]By the early 1920s, Meller shifted primarily to French productions, where she achieved greater prominence in the silent era.[13] She appeared in Flanders under Philip II (1923), playing Conception de la Playa Serra.[13] Her major breakthrough came with the French silent film Violettes impériales (1924), in which she starred as the lead Violetta, a role that capitalized on her charisma and emotional range honed through years of singing and acting on stage.[13]Meller continued starring in French silent cinema throughout the mid-1920s, including La terre promise (1925) as Lia, La ronde de nuit (1925) as Princesse Stefania, and Presto, abbracciatemi! (1927). She took the title role in Jacques Feyder's Carmen (1926), produced as a star vehicle by Films Albatros.[13] Her later silent appearances included Nocturne (1927), a short film in which she played Rosa Martinez de Almeida, and La venenosa (1928) as Miss Liana.[13] Throughout this period she maintained her singing career alongside her screen work.[13] Sound films and later roles Raquel Meller made her first foray into sound cinema with a pioneering short film produced by Fox Movietone in New York in 1926–1927, where she performed several of her most famous songs in one of the initial public demonstrations of synchronized sound technology. [14] This brief appearance marked an early milestone in her adaptation to the emerging medium, though her primary career remained rooted in live performances and international tours. [15]Her most significant sound-era role came in the French production Violettes impériales (Imperial Violets) in 1932, a talkie remake directed by Henry Roussel of her earlier silent success from the 1920s. [16] The film, which featured Meller in a leading role alongside Suzanne Bianchetti and Georges Péclet, revisited the historical story of a gypsy girl aided by Empress Eugénie and received positive attention upon release. [16]Meller's involvement in cinema diminished thereafter, as she shifted emphasis back to her stage work and extensive international tours across Europe and the Americas. [17] In 1936 she began filming Lola Triana, but the project was abandoned due to the outbreak of the Spanish Civil War. [18] No further completed film roles followed, reflecting a broader trend among performers of her generation who prioritized live audiences amid the challenges of transitioning fully to sound film. [17] International stardom Tours in Europe and the Americas Raquel Meller's international career took off in Paris, where she arrived in 1919 and quickly became a celebrated figure in the city's popular music scene. [19] She performed at venues such as the Concert Mayol and later the Olympia, captivating audiences with her emotive delivery of cuplés and songs like "El relicario" and "La Violetera". [19] Her success in post-World War I Paris, lasting through the mid-1920s, established her as a leading exponent of Spanish song in France, with critics and the public praising her dramatic style and exotic appeal. [19] This period marked her breakthrough beyond Spain, leading to further engagements across Europe.Meller extended her reach to the Americas in the mid-1920s. She toured South America, performing to enthusiastic crowds in cities including Buenos Aires and Rio de Janeiro. [20] In 1926, she undertook a significant tour of the United States, where she gave four performances a week for four weeks at a reported $6,000 per week, appearing in major theaters and drawing attention for her unique blend of Spanish repertoire and theatrical presence. [20] She also came to the United States in 1926 as a celebrated European actress-singer, contributing to her visibility in American entertainment circles. [21]These tours across Europe and the Americas positioned Meller as one of the earliest Spanish performers to achieve widespread international stardom, paving the way for later generations of artists from Spain in global popular entertainment. Recognition and cultural influence Raquel Meller gained widespread recognition as one of the foremost interpreters of the cuplé genre, elevating it from its origins in Spanish cabarets to an internationally appreciated form of popular entertainment during the early 20th century. [22] Her refined stage presence and sophisticated delivery helped make cuplé acceptable to bourgeois audiences, including women, thereby broadening its social reach and contributing to its transformation into a more polished and widely embraced style. [23] By performing successfully in Paris and other European centers, she bridged Spanish musical traditions with French chanson, facilitating the genre's crossover appeal across national borders and helping integrate Iberian elements into continental popular culture. [22]Her international success marked her as a pioneering female performer in early 20th-century media, becoming one of the first Spanish popular singers to achieve substantial acclaim throughout Europe and the Americas, including the United States, where her recordings found significant popularity. [4] This stature positioned her as a trailblazer for women in entertainment, demonstrating the potential for female artists to attain global stardom in music and early film at a time when such opportunities were limited.Meller's cultural influence extended into cinema through Charlie Chaplin's incorporation of her signature song "La Violetera" as a central leitmotif in his 1931 film City Lights, where the melody recurs as the theme for the blind flower-seller character. [24] Chaplin reportedly selected the tune as a tribute to Meller after unsuccessful attempts to cast her in the film, underscoring her impact beyond Spain on international filmmakers and highlighting how her work resonated in Hollywood and global popular culture. [24]
