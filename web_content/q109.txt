--- [Reza Aslan - Wikipedia] ---
Reza Aslan (Persian: رضا اصلان, IPA: [ˈɾezɒː æsˈlɒːn]; born May 3, 1972) is an Iranian-American scholar of sociology, writer, and television host. A convert to evangelical Christianity from Shia Islam as a youth, Aslan eventually reverted to Islam but continued to write about Christianity. He has written five books on religion: No God but God: The Origins, Evolution, and Future of Islam; Beyond Fundamentalism: Confronting Religious Extremism in the Age of Globalization; Zealot: The Life and Times of Jesus of Nazareth; God: A Human History; and in 2022 An American Martyr in Persia: The Epic Life and Tragic Death of Howard Baskerville.
Aslan has worked for television, including a documentary series exploring world religions on CNN called Believer, and served as an executive producer on the HBO drama series The Leftovers. Aslan is a member of the American Academy of Religion and the International Qur'anic Studies Association. He is a professor of creative writing at University of California, Riverside, and a board member of the National Iranian American Council (NIAC).

--- [Why Reza Aslan 2014 Intersections Honoree Still Matters for Religious ...] ---
Reza Aslan is a polarizing guy. Whether you love his scholarly take on Jesus or you’re still thinking about that infamous, cringe-worthy Fox News interview where he had to repeatedly explain why a Muslim would write about Christianity, he stays in the headlines. But if you rewind to a specific moment in Washington D.C., you’ll find the Reza Aslan 2014 Intersections honoree story. It wasn't just a trophy on a mantle. Honestly, it was a snapshot of a time when we still felt like "interfaith dialogue" could actually fix something. Intersections International gave him the award because, frankly, he was the loudest voice in the room trying to bridge the gap between the Middle East and the West. It wasn't just about his books. It was about his presence. The Night Reza Aslan Became a 2014 Intersections Honoree The setting was the Intersections International Global Honors gala. If you aren't familiar with Intersections, they’re a multi-faith organization based in New York, born out of the Collegiate Churches of New York. They don't just do "kumbaya" circles. They get into the grit of social justice, veteran rights, and global conflict. Giving the Reza Aslan 2014 Intersections honoree title to a man who was essentially the "rockstar" of religious studies at the time made total sense. Aslan had just come off the massive success of Zealot: The Life and Times of Jesus of Nazareth. He was everywhere. He was the guy people looked to when they wanted to understand how religion shapes identity without falling into the trap of fundamentalism. During the event, the vibe was about "The Power of Voice." Aslan’s voice, specifically. The organization recognized that he wasn't just writing history; he was actively challenging the narratives that lead to Islamophobia and religious tribalism. He spoke about how we often use religion as a wall when it should be a window. It’s a nice metaphor, but in 2014, with the rise of various extremist groups globally, it felt like a heavy responsibility. Why 2014 was a Turning Point for Aslan You have to remember what the world looked like back then. The Arab Spring’s optimism had mostly soured. The Syrian Civil War was getting darker by the day. In the U.S., the rhetoric surrounding Islam was hitting a fever pitch. Aslan was the guy on CNN and Comedy Central. He used his platform to argue that religion is a "language" people use to express their values, rather than a fixed set of instructions that dictate behavior. This nuance is why Intersections tapped him. They saw a scholar who could communicate with the masses. They saw someone who wasn't afraid to get his hands dirty in the cultural mud. He wasn’t the only one honored, of course. The gala usually highlights a few people across different sectors. But Aslan was the headliner. He represented the intellectual fight against bigotry. Beyond the Gala: The Ripple Effect of the Award Does an award actually change anything? Maybe not the world, but it validates the work. For the Reza Aslan 2014 Intersections honoree recognition, it served as a stamp of approval from the faith community itself. It showed that even traditional religious organizations saw his "unorthodox" approach as vital. Aslan has always pushed this idea that we need to stop looking at religions as "true" or "false" and start looking at them as "useful" or "not useful" for the human condition. He’s a storyteller. That’s his whole brand. Whether it’s his production company, Aslan Media, or his TV shows like Believer, he’s trying to show the human face of the "other." The Criticisms he Faced It wasn't all applause. Some critics felt that by honoring Aslan, groups like Intersections were glossing over his more controversial rhetorical flourishes. He’s been known to get heated on social media. He’s been called out for his "scholarship" by other academics who think he oversimplifies complex theological histories for a mass audience. But that’s the trade-off, isn't it? If you want to reach the person sitting on their couch in middle America, you can’t talk like a peer-reviewed journal. You have to talk like a human. Aslan knows this. He leans into it. The 2014 honor was a nod to that bridge-building, even if the bridge was a little shaky at times. The Core Philosophy of Intersections International To understand why Aslan was the perfect fit, you have to look at what Intersections actually does. They focus on three pillars: - Global Social Justice: They work in places like Pakistan and Palestine. - Service to those impacted by war: They have programs for veterans coming home. - The Arts: They believe art can say things politics can't. Aslan fits into all three. He’s an Iranian-American who has lived the immigrant experience. He’s written about the effects of war and occupation. And he uses the "art" of narrative to change minds. When he stood up to accept that award, he didn't just thank his agent. He talked about the necessity of empathy. He argued that if you can't see yourself in the story of someone who prays differently than you, then you aren't looking hard enough. Honestly, it’s a message that feels even more necessary now than it did over a decade ago. Practical Takeaways from Aslan's 2014 Recognition If we look back at the Reza Aslan 2014 Intersections honoree moment, what can we actually apply to our lives today? It’s easy to dismiss awards as self-congratulatory fluff, but there are real lessons here for anyone trying to navigate a divided world. First, identify the "language" of the people you disagree with. Aslan’s premise is that we all want the same basic things—safety, meaning, community—but we describe them using different religious or cultural vocabularies. If you can translate those vocabularies, the conflict usually shrinks. Second, don't be afraid of the "middle ground." Aslan is often attacked by both the far right and the far left. He’s too religious for some and not religious enough for others. But the "Intersections" of life are where the actual progress happens. It's messy. It's uncomfortable. But it's where the work is. Third, use your platform. Whatever size it is. Aslan used his to humanize a religion that was being demonized. You can use yours to humanize the person across the street. Moving Forward with the Intersections Logic If you’re looking to engage in the kind of work that earned Aslan that 2014 honor, start by diversifying your "narrative intake." Read a book on a faith you know nothing about. Watch a documentary about a culture you’ve only seen in the news. The work of Intersections International continues, and the issues Aslan highlighted in 2014—identity, belonging, and the power of story—haven't gone away. If anything, the "intersections" have become even more crowded and complicated. Instead of looking for reasons to win an argument, look for the "intersecting" values. It’s a lot harder than just being right, but it’s the only way we actually move the needle. Actionable Steps for Dialogue - Read Zealot or No god but God: Regardless of your own faith, these books provide a historical framework that strips away the purely emotional reactions we often have to religion. - Support Multi-Faith Initiatives: Organizations like Intersections International or the Interfaith Youth Core (now Interfaith America) provide actual toolkits for how to talk to people who fundamentally disagree with you. - Audit Your Media Diet: If your social media feed is an echo chamber of people who think exactly like you, you aren't at an intersection. You're in a cul-de-sac. Follow three people this week whose religious or political views challenge your own. - Practice "Narrative Empathy": The next time you see a headline about a global conflict, look for the personal story behind the statistics. This was the core of Aslan's 2014 message—that individual stories are the only thing that can break down systemic prejudice. The 2014 honors gala is a memory now, but the intellectual challenge it posed remains. We are still living in a world of walls. Building windows takes a lot more effort. Next Steps: To deepen your understanding of these themes, explore the current projects at Intersections International, specifically their work on the "Us & Them" initiative which tackles polarization. You can also review Reza Aslan's more recent documentaries to see how his approach to religious storytelling has evolved since the 2014 award. By engaging with these resources, you move from being a passive consumer of information to an active participant in the "intersections" of our modern world.

Aslan is a tenured Professor of Creative Writing at the University of California, Riverside, and holds a PhD in the Sociology of Religions from the University of California, Santa Barbara, a Master of Theological Studies from Harvard University, and a Master of Fine Arts from the University of Iowa, where he was named the Truman Capote Fellow in Fiction. He is the author of over four hundred scholarly articles on various aspects on the mind, published in journals ranging from Artificial Intelligence and Behavioral and Brain Sciences to Poetics Today and The Journal of Aesthetics and Art Criticism. William Schweiker Edward L. Ryerson Distinguished Service Professor of Theological Ethics, University of Chicago School of Divinity Ordained minister, United Methodist Church William Schweiker is the Edward L. Ryerson Distinguished Service Professor of Theological Ethics at the University of Chicago and an ordained minister in the United Methodist Church. Schweiker has written extensively on ethics and hermeneutical philosophy, and also theories of responsibility, religious identity, and the moral and religious complexity of our global information age. Concerned with the origins of religion, in cultural and social processes as well evolutionary ones, Schweiker has also been keen to explore the potential of religious texts and practices to open up moral spaces for future action and human cooperation. In a word, the origin of religion need not determine its future evolution. These concerns have involved Schweiker in ongoing work on the relation of religion and morality's, past, present and possible future, as well as developing an intellectual and ethical stance called “Theological Humanism” that, on one hand, challenges the religions to live by their own most humane insights and thereby to engage in critical self-examination, and, on the other hand, critiques views of human life that impoverish the complexity of human existence. In this way, Schweiker seeks to be a person of reason and faith. His books include Mimetic Reflections: A Study in Hermeneutics, Theology and Ethics (1990); Responsibility and Christian Ethics (1995); Power, Value and Conviction: Theological Ethics in the Postmodern Age (1998); Theological Ethics and Global Dynamics: In the Time of Many Worlds (2004); Religion and the Human Future: An Essay in Theological Humanism (2008, with David E. Klemm); Dust that Breathes: Christian Faith and the New Humanisms (2010); and, forthcoming, Religious Ethics: Meaning and Method. In 2014, Professor Schweiker was awarded an Honorary Doctorate from the University of Uppsala in Uppsala, Sweden. He was a Mercator Professor, Universität Heidelberg, Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft (2012-2013). He was the 2015–2016 President of the Society of Christian Ethics and the Director of The Enhancing Life Project (2014-2017), supported with a generous grant from the John Templeton Foundation.
