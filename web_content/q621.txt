--- [Polanyi Medal - Wikipedia] ---
The Polanyi Medal is a biennial award of the Royal Society of Chemistry for outstanding contributions to the field of gas kinetics. The medal is presented at the International Symposium on Gas Kinetics after a plenary lecture given by the prize winner.
The award is named after the Hungarian-British polymath Michael Polanyi, 1891-1976, whose research helped to establish the topic of gas kinetics and reaction dynamics. His son, John Polanyi, received the Polanyi Medal in 1988.

The Polanyi medal is named after Professor Michael Polanyi, 1891-1976, whose research helped to define the modern subject of gas kinetics and reaction dynamics. A native of Hungary, Polanyi received his PhD from Budapest University in 1917, on the subject of thermodynamics of adsorption. After working in Berlin, Polanyi emigrated to England in 1933 to become Professor of Physcial Chemistry at Manchester University, a position he held until 1948, when he moved to philosophy and became Professor of Social Science, also at Manchester. In 1959 he bacame a Senior Research Fellow at Merton College, Oxford. Polanyi published over 200 scientific papers between 1916-1948 on a wide range of topics: adsorption, reaction kinetics, x-ray diffraction to name a few. Polanyi's laboratory at Manchester attracted students and established scientists from all over the world. After the 2nd world war, Polanyi increasingly turned his attention from science to economics and Philosophy. His son, John Polanyi, was a recipient of the Polanyi Medal in 1988.

Polanyi Medal History Origins and Establishment The Polanyi Medal was established in 1979 by the Gas Kinetics Group of the Faraday Division of the Royal Society of Chemistry as a biennial award to recognize outstanding contributions to the field of gas kinetics.[1] This initiative arose within the Society's broader efforts to honor foundational advancements in reaction dynamics, a discipline pioneered by the medal's namesake, Michael Polanyi, whose early 20th-century work on adsorption and kinetics laid key groundwork for modern gas-phase studies.[1] The medal specifically aimed to celebrate pioneering research that shaped contemporary understandings of gas kinetics, emphasizing experimental and theoretical breakthroughs in molecular reaction mechanisms.[1] The first recipient was Richard Zare, awarded in 1979 for his innovative spectroscopic techniques in studying state-selected reactions.[1] This inaugural presentation occurred at the Faraday Discussion No. 67 on "Kinetics of State Selected Species" in Birmingham, UK, marking the award's debut in a key forum for kinetics research.[2] Subsequently, the tradition evolved to include presentation at the International Symposium on Gas Kinetics, with the recipient delivering the prestigious Michael Polanyi Lecture as a highlight of the event.[1] This integration underscored the medal's role in fostering international dialogue on gas kinetics from its outset.[1]Evolution of the Award Since its establishment in 1979, the Polanyi Medal has evolved from an irregularly scheduled honor to a firmly biennial award, reflecting the maturation of the field of gas kinetics and the need for consistent recognition of contributions. Initially, awards were not strictly every two years; for instance, medals were presented in consecutive years 1981 and 1982, then in 1984, followed by awards in 1986, 1987 (including a posthumous award to Fred Kaufmann for his pioneering work in molecular beam studies of elementary reactions), and 1988.[1][3] This pattern shifted to consistent biennial awarding starting from 1990 onward.[1] A key development in the award's evolution has been its deepening integration with the International Symposium on Gas Kinetics, where it has been presented since the early years but with growing prominence over time. The medal is now traditionally awarded during the symposium, immediately following the recipient's delivery of the Michael Polanyi Lecture—a plenary session that serves as the event's highlight and underscores the award's role in advancing discussions on gas phase reaction dynamics. This connection enhances the medal's visibility, as the lecture often contributes to the symposium proceedings, fostering broader dissemination of the recipient's insights within the community.[1][3] Administratively, the Polanyi Medal remains under the stewardship of the Royal Society of Chemistry, specifically selected by the committee of its Gas Kinetics Group within the Faraday Division. This governance structure has remained stable since inception, ensuring rigorous peer evaluation focused on outstanding contributions to gas kinetics, from fundamental dynamics to applied processes, while adapting to the field's expansion through biennial cycles aligned with major symposia.[1][3]Description Purpose and Scope The Polanyi Medal serves to recognize individuals who have made outstanding contributions to the field of gas kinetics, particularly in advancing the understanding of reaction dynamics and molecular processes in gases.[1] Awarded biennially by the Gas Kinetics Group of the Royal Society of Chemistry's Faraday Division, it honors work that has significantly shaped the discipline, emphasizing both experimental and theoretical innovations that elucidate the mechanisms of gas-phase reactions.[1] The scope of the medal is focused on gas kinetics, encompassing fundamental studies of gas-phase reaction dynamics and their applications to complex processes.[1] By commemorating the foundational research of Michael Polanyi, whose early 20th-century studies on bimolecular reactions and transition states helped establish the field, the medal underscores the importance of gas kinetics research.[4] This emphasis fosters ongoing innovation in understanding complex molecular processes, from atmospheric reactions to combustion dynamics.[1]Award Presentation and Format The Polanyi Medal is presented biennially during the International Symposium on Gas Kinetics, a key event organized by the Gas Kinetics Group of the Royal Society of Chemistry's Faraday Division.[1] This timing aligns with the symposium's schedule, ensuring the award highlights cutting-edge advancements in gas kinetics research within a global community of scientists.[5] Recipients are selected by the Gas Kinetics Group committee based on nominations that highlight exceptional impact in the field.[1] The standard format begins with the recipient delivering the prestigious Michael Polanyi Lecture, a plenary session that serves as the symposium's centerpiece and allows the winner to share their seminal contributions.[3] Following the lecture, the medal is formally conferred in a dedicated ceremony, often lasting about 15 minutes, where the recipient is recognized for their impact on the field.[6] This is typically complemented by a formal conference dinner or reception, fostering networking and celebration among attendees.[6] The 2024 presentation to Dr. Timothy J. Wallington exemplifies this ongoing format, with his Polanyi Lecture held at the University of Leeds, immediately followed by the medal award at the Royal Armouries, and concluding with a conference dinner.[6][7] This structure has remained consistent across recent symposia, emphasizing both intellectual exchange and ceremonial recognition.[5]Recipients Selection Process The selection process for the Polanyi Medal is overseen by the Committee of the Gas Kinetics Group within the Faraday Division of the Royal Society of Chemistry (RSC). This committee solicits nominations from the broader gas kinetics community, including researchers, academics, and professionals in the field.[8][1] Nominations are evaluated based on demonstrated excellence in original research related to gas kinetics, the nominee's impact on advancing the discipline, and their overall career contributions to the field. The process emphasizes innovative and influential work that has shaped understanding of gas-phase reactions and dynamics. For instance, the 2012 medal was awarded to Mario Molina in recognition of his pioneering studies on atmospheric kinetics, particularly the role of chlorofluorocarbons in ozone depletion, which highlighted the practical implications of gas kinetic processes in environmental science.[1] The nomination period typically aligns with RSC prize cycles, opening in the fall and closing several months prior to the biennial International Symposium on Gas Kinetics, allowing time for thorough review. The committee deliberates and selects the recipient, with the decision announced before the symposium to enable preparation of the Michael Polanyi Lecture delivered by the awardee.
